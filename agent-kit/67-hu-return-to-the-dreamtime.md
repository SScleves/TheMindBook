# Ch 9 — Return to the Dreamtime

## In one paragraph
In this closing chapter Talbot asks where the holographic model goes from here — and first shows where the idea has already been. He surveys ancient and traditional systems of thought (Tibetan and Zen Buddhism, Hinduism, Kabbalah, Sufism, Greek philosophy, and shamanic traditions from Hawaii to the Sudan to Australia) and argues that virtually all of them prefigured the model's two central features: Bohm's implicate/explicate structure and the nonlocal, whole-in-every-part organization of reality. He then sketches the model's emerging technical fruits — Zuccarelli's holophonic sound, Prigogine's dissipative structures read as the implicate becoming explicate, and holographic computing — before arguing that the model's deepest claim (that materiality may be an illusion within a vast, sentient nonphysical cosmos) demands a basic restructuring of science: acceptance of psychic and spiritual phenomena, a broader definition of evidence, and a shift from objectivity to participation. Finally, drawing on Ring, Grosso, Strieber, McKenna and others, he speculates that rising NDEs, Marian visions and UFO encounters are signs of an evolutionary thrust toward higher consciousness — a "shamanizing of modern humanity" — pointing back toward the plastic, dreamtime-like reality the aborigines say the earth once was and will be again. The book closes with the aboriginal image: we are just learning how to survive in infinity.

## Core claims
- Talbot argues that the holographic model, though new as a scientific theory, is an ancient idea: several of its aspects were foreshadowed or intuited in "virtually all of the world's philosophical and metaphysical traditions."
- Talbot argues that Bohm's implicate/explicate two-order structure appears independently as the Tibetan void/nonvoid, the Hindu Brahman/maya, the Kabbalistic view of creation as illusory projection of God, and the "vital essence" underlying visible forms that Douglas Sharon calls probably the central concept of shamanism everywhere.
- Talbot argues that the nonlocal, part-contains-the-whole organization of reality was likewise glimpsed by many ancient thinkers — the Sufi "macrocosm is the microcosm," Hermetic "As above, so below," Indra's net of pearls, Fa-Tsang's mirrored candle, Black Elk's "anywhere is the center of the world."
- Talbot suggests these ancient intuitions may even have fed the hologram's invention: Leibniz, arguably influenced by Hua-yen Buddhism, proposed monads and gave the world integral calculus — the mathematics that enabled Gabor to invent the hologram. The idea thus "comes full circle."
- Talbot argues that new discoveries are already flowing from the model: holophonic sound, a possible explanation of dissipative structures as the implicate becoming explicate, and holographically inspired computers — though he stresses the computing advances remain refinements of the mechanistic approach.
- Talbot argues that science, which has "repeatedly fallen short of the mark" in explaining the psychic and spiritual dimensions of human existence, needs a basic restructuring with three components: (1) accepting the existence of psychic and spiritual phenomena; (2) broadening what counts as scientific evidence so mass anecdotal testimony about important matters is not dismissed; (3) replacing detached objectivity with a participatory approach.
- Talbot argues that fear of ridicule may be as much of a stumbling block as disbelief — many psychiatrists privately believe in reincarnation and psychic phenomena but won't say so publicly; what is needed is "the parapsychological equivalent of a Rosa Parks."
- Talbot argues that science applies a double standard: the unproven Big Bang is accepted because it falls into the category of "fashionable things to believe," while equally compelling but unfashionable claims (e.g. that the NDE realm of light is an actual other level of reality) would be attacked.
- Talbot argues that in a holographic, omnijective universe — where the physicist's consciousness affects the particle, the doctor's attitude affects the placebo, the experimenter's mind affects the machine, and the imaginal spills into physical reality — "strict objectivity ceases to be possible."
- Talbot argues (following Ring) that NDEs are increasing and transforming people, evidence of "the shamanizing of modern humanity" and "an evolutionary thrust toward higher consciousness for all humanity"; Marian visions (Grosso) and UFO encounters (Raschke, Vallee, Ring, Strieber) may be parallel signs.
- Talbot speculates on two purposes of this transformation: to prepare us to safely manipulate an increasingly malleable reality (the return of the dreamtime), and to wake us to the claim of many traditions that humanity's true home is not the earth but a nonphysical realm of pure spirit.
- Talbot argues the afterlife realm itself is only a stepping stone — Swedenborg and NDEers describe still higher, more formless planes — and that we are not yet mature enough for that formlessness, which is why life confronts us with only "small doses of the omnijective" (UFOs and similar experiences), and why the beings of light say the purpose of life is to learn.
- Talbot closes with Bohm's warning that our conceptual pigeonholes are of our own making — "out there" is only the indivisible totality, Brahman — and we must be ready to advance "from soul-state to soul-state," as Sri Aurobindo put it, and from illumination to illumination.

## Concepts
- **Dreamtime** — the transcendent reality that the Australian aborigines regard as the true source of the mind (shamans know consciousness is not in the body); the aborigines also say there was a time when the entire world was dreamtime — a far more plastic, fluid reality — and that the day will come when the earth returns to it.
- **Implicate and explicate orders** — Bohm's two basic orders composing the universe; the chapter's touchstone, matched against each tradition's version of a hidden generative level and a visible one.
- **Void and nonvoid** — the Tibetan Buddhist pair: the nonvoid is the reality of visible objects; the void, like the implicate order, is the birthplace of all things, which pour out of it in a "boundless flux." Only the void is real; all forms in the objective world are illusory, existing merely because of the unceasing flux between the two orders. The void is "subtle," "indivisible," "free from distinguishing characteristics," and as a seamless totality cannot be described in words — yet the nonvoid, itself indescribable, still contains "an infinitely vast complex of universes."
- **Brahman** — the Hindu name for the implicate level of reality: formless, yet the birthplace of all forms in visible reality, which appear out of it and enfold back into it in endless flux; sometimes personified as pure consciousness — consciousness being not only a subtler form of matter but more fundamental than matter, so that matter emerged from consciousness rather than the reverse. Talbot pairs this with Bohm's remark that the implicate order can just as easily be called spirit.
- **Maya** — the Hindu term for the transitory, unreal, "second-generation" material universe created by veiled consciousness; maya "severs the united consciousness" so objects seem separate from the self, and is frequently likened to a web.
- **Veiling and projecting powers of consciousness** — per the Vedas, the twin powers through which the physical world is brought into being.
- **Baro ("to create")** — the Hebrew verb which, as the Zohar notes, implies the idea of "creating an illusion" — the Kabbalistic reflection of creation-as-illusion built into the language of Genesis.
- **Macrocosm is the microcosm** — the twelfth-century Sufi formula for the nonlocal whole-in-every-part organization of reality; variants include Hermes Trismegistus's "the without is like the within of things; the small is like the large," the alchemists' "As above, so below," and the Visvasara Tantra's "What is here is elsewhere."
- **Indra's network of pearls** — the Avatamsaka Sutra's image of a legendary net of pearls over the palace of the god Indra, "so arranged that if you look at one, you see all the others reflected in it" — each object in the world involves every other object and "is everything else."
- **Monads** — Leibniz's proposed fundamental entities constituting the universe, each containing a reflection of the whole universe; possibly inspired by his familiarity with Hua-yen Buddhism.
- **Holomovement** — Bohm's stress (echoed by Fa-Tsang about his own mirror model) that the universe is not simply a static hologram but is in constant dynamic movement.
- **Frequency domain** — Pribram's term, which Talbot equates with what Milarepa called "the border between mind and matter"; Talbot also notes reality seems to become "increasingly frequency-like as one penetrates deeper into the implicate."
- **Holophonic sound** — Zuccarelli's recording technique creating holograms made of sound instead of light, based on the fact that human ears emit sound (the audio equivalent of the reference laser); it produces sounds more realistic and three-dimensional than stereo and retains its three-dimensionality even through one headphone side.
- **Dissipative structures** — Prigogine's name for spontaneously appearing ordered systems (some mixed chemicals develop into more ordered, not more disordered, arrangements); he won a Nobel Prize for unraveling them, and he and others suggest they are evidence of the implicate aspects of reality becoming explicate.
- **Neural networks** — the recent computing strategy of connecting scores of little computers together to more closely resemble the biological structure of the human brain, instead of building single monolithic machines.
- **Multiplexed holographic gratings / optical memory** — Cohen's proposal that processors relying on interfering waves of light through such gratings might better mimic the brain's neural structure; Anderson showed holographic gratings could build an "optical memory" exhibiting associative recall.
- **Participatory science / participatory approach** — the replacement Talbot calls for of science's "enamorment with objectivity": since the observer's consciousness affects what is studied, the scientist must become a participant and experiencer; per Harman, "a willingness to be transformed is an essential characteristic of the participatory scientist."
- **Omnijective universe** — the term used here for the universe as a seamless continuum where mind and reality interpenetrate, in which strict objectivity ceases to be possible; UFO-type encounters are "small doses of the omnijective."
- **Psychonauts** — Talbot's speculative future heroes: participatory scientists who travel into the subtler realms themselves rather than merely interviewing NDEers and OBEers.
- **Shamanizing of modern humanity** — Ring's conclusion from the transformative effect of NDEs: modern NDEers, like tribal NDEers who become shamans, mutate into more loving, compassionate, more psychic individuals.
- **Evolutionary thrust toward higher consciousness** — Ring's answer to why NDEs are increasing: an evolutionary movement affecting all humanity, possibly also expressed in Marian visions and UFO encounters.
- **Interface between consciousness and its environment** — Jahn and Dunne's phrase for what we are learning to tinker with; Talbot speculates that as we grow more adept, reality may become malleable again, fulfilling the aboriginal prophecy of a return to the dreamtime.
- **Conceptual pigeonholes** — Bohm's warning image: the categories we use to parse the universe are of our own making and do not exist "out there," for "out there" is only the indivisible totality; Zen (per Sohl and Carr) likewise identifies confusing reality with the pigeonholes of language as "the basic ignorance."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Lame Deer (Lakota shaman), epigraph from *Lame Deer Seeker of Visions*** — Lame Deer says only human beings no longer know why they exist; they have forgotten the secret knowledge of their bodies, senses, and dreams, and bulldoze a "quick comfortable superhighway" to "the big empty hole" at its end. He says he has seen where it leads in his vision. Talbot uses this to open the chapter's theme of recovering ancient knowledge.
- **Tibetan Buddhist void/nonvoid doctrine (via Tibet scholar John Blofeld)** — the void is the birthplace of all things pouring out in "boundless flux"; only the void is real, visible forms being illusory. Blofeld: "everything interpenetrates, and is interpenetrated by, everything else; as with the void, so with the non-void — the part is the whole." Talbot presents this as a foreshadowing of Bohm's implicate/explicate orders.
- **Milarepa (eleventh-century Tibetan yogin, most renowned Tibetan Buddhist saint)** — taught that we cannot perceive the void directly because our "inner consciousness" (unconscious mind) is too "conditioned" in its perceptions; this conditioning hides "the border between mind and matter" (Talbot's gloss: the frequency domain) and also causes us to form a body for ourselves in the between-life state. He wrote that "in the invisible realm of the heavens ... the illusory mind is the great culprit" and counseled disciples to practice "perfect seeing and contemplation." Talbot says this prefigures Pribram's thinking.
- **Zen Buddhism (Robert Sohl and Audrey Carr, *Games Zen Masters Play*)** — Zen's main objective is to perceive reality's wholeness; confusing "the indivisible nature of reality with the conceptual pigeonholes of language is the basic ignorance from which Zen seeks to free us"; answers lie in "direct nonconceptual experience," not intellectual concepts. Talbot notes the words "could have been lifted right from one of Bohm's papers."
- **Hindu cosmogony (Vedas, Svetasvatara Upanishad, Kena Upanishad, Vedic scholar Sir John Woodroffe)** — Brahman as formless implicate source; matter emerging from consciousness; the world as maya. The Svetasvatara Upanishad: "One should know that Nature is illusion (maya), and that Brahman is the illusion maker." The Kena Upanishad: Brahman is an uncanny something "which changes its form every moment from human shape to a blade of grass." Woodroffe: maya splits the united consciousness into multitudinous objects, but "in the ultimate basis of experience the divergence has gone... in undifferentiated mass, experiencer, experience, and the experienced."
- **Kabbalah (Leo Schaya, Swiss expert on the Kabbalah; the Zohar, thirteenth-century Kabbalistic commentary on the Torah)** — "the entire creation is an illusory projection of the transcendental aspects of God," yet not complete nothingness, "for every reflection of reality... necessarily possesses something of its cause." The Zohar notes the Hebrew verb *baro*, "to create," implies "creating an illusion."
- **Hawaiian kahunas** — hold that everything in the universe is infinitely interconnected, almost like a web; the shaman sees himself at the center of the web and thus capable of affecting every other part of the universe. Talbot notes maya is also frequently likened to a web in Hindu thought.
- **Australian aborigines on the source of mind** — believe the true source of mind is in the transcendent reality of the dreamtime; normal people wrongly believe their consciousness is in their bodies, while shamans know otherwise, which is why they can contact subtler levels of reality. Talbot parallels this with Bohm's view that consciousness always has its source in the implicate.
- **The Dogon people of the Sudan** — believe the physical world is the product of a deeper, more fundamental level of reality, perpetually flowing out of and streaming back into it. A Dogon elder: "To draw up and then return what one had drawn — that is the life of the world."
- **Douglas Sharon, *Wizard of the Four Winds: A Shaman's Story*** — states that probably the central concept of shamanism, wherever found, is that underlying all visible forms there exists a vital essence from which they emerge and by which they are nurtured, to which everything ultimately returns. Talbot: the implicate/explicate idea is in virtually all shamanic traditions.
- **The macrocosm-microcosm lineage** — the twelfth-century Sufis said "the macrocosm is the microcosm" (which Talbot calls an earlier version of Blake's seeing the world in a grain of sand); the idea was embraced by the Greek philosophers Anaximenes of Miletus, Pythagoras, Heraclitus, and Plato; the ancient Gnostics; the pre-Christian Jewish philosopher Philo Judaeus; and the medieval Jewish philosopher Maimonides.
- **Hermes Trismegistus and the alchemists** — after a shamanic vision of the subtler levels of reality, the semimythical ancient Egyptian prophet said a main key to knowledge was that "the without is like the within of things; the small is like the large"; the medieval alchemists (for whom he became a kind of patron saint) distilled this into "As above, so below." The Hindu Visvasara Tantra states it more crudely: "What is here is elsewhere."
- **Black Elk's great vision on Harney Peak** — the Oglala Sioux medicine man, standing on Harney Peak in the Black Hills, "saw more than I can tell and I understood more than I saw... the shapes of all things in the spirit, and the shape of all shapes as they must live together as one being." He understood Harney Peak was the center of the world — but "Anywhere is the center of the world." Talbot pairs this with Empedocles, over twenty-five centuries earlier: "God is a circle whose center is everywhere, and its circumference nowhere."
- **The Avatamsaka Sutra's network of pearls** — its author likened the universe to a legendary network of pearls hung over the palace of the god Indra, arranged so that looking at one shows all the others reflected in it; "each object in the world is not merely itself, but involves every other object and, in fact, is everything else."
- **Fa-Tsang's candle and crystal demonstration for Empress Wu** — Fa-Tsang, seventh-century founder of the Hua-yen school of Buddhist thought, held the whole cosmos implicit in each of its parts and every point of the cosmos its center; he likened the universe to a multidimensional network of jewels each reflecting all others ad infinitum. When Empress Wu said she did not understand, he suspended a candle in the middle of a room full of mirrors (the relationship of the One to the many), then placed a polished crystal at the center so it reflected everything around it (the relationship of the many to the One). Like Bohm with the holomovement, he stressed his model was static and failed to capture the cosmos's dynamism and constant movement.
- **Leibniz, monads, and integral calculus** — the seventeenth-century German mathematician-philosopher appears to have been familiar with the Hua-yen school; some argue that is why he proposed the universe is constituted of "monads," each containing a reflection of the whole. Significantly, Leibniz also gave the world integral calculus — which enabled Dennis Gabor to invent the hologram. Talbot's point: the ancient intuition may have looped forward into the very invention that inspired the holographic model.
- **Hugo Zuccarelli's holophonic sound** — the Argentinian physiologist, drawing on Pribram's holographic brain model, developed a recording technique creating sound holograms. Basis: human ears actually emit sound; these naturally occurring sounds are the audio equivalent of the "reference laser" in holography. Results: a Times of London reporter listening to a recording "stole a look at the reassuring numbers on my watch," heard people approach from behind where there was only wall, and after seven minutes got "the impression of figures... a multidimensional 'picture' created by sound." Listeners move their feet on hearing recorded footsteps in front of them, move their heads at a match being lit too near the face — some reportedly even smelled the match. The recording keeps its three-dimensionality through only one side of a headphone (unlike stereo), and the same holographic principles appear to explain how people deaf in one ear can still locate a sound source without moving their heads. Major recording artists — Paul McCartney, Peter Gabriel, and Vangelis — approached Zuccarelli, but for patent reasons he has not disclosed the full technique. (A footnote offers a sample cassette for fifteen dollars from Interface Press, Box 42211, Los Angeles, California 90042.)
- **Ilya Prigogine and dissipative structures** — Talbot's illustration of entropy: drop a stereo off the Empire State Building and it becomes a pile of splintered parts, not a VCR. Yet Prigogine discovered some chemicals, when mixed, develop into a more ordered arrangement; he named these spontaneously appearing ordered systems "dissipative structures" and won a Nobel Prize for unraveling their mysteries. Prigogine and others suggest they materialize not from nowhere but indicate a deeper level of order — the implicate becoming explicate. Talbot: if true, this could illuminate how new complexities (attitudes, behavior patterns) pop into consciousness, and even how life appeared on earth several billion years ago.
- **Holographic computing (Marcus S. Cohen; Dana Z. Anderson)** — in the last half decade or so researchers moved from monolithic machines to "neural networks" of scores of connected little computers. Cohen, a computer scientist at New Mexico State University, pointed out that processors relying on interfering light waves passing through "multiplexed holographic gratings" might provide an even better analog of the brain's neural structure; Anderson, a physicist at the University of Colorado, showed holographic gratings could build an "optical memory" exhibiting associative recall. Talbot's caveat: these remain refinements of the mechanistic approach within the material framework.
- **Willis Harman on accepting psychic phenomena** — president of the Institute of Noetic Sciences and former senior social scientist at Stanford Research Institute International; has written extensively on the need for a basic restructuring of science and considers acceptance of psychic/spiritual phenomena crucial to science and to the survival of human civilization. He asks why we don't grant face validity to any class of experience reported "through the ages and across cultures."
- **Brian L. Weiss and the secret believers** — Talbot's setup: whether or not past-life memories under hypnosis are actual memories of previous lives has yet to be proved, but the human unconscious has a natural propensity for generating at least apparent memories of previous incarnations — a fact the orthodox psychiatric community in general ignores. Weiss is the Florida psychiatrist, Yale School of Medicine graduate, chairman of psychiatry at Mount Sinai Medical Center in Miami. After publishing his best-selling *Many Lives, Many Masters* (1988) — recounting his turn from skeptic to believer in reincarnation after a patient spontaneously talked about past lives under hypnosis — he was deluged with letters and calls from psychiatrists confessing they too are secret believers; some write that they've done regression therapy privately for ten to twenty years ("please don't tell anyone, but..."). Weiss: "I think that is just the tip of the iceberg." Talbot's point: fear of ridicule, not just disbelief, blocks psychic research.
- **Whitton on reincarnation's acceptance** — in a recent conversation, when Talbot asked whether reincarnation would ever become an accepted scientific fact, Whitton replied: "I think it already is. My experience with scientists is that if they've read the literature, they believe in reincarnation. The evidence is just so compelling that intellectual assent is virtually natural."
- **Anonymous survey of psychiatrists** — of 228 psychiatrists who responded (many heads of departments and deans of medical schools), after being assured of anonymity, 58 percent said they believed "an understanding of psychic phenomena" was important to future graduates of psychiatry; 44 percent admitted believing psychic factors were important in the healing process. Talbot: this bears out Weiss's and Whitton's opinions; we need "the parapsychological equivalent of a Rosa Parks."
- **OBE brain waves vs. Monroe's accounts (evidence-standards example)** — one of the few scientifically "valid" OBE discoveries is that brain waves change when an OBEer exits the body; yet Talbot argues that if accounts like Monroe's are real, they involve discoveries with as much potential impact on history as Columbus's discovery of the New World or the invention of the atomic bomb. Likewise, watching a truly talented clairvoyant conveys something more profound than "the dry statistics of R. H. and Louisa Rhine" — whose work Talbot says is nonetheless important. Mass anecdotal accounts should count as evidence; he cites Stevenson: "I believe it is better to learn what is probable about important matters than to be certain about trivial ones."
- **The Big Bang double standard** — most scientists accept without question that the universe began in a single primordial explosion, though no one has proved it; but a near-death psychologist flatly stating that the NDE realm of light is an actual other level of reality would be attacked, despite "equally compelling reasons." Science accepts the probable about fashionable beliefs but not unfashionable ones — a double standard Talbot says must be eliminated.
- **Valerie Hunt's alcohol rule** — Hunt discovered her experimental results were affected by the presence of individuals who had been drinking alcohol, and so won't allow such individuals in her lab while taking measurements. Cited as an early shift to a participatory approach — which Talbot says also appears to be why some laboratories achieve spectacular results in remote-viewing experiments while others fail miserably.
- **Dubrov and Pushkin's hypnosis protocol** — the Russian parapsychologists found they have more success duplicating other parapsychologists' findings if they hypnotize all test subjects present; hypnosis appears to eliminate interference from subjects' conscious thoughts and beliefs, producing "cleaner" results. Talbot suggests such practices, odd as they seem, may become standard operating procedure.
- **Harner and the ayahuasca (participatory anthropology)** — instead of just observing what happened to the Conibo after they consumed the soul-vine ayahuasca, Harner imbibed the hallucinogen himself; by becoming a participant instead of an observer he learned far more than he could have "sitting on the sidelines and taking notes." Talbot offers him as the model of the transformed, participatory scientist.
- **Lucid-dream researchers and Monroe's institute** — lucid-dream researchers are already exploring and reporting back on their own lucid-dream experiences. Monroe (not a scientist in the strictest definition) has developed recordings of special rhythmic sounds he feels facilitate out-of-body experiences, founded the Monroe Institute of Applied Sciences in the Blue Ridge Mountains, and claims to have trained hundreds of individuals to make the same out-of-body journeys he has made. Talbot asks whether these foreshadow a time when "psychonauts" become the heroes on the evening news.
- **Ring on increasing NDEs (*Heading toward Omega*)** — Ring points to compelling evidence that NDEs are on the increase; as tribal NDEers often become shamans, modern NDEers mutate into more loving, compassionate, more psychic individuals. Ring concludes we may be witnessing "the shamanizing of modern humanity," driven by "an evolutionary thrust toward higher consciousness for all humanity."
- **Grosso, Raschke, Vallee, Ring, and Strieber on parallel phenomena** — Grosso believes the last century's increase in Marian visions has evolutionary implications; Raschke and Vallee feel the explosion of UFO sightings over the last several decades has evolutionary significance; several investigators including Ring note UFO encounters actually resemble shamanic initiations. Strieber: "what we're dealing with is an exponential leap from one species to another... the process of evolution in action."
- **The aboriginal dreamtime prophecy and Edgar Cayce** — the aborigines say there was a time when the entire world was dreamtime, and that the day will come when the earth returns to it. Cayce echoed this: the earth was "at first merely in the nature of thought-forms or visualization... Then came materiality as such into the earth, through Spirit pushing itself into matter." Talbot speculates ("in the spirit of pure speculation") that as we learn to manipulate the hologram of reality — Jahn and Dunne's "interface between consciousness and its environment" — reality may become malleable again, and the evolutionary processes may exist to teach us to handle a plastic environment safely.
- **The Hindu ripple myth and Loye on Eden** — a Hindu myth says human consciousness began as a ripple that decided to leave the ocean of "consciousness as such, timeless, spaceless, infinite and eternal," then forgot it was part of the infinite ocean and felt isolated. Loye has argued that Adam and Eve's expulsion from the Garden of Eden may be a version of the same myth — an ancient memory of consciousness leaving its home in the implicate. In this view the earth is a playground "in which one is free to experience all the pleasures of the flesh provided one realizes that one is a holographic projection of a... higher-order spatial dimension."
- **Strieber on UFOs as midwives** — Strieber believes UFOs "are probably midwifing our birth into the nonphysical world — which is their origin"; the physical world is "only a small instant in a much larger context," reality unfolds primarily nonphysically, and "being, as consciousness, probably predates the physical."
- **Terence McKenna's fifty-thousand-year crisis** — the writer and longtime supporter of the holographic model holds that from the awareness of the soul's existence to "the resolution of the apocalyptic potential" spans roughly fifty thousand years; we are "in the final historical seconds of that crisis," which involves the end of history, our departure from the planet, the triumph over death — "the freeing of life from the dark chrysalis of matter." Talbot files this and Strieber's view as speculation, but concludes we are "following some track of spiritual evolution."
- **Swedenborg's higher heaven and Whitton's subject on the planes** — Swedenborg said that beyond the heaven he visited was another heaven so brilliant and formless it appeared only as "a streaming of light." One of Whitton's subjects similarly reports: "There are many higher planes, and to get back to God... you have to drop your garment each time until your spirit is truly free... each one is lighter and brighter than the one before." Talbot's inference: even the plastic afterlife realm is a mere stepping stone, and reality grows increasingly frequency-like deeper in the implicate.
- **The coloring-book / LSD analogy and the beings of light** — Talbot says we are still like children needing the security of a coloring book, not ready to draw free-form; plunging into Swedenborg's streaming light would be like a completely fluid LSD hallucination, and we lack the emotional maturity to handle the monsters our psyches would create there. Perhaps that is why we get only "small doses of the omnijective" (UFOs and similar experiences) — and why "the beings of light tell us again and again that the purpose of life is to learn."

## Thread
As the book's final chapter, this closes the circle Talbot has been drawing since the opening chapters on Bohm and Pribram: the holographic model is presented not as a novelty but as science's rediscovery, in its own vocabulary, of what mystical and shamanic traditions have always taught — a two-tiered, nonlocal, whole-in-every-part reality. Talbot leaves the reader with a program (restructure science toward participation and acceptance of the psychic) and a destiny (an evolutionary shamanizing of humanity that may return reality to the plastic dreamtime and eventually carry consciousness beyond the physical altogether). The closing image reframes the entire book as a shaman's journey: we are "mere children struggling to become technicians of the sacred," dreaming a comfortably solid hologram until we outgrow our conceptual pigeonholes — "just learning how to survive in infinity."
