# Ch 9 — Pixels, Quanta, and the Structure of Space-Time

## In one paragraph
Virk closes Part II's physics argument by attacking the assumption, inherited from Newton, that space and time are continuous. He walks through how video games actually work — pixels on grids, RGB values in memory, rendering engines, compression, clock speeds, server time — and then lines each mechanism up against a matching discovery in modern physics: quanta of energy (Planck, Einstein), a minimum measurable length (the Planck length), time dilation and the breakdown of simultaneity (special relativity), and a derivable minimum time increment (the Planck time). The pattern he draws out is that physics has been steadily moving from an analog (continuous) picture of the world to a digital (discrete) one, which is exactly what you'd expect if our "physical" world were a pixelated, rendered reality running on an underlying computation engine with a fixed clock speed. He ends with three speculative ways of traversing space-time without crossing the pixels in between — teleportation, wormholes, and quantum entanglement — arguing that if any of them lets us get around the speed of light, it strongly suggests something exists *outside* space-time, making physical reality look like a virtual world inside something bigger.

## Core claims
- Virk argues that the discrete, quantized character of space (and theoretically of time) discovered by modern physics is more explainable if we live inside a pixelated reality, "just like the virtual worlds that exist inside video games."
- Virk argues that physics' historical transition from continuous ("analog") to discrete ("digital") descriptions parallels the rise of information science, and that computation and information may be a better model for the physical world than the Newtonian model of objects moving in continuous lines.
- Virk argues that the Planck length is "for all practical purposes" the "pixel" of our physical, three-dimensional world: values smaller than it can still be calculated and referenced in a computation, but cannot be rendered or measured in the world — exactly how sub-pixel values behave in a video game.
- Virk argues that Zeno's paradox is resolved physically (not just mathematically) by pixelation: below the pixel distance, motion is not rendered until the next pixel is reached — "This is exactly how motion in a video game works, and how quantization in physics works."
- Virk argues that the constancy of the speed of light is an unexplained oddity of physics that "points to the simulation hypothesis in and of itself": if our reality is a computer simulation that utilizes electromagnetic signals, the specialness of light — the same speed at which computers send signals — makes sense.
- Virk argues that the speed of light is a constraint of our reality — of the physics engine our simulated world is using — not necessarily a physical constraint.
- Virk argues (following Thomas Campbell) that if space is quantized and c is fixed, a minimum quantum of time can be calculated (L = c × T), and that the resulting Planck time is analogous to the clock speed of a simulation. If quantized time proves correct, it "lends even more credence" to there being a clock speed behind the 3D world.
- Virk argues that simultaneity is an illusion both in relativity and in multiplayer video games, where distributed machines and signal delays force servers to settle for preserving order of operations rather than true simultaneity.
- Virk argues that teleportation, wormholes, and quantum entanglement are three candidate ways of moving through space-time *without* passing through the intervening virtual pixels; he explicitly labels these "speculative areas."
- Virk argues that if any method can be used to get around the speed of light, "this suggests very strongly there is something outside space-time" — and if so, physical reality "starts to resemble a virtual world inside something bigger."
- Virk argues that quantum entanglement is "completely unexplainable given our current models of physical reality," but has a natural analog in two screen pixels reading their value from the same memory location.

## Concepts
- **Pixel** — quoting Technopedia: "the smallest unit of a digital image or graphic that can be displayed and represented on a digital display device"; shortened from *picture element*. Each pixel has two values: a color (typically RGB) and an address in a grid.
- **Pixel grid / resolution** — pixels don't exist by themselves; they belong to a grid defining pixel resolution (physical on a screen, or logical in an image). On a screen a pixel is both a logical and physical element, which Virk says raises philosophical questions about what a pixel is and how long it exists.
- **Bitmap / raster image** — the complete set of color values of all the pixels in an image, whether in memory or on disk. Rarely stored raw today; JPEG/PNG compression avoids storing every redundant pixel color.
- **Rendering engine** — the process/program producing the bitmap displayed on screen: taking what should logically be in a scene and calculating each pixel's color from 3D object models and the viewing character's current perspective.
- **3D pixels** — pixels extrapolated into three dimensions; demonstrated by 3D printers, which hold in memory a list of the 3D pixels comprising an object and render solid objects from bits of information.
- **Zeno's paradox** — the Greek philosopher Zeno (or Xeno) of Elea's puzzle: if space is continuous, you must always cover half the remaining distance and can never actually reach a wall (or Achilles can never catch the tortoise ahead of him).
- **Quanta / quantization** — discrete quantities in which things happen at the subatomic level; discrete bits of "something" rather than continuous physical objects. First postulated by Planck in 1900: any energy-radiating system can be divided into discrete amounts of energy (quanta) defined by the quantum equation relating that amount to a frequency.
- **Photoelectric effect** — ejection of electrons from metal surfaces, first written about by Heinrich Hertz in 1887; electrons were ejected only when threshold quantities of energy were met, not continuously. Einstein showed this also applied to light — discrete "particles of light," named *photons* only in 1926.
- **Planck length** — a unit of length equal to 1.616255 × 10⁻³⁵ meters, considered the smallest possible length of space we can reliably measure; defined by three fundamental constants (speed of light in a vacuum, Planck constant, gravitational constant). Virk treats it as the pixel of physical space.
- **Quantum foam** — Wheeler's term (recalled from earlier chapters) for the random noise of quantum mechanics; below the Planck length, ordinary ideas of length and time break down.
- **Time dilation** — Einstein's (experimentally verified) result that the closer one gets to the speed of light, the more time slows down for those in the traveling inertial frame.
- **Twin paradox** — a twin traveling near light speed returns younger than the twin who stayed on Earth; how much younger depends on speed and duration.
- **Relativity of simultaneity** — an implication of relativity: there is no way to tell if two events are actually simultaneous; it depends on the observer's inertial reference frame.
- **Space-time** — after general relativity, time treated as an additional dimension of space: events at coordinates (x, y, z, t) rather than (x, y, z).
- **Clock speed** — the maximum frequency at which a processor performs operations (e.g., 1.7 GHz = 1.7 billion cycles per second); all measurements of time on the processor are multiples of it. In a digital simulation, each clock step updates all elements of the simulation; the logical step may bear no fixed relation to "real" time.
- **Universal server time** — in multiplayer games, the server-side time against which operations are logged so order of operations can be determined, since individual machine clocks can't be trusted and signals cross the internet.
- **Planck time** — the time required for light to travel 1 Planck length in a vacuum, approximately 5.39 × 10⁻⁴⁴ seconds; derived by dividing the Planck length by c. Currently "just a mathematical equation" — no conclusive evidence yet that time is or isn't quantized.
- **Physics engine** — the rule system governing motion/speed in a virtual world (e.g., how many virtual units it takes to cross Second Life terrain); Virk's stand-in for the laws of physics of our own reality.
- **Teleportation** — instantly de-rendering a character from one part of a virtual world and re-rendering it in another: setting new (x, y, z) coordinates without going through the points in between.
- **Wormhole / Einstein-Rosen bridge** — a tear in space-time created by a gravitational singularity of significant mass, allowing matter to enter at one point in space-time and exit at a different one, without moving through 3D space faster than light — it "pops out of ordinary space-time and back in."
- **Quantum entanglement** — when two particles become "entangled," the state of one theoretically lets you guess the state of the other instantaneously, however far apart; Einstein's "spooky action at a distance," now shown to be a real phenomenon.
- **Quantum teleportation** — sending the quantum state of a particle from one location to another using entangled particles (achieved; distinct from physically transporting objects).
- **Analog vs. digital** — the continuous view of the world versus discrete bits; Virk frames twentieth-century physics as a migration from the first to the second.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
1. **Pixels as a two-dimensional grid (screen anatomy)** — Every computer image is a 2D grid of pixels, each with (a) an RGB color value blending three primaries to produce almost any visible color, and (b) a grid address: in a 700 × 900 pixel image, the first pixel upper (or bottom) left might be 0 × 0, then 0 × 1, and the last pixel of the first row 0 × 899. Display resolution is changeable, e.g., from 1200 × 1024 to 1400 × 1200 pixels. Shows the machinery Virk will map onto physical space.
2. **The TIFF compression test (do-it-yourself)** — Virk challenges the reader: save a JPEG or PNG as an uncompressed TIFF (each pixel stored as a separate RGB value, redundant ones included) and "most likely you will see the size of the file balloon by at least ten and often more than a hundred times." Shows rendered reality is normally stored compressed, not raw.
3. **Toy Story rendering** — Computer-generated films like Toy Story required many hours to render each individual frame of each scene. Shows how computationally heavy it is to compute every pixel from 3D models plus viewing angle.
4. **World of Warcraft field-of-view rendering** — Virk's own play: his physical screen shows only the pixels of the scene his character can see; as the character moves or merely looks around, pixels update "pretty much instantly" — though not truly instantly, since many calculations run behind the scenes (more still with multiple windows open). Echoes the render-only-what's-observed argument of earlier chapters.
5. **3D printers and Star Trek replicators** — 3D printers take pixels into three dimensions: a list of 3D pixels for an object exists in memory, and computation renders the object into the world — showing that "solid" 3D objects are actually bits of information. Star Trek replicators do the same in fiction: create an object from a pattern, assembling bits via three-dimensional pixels. Virk cites this as evidence there's no reason pixels couldn't exist in 3D.
6. **Zeno's paradox (Achilles and the tortoise; the MIT dorm anecdote)** — A classmate explained Zeno's paradox to Virk one cold winter night as an MIT student (philosophy crept into post-midnight problem-set breaks). The puzzle: if space is continuous like the numbers (infinitely many numbers between any two), you can never touch a wall — you'd always cover half the distance and never arrive; likewise Achilles can never catch a tortoise ahead of him (Figure 29). Virk's instant reaction: space must be quantized — a minimum traversal distance must exist. The accepted scientific resolution is mathematical (an infinite series of successive halves sums to a finite number), but Virk objects that this "doesn't have a physical analog" — it still needs translating into a physical process. His pixel answer: below the pixel distance, movement isn't rendered until the next pixel is reached — exactly how video game motion and physical quantization work.
7. **Planck's 1900 quanta postulate** — Max Planck first postulated in 1900 that any energy-radiating system could be divided into discrete amounts of energy (quanta), defined by the quantum equation relating the amount to a frequency. The discrete-packets idea then took shape through the early twentieth century via Einstein, Max Born, Werner Heisenberg, Niels Bohr, "and many others." Shows quantum physics itself is built on discreteness, not continuity.
8. **The photoelectric effect (Hertz 1887 → Einstein's Nobel)** — First written about by Heinrich Hertz in 1887: electrons ejected from metal surfaces not continuously but only when certain threshold quantities of energy were met. Einstein showed the same applied to light — discrete "particles of light," not called photons until 1926 — and this work (not relativity) won him his sole Nobel Prize in Physics. Validated Planck's quanta.
9. **Rutherford and the subatomic zoo** — The Greek atom was supposed to be the smallest indivisible particle of matter; the modern term stuck because constituent parts weren't known until Lord Rutherford's discovery of the atomic nuclear model in the early twentieth century — since which electrons, neutrons, protons, and a large number of other subatomic particles have been found. Context: the "graininess" intuition about space has been around since the time of the Greeks, independent of Zeno's paradox.
10. **The Planck length and Wheeler's boundary** — The smallest length anyone has been able to measure, discovered by Max Planck himself: 1.616255 × 10⁻³⁵ meters, definable from the speed of light, the Planck constant (derived from the energy of a photon in relation to its frequency), and the gravitational constant. John Wheeler, investigating gravity waves, found this length marked a boundary below which measuring anything was difficult; beneath it, he writes in *Geons, Black Holes and Quantum Foam*, "Ordinary ideas of length would disappear. Ordinary ideas of time would evaporate." Virk: for all practical purposes this is the pixel of the physical 3D world — though larger pixels (multiples of the Planck length) could exist, just as a screen can run at lower resolution with fewer, larger pixels.
11. **The magic carpet thought experiment (sub-pixel computation)** — Imagine a magic carpet 1.616255 × 10⁻³⁶ meters long (10× smaller than the Planck length) with a fly on it 1.616255 × 10⁻³⁷ meters long (10× smaller than the carpet). You can theoretically multiply, add, and subtract these numbers, but you can't render or measure anything that size in the real world: the carpet would have to move its own length 10 times before showing up rendered. Shows values below the pixel can live in the computation but never on the "screen" of reality.
12. **The Scott/Mark Kelly twin experiment and atomic clocks** — NASA's experiment with astronaut Scott Kelly, who spent a year in space: it found him slightly younger than his identical twin brother Mark — when Scott got back, he was approximately 13 milliseconds younger than he was when he left. At 28,000 kilometers per hour he was traveling at less than 1 percent of the speed of light, hence the tiny effect. Also: atomic clocks synchronized to sub-milliseconds, taken up in spaceships, have come back out of sync with their Earth-bound counterparts. Virk's proof that time dilation — quantized reality's variable time — is real, not just theoretical.
13. **The London/New York car crashes (simultaneity)** — Two car crashes, one in London, one in New York. Standing in New York, you'd see the New York crash first (light would have to travel across the Atlantic Ocean); standing mid-ocean, you'd see both at the same time. Which happened "first" depends on your frame of reference more than on the events themselves. Shows simultaneity is observer-dependent in physics — as it is between video game clients.
14. **Video game latency = light-speed limit** — In multiplayer games there is a fixed speed for sending signals between one player and the server and on to another player; simultaneity is "only an illusion," even for characters in the same scene. Theoretically the fastest information could travel through a game is exactly the speed of electromagnetic signals — the speed of light. Virk's open question: why is the fastest speed for information in the physical world the same speed used for electromagnetic signals? If we're in a computer-simulated reality using electromagnetic signals, the mystery of why light is special "actually makes sense."
15. **Clock speed and simulation steps** — A 1.7-gigahertz processor performs 1.7 billion cycles per second — the maximum frequency for a single processor's operations (modern machines add multiple cores and GPUs, which compute graphics much faster than CPUs). In a digital simulation, each clock step updates all elements, but a logical step could take one millisecond, five seconds, or an hour of "real" time depending on the calculations needed. Examples: a fractal-process simulation run for a thousand steps, each step one program iteration; a fruit-fly population simulation where each logical step is one reproduction cycle (many species reproduce in regular intervals, e.g., yearly). Also noted: game code written in high-level languages (Java, C#) compiles to bytecode running on a virtual machine — one high-level line may translate into hundreds of low-level operations. All in-simulation time is multiples of the underlying clock — Virk's model for quantized physical time.
16. **Universal server time and the sword fight** — Multiplayer games log operations against a universal server time to determine order of operations: "if I swing my sword against your player first, theoretically this needs to be recorded correctly as part of our 'fight.'" Individual machines' clocks can't be trusted (they drift and can be manipulated via settings), signals cross the internet, so conflict resolution is needed; games "do the best they can" to preserve order of operations even without physical simultaneity. Given human response times this isn't a real problem today — but two competing AIs with sub-millisecond response times in different inertial frames would make determining who struck first very important and, per Einstein, potentially impossible.
17. **Campbell's quantized-time calculation (My Big TOE)** — Thomas Campbell shows that if the speed of light is constant and space is quantized (what he calls 3D pixels), the minimum quantum of time is calculable. Defining delta T (written T) as the smallest possible time increment — the clock speed of the simulation — Campbell's equation is L = c × (T), and inversely L / c = (T). Plugging in the Planck length yields the Planck time: the time for light to travel 1 Planck length in a vacuum, approximately 5.39 × 10⁻⁴⁴ seconds. Virk notes physicists stress the Planck time is currently just a mathematical equation — no conclusive evidence that time is or isn't quantized — but argues that given the space-time relationship, the light-crossing time of the minimum length is plausibly a lower bound on measuring time.
18. **Giddings on quantized space-time** — Steven B. Giddings, theorist at the University of California, Santa Barbara: "We've long suspected that space-time had to be quantized." Cited as physics-establishment support for the quantization of time being implied whenever physicists say "space-time." Virk notes the quantization of space has been accepted by many physicists while quantized time is a relatively new concept — but if space is quantized, goes the argument, "it is almost impossible for time not to be as well."
19. **Tifft's red-shift research** — William G. Tifft, professor of astronomy at the University of Arizona, says his research measuring red-shifted light from stars seems to suggest the light comes in multiples of some value — meaning there could be a minimum quantum of time just as there is a minimum quantum of space. The chapter's one piece of claimed observational evidence for quantized time.
20. **Second Life teleportation** — In Second Life your character can walk (relatively slow) or fly (same virtual distance, quicker) — both governed by the physics engine, always costing so many virtual units of distance. But you can also teleport: the character instantly de-renders from one part of the virtual world and re-renders in another, taking no time at all regardless of "distance" — just setting new (x, y, z) coordinates without passing through the points between. Virk: if we ever develop teleportation, it would be further evidence a physics engine governs our world.
21. **Star Trek teleportation as economy** — In the original series, teleporting was introduced for production economy: crew on the Enterprise one instant, on the planet the next, sparing the team shuttle-landing visual effects. Video game teleporting is also economy — of time. Star Trek's version was limited to relatively close distances (no teleporting to another solar system); a virtual world's point is reaching *any* part of the world instantly.
22. **Wormholes: Flamm, Einstein-Rosen, Wheeler** — Spawned from general relativity: Austrian physicist Ludwig Flamm proposed in 1916 that if black holes existed per Einstein's theory, so-called white holes could too. In 1935 Einstein and Nathan Rosen wrote their paper on "bridges" (Einstein-Rosen bridges); the term *wormhole* was coined in 1957 by "none other than the ubiquitous John Wheeler." A gravitational singularity of significant mass could tear space-time, creating a wormhole (Figure 30): matter enters at one point of space-time and exits at another, going from A to B without traversing the space between. Information/matter transfer beats light speed only measured across the total separation of the two mouths — the traveler pops out of ordinary space-time and back in, traveling at ordinary speeds from their own inertial view. Practical problems Virk lists: wormholes may be microscopic (information could pass but not spacecraft); they are not thought to be stable for long — possibly existing too briefly to detect or use; and the only phenomenon that can produce even a theoretical one is a black hole, whose gravity (so strong even light cannot escape) would likely crush any traveler instantly in the singularity.
23. **Maldacena and Susskind: wormholes as macro entanglement** — Juan Maldacena (Institute for Advanced Study) and Leonard Susskind (Stanford) have theorized that wormholes are like quantum entanglement at a macro level, linking two points in space together just as two quantum particles can be linked. Virk: if wormholes are real, linking points without the intervening distance would be "even more evidence" for the simulation hypothesis — analogous to Second Life teleporting.
24. **Star Trek: Deep Space Nine and Voyager (scale of the problem)** — DS9's wormhole connects the alpha quadrant to the gamma quadrant, 90,000 light-years apart — impractical even for warp-capable fictional ships. Voyager's whole plot rests on being transported 70,000 light-years from Earth with at least a 70-year trip home even at top warp (episodes spent hunting shortcuts before the show ended after seven seasons); worse, communication with Earth at light speed would take 70,000 years each way. Real numbers Virk gives: Alpha Centauri, the nearest star system, is 4.5 light-years away; the Milky Way is approximately 100,000 light-years across. Time dilation would shorten the traveler's experienced trip but "we wouldn't be able to get back to the same point." Sets up why entanglement's instantaneity matters.
25. **Quantum entanglement and the shared-memory-pixel analogy** — Entangled particles theoretically let you guess one's state from the other instantaneously at any distance — Einstein dismissed it as "spooky action at a distance," but it's a demonstrated real phenomenon, already used in applications like quantum cryptography (though debate continues over whether entanglement truly sends information from A to B faster than light; no one knows exactly how or why it works). Virk's video game model: if two pixels on a screen get their value from the same point in memory, changing that memory value (say RGB red to green) changes both pixels at once, no matter how far apart on screen they are; deciding a pixel no longer reads from that location "unentangles" them.
26. **Chinese quantum teleportation to a satellite (2017)** — Writing the 2024 second edition, Virk notes we haven't teleported objects, but quantum teleportation has been achieved: 2017 headlines said Chinese researchers teleported a particle to a satellite orbiting 1,400 kilometers above Earth. Closer examination: they sent the quantum state of a particle from one location to another using entangled particles — far short of Star Trek physical transport. But through the simulation-theory lens, Virk suggests the two "might just be the same thing," citing John Wheeler's assessment (to be discussed in Part IV) that particles are just information and there is no such thing as a physical particle.

## Thread
This is the capstone of Part II's physics case. Earlier chapters covered quantum weirdness (observation, probability); this one goes deeper, to the fabric itself: if space has a minimum length and time plausibly a minimum tick, reality is discrete — digital, not analog — and every discrete feature has a working precedent in video game architecture (pixels, rendering, clock speed, server time, teleports, shared memory). The argument escalates from analogy (pixels look like quanta) to explanation (a simulation would *predict* a constant light speed, a Planck length, a Planck time, and entanglement) to implication (workarounds to light speed would mean something exists outside space-time — a rendering context). Virk closes by signposting the book's pivot: Part III leaves physics for Eastern mysticism, Western religion, and unexplained phenomena; Part IV will revisit these ideas through computation and information science, including Wheeler's "particles are just information."
