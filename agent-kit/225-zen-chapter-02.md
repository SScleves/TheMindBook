# 2

## In one paragraph
Chapter 2 carries the party out of the Central Plains and onto the prairie as a storm gathers on the southwest horizon, and uses the long, monotonous ride to open the book's first sustained maintenance parables. The narrator recalls two disasters born of not caring about the machine: a rain-soaked Canada trip with Chris that ended because he never checked the gas tank, and a nightmarish shop experience in which cheerful, radio-blasting mechanics butchered his engine through three failed overhauls while the real fault was a sheared twenty-five-cent pin. From these he draws the chapter's central diagnosis: the mechanics — and the computer manuals he edits for a living — share a "spectator" attitude, an involvement in technology without care, a separation of what man is from what man does. He proposes that exploring this separation, slowly and without hurry, is a major reason for the Chautauqua. Threaded through are small narrative unsettlements: a missed turn he never saw, a storm he forgets to warn the others about, and a lingering sense of being watched or followed.

## Core claims
- The narrator argues that physical discomfort is important only when the mood is wrong: "Then you fasten on to whatever thing is uncomfortable and call that the cause. But if the mood is right, then physical discomfort doesn't mean much." This is his case for Sylvia riding through the Dakotas rather than flying to Billings.
- He claims that how you arrive shapes what you see: reaching the Rockies by plane makes them "pretty scenery," while arriving after days of hard prairie travel makes them "a goal, a promised land" — and mismatched ways of seeing would create more disharmony in the group than heat and monotony would.
- He believes something real exists on the prairie "because everything else does not," a thing that "sometimes comes when monotony and boredom are accepted" — he has no name for it and has given up talking to others about it.
- He asserts a body of storm lore as practical rider knowledge: cold fronts (a long dark streak with no preceding cirrus) are violent, worst from the southwest, often contain tornadoes, but pass quickly and leave good riding; warm fronts are "the worst" because they can last for days; a slow-moving front means you can get stuck for a long time.
- From the Canada breakdown he concludes that foolish quick assumptions (blaming the rain instead of checking the gas) are what defeated him — "I didn't understand then how foolish quick assumptions like that are" — and this is why he now takes maintenance of the twenty-eight-horse machine very seriously.
- He argues the shop mechanics' butchery cannot be blamed on hostility to technology — "These were not people running away from technology, like John and Sylvia. These were the technologists themselves" — yet they "performed it like chimpanzees," and asking why has "become a major reason for wanting to deliver this Chautauqua."
- He identifies three clues to the mechanics' failure: the radio (you can't think hard and listen to the radio at once), their hurry (faster means more money, "if you don't stop to think that it usually takes longer or comes out worse"), and above all their expressions — good-natured but uninvolved, spectators with "no identification with the job."
- He claims the mechanics, the manuals, and he himself when he dropped the bike off all shared one attitude: "We were all spectators." The manuals' very format implies the machine is "isolated in time and in space from everything else in the universe," with no relationship to the reader.
- He asserts that "there is no manual that deals with the real business of motorcycle maintenance, the most important aspect of all. Caring about what you are doing is considered either unimportant or taken for granted."
- He proposes that "in that strange separation of what man is from what man does we may have some clues as to what the hell has gone wrong in this twentieth century."
- He argues that hurrying is itself "a poisonous twentieth-century attitude": "When you want to hurry something, that means you no longer care about it and want to get on to other things." The right method is slow, careful, thorough — the same attitude that found the sheared pin: "It was that attitude that found it, nothing else."

## Concepts
- **Chautauqua** — the narrator's name for the philosophical talk he is delivering across the trip (introduced earlier); here he states that the question of why the mechanics butchered his machine "has become a major reason for wanting to deliver this Chautauqua," and that he wants to get at it "slowly, but carefully and thoroughly."
- **Spectator attitude** — the mechanics' stance toward their work: good-natured, friendly, easygoing "and uninvolved," "like spectators," as if "they had just wandered in there themselves and somebody had handed them a wrench." No identification with the job, no "I am a mechanic"; their selves stood outside the work, "involved in it but not in such a way as to care." The narrator says they were achieving, in their own way, the same thing as John and Sylvia: "living with technology without really having anything to do with it."
- **Spectator manuals** — the narrator's coinage for the technical manuals he edits: full of errors, ambiguities and omissions, and with the spectator attitude "built into the format of them." Implicit in every line: "Here is the machine, isolated in time and in space from everything else in the universe. It has no relationship to you, you have no relationship to it, other than to turn certain switches, maintain voltage levels, check for error conditions…"
- **Caring** — the missing element the narrator identifies as "the real business of motorcycle maintenance, the most important aspect of all," which no manual deals with because it is "considered either unimportant or taken for granted."
- **Seizure** — on an air-cooled engine, extreme overheating in which "the pistons expand from too much heat, become too big for the walls of the cylinders, seize them, melt to them sometimes, and lock the engine and rear wheel and start the whole cycle into a skid." His current machine has had three; he checks it "the same way I would check a patient who has had a heart attack."
- **Cold front** — a storm type identifiable as "that long dark streak without any preceding cirrus clouds"; violent, most violent when from the southwest, often containing tornadoes. Best to "hole up and let them pass over"; they don't last long, and "the cool air behind them makes good riding." A slow-moving one, however, means "you can get stuck for quite a time."
- **Warm front** — "the worst" kind of front for a rider because "they can last for days"; the front that ruined the Canada trip.
- **Quick assumptions** — the narrator's term for the failure that ended the Canada trip: he assumed the rain had caused the engine failure and so never checked the tank carefully; "I didn't understand then how foolish quick assumptions like that are."
- **Mood versus physical discomfort** — the narrator's claim that discomfort matters only when the mood is wrong; with the wrong mood you "fasten on to whatever thing is uncomfortable and call that the cause," while a right mood makes discomfort insignificant.
- **Separation of what man is from what man does** — the chapter's diagnostic phrase for the spectator condition; the narrator proposes exploring it on the trip for "clues as to what the hell has gone wrong in this twentieth century."
- **Hurrying as a poisonous attitude** — "a poisonous twentieth-century attitude"; wanting to hurry something means "you no longer care about it and want to get on to other things."
- **Being in the scene** — recalled from earlier ("as I said before, on a cycle you're in the scene, not just watching it"), invoked here to accept that "storms are definitely part of it."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The Canada trip breakdown** — A few years earlier, the narrator and Chris (then about eight) rode a little six-and-one-half-horsepower cycle, "way overloaded with luggage and way underloaded with common sense" (top speed about forty-five mph against a moderate head wind), about 130 miles into a trip to Canada before being caught in a warm front they had warning of but didn't understand. Camped at a large North Woods lake, he forgot to dig a trench around the tent; at about 2 A.M. water soaked both sleeping bags. Next day, ponchos worn as rain gear "spread out like sails," cutting speed to thirty wide open; water on the road ran two inches deep; lightning crashed around them; a woman in a passing car stared in astonishment. The cycle slowed to twenty-five, twenty, then began missing and sputtering, and at five or six mph they pulled into a run-down filling station (also a beer joint and restaurant, where they ate "burned-up steak"). He checked plugs, points, carburetor, kicked the starter to exhaustion; Chris's questions angered him until he gave up, and Chris, finally understanding the trip was over, cried. They hitchhiked home, retrieved the cycle by trailer, and finished the vacation by car — "it wasn't the same." Two weeks later, removing the carburetor at home, he turned the tank stopcock and nothing came out: the tank was out of gas; the sloshing he'd felt was the reserve tank he had never turned on. He has "kicked myself mentally a hundred times for that stupidity." Shows what quick assumptions and ignorance of maintenance cost, and why he now maintains the twenty-eight-horse machine seriously.
- **The first seizure** — Riding with a passenger, the engine seized: his head pitched over the front wheel, the passenger nearly on top of him; at about thirty it freed and ran again. The passenger could only say, "What did you do that for?" The engine was so hot the air shimmered; a wet finger on it "sizzled like a hot iron." They rode home slowly with a new slapping sound meaning the pistons no longer fit and an overhaul was needed. Establishes the seizure concept and sets up the shop story.
- **The shop nightmare (three overhauls)** — Thinking the job "wasn't important enough to justify getting into myself" ("sort of John's attitude"), he took the machine to a shop that was "a different scene from the ones I remembered": the mechanics, "who had once all seemed like ancient veterans, now looked like children." A radio blasted, and one barely listened to the piston slap before diagnosing "Oh yeah. Tappets." Two weeks and 140 dollars later, he broke the engine in carefully over a thousand miles, opened it up, and it seized again at about seventy-five, freeing at thirty. The shop blamed his break-in, argued, overhauled it again, road-tested it themselves at high speed — and it seized on them. After a third overhaul two months later they replaced the cylinders, installed oversize main carburetor jets, retarded the timing, and told him, "Don't run it fast." He got it back greasy and non-starting (plugs disconnected), with genuine tappet noise (never adjusted); a kid with a wrongly set open-end adjustable wrench rounded both sheet-aluminum tappet covers, then attacked them with hammer and cold chisel, punching through a cover into the engine head and, missing the chisel entirely, breaking off part of two cooling fins with the hammer. "Just stop," the narrator said politely, "feeling this was a bad dream." Riding away he felt a bad vibration over twenty mph and found two of four engine-mounting bolts missing, a nut gone from the third — "The whole engine was hanging on by only one bolt" — and the overhead-cam chain-tensioner bolt missing too, making tappet adjustment hopeless anyway. Afterward: "The thought of John putting his BMW into the hands of one of those people is something I have never brought up with him. Maybe I should." The case that generates the whole spectator analysis.
- **The sheared twenty-five-cent pin** — A few weeks later the narrator himself found the true cause of the seizures: "a little twenty-five-cent pin in the internal oil-delivery system that had been sheared and was preventing oil from reaching the head at high speeds." Moreover, a mechanic had sheared it in the first place by assembling the side cover plate improperly — the previous owner had been told by a mechanic that the plate was "hard to get on," and the shop manual had warned about exactly this. Shows that care, not expertise alone, finds faults: "It was that attitude that found it, nothing else."
- **The computer manuals** — At his job editing digital computer manuals (his living "the other eleven months of the year"), he knew they were "full of errors, ambiguities, omissions and information so completely screwed up you had to read them six times to make any sense out of them" — and realized for the first time that their format agreed with the shop's spectator attitude. Extends the diagnosis beyond one bad shop to a general condition.
- **The Sylvia-flies-to-Billings debate** — John, worried Sylvia couldn't take the prairie discomfort, planned to have her fly to Billings, Montana; Sylvia and the narrator talked him out of it, the narrator arguing his mood-versus-discomfort claim and that arriving by plane versus by hard travel would split the group's way of seeing the mountains. Also a confessed self-interest: "Anyway, I like to talk to her and I'm thinking of myself too."
- **The missed turn** — John signals a stop: they missed their turn, marked by a sign "Big as a barn door" that the narrator never saw; they have already crossed a freeway he "hardly noticed." John decides to go ahead rather than back: "We'll get there one way or another." Combined with forgetting to tell the others about the storm, the narrator admits, "Things are getting a little unsettling" — continuing the chapter-opening sense of "being watched or followed."
- **The engine-temperature check** — Riding, he removes a glove with his teeth and feels the aluminum side cover: "Too warm to leave my hand there, not so hot I get a burn. Nothing wrong there." His practiced, caring vigilance over a machine with a seizure history.
- **Geography as narrative marker** — Flax fields "blue with flax blossoms moving in long waves like the surface of the ocean"; the unmarked, gradual transition from Central Plains to Great Plains likened to sailing out of sight of land; trees no longer native but planted as windbreaks; finally the land flattening "into a Euclidian plane" announcing the Red River Valley, with the Dakotas ahead.

## Thread
Chapter 2 converts Chapter 1's observed divide — John and Sylvia's flight from technology versus the narrator's engagement with it — into a sharper puzzle: even the technologists themselves can be estranged from technology. The two maintenance stories (the gas tank he never checked; the shop that butchered three overhauls while a twenty-five-cent pin went unfound) supply the evidence, and the "spectator" diagnosis names the disease: involvement without care, self separated from work. The chapter explicitly commissions the rest of the Chautauqua — the why of the butchery is "a major reason for wanting to deliver" it — and prescribes its method: slow, careful, thorough, unhurried, the attitude that found the pin. Meanwhile the narrative layer plants seeds of the narrator's own unreliability (the unseen barn-door sign, the unnoticed freeway, the unmentioned storm, the feeling of being followed) and drives the party toward the Dakotas with a slow cold front closing in.
