# How to Identify a Lie?: Key Behaviors that Indicate Deception

## In one paragraph
William Cooper devotes this chapter to observable "tells" that reveal deception, working region by region through the body — eyes, body language, hands, face, and voice — before pivoting to a conceptual distinction between "big lies" (told for self-interested gain) and "white lies" (told to please or motivate). He illustrates the big lie/white lie split with two case studies: Dylann Storm Roof's 2015 Charleston church shooting as an example of a destructive big lie built on a racist narrative, and a NUI Galway study on telling white lies to dementia patients as an example of a beneficial white lie. The chapter closes with practical guidance on when telling a white lie is acceptable (small social niceties) versus when it is not (situations where withholding truth allows harm to compound).

## Core claims
- Cooper argues deception can be detected through non-verbal cues, primarily via the eyes, body language, hands, face, and voice, and that this is a learnable skill useful both professionally (e.g., in interviewing/investigation) and interpersonally.
- He claims a break in eye contact partway through a conversation is a determinant of deception, tied specifically to the emotion of shame, whereas truthful people maintain direct eye contact throughout.
- He states that detecting deception via the eyes is complex and requires an interviewer to keep asking a series of questions, since a liar who maintains eye contact to avoid detection will still be unable to manage other movements.
- He asserts consistent head movement (e.g., steady nodding) indicates truthfulness, while erratic head movement indicates lying, and that investigators often fail because they don't correlate non-verbal behavior with spoken words.
- He claims liars suffer faster breathing due to increased heart rate driven by fear, and that this breathing-pattern change is detectable.
- He argues truthful people spread their fingers freely, while insecure/lying people fold their palms, occupy minimal space, fold their legs, tremble in the fingers/hands (without a climate-related cause), or try to cover their hands with a sleeve.
- He states that expert liars will barely move their hands or body and speak in a measured tone to avoid giving away extra information, and that a lack of any movement at all ("no one sits completely still") is itself a red flag when interviewing a known conman.
- He claims slouching boosts a liar's confidence, and that deceptive people stretch their hands while yawning as a way to confuse or misdirect the person they're addressing; he generalizes that liars either have a good poker face, fidget, or make weird/disjointed gestures.
- He states that blinking four or five times within under a minute is a common indicator of lying.
- He claims that closing the eyes for more than two seconds when answering a question is a clear indication of dishonesty, whereas normally a person should not close their eyes at all while answering.
- He asserts that when a right-handed person is asked about something they saw in the past and looks left and upward, they are lying and trying to divert attention; looking right and upward instead indicates sincerity.
- He claims a trembling voice when answering complex questions is evidence of lying, reflecting a defense mechanism used by people who cannot justify their claims, whereas tone should not vary between answers under normal circumstances.
- He distinguishes "big lies," where the liar seeks to exploit the deceived for self-interested gain (condemned across cultures), from "white lies," which manipulate information to motivate or please the deceived and usually don't harm feelings.
- He argues white lies are sometimes encouraged depending on context but can cause harm when used to foster relationships, e.g., agreeing with others' opinions to bond with or gain favor from them, which can hamper decision-making.
- He lists specific situations where a white lie is okay (being asked "how are you," complimenting someone's haircut, lying about being late for work) and situations where it is not okay (delaying revealing something inevitable, staying in a relationship you want to end, withholding a "wake-up call" someone needs to improve).

## Concepts
- **Deception** — communicating misleading information with the intent to deceive another person or group; the chapter's overall subject.
- **Eye-contact break** — refraining from directly looking at the other party partway through a conversation; per the author, associated with shame and a clear indicator of a shift in intention/deception, whereas truthful people maintain eye contact throughout.
- **Body language (as a deception cue)** — unconscious gestures and movements, including head movements and breathing, that reveal what an individual truly feels; inconsistency between body language and verbal statements is presented as evidence of deception.
- **Head-movement consistency** — steady, consistent head movement indicating truthfulness versus erratic/inconsistent nodding indicating lying.
- **Breathing-pattern change** — fast breathing driven by an increased heart rate and fear, presented as a physical sign that someone is lying.
- **Hand and finger behavior** — spreading fingers freely signals truthfulness; folding palms, minimizing occupied space, and trembling fingers/hands (absent any climate cause) signal insecurity/lying.
- **Folded legs** — liars are said to fold their legs because they believe occupying less space makes them less likely to be detected.
- **Sleeve-covering** — attempting to cover one's hands/appendages with a sleeve, listed as a hand-related deception cue.
- **Poker face / fidgeting / weird or disjointed gestures** — the three alternative behavioral patterns the author says liars exhibit; expert liars specifically show a near-total lack of hand or body movement and speak in a measured tone to avoid revealing extra information.
- **Slouching** — posture the author says liars adopt because it helps boost their confidence.
- **Misdirection gestures (e.g., stretching while yawning)** — deliberate motions the author says liars use to confuse or divert the attention of the person they're addressing.
- **Facial expressions (as deception cues)** — the most expressive region of the body, caused by emotions like nervousness, used as a scale for detecting dishonesty via blink rate, eye-closure duration, and eye direction.
- **Blink rate** — blinking four or five times in under a minute, cited as a common sign of lying.
- **Eye-closure duration** — closing the eyes for more than two seconds while answering a question, cited as a clear indication of dishonesty (versus not closing the eyes at all under normal, truthful circumstances).
- **Eye direction (left-up vs. right-up)** — for a right-handed person recalling something seen in the past: looking left and upward indicates lying/diversion of attention; looking right and upward indicates sincerity.
- **Voice trembling** — shakiness in the voice while answering complex questions, described as evidence of a defense mechanism used by people who can't justify their claims.
- **Big lie** — a lie in which the deceptive person seeks to gain something from or exploit the deceived party out of self-interest; condemned across cultures.
- **White lie** — a lie that manipulates information to motivate or please the deceived party, usually without harming their feelings; sometimes encouraged depending on context, but capable of causing harm (e.g., in relationship-building) or being inappropriate in specific situations.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Used-electronics dealer (big-lie illustration)** — a used electronics dealer misleads customers about a TV's condition, passing off a faulty set as being in good working order in order to sell it at a high price and maximize profit; the author uses this as a plain example of a self-interested big lie.
- **Friend's haircut (white-lie illustration)** — telling a friend their new hairstyle looks great even though you secretly dislike it, done to make the friend happy; used as a baseline example of an innocent, harmless white lie.
- **Case Study 1 — "Black Men are Inherently Violent Towards White People" (big lie)**: the author describes a long-standing white-supremacist narrative that black people pose a threat of violence and rape to white people, used by nationalists to justify oppression under the guise of protecting the country. He cites Dylann Storm Roof, a 21-year-old white man who in June 2015 walked into a Charleston church and fired 70 rounds at a Bible study group, killing and injuring people while shouting "you have been raping our white women, you have been taking over the world." The author states Roof saw himself as a victim standing up for "oppressed whites" rather than as the aggressor he was, and notes white-supremacist websites (naming the Council of Conservative Citizens as an example) that peddle the same big lie of a black "war" against whites.
- **Case Study 2 — "Telling White Lies to People with Dementia can Actually Help Them" (white lie)**: the author cites new research from NUI Galway, Ireland on people with memory problems, particularly those who repeatedly ask about loved ones (including deceased ones). The study reportedly found that lying or giving incorrect answers in these situations prevents distress and can ease stress and reassure patients, though it drew a line at blatant lying that deceives and harms, which it considered unacceptable. The study also reportedly found it may be acceptable for caregivers/health professionals to tell white lies in certain situations, but stressed considering the family's wishes first, since every patient has unique needs. Footnote [75] is attached to this material.
- **When a white lie is okay** — three scenarios: (1) responding "I am fine, thank you" when someone asks how you are out of politeness rather than genuine interest; (2) complimenting a friend's new haircut you dislike, since there's nothing they can do to change it now; (3) crafting a good excuse for being late to work when the truth would get you in trouble.
- **When a white lie is not okay** — three scenarios: (1) using small lies to delay revealing something inevitable — illustrated by spotting a close friend's significant other out with someone else, where the author says you should tell your friend immediately rather than let the situation spiral; (2) giving someone false hope with white lies when you want to end a relationship, rather than communicating your loss of interest as soon as possible; (3) withholding a "wake-up call" a person needs for better decision-making — illustrated by an artist with a horrible performance, who the author says needs honest truths rather than white lies in order to improve.
