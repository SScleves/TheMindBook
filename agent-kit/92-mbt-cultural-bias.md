# Ch 21 — Cultural Bias

## In one paragraph
Campbell argues that Western culture's deepest bias is its dogmatic belief that PMR's objective causality is universal — that every event must have a physical, objective, local cause. This belief, he claims, forces science into a self-referential trap: science logically demands a mystical beginning for our reality (as he argued in Chapter 18) while simultaneously condemning any investigation of that beginning as irrational and superstitious. His solution is to see PMR as a subset of a larger reality with its own more general causality, from whose perspective our "mystical" origins are ordinary science. The chapter then broadens into a psychology of belief: humans prefer any plausible answer to no answer, so they anthropomorphize gods and embrace dogma (scientific and religious alike) as pseudo-knowledge that soothes fear while stunting long-term growth. Campbell positions open-minded skepticism as the alternative, previews his coming technical definitions of consciousness quality (spiritual growth = increasing love = decreasing consciousness entropy), and closes with the Flatland analogy: the larger reality is real even if belief-limited beings cannot comprehend it from inside their local causality.

## Core claims
- Campbell claims objective causality is the philosophical underpinning of PMR science and has been extremely useful for the material realm, but we wrongly force everything into "the PMR causality straight jacket," hypothesizing rationalized causes even where none exist within PMR.
- Campbell argues that invoking the unknown as a logical explanation for a difficult event is "not logical or even particularly rational in most circumstances."
- Campbell claims our beliefs set the boundaries of our science — always have — and that every generation sees its forbearers' belief-blindness clearly while its own remains invisible; moderns are still "seriously and dramatically limited" by beliefs.
- Campbell claims major conceptual breakthroughs "must always lie outside the solution space defined by what is generally accepted"; there is no viable middle ground between bold leaps and dogmatic limitation — that middle ground is "a comforting delusion."
- Campbell argues an effect could have a component beyond PMR objectivity; such an effect would be labeled paranormal/mystical and is rejected only because it conflicts with cultural and scientific belief.
- Campbell claims the Western commitment to universal objective causality creates "an objective Catch 22": science "simultaneously logically demands and rationally denies a mystical beginning."
- Campbell's resolution: objective causality is local to PMR, not universal. Once PMR is seen as a subset of a larger reality, our beginning becomes "the result of a more general causality working within the rules of its own science" — analyzable if we gain that more general perspective.
- Campbell claims higher, more complete knowledge reflecting the science of our origins must necessarily appear mystical to us, and is only available "through the expansion of our perspective into the next higher dimension of existence."
- Campbell states the goal of his Big TOE: to turn what looks mystical from the PMR-only viewpoint "into hard science in broad daylight" via "impeccable logic applied to two reasonable assumptions."
- Campbell argues the belief that a more general science cannot exist (because it can't be derived from PMR-only science) is a self-referential circular argument — a belief trap that closed twentieth-century minds.
- Campbell claims our source is "both initiating and sustaining" — we cannot exist independently of our parent reality any more than organs can exist independently of the body; assuming the source disappeared is delusional ice-cube thinking.
- Campbell defines coming to the logical conclusion that a larger reality could possibly exist beyond PMR as open-minded skepticism — allowing the possibility and exploring it honestly and scientifically is all that's needed to grow a personal Big TOE that accelerates consciousness evolution.
- Campbell claims a higher-level source should not be expected to be obvious or comprehensible, just as our machines, pets, and internal organs cannot fathom human motivation.
- Campbell argues deep ignorance and deep fear produced the thousands of anthropomorphized gods people conjured "for their own needs, in their own image, or in the image of their fear" — and notes every religion agrees other people's gods are false.
- Campbell claims that in matters of Big Truth "you cannot teach, much less force, someone to get it"; understanding cannot exceed what the quality of one's consciousness can naturally support, and each individuated unit of consciousness must develop in its own way, powered by free will driving its intent.
- Campbell claims humans vastly prefer any plausible (even wrong) answer to no answer; pseudo-knowledge calms anxiety short-term but severely stunts long-term growth; this is why open-minded skepticism, though obviously the most correct approach, is rarely implemented.
- Campbell claims beliefs and fantasies "almost always have an effect that is opposite to what is intended," and that "the process of denying a fear generally causes what is feared to manifest in your reality."
- Campbell claims the richness, importance, and meaningfulness of our subjective existence "directly conflicts with the notion that if you cannot measure or physically experience it, it is either non-existent or irrelevant," and that the abundance of reputable, scientifically collected paranormal data likewise "flies in the face of our limited reality."
- Campbell argues modern physics itself (wave-particle duality, instantaneously communicating entangled particle pairs, statistically based material existence) is "pushing the notion of our cherished objective reality into the subjective mind-space of the experimenter," and that most modern physicists agree our local 3D time-ordered causal reality is "merely a perceptual illusion."
- Campbell claims an abundance of reputable, scientifically collected paranormal data exists (citing Targ & Puthoff and Radin), and that dismissing it signals a need to assess one's beliefs.
- Campbell claims a "cultural implosion" — a spinoff of the information-computer-networking revolution linking the planet's mind-space — makes a correct, comprehensive Big TOE especially timely now, at the dawn of the Information Age.
- Campbell claims serious explorers of reality "rarely come home empty handed": they inevitably find a greater reality beyond objective PMR, worth far more than the effort.
- Campbell argues the endless science-vs-belief (and science-vs-religion) debate violates the Rule of Rationality — a "perpetual wasted motion machine within a logical black hole" described by his tongue-in-cheek Third Law of No Motion.
- Campbell claims consciousness quality is strictly a personal achievement, "not a group endeavor": it "has absolutely nothing to do with creed, dogma, or belief" and cannot be increased "one single iota" by belief, accumulating information, improperly motivated good deeds, talking to others, or reading books.
- Campbell asserts an equivalence he will later make technical: spiritual growth, personal growth, improving consciousness quality, evolving your being, increasing your capacity to love, and decreasing the entropy of your consciousness "are all essentially synonymous and equivalent."
- Campbell claims most religions do not believe that PMR is "all there is," and that religion, like science, is a significant part of our cultural heritage; nonetheless, he argues the "organization and codification of mysticism into various religious doctrines and dogmas is of little value," and that letting this narrow, culturally sanctioned mysticism coexist and blend with scientific dogma "serves only to confuse, distort, and restrict our ability to deal with the real issue of consciousness quality."
- Campbell clarifies he criticizes only institutionalized/organized religion, not individual seekers; a few individuals subtract organization, dogma, creed, and ritual and still retain a genuinely significant personal spiritual experience — a valid path with "no intrinsic benefit or penalty."
- Campbell claims he does not condemn belief and dogma merely for being illogical and unnecessary ("Inefficiency is not a crime"), but for most people dogma erects growth barriers, snags them in belief traps, and makes them more egocentric, arrogant, and self-righteous — though rarely, dogma-plus-ritual can function like a meditation mantra for those who have inwardly outgrown it.
- Campbell claims neither science nor religion, as a general rule, provides "a significant boost to the inner quality of an individual's life"; consciousness quality must grow independently "in the shadow of both."
- Campbell concludes: "Our ignorance does not impose limits on the larger reality — only upon our understanding of it."

## Concepts
- **cultural bias / belief blindness** — Campbell's term for the invisible, deeply ingrained, widely held "belief-limited blindfold we all wear" that feels natural and obviously correct; it limits the scope (breadth, depth, quality) of the thoughts we can think and the size of the picture we can comprehend, and we may vehemently deny it exists.
- **PMR (Physical Matter Reality)** — the local physical reality we appear trapped in; in this chapter treated as a subset of a larger reality, whose local science and objective causality do not apply universally.
- **objective causality** — the belief that every effect and every event has an objective cause, whether known or not; the fundamental philosophical underpinning of PMR science. Campbell argues it is local to PMR, not universal.
- **local causality** — objective causality understood as applying only within PMR; belief in its "supremacy" prevents considering that an effect might have a component beyond PMR objectivity.
- **mystical beginning** — the origin our reality system logically requires (per Chapter 18) which appears mystical only from the PMR viewpoint; from the parent reality's perspective it is "ordinary, mundane and well understood," the result of a more general causality's own science.
- **paranormal** — Campbell's label for an effect with at least one component lying beyond PMR objectivity; it "would appear mystical when viewed from a PMR perspective" and is rejected because it conflicts with cultural/scientific belief.
- **parent reality / superset** — the more capable, powerful, more fundamental source reality operating at a higher (more general) level of existence or dimension; it is "both initiating and sustaining" — PMR cannot exist independently of it.
- **the bigger picture / little picture** — Campbell's recurring framing: the bigger picture includes the larger reality containing PMR; the little picture is the PMR-only view maintained by limiting beliefs.
- **belief trap** — a self-referential, circular, belief-based non-logic posing as obvious truth (e.g., "a more general science cannot exist because it can't be derived from PMR-only science") that blinds and restricts the growth options of those caught in it.
- **open minded skepticism** — allowing the logical possibility that a larger reality exists beyond PMR (however remote you judge it) and having the gumption and commitment to explore that possibility honestly and scientifically; Campbell calls it the most correct, beneficial, and productive approach to new ideas, rarely implemented because it offers no immediate closure and requires work.
- **Big TOE** — Campbell's Big Theory Of Everything; here its goal is stated: to take what appears mystical from the PMR-only viewpoint and, through impeccable logic applied to two reasonable assumptions, "turn it into hard science in broad daylight." A personal Big TOE can "accelerate the evolution of your consciousness."
- **Big Truth** — higher, more correct and complete knowledge reflecting the science of the place of our beginning or beyond; necessarily appears mystical to us, cannot be taught or forced, and cannot be accessed beyond what one's consciousness quality can naturally support.
- **knowledge vs. pseudo-knowledge** — knowledge received from others without the personal quality to have derived it oneself guarantees misunderstanding and makes distinguishing knowledge from pseudo-knowledge impossible; pseudo-knowledge is the "short-term feel-good solution" that calms anxiety while stunting growth.
- **consciousness quality / consciousness evolution / personal growth / spiritual growth** — deliberately left vague here pending technical definition; Campbell states they, plus increasing your capacity to love and decreasing the entropy of your consciousness, "are all essentially synonymous and equivalent." Quality is a strictly personal achievement, independent of creed, dogma, belief, information, or unmotivated good deeds.
- **entropy (of consciousness)** — used here without full definition: decreasing the entropy of your consciousness is equated with spiritual growth and increasing love; a technical definition is promised later.
- **love** — appears as "increasing your capacity to love," one of the synonymous formulations of consciousness quality improvement; Campbell says a comprehensive Big TOE must logically interrelate physics, spirituality, consciousness, and love.
- **free will** — the power that drives the intent of each individuated unit of consciousness, which "must develop in its own unique way."
- **individuated unit of consciousness** — Campbell's term (used, not defined here) for an individual consciousness that must develop uniquely, powered by free will driving its intent.
- **dogma** — non-negotiable belief; scientific and religious dogma alike. For most people it erects barriers, distracts, confuses right and wrong, snags them in belief traps, and breeds ego and arrogance — though Campbell "welcomes" responsible dogma-owners (the dog-walking metaphor) and allows that rare individuals turn dogma-plus-ritual into something like a meditation mantra.
- **organized religion** — Campbell restricts his critique to "institutionalized dogma or organized religion," how "the great majority of people are connected to religion"; he does not denigrate individual seekers who find genuine spiritual quality within a religious setting.
- **Rule of Rationality** — invoked (not restated) as what the endless science-vs-belief debate violates by forming "a perpetual wasted motion machine within a logical black hole."
- **Campbell's Third Law of No Motion (law of inaction-reaction)** — Campbell's satirical law describing the science-vs-religion debate: "For every irrational rationalization there exists an equal but opposite irrational rationalization."
- **cultural implosion / Information Age** — Campbell's prediction that the information-computer-networking revolution is linking and integrating "the mind-space of our planet," mixing East/West values and producing a cacophony of conflicting concepts; one must find an optimal synthesis and customize it, making a correct Big TOE especially timely.
- **mind-space** — used twice: the "subjective mind-space of the experimenter" into which modern physics is pushing objective reality, and the planetary "mind-space" being integrated by communication technology.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The "why" questions** — "Why do I love you? Why do I enjoy music? Why do fractal images look like natural landscapes? Why am I obsessed with frogs?" Campbell uses these to show how PMR culture insists on hypothesizing PMR-causal reasons even where the cause may lie outside PMR (he jokes that "some unknown neurological or psychological function or dysfunction explains all but the fractals").
- **History of science as evidence** — Campbell claims "any reasonably accurate history of science" verifies that beliefs have always set the limits of science; each era sees its predecessors' belief-blindness as ridiculous while its own remains invisible.
- **The box / frying-pan-into-the-fire analogy** — getting out of the box (of accepted paradigms) requires stepping over its edge, which frightens most "box dwellers"; don't let fear of the fire keep you in the frying pan — open-minded skepticism, careful science, and work let you exit "without getting hurt, burnt, or deluded."
- **The objective Catch 22** — Campbell's named logical bind: Western belief in universal objective causality requires (per his Chapter 18 argument) a mystical beginning, while condemning investigation of it as irrational; "Science simultaneously logically demands and rationally denies a mystical beginning."
- **The spherical chicken line** — to Western ears, "Assume the existence of an apparently infinite absolute unbounded oneness" sounds less credible than "assume the existence of a spherical chicken" — Campbell's jab at physics-style idealization being culturally acceptable while mystical premises are not.
- **The delusional ice-cubes** — assuming the creative source has disappeared is like ice-cubes in Campbell's automatic ice-maker bucket believing the compressor "must have stopped working years ago"; they don't understand the bigger picture. Shows the source is ongoing (sustaining), not just historical.
- **Internal organs / body dependency** — we cannot exist independently from our initiating-and-sustaining source any more than internal organs can exist independently from the body.
- **Machines, computers, pets, designer viruses, intestinal bacteria, internal organs** — none can comprehend more than "a shallow one-dimensional sense of us"; likewise we shouldn't expect our higher-dimensional source to be obvious, easy to understand, and just like us.
- **The catalog of gods** — Sun, Tree, Moon, Fire, River, War, Fertility, Tribal, Ocean, Storm, Animal gods, "even Booze, Sex, and Party gods (Bacchus)" — thousands of gods conjured from deep ignorance and deep fear. Campbell adds the ironic observation that people from every world religion will agree these others are false gods ("Does unanimous agreement from such a contentious group constitute a miracle?").
- **Cited books (reputable data and modern physics)** — Campbell points readers to: *Mind-Reach — Scientists Look at Psychic Ability* by Russell Targ and Harold Puthoff (Delacorte Press, 1977) and *The Conscious Universe: The Scientific Truth of Psychic Phenomena* by Dean I. Radin (Harper Collins, 1997) as reputable paranormal data; and *The Evolution of Physics* by Albert Einstein and Leopold Infeld (Simon and Schuster, 1961) as a non-mathematical account of relativity and quantum mechanics. He says such books show material reality is "much stranger than you ever imagined" and how little science knows about reality's fundamentals.
- **Modern physics evidence** — wave-particle duality, "paradoxical entangled particle pairs that instantaneously communicate," and statistically based material existence: Campbell's evidence that physics itself is pushing objective reality into the subjective mind-space of the experimenter, and that ~sixty years after quantum physics "destroyed the widely accepted material foundation of physical reality," what lies behind the "persistent perceptual illusion" remains mysterious to little-picture science.
- **Campbell's Third Law of No Motion** — a mock-Newtonian model of the science-vs-religion debate: "For every irrational rationalization there exists an equal but opposite irrational rationalization" — the two limiting belief systems' illogical excesses each create the rational necessity for the other, a "perpetual wasted motion machine."
- **The "great majority of people" loophole** — Campbell's self-referential joke: the great majority feel strongly they can't be grouped with the great majority; "We humans are generally as aware of our individuality as we are blind to our conformity." He offers this as a "logical loophole" for readers to squeeze back into their comfort zone.
- **Walking your dogma** — the dogma-as-dog metaphor: you're welcome to walk your dogma in Campbell's neighborhood if you clean up after it, keep it under control, and don't let it bite, dig, kill, or terrorize — his model of tolerant coexistence with belief systems responsibly held.
- **Dogma as meditation mantra** — the rare case: individuals who outwardly pursue dogma by habit of ritual and association but have inwardly grown beyond its limitations; for them dogma-plus-ritual becomes "a familiar pattern of doing" functioning like a mantra.
- **The rubber chicken parable** — offering science or religion to someone in dire need of internal substance (i.e., everyone) is like giving a starving man a rubber chicken: hope and confidence, endless chewing, continued starvation. Real food may be hidden inside, but he can smell it without accessing it; the hungrier he gets, the more fixated and less capable he becomes, dying "forever grateful to the Great Benevolence" for the gift of hope.
- **The pudding recipe cycle** — Campbell's iterative empirical method for consciousness work: be skeptical, be open-minded, "demand measurable objective results after a reasonable effort"; if none, try a different recipe; assess, adjust, repeat — until "you will be winning prizes at the county fair for the quality of your consciousness."
- **Flatland** — the chapter's closing model: two-dimensional beings who "cannot understand the connection between solid geometry and their stomachs" had to go inside (through) themselves on what was, for them, a mystical and metaphysical journey to the third dimension, transcending beliefs born of objective experience in their local causal system, via subjective experience combined with careful scientific reasoning — not dogma. Campbell says we must do the same; solid geometry is real even though it lies beyond belief-limited Flatlanders' comprehension.

## Thread
This chapter belongs to Section 2's project of demystifying mysticism. It builds directly on Chapter 18's argument (explicitly cited) that our reality system logically requires a mystical beginning, and converts that result into a diagnosis: the contradiction is not in reality but in the Western belief that PMR's objective causality is universal rather than local. By relocating causality to a parent superset reality, Campbell dissolves the Catch 22 and states the trilogy's mission outright — turning the apparently mystical into "hard science" via logic applied to two reasonable assumptions (the assumptions his model builds on). The chapter also seeds forward-pointing IOUs: consciousness quality, consciousness evolution, entropy of consciousness, and love are named as synonymous but deliberately left undefined pending later technical development, and the equivalence of physics, spirituality, consciousness, and love is announced as something a comprehensive Big TOE "must necessarily accomplish." The extended treatment of belief, dogma, pseudo-knowledge, and open-minded skepticism continues the epistemological groundwork of Section 1 (belief traps vs. knowledge) and prepares the reader-as-explorer stance (the pudding cycle, the Flatland journey inward) that the rest of the trilogy assumes.
