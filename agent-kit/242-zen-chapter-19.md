# 19
## In one paragraph
Chapter 19 interleaves the second day of the narrator and Chris's mountain climb with the "second wave of crystallization, the metaphysical one" in Phaedrus's story. The narrator wakes from a recurring dream of Chris behind a glass door, and Chris claims his father talked all night about meeting him at the top of a mountain — a report the narrator brushes off as Chris's dream. On the trail, the Chautauqua recounts how the English faculty at Bozeman hit Phaedrus with a classic dilemma: is Quality objective (then why can't instruments detect it?) or subjective (then it's just whatever you like?). Pirsig walks through Phaedrus's attempted refutations of both horns — Locke's qualities argument fails on the objective side; on the subjective side he excises the word "just," dismantles scientific materialism by reductio ad absurdum (the number zero), but is nearly wrecked by classic formalism, which splits Quality into a romantic and a classic kind. Phaedrus finally goes between the horns and declares Quality a third entity independent of mind and matter, then arrives at the culminating insight that Quality is an event — the event that creates subjects and objects rather than being caused by them. The chapter ends with Chris beating his father in a dash to the summit, shouting "The Winner!"

## Core claims
- Pirsig (through Phaedrus's story) argues that the faculty's question — is Quality in the object or in the observer? — was not an innocent inquiry but "a finisher-offer, a knockdown question," an ancient logical dilemma designed to impale him on either horn.
- He states that every dilemma affords three classic logical refutations (take the left horn, take the right horn, go between the horns) plus rhetorical ones (throw sand in the bull's eyes, sing the bull to sleep, refuse to enter the arena).
- The narrator says Phaedrus rejected the mystic escape (simply declaring Quality undefinable, as DeWeese counseled) because the Church of Reason is "irreversibly in the arena of logic" — mysticism belongs in a monastery, not a University — and, secondly, out of egotism about his own skill as logician and dialectician, "the beginning of all his troubles."
- Pirsig argues that Locke's truth — no object is knowable except in terms of its qualities — at first seemed to smash the objective horn, but failed: laboratory qualities (color, heat, hardness) are measurable physical properties, while Phaedrus's Quality ("excellence," "worth," "goodness") is not; the term quality is ambiguous.
- He claims the word "just" in "Quality is just what you like" contributes nothing logically; removed, the sentence becomes the innocuous truism "Quality is what you like." The pejorative "just" trains people to prefer what authorities like — "a good slave… When you learn not to do 'just what you like' then the System loves you."
- He argues the condemnation of "what you like" rests on two logically separate positions: scientific materialism (only what is measurable matter-energy is real, so the unmeasurable "what you like" is unreal) and classic formalism (what isn't understood intellectually isn't understood at all).
- Phaedrus, per the book, destroyed scientific materialism by reductio ad absurdum (the number zero cannot be derived from mass-energy, yet computers run on ones and zeros), and concluded that "if subjectivity is eliminated as unimportant… then the entire body of science must be eliminated with it."
- But this refutation seemed to land him in philosophic idealism — logically sound but rhetorically hopeless, "way too tedious and difficult for a course in freshman composition."
- The book says classic formalism was the truly ominous argument: it implied not one Quality but two — a romantic Quality (immediate "just seeing," the students', hip) and a classic Quality (overall understanding, the teachers', square) — so the hip-square cleavage had cut Quality itself in two. "The knife of subjectivity-and-objectivity had cut Quality in two and killed it as a working concept."
- Phaedrus's resolution, which the narrator says followed "a path that to his knowledge had never been taken before in the history of Western thought": Quality is neither mind nor matter but "a third entity which is independent of the two."
- Refining the trinity, Pirsig has Phaedrus conclude that Quality is not a thing but an event — "the event at which the subject becomes aware of the object" — and, further, that "The Quality event is the cause of the subjects and objects, which are then mistakenly presumed to be the cause of the Quality!"
- He notes Quality is opposed to subjectivity: "Quality takes you out of yourself, makes you aware of the world around you," and feelings of Quality can occur without any object at all.

## Concepts
- **dilemma** — Greek for "two premises"; an ancient logical construct "likened to the front end of an angry and charging bull": accept either premise and you are impaled on one horn or the other.
- **the three classic refutations of a dilemma** — take the left horn (refute one premise's implication), take the right horn (refute the other's), or go between the horns (deny the two premises are the only choices). Phaedrus "tested out all three."
- **throwing sand in the bull's eyes** — a rhetorical (illogical) refutation: attack the questioner, e.g. Phaedrus's earlier statement that not knowing what Quality is constitutes incompetence — "pure sand," since a speaker's competence has no relevance to the truth of what he says.
- **singing the bull to sleep** — a second rhetorical dodge: plead humble inability to answer and ask the questioners to help find the answer; the narrator says it was "way too late for lullabies like that."
- **refusing to enter the arena** — the third rhetorical alternative, "the best one in my opinion": declare that classifying Quality as subjective or objective is an attempt to define it, and it has already been said to be undefinable. DeWeese counseled this; Phaedrus disregarded it.
- **second wave of crystallization (metaphysical)** — the phase of Phaedrus's Quality work triggered by the faculty's subjective-or-objective question, in which his teaching term hardens into metaphysics.
- **Church of Reason** — the academy, "concerned exclusively with those things that can be defined"; one who steps outside logical disputation steps outside academic consideration. "Universities are places where things should be spelled out."
- **philosophical mysticism** — "the idea that truth is indefinable and can be apprehended only by nonrational means"; with us since the beginning of history and "the basis of Zen practice," but not an academic subject — a mystic's place "is in a monastery, not a University."
- **scientific materialism** — "commoner among lay followers of science than among scientists themselves"; holds that what is composed of matter or energy and measurable by instruments is real, and anything else unreal or unimportant — so "what you like," being unmeasurable, is unreal.
- **classic formalism** — insists "what isn't understood intellectually isn't understood at all"; Quality is unimportant because it is an emotional understanding unaccompanied by the intellectual elements of reason. Often found in the same person as scientific materialism but logically separate.
- **reductio ad absurdum** — the argument form Phaedrus used on scientific materialism, resting "on the truth that if the inevitable conclusions from a set of premises are absurd then it follows logically that at least one of the premises that produced them is absurd."
- **philosophic idealism** — the position (Berkeley, Hume, Kant, Fichte, Schelling, Hegel, Bradley, Bosanquet) that the world is all mind; "logical to the last comma" but rhetorically a burden — too tedious and "far-fetched" for freshman composition.
- **romantic Quality vs. classic Quality** — the split classic formalism forced: romantic Quality is "just seeing," immediate appeal, the students' hip Quality; classic Quality is "overall understanding," the teachers' square Quality. "Squareness was not the absence of Quality; it was classic Quality."
- **the analytic knife** — Phaedrus's cutting instrument, wielded here to excise "just" from "Quality is just what you like" and later to cut the presumption that Quality is the effect of subjects and objects; also the danger — "the knife of subjectivity-and-objectivity had cut Quality in two" — an "analytic meat grinder" Quality had to be saved from.
- **metaphysical trinity** — Phaedrus's interim world-picture: reality composed of three things — mind, matter, and Quality — with no relationship yet established among them.
- **monism / dualism / pluralism** — the metaphysician's usual options: one thing (such as God), two things (such as mind-matter), or an indefinite number of things; "three is an awkward number," which is why the trinity demanded a relationship.
- **the Quality event** — Phaedrus's culminating formulation: Quality is not a thing but an event, "the event at which the subject becomes aware of the object," and since objects create the subject's awareness of himself, "the event at which awareness of both subjects and objects is made possible" — the cause, not the effect, of subjects and objects.
- **the casuist answer** — Phaedrus's earlier classroom dodge to "If everyone knows what quality is, why is there such a disagreement about it?": pure Quality is the same for everyone but the objects it inheres in vary. He knew it "had the smell of falseness about it."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The glass-door dream.** The narrator wakes on a mat of sunlit pine needles from a recurring dream: in a white-painted room he faces a glass door; beyond it stand Chris, his brother, and his mother (she in tears). Chris's smile is "fixed and artificial" over deep fear; he motions for the narrator to open the door, but the narrator turns and walks away. The narrator says its meaning is obvious: Chris "is trying to relate to me and is afraid he never will."
- **The night-talk about the mountain.** Chris guesses "I bet we didn't go to sleep until three," then accuses his father, "You kept me awake," "Talking," "about the mountain!" — "Well, you talked all night about it. You said at the top of the mountain we'd see everything. You said you were going to meet me there… You sounded like you were drunk or something." The narrator, who remembers nothing, decides Chris was dreaming — but notes "Something is odd here."
- **The forgotten canteen.** The narrator left the canteen behind expecting to find water on the way ("Dumb"), so breakfast must wait until they cross the ridge and find a spring on the far side.
- **The faculty's dilemma at Bozeman.** The English faculty of Montana State College, "informed of their squareness," ask Phaedrus whether his undefined Quality exists in the things we observe or only in the observer — accompanied by good-natured smiles. Pirsig uses this as the trigger for the entire metaphysical phase.
- **The hypothetical Socrates counterattack.** The narrator imagines Socrates, "that ancient enemy of rhetorical argument," accepting the premise of his own incompetence, asking Phaedrus to show an incompetent old man what Quality is, then flattening him with questions proving Phaedrus didn't know either — illustrating why the incompetence charge was "pure sand."
- **Locke's qualities argument (failed refutation).** Locke's statement that no object is knowable except in terms of its qualities suggested scientists can't detect Quality because Quality is all they detect — exciting, then false: lab qualities (color, heat, hardness) are measurable; Phaedrus's Quality is not. He makes a mental note to dig into the historic roots of the word "quality."
- **Excising "just."** Faced with "Quality is just what you like," Phaedrus studies the sentence, brings out the knife, and removes the single word "just," whose logical contribution "was nil" — turning an angering insult into an innocuous truism and exposing the authoritarian training behind it (parents, teachers, supervisors, policemen, judges, officials, kings, dictators).
- **Banks and bank robbers.** Against the fear that doing "just what you like" means shooting heroin, robbing banks, and raping old ladies, Phaedrus notes people may not rob banks because they've considered the consequences and don't like to — and that banks exist in the first place because they're "just what people like," providers of loans.
- **The great artists.** Phaedrus is angered that under the "whatever you like" reading, Raphael, Beethoven, and Michelangelo "were all just putting out what people liked… no goal other than to titillate the senses in a big way."
- **The number zero.** His reductio starter against scientific materialism: zero, "originally a Hindu number," was introduced to the West by the Arabs during the Middle Ages and unknown to the ancient Greeks and Romans — millions of them. Zero can't be derived from any form of mass-energy; if that makes it "unscientific," digital computers, which function exclusively in ones and zeros, "should be limited to just ones for scientific work." Absurd.
- **The law of gravity.** He ends the reductio with the gravity example — the one the narrator says he gave John, Sylvia, and Chris on the first night of the trip — concluding that eliminating subjectivity eliminates the entire body of science.
- **Bubble gum and the paper mill.** Classic formalism's everyday voice: kids told not to spend the whole allowance on bubble gum (immediate impulse) because they'll want it for something else later (big picture); adults told the paper mill "may smell awful even with the best controls" but without it the town's economy collapses. In the book's old dichotomy: don't base decisions on romantic surface appeal without considering classical underlying form — "something he kind of agreed with."
- **Classroom popularity contests.** The formalists' challenge: popularity contests can determine a composition's immediate appeal, but is that Quality — or might Quality be subtler, visible "only after a long period of time?" This argument "looked like the one that might do in his whole thesis."
- **"Holy, holy, holy… blessed Trinity."** After going between the horns, Phaedrus is heard along the corridors and stairs of Montana Hall softly singing this to himself — then, per a faint and possibly imagined memory, lets the whole thought structure sit for weeks.
- **"The sun of quality" passage.** Phaedrus writes that the sun of quality "does not revolve around the subjects and objects of our existence… It has created them. They are subordinate to it!" — and at that point knows he has reached "some kind of culmination of thought."
- **The deer and the unlogged forest.** The narrator spots a deer about two hundred yards ahead through the pines (gone before Chris looks). The forest floor has no underbrush — the narrator explains that when a forest is left alone for centuries the trees shut it out; Chris says "It's like a park." Rain-soaked needles make good walking; dry, they'd be slippery.
- **The race to the summit.** Told to look for blue sky between the trees, Chris shouts "Blue sky!"; fifty yards from the top the narrator dashes, but Chris passes him giggling, gets there first, raises his arms and shouts "The Winner!" ("Egotist," the narrator thinks.) They collapse against rocks above the Gallatin Valley, Bozeman visible at one corner; a grasshopper soars away; the narrator dries his soggy boots and socks on a rock and stares at them "meditatively as vapors from them rise up toward the sun."

## Thread
This chapter is the metaphysical hinge of Phaedrus's story. It follows the first, classroom wave of crystallization (Quality as an undefined teaching term at Bozeman) and the narrator's earlier account of the classic-romantic split and the analytic knife; the gravity discussion from the trip's first night is explicitly reused as ammunition here. Under the faculty's dilemma, Quality is forced out of rhetoric and into metaphysics: Phaedrus tries and discards both horns, watches the hip-square cleavage nearly destroy Quality itself, and escapes by declaring Quality a third entity and then an event — the pre-intellectual source of subjects and objects. The narrator flags that Phaedrus's egotism in entering the arena "may have been the beginning of all his troubles," foreshadowing the breakdown, and the chapter ends the mountain-climb strand on a summit that echoes Chris's report of a dream-promise to "meet me there" — deepening the uncanny thread between the narrator and Phaedrus in the father-son story.
