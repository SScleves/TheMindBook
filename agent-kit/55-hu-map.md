# The Holographic Universe — Michael Talbot (1991): Book Map

## The thesis in one page

Talbot's central claim is that "some scientists are beginning to believe the universe itself is a kind of giant hologram, a splendidly detailed illusion" — that our world and everything in it, "from snowflakes to maple trees to falling stars and spinning electrons," are ghostly images, projections from a level of reality literally beyond both space and time. The idea has two architects who, Talbot stresses, arrived at it independently from opposite directions. Physicist David Bohm — a University of London professor, protégé of Einstein, and one of the world's most respected quantum physicists — came to it from years of dissatisfaction with standard quantum theory's inability to explain what physics kept finding: interconnectedness in twin particles, plasmas that behave like organisms, nonlocality. His mature formulation holds that visible everyday reality (the "explicate order") continuously unfolds out of and enfolds back into a deeper generative level (the "implicate order") — a dynamic "holomovement" in which everything, including consciousness, is one unbroken whole. Neurophysiologist Karl Pribram, of Stanford, came to it from the failure of standard brain theories: Lashley's rat experiments showed memory survives no matter what brain tissue is removed, and the hologram's "whole in every part" property gave Pribram the mechanism for a brain that stores memory and processes vision as distributed interference patterns, using the same Fourier mathematics holography uses.

Welded together (as chapter 2 does explicitly), the two halves say: the brain is a hologram enfolded in a holographic universe — our brains mathematically construct objective reality by interpreting frequencies projected from a deeper, dimension-transcending order. What does "reality as hologram" claim to explain? Talbot's list starts modest — why no theory can account for all natural phenomena, how people with hearing in one ear localize sound, how we recognize a long-unseen and changed face — and then makes the book's most sweeping move: the model "helped explain virtually all paranormal and mystical experiences," including telepathy, precognition, psychokinesis, mystical oneness with the universe, near-death experiences, lucid dreams, and synchronicities. Talbot argues the traffic runs both ways: the model accounts for the paranormal, and the accumulated weight of paranormal evidence "actually seems to necessitate" the model. He frames science's resistance as human prejudice and "belief addiction" (Siegel's phrase) rather than objectivity — while conceding up front that the holographic idea is controversial, unfinished, "an idea in the making," and that Bohm and Pribram themselves do not endorse everything in the book.

## How the argument builds

The book runs in three parts, and each chapter explicitly hands off to the next; the escalation is deliberate — lab neuroscience → physics → psychology → medicine → miracles → auras → time → space/NDEs/UFOs → ancient traditions and the future.

**Part I — A Remarkable New View of Reality** builds the model itself. Chapter 1 establishes the brain-side half through Pribram's decades of work (Lashley's unerasable memories, the DeValoises' Fourier findings, Pietsch's shuffled salamander brains) and ends on the pivot that powers the whole book: if the picture of reality in our brains is a hologram, what is it a hologram *of*? — the question that leads Pribram to Bohm. Chapter 2 supplies the cosmos-side half through Bohm's intellectual biography (plasmas, the quantum potential, the ink-drop-in-glycerine device, the implicate order), backs it with Aspect's 1982 nonlocality experiment, then explicitly fuses the two: brains construct concrete reality out of a frequency domain that is itself a projection from the implicate order. Its closing lines announce the plan for the rest of the book — exploring the model's effect on other fields, "beginning with Part II."

**Part II — Mind and Body** cashes the model out in escalating applications. Chapter 3 makes the first cash-out in psychology: collective unconscious, ESP dreams, psychosis as unmanaged glimpses of the implicate, Grof's transpersonal LSD data, MPD, synchronicity — and flags the next chapter twice via MPD's per-personality physiological changes. Chapter 4 dissolves the mind/body border (imagery therapy, placebo, multiples switching biological states, hypnotic cure of genetic disease, Lourdes, stigmata), deliberately restricting itself to phenomena that "do not seem to violate any of the known laws of physics" — explicitly setting up chapter 5, which crosses that line: psychokinesis in the lab (PEAR), the convulsionaires' invulnerability, fire immunity, materialization, and whole-reality transformations, ending in the claim that physical laws are habits and consensus reality a collectively sustained "reality-field." Chapter 6 turns from what the mind can do to what it can perceive: the human energy field/aura as a glimpse of the frequency domain, Hunt's electromyographic measurements, the field as the body's blueprint ("its own version of an implicate order") — and hands off directly to Part III: if even time unfolds out of the implicate, the division into past, present, and future is another construct of the mind.

**Part III — Space and Time** demolishes both coordinates and then closes the circle. Chapter 7 takes time: retrocognition and psychic archaeology (the past persists in the implicate), precognition and its free-will resolution (Loye's parallel holographic futures and "hololeaps"), then reincarnation research (Whitton, Stevenson) as evidence that a "metaconscious" stratum of the psyche scripts our lives and even our bodies; it closes promising "still other shimmerings." Chapter 8 takes space: OBEs and NDEs as evidence that consciousness is nonlocal, the afterlife as a thought-built holographic realm differing from ours in degree not kind, then Marian visions and UFOs as "omnijective" phenomena — deferring the question of a "coming confluence between science and the spiritual" to the close. Chapter 9 answers it by looking backward and forward at once: the model's two central features (implicate/explicate structure, whole-in-every-part nonlocality) were prefigured in virtually all the world's mystical and shamanic traditions; science needs restructuring toward participation and acceptance of the psychic; and rising NDEs, Marian visions, and UFO encounters may signal an evolutionary "shamanizing of modern humanity" — a return to the plastic, dreamtime-like reality the aborigines say the earth once was. The final line reframes the whole book: "We are, as the aborigines say, just learning how to survive in infinity."

## Chapter index

**[00-introduction.md] — Introduction.** Opens with the Princess Leia hologram and states the thesis: the universe as a giant hologram, with Bohm and Pribram as its independent two-track architects. It argues the model explains "virtually all paranormal and mystical experiences," previews the supporting evidence (Pribram's corroborated predictions, Aspect 1982, the PEAR lab), and mounts the book's polemical frame — science's rejection of the paranormal as prejudice and belief-addiction (the skeptical-physicist anecdote, Child on the ignored Maimonides experiments, Siegel on belief addiction). Its strongest material is the origin-story credentialing: Bohm's refusal to testify against Oppenheimer, Pribram's refusal to allow lobotomies. Its role: the whole book in miniature, plus the honest caveats — model-not-theory, Bohm/Pribram don't endorse everything, Talbot's own experiences are anecdotal.

**[01-the-brain-as-hologram.md] — Ch 1, The Brain as Hologram.** Traces how Pribram concluded memory is distributed, not localized: Penfield's engram evidence undone by Lashley's rat surgeries, Pribram's own clinical observations, and the mid-1960s hologram article that hit him "like a thunderbolt." Extends the model to vision, memory capacity, recall, recognition, skill transfer, phantom limbs, and the brain-constructed "world-out-there," grounded in Gabor's Fourier mathematics. Strongest material: Lashley's unerasable maze memories, Pietsch's 700 salamander-brain operations, the DeValoises' 1979 finding that visual-cortex cells respond to Fourier translations of patterns. Role: the brain-side pillar, ending on the pivot question — what is reality a hologram *of*? — that leads to Bohm.

**[02-the-cosmos-as-hologram.md] — Ch 2, The Cosmos as Hologram.** Bohm's intellectual biography, from quantum strangeness (wave-particle duality, the EPR paradox) through plasmas and plasmons, the quantum potential and nonlocality, to the mature implicate/explicate framework and the holomovement — reality as undivided wholeness, consciousness as a subtler form of matter, space as a plenum of energy. Strongest material: the fish-and-two-cameras analogy for twin-particle correlation, the ink-drop-in-glycerine device, and the Aspect 1982 experiment, which Talbot says doesn't prove Bohm's model but gives it "tremendous support." Role: the cosmos-side pillar and the explicit weld — the brain as a hologram enfolded in a holographic universe — plus the launch of Part II.

**[03-the-holographic-model-and-psychology.md] — Ch 3, The Holographic Model and Psychology.** Applies the model to anomalous psychology: Jung's collective unconscious (the solar-phallus patient) explained by implicate interconnectedness; Ullman's Maimonides ESP-dream results and the wisdom of dreams; psychosis and mania as unmanaged glimpses of the implicate; Wolf's lucid dreams as "real-image" holograms and parallel-universe visits; Grof's thousands of LSD and holotropic sessions (womb memories, ancestral and racial memories, the Ladislav/Kromeriz case); MPD as holographic fragmentation; and synchronicity, which Davies and Peat now read through nonlocality. Strongest material: the Grof corpus and the per-personality brain-wave changes in multiples. Role: the model's first big cash-out beyond physics and neuroscience, and the bridge (via MPD physiology) to health.

**[04-i-sing-the-body-holographic.md] — Ch 4, I Sing the Body Holographic.** Argues the mind/body cannot distinguish neural holograms of imagined reality from experienced reality, so images and beliefs produce dramatic, specific physiological change: Simonton's and Achterberg's imagery work, the placebo effect (sham angina surgery, the Wright/Krebiozen tragedy, placebo LSD trips), multiples switching allergies, diabetes, and eye color "as rapidly as the flutter of a deck of cards," the belief typology (Schwarz, Dajo, Mohotty), hypnotic cure of the genetic Brocq's disease, Michelli's Vatican-documented bone regeneration at Lourdes, and stigmata as "ejection of psychophysical structures." Closes with acupuncture microsystems and hand-pattern diagnostics as the body's own part-contains-whole evidence. Role: mapping the *range* of mind-over-body, deliberately staying inside known physics to set up chapter 5.

**[05-a-pocketful-of-miracles.md] — Ch 5, A Pocketful of Miracles.** Recasts miracles as latent human psychokinesis: PEAR's REG and pinball data (small but real PK in most people, with stable "signatures"), the San Gennaro blood, the Jansenist convulsionaires' invulnerability documented by Montgeron, fire immunity from the Camisards to kahuna lava walks, and materialization (Kamro's blood, Sai Baba, Talbot's own poltergeist). Explains what PK alone cannot via Grof's direct intervention in the implicate "generative matrix": the laws of physics as habits, consensus reality as a "reality-field," the universe as Tiller's holodeck. Strongest material: the PEAR corpus and the Saint-Médard documentation. Role: the boldest escalation — from mind-over-body to mind-over-reality — setting up the later treatment of space, time, and self as constructs.

**[06-seeing-holographically.md] — Ch 6, Seeing Holographically.** Since ordinary vision is already a brain-made construction (the blind spot, temporal-lobe editing), aura-seers may be glimpsing the frequency domain: Brennan's and Dryer's diagnostic readings, Karagulla's secret network of doctors with "higher sense perception," Krieger's therapeutic touch, and Valerie Hunt's electromyograph measurements (100–1600 cps radiation strongest at the chakra points, the "chaos holograph pattern"). Proposes the energy field as the body's blueprint — illness appears there first — and, via Libet/Feinstein and Hunt's EMG-before-EEG data, that the mind may reside in the field, not the brain. Strongest material: Hunt's instrumented work and Karagulla's hospital-verified "Diane" diagnoses. Role: carries the model from healing into perception, and hands Part III its opening premise — linear time as a mental construct.

**[07-time-out-of-mind.md] — Ch 7, Time Out of Mind.** In a holographic universe past and future stay "recorded" in the implicate: retrocognition and psychic archaeology (Ossowiecki's verified Stone Age details, Emerson and McMullen's longhouse), precognition (Schmidt's billion-to-one machine results, the Maimonides dream lab, Croiset's chair tests), and the free-will resolution — Loye's parallel holographic futures and "hololeaps," Bohm's "shadows cast deep in the implicate order," the plastic future of the Titanic premonitions. Its second half reads Whitton's interlife regressions and Stevenson's birthmark-carryover children as evidence a metaconscious self scripts life and body — echoed by kahuna, Tibetan, Sufi, and Cayce traditions of "thought as builder" — then closes with mass future-progressions, retroactive PK, and the Moberly–Jourdain walk into 1789 Versailles. Role: extends nonlocality from space to time and shifts the book's claim from mind *perceiving* the hologram to mind *authoring* it.

**[08-traveling-in-the-superhologram.md] — Ch 8, Traveling in the Superhologram.** Argues location itself is an illusion, using OBEs (Maria's tennis shoe, Sabom's cardiac-patient comparison, Monroe, Tart's Miss Z) and above all NDEs — worldwide, demographically flat, cross-culturally consistent, veridical even in the blind, occurring on flat EEGs. Reads the NDE's features (thought-sculpted bodies, the realm of light, the instantaneous holographic life review, "thought balls" of total knowledge, life plans and flashforwards) as a shift of consciousness into the frequency-like levels of the hologram, corroborated by Swedenborg, the Sufis' alam almithal, Sri Yukteswar, Sri Aurobindo, and shamanic dreamtime traditions. Extends the logic to Marian visions and UFOs as "omnijective" — real yet mind-created. Role: completes the demolition of space-time and fuses nearly every earlier thread into "the dream is dreaming itself."

**[09-return-to-the-dreamtime.md] — Ch 9, Return to the Dreamtime.** Shows the model is an ancient idea rediscovered: the Tibetan void/nonvoid, Brahman/maya, the Kabbalah's illusory projection, Indra's net, Fa-Tsang's mirrored candle, Black Elk's "anywhere is the center of the world" — with Leibniz's monads possibly looping Hua-yen intuition forward into the calculus that enabled Gabor's hologram. Surveys the model's technical fruits (Zuccarelli's holophonic sound, Prigogine's dissipative structures, holographic computing), then argues science needs restructuring: accept psychic and spiritual phenomena, broaden what counts as evidence, replace detached objectivity with participation. Ends speculatively: rising NDEs, Marian visions, and UFO encounters as "the shamanizing of modern humanity," pointing toward a return of the plastic dreamtime. Role: the closing of the circle and the book's program and prophecy.

## The evidentiary strategy

Talbot builds his case from four recurring kinds of evidence, deliberately braided together:

- **Laboratory experiments and instrumented measurements** — Lashley's rat surgeries and the DeValoises' Fourier work (ch 1); Aspect's 1982 twin-photon experiment (ch 2); the Maimonides dream lab, Schmidt's random-event machines, Puthoff and Targ's remote viewing, and PEAR's REG/pinball trials (chs 3, 5, 7); Achterberg's cell-specific imagery study and Quinn's double-blind therapeutic-touch study (chs 4, 6); Hunt's electromyograph data (ch 6); Schmidt–Schlitz retroactive PK (ch 7). This is the book's front line: Talbot leads with Bohm's and Pribram's credentials and with results he presents as statistically overwhelming ("an incontrovertible aberration of substantial proportions," per Jahn and Dunne).
- **Clinical cases and physician testimony** — Simonton's Frank, Mason's Brocq's-disease cure, the Vatican-documented Michelli regeneration, Braun's and Howland's multiples, Karagulla's HSP doctors and "Diane," Sabom's cardiac comparison, Morse's child NDE study, Whitton's and Stevenson's reincarnation files. The book leans on the professional standing of the reporters (JAMA's praise of Stevenson's "painstakingly and unemotionally collected" data is quoted approvingly).
- **Historical and religious records** — Penfield's and Bede's-era accounts, Montgeron's four volumes on the Saint-Médard convulsionaires (with Hume and Voltaire as period witnesses), Thurston's stigmata and inedia cases, San Gennaro, the Camisards, Swedenborg's twenty volumes, the Tibetan and Egyptian books of the dead, and chapter 9's whole survey of mystical traditions. Talbot's framing device here is convergence: many independent witnesses, cultures, and centuries reporting the same structures ("when many reliable individuals independently report the same kind of phenomena, it is time science takes cognizance of it," Karagulla).
- **Personal anecdote** — Talbot's psychic family, his poltergeist and its materializations, the umbrella incident, his aura sight and readings with Carol Dryer, his teenage OBE. He flags these himself from the Introduction on: they "can only be viewed as anecdotal evidence," though for him they are "the most compelling proof of all."

The book is explicit about how it wants this mix weighed. Chapter 9 states the standard outright: mass anecdotal testimony about important matters should count as evidence, quoting Stevenson — "I believe it is better to learn what is probable about important matters than to be certain about trivial ones" — and accusing science of a double standard (the unproven Big Bang accepted as fashionable; the NDE realm attacked as unfashionable). And the book carries its own caveats throughout: the holographic idea "has [not] achieved the status of a model or theory in the strictest sense" (Introduction); Bohm and Pribram do not embrace all the views and conclusions in the book; Jahn and Dunne do not identify themselves with the holographic model and differ from Bohm "in several significant ways" (ch 5); the reincarnation chapters state plainly that "the evidence does not prove that reincarnation exists, nor is it the intention of this book to make such an argument" (ch 7); Haraldsson admits he cannot conclusively rule out sleight of hand in the Sai Baba material (ch 5); and chapter 4 keeps its own counterpoints (vitamin C cures scurvy and insulin works for skeptical diabetics — not everything is placebo; not all drug side effects are placebo, consult a physician).

## Reading it critically

The notes themselves record the following tensions and caveats — the book's own, and the transcription's:

- **Contested scientific status, stated in the text.** The Introduction concedes the model is highly controversial, not accepted by a majority of scientists, and "an idea in the making." Chapter 1 reports that most scientists remain unpersuaded by the holographic brain, that rival theories (brain chemicals, electrical fluctuations) also have evidence, and quotes skeptic Frank Wood: "there are precious few experimental findings for which holography is the necessary, or even preferable, explanation." Chapter 2 records that the reaction to Bohm's 1952 quantum-potential proposal was mainly negative — "little more than heresy" — that the Aharonov-Bohm effect is still occasionally disputed in print, that some physicists dismiss the zero-point energy calculation as error, that Lee Smolin does not find Bohm's theory "very compelling, physically," and that Abner Shimony admits he doesn't understand it; Bohm himself answers the untestability charge only by pointing at superstring theory's similar horizon. Aspect's experiment is expressly framed as support, not proof.
- **Principals who decline parts of the thesis.** Bohm and Pribram do not endorse everything in the book (Introduction). Jahn and Dunne's theory "differs from Bohm's thinking in several significant ways" and they do not identify with the holographic model (ch 5). Bohm prefers not to speculate on specific miracles; Pribram insists "I don't believe anything goes"; and Lyall Watson is no longer convinced any current physics, including the holographic idea, explains supernormal abilities (ch 5).
- **Claims the book itself marks as unproven or self-limiting.** Chapter 7: reincarnation evidence is suggestive, explicitly not proof; the psychiatry textbook it cites acknowledges apparent past-life memories while rejecting rebirth. Chapter 5: once two or more people can create a shared reality, consensus verification fails as a litmus test — Talbot concedes the reality-field thesis "can never be proved" and becomes a matter of personal philosophy (Globus admits "a nice theory postulates existence" as bias). Chapter 5's neutrino-mass story remains unresolved (Soviet vs. U.S. laboratory split). Chapter 4: Simonton's critics note his patients were self-selected fighters; the British-vs-American aspirin discrepancy is left an open question; Phillips's appearing/disappearing tumors are of unspecified kind; reflexology is "not experimentally vindicated" and iridology studies are "extremely conflicting" (chs 4, 6).
- **The participatory-reality caveat cuts against the book's own witnesses.** Chapter 6 warns that psychics may be partly creating the structures they claim to discover: Brennan's seven layers grow clearer the more clients understand them, the tantras' authors perceived only three, and the Sanskrit letters some psychics see in chakras may be structures imposed by ancient Hindu belief. Talbot explicitly says psychic testimony is presented as evidence to be taken seriously, not established scientific fact, and that some claims (e.g., which aura layer produces thought-images) cannot yet be confirmed or denied. Chapter 8 similarly ends with competing, unreconciled readings of UFOs (psychic projection vs. extradimensional intelligence vs. both), Talbot conceding "probably no single explanation accounts for all aspects," and leaving the identity of the beings of light an open question. Chapter 9 files Strieber's and McKenna's evolutionary visions as speculation and offers the dreamtime-return scenario "in the spirit of pure speculation," while noting holographic computing remains a refinement of the mechanistic approach.
- **OCR/transcription gaps flagged in the notes.** Chapter 1: the source scan rendered "engram" as "engrains." Chapter 4: the Breznitz soldiers passage is fragmentary in the scan. Chapter 7: the Ossowiecki session date reads "February 14, 19S5" (the digit is corrupt — 1935 or 1955); the Evans-Wentz fairy-faith section is cut mid-sentence; and the connecting passage on dream-divination traditions (Pharaoh's dream, the "primal ocean" remark) survives only partially, beginning mid-sentence.
- **The book's polemic is a frame, not a finding.** The claims that science's rejection of the paranormal stems from prejudice and belief-addiction, that a "parapsychological Rosa Parks" is needed, and that the Big Bang enjoys a fashionable double standard are Talbot's arguments about the scientific community, presented as such in the Introduction and chapter 9 — and they sit alongside his acknowledgment that psychic functioning's elusiveness in the laboratory is real and is part of why scientists doubt it.
