# Ch 10 — But Is It Real?

## In one paragraph
Campbell slips back in time to recount how he and his fellow researcher Dennis, working with Bob Monroe at his lab, moved from asking "Is this stuff real?" to being convinced by their own evidence that it was. He describes the discipline they imposed on themselves (no drugs, alcohol, food additives, preservatives, caffeine, or sugar — anything that could muddy the perception of subtle shifts in consciousness) and the thousands of logged, taped, and measured hours of exploration in the nonphysical. The pivotal event is a controlled joint out-of-body experiment in which he and Dennis, in separate soundproof chambers, gave real-time taped descriptions of a shared nonphysical trip that correlated astonishingly — Campbell's conclusion: "THIS STUFF WAS REAL!" Hundreds of further data points followed (reading blackboard numbers while asleep, visiting people's homes, remote diagnosis and healing, remote viewing verified at a sleep & dream lab, uniquely focused EEG results confirmed at Duke). Campbell argues that no amount of second-hand data can convince anyone — only unambiguous firsthand experience shatters old beliefs — and closes with the team's search for a self-consistent scientific model of how reality works, tentatively adopting Jane Roberts' channeled Seth material as an imperfect starting point.

## Core claims
- Campbell claims that clear perception of subtle shifts in consciousness required eliminating every consciousness-affecting substance: he swore off all drugs and alcohol, and later permanently banished food additives, preservatives, caffeine, and sugar — and says the difference was dramatic.
- Campbell claims that, unlike his previous TM meditation, in this mental space he and Dennis were active, willful, autonomous agents within a larger nonphysical reality — going places, doing things, and communicating with nonphysical beings.
- Campbell claims Bob Monroe deliberately "never led the witness": he acted as a neutral observer, never hinting at what they might experience, because he wanted real science, not an echo of his own experiences.
- Campbell claims the central question at the start — for him, Dennis, and Bob — was whether the experiences were inside (imagined) or outside (having existence independent of them), and that this had to be settled by evidence of operational significance.
- Campbell claims the first joint-trip experiment produced astonishing correlation between his and Dennis's independently taped real-time descriptions: same visuals, same telepathic conversations, same clarity — leaving only one good explanation: the stuff was real (his fallback alternative, one imagining and the other reading thoughts telepathically, being "almost as far out").
- Campbell claims the phenomenon did not depend on the two of them — he and Nancy Lea shared "equally astonishing" joint experiences — and that hundreds of data points accumulated, with later evidentiary experiences tending to be clearer and more dramatic than the initial ones.
- Campbell claims he and Dennis remained the same demanding, skeptical scientists but stopped asking if it was real: "We now knew the answer."
- Campbell claims that firsthand experience is epistemically indispensable: nobody else can convince you; all the data in the world become suspect if you are not there to know the truth firsthand; old beliefs must be shattered before you can imagine a bigger picture.
- Campbell claims that once you find true knowledge, ignorance is no longer an option — and if the knowledge is unusual, "strange becomes a way of life."
- Campbell claims their EEG data, analyzed at Duke University (which he says was recognized worldwide in the 60s–70s as the leader in parapsychological research), were blatantly unique — independent corroboration that their techniques produced a physical manifestation of specific altered states.
- Campbell claims the existing models of reality they surveyed were "mostly a mishmash of emotion laden, belief focused, unscientific balderdash" with little or no reproducible hard evidence — unsuitable for a scientific inquiry.
- Campbell claims the Seth material (Seth Speaks, Jane Roberts) was the best available candidate model — imperfect but more or less rational, consistent, and coherent — a theoretical basis to begin from tentatively and skeptically, not a place to end up; and that its being channeled was a plus, not a problem, since they were personally familiar with the nonphysical and its sentient beings.
- Campbell claims that as a physicist he was driven to find the science at the root — a self-consistent model where all the data are consistent and make sense: "How could any self-respecting physicist not ask these questions?"

## Concepts
- **NPMR (nonphysical reality)** — The larger, bodiless reality in which Campbell and Dennis operated during altered states: a "mental space" that was "nonphysical — bodiless," where they were active, willful, autonomous agents who went places, did things, and communicated with nonphysical beings. The chapter uses the acronym NPMR for it without expanding it here.
- **altered states of consciousness** — The various distinct mental states Campbell and Dennis learned, with years of practice, to differentiate among, get to, shift between, and return from at will; the research hinged on clearly perceiving subtle shifts among them.
- **operational significance** — The evidential test Bob set for their subjective experiences: whether what they experienced in NPMR produced consistent, objectively checkable results (correlated descriptions, verified numbers, confirmed remote observations) — the criterion separating "inside/imagined" from "outside/independent."
- **inside vs. outside** — Campbell's framing of the burning question: were the experiences inside (imagined by them) or outside (possessing their own existence independent of the experiencers)?
- **firsthand experience** — Campbell's epistemological requirement: "the inescapable logic of unambiguous firsthand experience" is the only thing that makes the truth sink in deeply; secondhand data, however carefully taken, remain suspect to one who was not there.
- **discovery mode** — The stance Campbell says he and Dennis held: neither optimistic nor pessimistic, open to all possibilities, wanting only the truth, trusting that with sound methodology enough results would accumulate to "tell their own story."
- **intent focusing tools** — Tools Campbell says they designed, generated, and tested for their own use in the nonphysical (mentioned as part of the evidence-gathering program; not further specified in this chapter).
- **healing with mind and intent** — Attempting to heal people's illnesses with their minds and intent, which Campbell calls "a good technique for interacting evidentially with the energy of others"; related to diagnosing illnesses in people they never met but that somebody else knew well.
- **remote viewing** — Describing, under controlled conditions, pictures being displayed in another room — the experiments Campbell and Dennis volunteered for at a well-known sleep & dream lab, where they described all the pictures correctly.
- **alpha and theta waves** — EEG brainwave measures: Dennis's results produced the highest alpha levels ever recorded at Duke; Campbell's showed strong simultaneous alpha and theta with unprecedented narrowness of peaks — brainwaves "tightly focused to specific, nearly single frequencies."
- **EEG / GSR** — The physiological instrumentation of the lab sessions (electroencephalogram and galvanic skin response electrodes the subjects wore and pulled off after sessions); EEG scrolls were sent to Duke for detailed analysis.
- **TM meditation** — Campbell's previous meditation practice, contrasted with the lab work: in TM he was not (as here) an active, willful, autonomous agent within a larger nonphysical reality.
- **channeling / the Seth material** — Seth Speaks by Jane Roberts, a channeled text the team adopted as their candidate model of reality: imperfect but more or less rational, consistent, and coherent; its channeled origin was "a plus" ("Would you ask a fish about mountain hiking trails?"). They spent much lab training time testing and interpreting Seth's concepts and procuring information from their own nonphysical sources — always with evidence required.
- **models of reality / the Big Picture** — What Campbell sought beyond raw data: a self-consistent model explaining the how's and why's, defining the interactions — the processes, limits, and rules of how reality functions — "where all the data are consistent and makes sense."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The joint out-of-body trip (the pivotal experiment).** Moving parts: Campbell and Dennis in separate soundproof chambers, each with a microphone suspended from the ceiling recording real-time descriptions to tape; EEG and GSR electrodes attached; Bob in the control room; neither could hear the other. Both achieved the altered state, left their bodies, met in the nonphysical as planned, went places, saw things, and conversed with each other and with several nonphysical beings encountered along the way. Afterward Bob deadpanned "So, you two think you were together?" then played the tapes: for almost two hours the correlation was astonishing — same visuals, same telepathic conversations, same clarity. Campbell says this left only one good explanation: "THIS STUFF WAS REAL!" — the only alternative he could muster (one imagining, the other reading thoughts telepathically) being nearly as far out. He repeated "This stuff might actually be real" to himself fifty times over the next few days.
- **Replications with other partners.** The experiment was repeated with similar results; Campbell and Nancy Lea shared "equally astonishing" joint experiences — showing the phenomenon did not depend on Campbell and Dennis specifically.
- **Blackboard number reading.** Someone would write a random three- or four-digit number on a blackboard next to the control room; the subjects would read it while their bodies lay asleep; it was erased and rewritten repeatedly. Presented as objective verification of nonphysical perception.
- **Remote visits to people's homes.** They traveled (nonphysically) to people's homes, saw what the occupants were doing, then called or spoke to them the next day to check it out.
- **Time travel.** They "traveled into the future and into the past" (stated without elaboration in this chapter).
- **Remote healing and diagnosis.** They tried to heal people's illnesses with mind and intent, and diagnosed illnesses in people they had never met but that somebody else knew well — chosen as evidential interaction with others' energy.
- **Intent focusing tools.** Designed, generated, and tested tools for focusing intent in the nonphysical; part of a three-year painstaking trial-and-error refinement of processes, during which they discerned subtleties of the altered states where things worked well and where they did not.
- **The sleep & dream lab remote viewing experiments.** External, controlled experiments at a well-known sleep & dream lab: describe pictures displayed in another room. They described all the pictures correctly — but Campbell says that was not the most remarkable result.
- **The Duke University EEG analysis.** The lab's EEG scrolls, sent to Duke for detailed analysis, showed Dennis producing the highest alpha levels ever recorded at Duke and Campbell showing strong simultaneous alpha and theta unlike anything the researchers had seen — singular events due to the narrowness of the peaks, out of thousands and thousands of results ("Your data blew them away at Duke"). Campbell takes this as independent physical corroboration that their techniques uniquely produced specific altered states, noting Duke's 60s–70s standing as the worldwide leader in parapsychological research.
- **The fish analogy.** "Would you ask a fish about mountain hiking trails?" — Campbell's argument that a channeled (nonphysical-sourced) model of the nonphysical is a plus, not a defect: you consult a source native to the terrain.
- **The Seth Speaks model-testing program.** After surveying existing models and finding them unscientific balderdash, the team adopted Seth Speaks (Jane Roberts) as a tentative theoretical starting point and spent years of lab training time testing and interpreting Seth's concepts and procuring information from their own nonphysical sources — "sometimes confusing, sometimes clarifying... always evidence was required."
- **The team's division of labor.** Campbell the theoretician (the physicist), Dennis into applications (the engineer), Bob the practical man focused on what worked and on objective credibility, Bill Yost contributing engineering insight, management, and support, Nancy Lea doing daily support and becoming a full partner in nonphysical exploration (as her sister Penny Honeycutt had a few years before), families participating from time to time. Bob facilitated rather than directed, with "the wise and knowing smile of all good teachers who know how to let their students figure it out for themselves."

## Thread
This chapter is the evidential hinge of Section 1 ("Delusion or Knowledge"): the autobiographical narrative of Campbell's years at Monroe's lab reaches the point where, by his account, the question in the section's title gets answered for him personally — the subjective experiences pass objective tests and become "knowledge" rather than possible delusion. It follows from the preceding chapters' account of how Campbell came to Monroe's lab and learned to reach altered states, and it sets up the rest of the trilogy in two ways: it establishes his core epistemology (open-minded skepticism plus the indispensability of firsthand experience — the "data points" he will repeatedly invoke), and it launches the theory-building quest — the hunt for a self-consistent Big Picture model of reality, beginning tentatively from the Seth material — that becomes My Big TOE itself. The chapter also seeds the term NPMR, used here for the nonphysical arena the later model formalizes.
