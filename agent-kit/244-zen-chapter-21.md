# 21

## In one paragraph
This short chapter narrates the final descent from the mountain while the Chautauqua turns from lofty metaphysics toward "solid, practical, day-to-day information." The narrator and Chris eat lunch, redistribute pack weight since Chris says he can carry more, then hack through thick brush and deadfalls down a steep, mucky slope to a creek, cross it by rope, hitch a ride into Bozeman, and check into a hotel for the night rather than wake the DeWeeses. Interleaved with the descent, the narrator reflects that discovering Quality equals the Buddha "at some mountaintop of personal experience" is spectacular but unimportant unless it can be carried back down to ordinary, dreary working life — Sylvia's "funeral procession" of people. He judges that Phaedrus's mystical equation of Quality with the Tao did nothing for Quality or the Tao themselves, but did benefit reason, by showing how reason can expand to assimilate what had been considered irrational. The chapter closes by previewing the next task: showing Quality as the unifying term of Religion, Art, and Science, with Science — because of its "value-free" doctrine — being the area needing the relationship established most urgently.

## Core claims
- He observes that pioneers are "invariably, by their nature, mess-makers": forging ahead toward their noble, distant goal, they never notice the crud and debris they leave behind, and someone else is left to clean it up — a low-key, undepressing but necessary job you first have to "depress" into before it doesn't seem so bad.
- The narrator argues that a metaphysical discovery of "Quality and the Buddha" made at a mountaintop of personal experience is "very spectacular. And very unimportant" unless it bears on the valleys, the dull jobs and monotonous years everyone else lives in.
- He states he doesn't know whether Phaedrus's claim that "Quality is the Tao" is true, since Phaedrus only compared his understanding of one mystic entity to another, and may not have completely understood what Quality was — or, more likely, may not have understood the Tao. "He certainly was no sage," and there's "plenty of advice for sages in that book he would have done well to heed" (the source does not name the book in this passage).
- He judges that all of Phaedrus's "metaphysical mountain climbing did absolutely nothing" to further understanding of either Quality or the Tao — "Not a thing."
- He argues this is not really a rejection, because any description of Quality is a kind of definition that falls short of its mark, and Phaedrus himself would likely have agreed — indeed would have said that statements falling short of the mark are worse than no statement, since they can be mistaken for truth and thereby retard understanding of Quality.
- He states that what Phaedrus's work did benefit was reason itself: it showed a way for reason to expand to include elements previously unassimilable and considered irrational, and he attributes the "chaotic, disconnected spirit of the twentieth century" to the overwhelming presence of these irrational elements crying for assimilation.
- He claims that if Quality can be shown to be the central term of Religion, Art, and Science, and to be of one kind only rather than many, then these three now-disunified areas of human experience have a rational basis for "introconversion."
- On Art, he asserts the relationship to Quality has already been shown "rather exhaustively" through Phaedrus's understanding of Quality in the art of rhetoric, and needs no further analysis: "Art is high-quality endeavor," equivalently "Art is the Godhead as revealed in the works of man" — two very differently sounding statements he calls "actually identical."
- On Religion, he notes (to be developed later) that the old English roots for "Buddha" and "Quality," and for "God" and "good," appear to be identical.
- On Science, he states this is where he wants to focus next, because the dictum that science and its offspring technology are "value free" (i.e., "quality free") "has got to go," and that this value-freedom underlies the "death-force effect" raised earlier in the Chautauqua.

## Concepts
- **Chautauqua** — the narrator's ongoing name (used again here) for the running philosophical discourse he delivers alongside the road trip; in this chapter he explicitly redirects it away from "intellectual abstractions of an extremely general nature" toward "solid, practical, day-to-day information."
- **Pioneers as mess-makers** — the narrator's observation that pioneers are "invariably, by their nature, mess-makers": they forge ahead seeing only their noble, distant goal and never notice the crud and debris left behind, which someone else gets to clean up, and it's "not a very glamorous or interesting job," requiring one to "depress for a while" into a low-key mood before it isn't so bad; introduced right after the narrator announces he wants to move the Chautauqua from abstraction toward practical, day-to-day material.
- **Quality is the Tao** — Phaedrus's claim, evaluated here as an unverifiable comparison of "his understanding of one mystic entity with another," not something the narrator can test for truth.
- **Metaphysical mountain climbing** — the narrator's dismissive term for Phaedrus's abstract philosophical pursuit of Quality/Tao/Buddha equivalences, which he says did nothing to advance understanding of Quality or the Tao themselves.
- **Reason (as beneficiary)** — what the narrator says Phaedrus's work actually served: expanding reason's capacity to assimilate previously "irrational" elements, rather than settling metaphysics.
- **Introconversion** — the narrator's term for the rational convertibility between Religion, Art, and Science that would follow if Quality is shown to be their shared, single-kind central term.
- **Art as Godhead revealed in the works of man** — the narrator's formulation equating "Art is high-quality endeavor" with this more "high-sounding" phrase, presented as identical statements.
- **Value freedom / "quality free"** — the doctrine, attributed to science and technology, that they are free of value; the narrator says this dictum "has got to go" and links it to the "death-force effect" mentioned earlier in the Chautauqua.
- **Funeral procession** — Sylvia's phrase (recalled from earlier in the book) for the oncoming people/traffic she noticed; here recast as the ordinary, dreary working world that the mountaintop insight about Quality must be brought back down to serve.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Lunch and pack redistribution**: at a lower point on the mountain, the narrator and Chris finish lunch (salami, candy, spring water), and Chris asks to carry a heavier load; the narrator transfers some of his own pack weight to Chris and notices the difference — presented as a small concrete beat of Chris maturing/becoming "considerate when he's in the mood."
- **Hacking through brush with the machete**: the slope has been logged and is overgrown; Chris fetches the machete from the narrator's pack, and the narrator must cut two or three branches per step, illustrating the slow, effortful, step-by-step nature of the descent that mirrors the shift to "practical, day-to-day" material.
- **Descent over deadfalls, cliff, and rocky crevice**: they climb over grey weathered deadfall trunks, follow a cliff edge to find a descent, go down a narrow draw with a rivulet, then hear a larger creek; they cross it via a rope which they leave behind — concrete staging of the return from the metaphysical "mountaintop" to ordinary ground.
- **Hitchhike and hotel check-in**: other campers give them a ride into Bozeman; rather than wake the DeWeeses, they check into the main downtown hotel, where tourists stare at the narrator's Army clothes, walking stick, two-day beard, and black beret ("some old-time Cuban revolutionary, in for a raid"); they dump gear on the floor, the narrator empties stones from his boots and sets them to dry, and they collapse into bed — the concrete close of the physical journey down the mountain paralleling the Chautauqua's move from abstraction toward practicality.

## Thread
Follows directly from Chapter 20's culminating metaphysical insight (Quality as an event prior to subject/object) by explicitly stepping back from it: the narrator refuses to let the Chautauqua rest on mystical peak experience and instead commits to bringing Quality's implications down into practical life, naming the next targets as Religion, Art, and Science — with Science singled out as the urgent next stop, directly setting up the following chapters' critique of value-free science/technology and its connection to the "death-force" theme raised earlier in the book. Narratively, it completes the mountain-climb frame (begun with the ascent) with the descent into Bozeman, closing that physical arc as the Phaedrus/Quality material pivots toward its next phase.
