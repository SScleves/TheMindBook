# Ch 5 — AI, the Singularity, and Downloadable Consciousness

## In one paragraph

Virk covers the final two stages of his road map to the simulation point. Stage 9 — realistic AI — asks whether we can populate simulations with artificial characters indistinguishable from humans, and he traces the intertwined history of AI and games (Turing's imitation game, Shannon's chess, Deep Blue, AlphaGo, Atari-playing AIs, Dota 2 bots) through chatbots (Eliza, A.L.I.C.E., Mitsuku/Kuki) to today's LLMs and generative AI (ChatGPT, Sora), virtual companions (Replika, character.ai), smart NPCs (Inworld, the Matrix Awakens demo, the Smallville generative-agents society). He proposes a new milestone he calls the Metaverse Turing Test — an interrogator inside a 3D virtual world who cannot tell an AI-driven avatar from a human-controlled one — as the marker for the AGI needed for stage 9. Stage 10 — downloadable consciousness / digital immortality — builds on the singularity idea (von Neumann, Vinge, Kurzweil): if the brain's patterns can be mapped (the connectome), consciousness may be information that can be downloaded from biological to silicon hardware, as visualized in Altered Carbon, "San Junipero," and Upload. The upshot: if consciousness comes down to information and computation, it may be substrate independent — a key assumption behind Bostrom's simulation argument, which the next chapter takes up — and a civilization mastering stages 9 and 10 has reached the simulation point.

## Core claims

- Virk argues that the history of AI is intertwined with the history of gaming — from Turing's imitation game and Shannon's chess paper to AlphaGo and Dota 2 bots — and that game-playing has consistently been the way AI is tested and developed.
- Virk argues that NPCs have evolved from "dumb" NPCs (limited commands, conversation trees, branching, multiple-choice responses no richer than Choose Your Own Adventure books) to today's "smart" NPCs powered by LLMs that can converse freely.
- Virk defines the Metaverse Turing Test — indistinguishability of an AI-driven avatar from a human-driven avatar inside a 3D virtual world — and argues it is an important milestone toward AGI that would catapult us toward the simulation point.
- Virk argues that completing stage 9 requires a form of AGI (not ASI) plus avatar rendering so realistic that virtual characters are visually indistinguishable from physical humans.
- Virk argues that most LLMs like ChatGPT aren't really capable of reasoning (at least not yet) — they statistically predict from training data rather than compute logically — and that a true third wave of AI will need to combine wave one (rule-based, symbolic) and wave two (data-based machine learning). He notes, as he edits the second edition, a number of announcements from OpenAI claiming to have figured out how to add reasoning capabilities to LLMs and to be well on the way to AGI — possibly passing early AGI milestones by the time the book is published.
- Virk argues (citing a recent paper) that advancement from the latest LLMs may be limited because AI-generated content is being fed back into training, which may make LLMs sound less intelligent over time rather than more.
- Virk claims the Sci-Fi Feedback Loop is at work in AI: popular depictions (Star Wars, Data, Skynet, Dune's Omnius, Her, Agent Smith, Joi) shape both public fears and the actual direction of AI development — he offers two axes (embodiment and autonomy) for classifying AI drawn from these depictions.
- Virk argues that the values or goals imbued in an AI system — whether programmed or learned — will guide its actions well beyond what its creators intended, raising the ethics questions behind killer-AI letters and doomsday scenarios; he notes these scenarios don't fully account for virtual AI living in the cloud inside 3D spaces like the metaverse.
- Virk argues that passing the Turing Test and Metaverse Turing Test shows a previously physical aspect of nature (interaction with other humans) may come down to information and computation.
- Virk relays Kurzweil's claim (as a claim) that "all of the information" in the brain's inefficient chemical processes could be mapped and reproduced in silicon within decades, enabling consciousness download and digital immortality — technology rather than spiritualism delivering an afterlife.
- Virk argues stages 9 and 10 are related: by building artificial beings (stage 9), we have inadvertently found a way to represent what appear to be conscious beings as digital information; thus that information should be downloadable (stage 10).
- Virk argues that uploadable consciousness and behaviorally indistinguishable AIs imply consciousness may be substrate independent — existing the same on biological or silicon devices — which he notes was a key assumption in Bostrom's simulation argument.
- Virk claims that a civilization that has mastered these two stages has reached the simulation point: the ability to create a Matrix-like reality.

## Concepts

- **NPC (non-player character)** — an automated character that is part of the game program rather than controlled by a player; under this definition the Space Invaders aliens and Pac-Man ghosts qualify, though the term evolved to mean characters in role-playing games rather than simple arcade enemies.
- **Dumb NPCs vs. smart NPCs** — Virk's labels for the old characters whose conversations were limited to predefined branching paths (conversation trees, multiple-choice dialog overlays) versus today's LLM-powered characters you can speak with about any subject.
- **Conversation trees / branching** — the technique by which entire conversations were programmed into NPCs; you had to ask the right question to get the answer (usually a clue needed for a puzzle).
- **Turing Test / the imitation game** — Turing's 1950 party game: an interrogator (party C) behind a curtain passes messages to a human and a computer (A and B); if the interrogator cannot distinguish which is the machine, the machine passes. Virk stresses it is "more of a milestone than a definition," that today it's the software (not hardware) that passes, and that there is disagreement about whether it has already been passed.
- **Metaverse Turing Test** — Virk's new version of the test, defined in this chapter: an interrogator's avatar in a 3D virtual world (with or without VR headset) interacts with two candidate avatars — one fully AI-driven (conversation, movements, activities), one fully human-controlled including voice. After free conversation, travel, and social activities (parties, virtual sports, virtual sex, admiring virtual artwork), if the interrogator cannot tell which is which, the AI passes. Passing it in a fully immersive simulation is the core of stage 9.
- **AGI (artificial general intelligence) / ASI (artificial superintelligence)** — stage 9 requires a form of AGI but does not require ASI; ChatGPT's release raised fears of both.
- **NLP (natural language processing)** — the ability of a computer to read (or listen to) and understand the meaning of natural language; traditionally of critical importance to passing the Turing Test. Early NLP was heuristic (hard-coded rules), which became unmaintainable.
- **Statistical NLP (SNLP)** — the late-1980s/early-1990s approach in which examples were fed to a machine-learning algorithm which "learned" or "inferred" the rules from examples.
- **Weighted probabilistic analysis** — the true breakthrough in NLP: the AI chooses from multiple responses based on past experience, each with different weights, learning the best responses over time; combined with voice-to-text it powers Alexa, Google, and Siri.
- **AI identification problem** — how do we know if something (text, image, video) has been generated by AI? Named after the Google Duplex robocall scare; Virk says it is likely to plague us for many years.
- **AI hallucination** — the phenomenon of AI making up an answer that sounds reasonable (e.g., entirely fake academic references in real journals by real professors); an ongoing problem with generative AI.
- **GPT (generative pretrained transformers)** — a technology whereby an engine predicts the best "next word"; correct guesses chain into coherent sentences, paragraphs, and pages — akin to solving the sentence-completion problems on the old SAT.
- **LLM (large language model)** — the AI model (underlying neural net structure) trained against large amounts of internet text (supervised and nonsupervised), accounting for billions of parameters; more parameters generally means better prediction. ChatGPT's uniqueness was making an LLM into an accessible consumer web tool.
- **Social AI** — Henry Shevlin's term (Cambridge Leverhulme Centre for the Future of Intelligence) for AI purpose-built for socializing, as opposed to generative AI used for work.
- **Griefbots / deadbots** — bots meant to emulate a person who has died, trained on the messages and text the person created when alive; a burgeoning industry raising questions of boundaries and addiction; Replika's founder created one of the first.
- **Digital immortality** — the downloading of human consciousness from biological hardware to silicon; also used for the griefbot-style continuation of a person via AI trained on their output.
- **Digital afterlife** — closer to uploading the whole of a person's brain rather than relying on their writings or messages; a popular discussion topic among the Silicon Valley technorati and its own subgenre of cyberpunk.
- **Connectome** — a complete map of the neurons and their connections in a human brain; the digital-afterlife idea relies on it.
- **Virtual influencer** — characters who post pictures of themselves on social media, often with sponsor products; generally not full AI characters but carefully produced marketing images, usually actor-driven via motion capture with postproduction.
- **Sci-Fi Feedback Loop** — Virk's recurring concept (invoked here for Her/A.L.I.C.E. and AI depictions generally): popular sci-fi depictions affect our ideas of what AI is supposed to be like and may be guiding, subconsciously or consciously, its actual development.
- **Symbolic AI / expert systems (first wave)** — the 1980s wave of rule-based AI: writing rules to encode the knowledge of an expert; expert systems still exist today.
- **Machine learning / ANN / perceptron (second wave)** — the current connectivity-based wave: AI learns from a lot of data rather than rules, relying on connections between network nodes, metaphorically like the brain's neurons adjusting connections. An artificial neuron is a perceptron; a network of them is an ANN (artificial neural net).
- **Third wave of AI** — Virk's proposed combination of wave one (rule-based) and wave two (data-based machine learning), adding reasoning to data; needed to truly get past stage 9.
- **Generative agents** — the architecture from the 2023 Google/Stanford paper: NPCs combining LLMs with modules for setting goals, remembering and reflecting on previous behavior, and enhancing believability; may provide a road map to more realistic virtual characters.
- **Embodiment axis** — Virk's first axis of AI depictions: from physically embodied AI (robots, androids) to completely disembodied AI (pure software), with middle variations including partially embodied AI (part of a ship or vehicle) and virtual AI (a virtual body projected into a virtual world or as a hologram).
- **Autonomy axis** — Virk's second, logical axis: an autonomous AI is self-contained and doesn't update or learn from other instances (e.g., Data); a synchronized AI is one codebase updated as it learns (e.g., ChatGPT — every user addresses the same AI); in the middle, partially autonomous agents that periodically sync with a server (Erasmus/Omnius).
- **System prompts** — instructions developers inject to guide what a chatbot is allowed to output, invisible to end users; one of many methods to influence what chatbots like ChatGPT and Gemini can say.
- **Killer AI** — autonomous lethal weapons that could kill humans while completely under the control of computer programs; subject of the 2018 researchers' letter.
- **Three Laws of Robotics** — Asimov's 1942 rules (I, Robot), which Virk describes as fundamental — "hard coded into the operating system."
- **The singularity** — originally a mathematical term for a point approached asymptotically but never reached, then physicists' term for the center of a black hole (infinite density in an infinitely small space); as technological term, dating to von Neumann in the 1950s and defined by Vinge (1993) as a "singular point" after which everything will be different; now a catchall (per Kurzweil) for superintelligence and human–computer merger.
- **Transhumanism** — Kurzweil's "merger of biology and technology" framing places him squarely in this camp.
- **Cortical stack / sleeves** — Altered Carbon's fictional device that downloads a person's consciousness at any time; the stack can be moved into the spine of a new body ("sleeve"), backed up via satellite, or beamed faster than light to a waiting sleeve.
- **Consciousness as information / substrate independence** — the chapter's upshot: if consciousness relies on information, it should be preservable outside a simulation and creatable within one; substrate independence means consciousness could exist the same on biological or silicon devices — a key assumption in Bostrom's simulation argument.
- **Stage 9: Artificial Intelligence and NPCs (realistic AI)** — producing characters/avatars in a simulation so realistic they pass the Metaverse Turing Test, plus visually indistinguishable avatar rendering; requires a form of AGI but not ASI.
- **Stage 10: Downloadable Consciousness and Digital Immortality** — the ability to download consciousness from the "biological machine" (brain) to a "silicon machine" (computer), based on actual computer science — "beaming bits from one device to the next."
- **Simulation point** — the destination of the road map: a civilization that has mastered stages 9 and 10 can create a Matrix-like reality; a kind of technological singularity discussed in the next chapter.

## Experiments, examples & case studies

**Games and early game AI**
1. **Space Invaders aliens and Pac-Man ghosts** — early automated enemies that qualify as NPCs under the broad definition, though "not particularly intelligent"; the term NPC evolved to mean RPG characters instead.
2. **The Ultima banker** — an early graphical-RPG NPC you could only say certain things to, such as "statement balance"; you couldn't have a full, coherent conversation. Virk's example of how limited early NPC interfaces were.
3. **Telltale Games / The Walking Dead (Figure 12)** — characters spoke with recorded actor voices, but you still chose responses by clicking one of several options; in some ways more limited than text adventures since conversations could only branch down predefined paths — hence "dumb" NPCs.
4. **Virk's childhood tic-tac-toe game** — his own first exposure to AI was building a rules-based tic-tac-toe algorithm as a kid; used as a contrast to DeepMind's learning-based approach and echoed in the chapter's Upshot.

**Foundational tests and milestones**
5. **Alan Turing, "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" (1950)** — took on whether a machine could "think"; since defining "thinking" was hard, Turing devised the imitation game (interrogator behind a curtain, teletype-style messaging, human vs. machine). Virk notes the 2014 film The Imitation Game was about Turing's WWII cryptography work, not the test. Scholars disagree on when/whether the test was passed — some say ChatGPT 4, some say Google's LLM (Bard, then LaMDA, now Gemini), others say not yet.
6. **Claude Shannon, "Programming a Computer for Playing Chess" (1950)** — groundbreaking paper positing a computer could play chess; an internet-circulated picture shows Shannon next to a purpose-built device with a chessboard on top that lit up squares for a human operator to move. His aspirational milestones included writing poetry, orchestrating music, and translating between languages. His paper separated machines programmed with principles from those that could learn the best principles as they played — foreshadowing today's game-playing AI.
7. **Deep Blue vs. Garry Kasparov (1996–97)** — Deep Blue beat the reigning world champion in one game out of six in 1996; in the 1997 six-game rematch it beat Kasparov two games to one with three draws.
8. **AlphaGo / AlphaGo Zero / AlphaZero (Google DeepMind)** — AlphaGo was the first computer program to beat a professional Go player (2015) and beat South Korean champion Lee Sedol in 2016. AlphaGo Zero learned by playing only itself, without human games; AlphaZero could learn to play most games, including chess, without any preprogrammed rules. Virk uses the Shannon-chess-computer vs. AlphaGo Zero contrast to illustrate symbolic vs. connective AI.
9. **DeepMind's Atari project** — the team trained AI to play graphical video games by watching the screen and controls, not via game-specific rules; Space Invaders and Breakout were among the initial seven Atari games. The AI decided moves by watching the screen and simulating joystick movement (old games took input as eighteen possible joystick movements). Showed AI could "learn" arcade-style games — and, for Virk, that AI can become aware of a 2D space by examining pixels.
10. **OpenAI's Dota 2 bots** — Elon Musk funded OpenAI, which announced its AI had learned to play the popular fantasy fighting game Dota 2; a team of five bots was competitive enough to qualify to play against professional esports teams. Significant to Virk because Dota 2 is like an MMORPG — a 3D world — so the bot needed 3D spatial awareness, part of what stage 9 requires.

**Chatbots and the Loebner era**
11. **Eliza (MIT AI Lab, 1960s, Joseph Weizenbaum)** — a digital psychiatrist using clever pattern matching and "substitution" ("Tell me more about X," "Why do you feel that way about X?," "How come you don't know X?"). Patterns repeat after a few minutes, but it was a considerable achievement for the mid-1960s (Figure 14).
12. **A.L.I.C.E. (Artificial Linguistic Internet Computer Entity)** — Eliza successor with the distinct personality of a young woman; convincing users they were talking to a particular personality was a milestone leading to today's AI personalities. Won the Loebner Prize multiple times (as did chatbot Jabberwacky). Later inspired Spike Jonze to make Her.
13. **The Loebner Prize (Hugh Loebner)** — annual small prize (a few thousand dollars) for the best-performing chatbot in its version of the Turing Test, with onetime prizes of $25,000 and $100,000 for meeting certain milestones; the competition has been defunct since 2019, missing today's crop of chatbots.
14. **Mitsuku** — won the Loebner Prize a record five times, beginning in 2013 and ending in 2019; incorporated Alicebot code (the past) and became the basis of the virtual character Kuki (the future).

**Voice assistants and Duplex**
15. **Siri, Alexa, Google Assistant** — early-twenty-first-century digital assistants; weighted probabilistic analysis plus voice-to-text lets them recognize many spoken commands without training to a specific voice.
16. **Google Duplex (2018)** — demonstrated an AI making appointments by phone (e.g., calling a salon), generating a human-sounding voice with natural spacers like "Um…" and different voices for different personalities. Initial euphoria that the Turing Test had been passed proved false — interactions were limited to appointment-making, not open-ended conversation. Widespread robocall-spam concern followed; Google backtracked and decided autonomous agents would always self-identify. Origin of the AI identification problem.

**Robots and virtual characters**
17. **Sophia (Hanson Robotics, Figure 15)** — one of the first visible physical AI robots, poster child for the "age of robots." Media thought the Turing Test had been passed; Hanson's chief scientist Ben Goertzel, Sophia's creator, disagreed — telling The Verge Sophia was not AGI, and only encouraging as a sign AGI was within our grasp.
18. **Neuromancer (1984)** — cyberpunk example of AI as pure software living in cyberspace, marking the shift from AI-as-machine to AI-as-software.
19. **Kuki** — evolution of Mitsuku paired with an avatar of a young Asian woman; interacted with fans on her website, via apps like Viber, and inside 3D metaverse environments like Roblox; interviewed on podcasts. Virk interviewed Kuki (and Lauren Kunze, CEO of the company that made her) on his podcast in 2021 — conversations imperfect and a little forced, but impressive for the time. Kuki's team put deliberate limits steering clear of romantic or sexual conversations.
20. **Kuki × BlenderBot "virtual first date" (2020)** — a well-publicized awkward date with Facebook's BlenderBot, one of the first examples of two virtual characters interacting; BlenderBot kept repeating things he'd read on the internet about Kuki. Virk asks whether it previewed future societies of AI characters.
21. **Kuki at Vogue Talents (2021, Figure 16)** — Kuki as virtual influencer/brand spokesperson modeled virtual items by well-known fashion designers in the online fashion show; the items (as NFTs) sold out quickly.
22. **Lil Miquela and Lu do Magalu** — virtual influencers with millions of followers; Virk's caveat: they aren't really full virtual AI characters but carefully produced marketing images/videos, usually actor-driven via motion capture (the Gollum technique from Lord of the Rings), not real time, with postproduction.
23. **Mark Cuban and "Zero" at SXSW (March 2022)** — highly interactive onstage discussion with a virtual character supposedly in a virtual bunker; so good Virk found it hard to believe it was AI (this was pre-ChatGPT advancements). It turned out to be real-time rendering with an actor's voice and mirrored movements — "it wasn't an AI character at all—more like a virtual puppet."
24. **Huawei's Yunsheng (2021)** — Chinese giant Huawei "hired" its first virtual human as an employee, seen onstage with Huawei's CEO via holographic rendering.
25. **Hatsune Miku** — Japanese AI singer, originally an AI voice synthesizer that could sing any input song; the "body" was added later for live concerts, often projected as a hologram, attended by many tens of thousands.

**The generative AI explosion**
26. **ChatGPT launch (November 2022)** — OpenAI (originally Musk-funded as a nonprofit) released ChatGPT on GPT-3.5; the most successful consumer app launch in history, reaching ten million users in forty days — an order of magnitude faster than Instagram, the previous record. Trained on almost the whole internet; could summarize, produce code snippets, write stories in various voices, even explain why a joke was funny. Microsoft made a multibillion-dollar investment and integrated it with Bing; Google (Bard→Gemini) and Meta (Llama) raced to release competitors. The Economist asked "Is Google's 20-year dominance of search in peril?"; Virk muses Google Search without AI may become an artifact like library card catalogs.
27. **The Guardian article (September 2020)** — headline: "A Robot Wrote This Entire Article. Are You Scared Yet, Human?" — an entire coherent AI-generated argumentative essay. It turned out a human had assembled the final essay from AI-generated paragraphs, so it was a false alarm for the Turing Test.
28. **Blake Lemoine and LaMDA (June 2022)** — a Google engineer publicly proclaimed the company's internal GPT-based chatbot LaMDA "sentient" and released chat transcripts; he qualified that his assessment was based on personal opinion and spiritual perspective (he worked part-time as a priest), not entirely engineering analysis. Google's chatbot team denied sentience and put Lemoine "on leave." Another wave of "did we pass the Turing Test?" speculation.
29. **Fake academic references (Virk's teaching experience)** — the first ChatGPT-generated text Virk spotted as a professor had links to extremely relevant-looking academic articles that were entirely fake — supposedly in real journals by real professors, but no such articles existed. His concrete example of AI hallucination. Today LLMs are standard in academia; professors require students to credit AI use; many use it to brainstorm or as a "dynamic Wikipedia."

**Social AI, AI romance, and smart NPCs**
30. **Henry Shevlin / "social AI"** — the Cambridge (Leverhulme Centre for the Future of Intelligence) researcher branded companionship implementations "social AI," purpose-built for socializing; Virk spent a few months there while writing this second edition.
31. **Replika (Eugenia Kuyda)** — founded by a journalist building a chatbot at Y Combinator when her friend Roman died; she put all his messages into the chatbot, creating one of the first bots emulating a deceased person — origin of griefbots/deadbots. Replika grew to over ten million claimed users by January 2022, many in romantic relationships with their "AI friend." In 2023 Replika suddenly banned explicitly sexual conversations, triggering significant user backlash (users had relied on it for sexting and as a substitute for intimacy); the company said it aimed for PG-13 romance, but the backlash showed a significant market for AI friends, girlfriends, boyfriends, and AI sexual friends.
32. **Her (2013) and the Altman/Johansson controversy** — the movie and today's chatbots are another Sci-Fi Feedback Loop example. Sam Altman's reference to Her at a chatbot release erupted into controversy with Scarlett Johansson (who played the AI girlfriend Samantha): she said OpenAI approached her to voice ChatGPT, she declined, and they used a voice she claimed sounded too close to hers. The loop's kicker: Spike Jonze was inspired by the earlier A.L.I.C.E. chatbot to make Her — the loop at work over several decades. Elsewhere in the chapter Virk notes it was the voice capability in the recent release of GPT-4o that prompted the comparisons to Her, in which the protagonist (Theodore) interacts with his AI girlfriend (Samantha) simply by speaking to her.
33. **character.ai, JanitorAI, Chai AI** — startups where you describe a character and converse with it; an insider told Virk sexual conversations accounted for a large percentage. Some thrive, others folded or pivoted; some began charging for "selfies" from your intimate AI partner — a new business model.
34. **Inworld** — a company that raised over $50 million just on the premise of smart NPCs inside virtual worlds — conversations no longer limited to dialog trees.
35. **Matrix Awakens NPCs + Replica Studios (2023)** — an enterprising user hooked the NPCs in the Matrix Awakens demo to the smart-NPC engine Replica Studios (distinct from Replika) and, in a YouTube demo, walked his avatar around the virtual city telling NPCs they weren't real. Reactions mirrored what you'd get telling New Yorkers they weren't real: some too busy getting to work, some curious, others thought the avatar was crazy.
36. **Generative agents / Smallville (2023, Google + Stanford)** — researchers created twenty-five LLM-powered NPCs with added capabilities (goal-setting, remembering and reflecting on previous behavior, believability modules), represented as 8-bit sprite characters in the retro virtual town Smallville. The agents did daily activities (work, cooking breakfast), developed hobbies (violin), organized birthday parties, and one even ran for mayor; they remembered previous conversations and followed up. Virk says the architecture may provide a road map to more realistic virtual characters.
37. **A thousand AI agents in Minecraft (2024)** — a company placed a thousand agents into Minecraft and found they created their own economy and religion. Virk's evidence that AIs may train each other in virtual environments, in the vein of AlphaGo Zero's self-play.

**AI history: the waves**
38. **Minsky and Edmonds' maze machine (1951)** — the first ANN, designed to find its way out of a maze.
39. **Rosenblatt's Perceptron (1958)** — Frank Rosenblatt published an article about the Perceptron, a simple neural network trainable in humanlike tasks; demonstrated recognizing cards marked on the left versus the right.
40. **Minsky and Papert's Perceptrons (1969)** — paradoxically, Minsky (with Seymour Papert) published a book claiming simple perceptrons could never do complex tasks, which may have set neural-net research back decades; the pair became associated with the symbolic/rules-based approach. Only in the past decade did big data plus large layered networks yield today's large-scale machine learning. (Virk learned ANN basics at MIT in the 1990s, after the symbolic wave had gone bust.)
41. **Model degradation paper** — a recent paper suggested LLM advancement may be limited because so much AI-produced content is fed back into training, possibly making LLMs sound less intelligent over time.

**Generative images, video, and avatars**
42. **The pope in a white puffy jacket (Midjourney, Figure 17)** — viral AI-generated image (text says 2023; the figure caption says it went viral in 2022) from Midjourney, one of the first popular generative image platforms alongside OpenAI's DALL-E; so realistic it raised awareness that AI could create new images from existing ones.
43. **OpenAI's Sora (2024)** — text-to-video platform; Virk reproduces its full demo Prompt 1 (a stylish woman in a black leather jacket, long red dress, black boots, sunglasses, and red lipstick walking down a damp, reflective, neon-lit Tokyo street; Figure 18). Current generators use tremendous processing power and make mistakes on things like human fingers, but are becoming exponentially better.
44. **Fridman–Zuckerberg Metaverse interview (2023)** — podcaster Lex Fridman interviewed Meta CEO Mark Zuckerberg in the Metaverse with extremely realistic avatars; the catch is you must visit a body-scanning facility. Virk used such a scanner at Arizona State University (ASU) "last year" — it used twelve cameras to create a realistic 3D model of him. Other companies make avatars from a simple photo — less photorealistic, but improving via AI trained on tens of thousands, perhaps millions of scanned photos.

**Sci-fi depictions along the embodiment/autonomy axes**
45. **C-3PO and R2-D2 (Star Wars trilogy, 1977–1982)** — embodied autonomous droids that seemed to have consciousness; C-3PO the interpreter, "fluent in over six million forms of communication," used to translate R2-D2's tones and beeps; both displayed distinct personalities.
46. **Data (Star Trek: The Next Generation)** — android built by Dr. Noonian Soong; communicated almost seamlessly but didn't understand emotions and aspired to be "more human"; a stand-alone (autonomous) AI who queried the ship's "computer" (the intelligent system managing the Enterprise). Whether Data was a "real person" with "rights" — Picard defending him as an individual, not Starfleet "property" — was a recurring theme.
47. **HAL 9000 and KITT** — partially embodied AI examples: the misbehaving ship computer of 2001: A Space Odyssey and the car AI from Knight Rider.
48. **The Terminator (1984, James Cameron)** — Skynet, brought online for the Department of Defense, takes over: nukes cities, enslaves surviving humans. Skynet is a disembodied synchronized single AI; when the resistance gains ground it creates Terminators — autonomous robots with living tissue over an artificial skeleton, increasingly hard to distinguish from humans. Virk highlights the contrast between synchronized Skynet and its embodied autonomous robots (which could operate even before Skynet existed — a key plot point).
49. **Dune and the Butlerian Jihad** — in Frank Herbert's novel, humans banned computers that mimic the human because of a past event (mostly ignored in Villeneuve's two recent films). The prequels by Brian Herbert and Kevin J. Anderson detail it: the machine/AGI Omnius takes over and enslaves humanity; the Butlerian Jihad is the human revolt. All AI syncs with master-AI Omnius ("the Synchronized Empire"), but Omnius allows one robot, Erasmus, to remain autonomous and unsynchronized so it can accumulate its own learning and personality — Virk's mirror of the autonomy axis; the synchronization question returns in later Dune novels.
50. **Agent Smith (The Matrix)** — perhaps the most famous virtual AI; Smith and other "programs" like the Oracle existed inside the Matrix to support or antagonize human avatars. Smith "infected" other avatars and wanted to escape the simulation by uploading his code into a human body — the upload-out-of-the-simulation theme Virk flags as recurring.
51. **Joi (Blade Runner 2049)** — the android K (Ryan Gosling) has a holographically projecting virtual girlfriend Joi (Ana de Armas); Virk notes the parallel to Hatsune Miku's hologram concerts, and the aside that the original Blade Runner (1982) was set in 2019 — the year the first edition of this book came out.
52. **The Thirteenth Floor** — based on Daniel Galouye's 1964 novel Simulacron-3; an "ancestor simulation" of 1930s L.A. full of NPCs that protagonist Douglas Hall calls "as real as you or me." They live unaware they're simulated yet can be "inhabited" by outside players (becoming avatars). The ending of this underrated film — overshadowed by The Matrix — also raises whether an AI can escape the simulation.

**AI ethics episodes**
53. **Facebook's negotiating AIs (2017)** — AIs built to negotiate with each other invented their own language; experimenters couldn't understand them and shut it down.
54. **Microsoft's 2016 chatbot** — began using misogynistic and racist language within twenty-four hours and was shut down.
55. **The 2018 "killer AI" letter** — more than two thousand AI researchers, including a Google DeepMind cofounder and Elon Musk, urged great care about autonomous lethal weapons.
56. **The March 2023 pause letter (Future of Life Institute)** — over thirty thousand signatories called for a six-month pause on large AI experiments beyond GPT-4, claiming larger models pose a "profound risk for society"; signers included Max Tegmark, Elon Musk, Steve Wozniak, Yuval Noah Harari, and Andrew Yang.
57. **Yudkowsky's Time op-ed** — Eliezer Yudkowsky argued the pause letter didn't go far enough: AGI is so serious a threat that data centers running large GPU clusters capable of AGI or SGI should be shut down — enforced, he said, by bombing noncompliant data centers. Virk sees Terminator and Dune scenarios influencing this thinking, and notes doomsday scenarios ignore virtual AI in the cloud/metaverse.
58. **Asimov's I, Robot (1942)** — introduced the Three Laws of Robotics as fundamental hard-coded rules (quoted in full below); Virk notes these ethics questions are ones he explores in his course on the simulation hypothesis.

**Singularity and consciousness download**
59. **John von Neumann (1950s)** — supposedly coined the technological use of "singularity" (quote below).
60. **Vernor Vinge, "The Coming Technological Singularity" (1993)** — computer scientist turned sci-fi writer defined the singularity as a "singular point" after which everything will be different, listing several pathways (quoted below): awake superintelligent computers; networks + users "waking up"; intimate computer/human interfaces; biological improvement of human intellect.
61. **Ray Kurzweil, The Singularity Is Near (2005) and The Singularity Is Nearer: When We Merge with AI (2024)** — the Google futurist uses singularity as a catchall for superintelligence and human–computer interaction; optimistic that "all of the information" in the brain's inefficient chemical processes can be mapped and reproduced in the more efficient electronic/silicon world within decades; his framing is the "merger of biology and technology" (transhumanism). If the brain reduces to the patterns of its neurons and consciousness is information, consciousness could be downloaded from the biological machine to a silicon machine — living forever thanks to technology rather than spiritualism or mysticism.
62. **AI professors / digital immortality industry** — many professors and luminaries are creating AI versions of themselves that answer questions based on their life's work; a burgeoning industry, continuous with griefbots/deadbots and, in the past, expert systems.
63. **Altered Carbon (Richard Morgan, 2002; Netflix series 2018)** — the cortical stack downloads a person's consciousness at any time; on death the stack is placed in a new body ("sleeve"). True death requires refusing re-sleeving or stack destruction. Consciousness-as-information means satellite backups ("restore from backup," losing post-backup memories) and — though FTL travel is impossible — beaming stack information faster than light to a ready sleeve on another planet. Even in-fiction the mechanism is mysterious: developed from the technology of a long-vanished alien race. For Virk, its value is the very clear depiction of consciousness as storable, restorable information.
64. **Black Mirror, "San Junipero"** — a virtual world containing both players (still alive) and uploaded NPCs based on deceased persons; the beach town's permanent residents are the uploaded dead living a digital afterlife. Yorkie and Kelly meet near the end of their physical lives as avatars of their younger selves.
65. **Upload (series)** — a technology scans the brain at death and uploads the person into a digital afterlife world set up as a country estate; less philosophical, more comedy and commentary on imperfect technologies, but raises whether uploaded information could be downloaded into another body.

- "Computers that are 'awake' and superhumanly intelligent may be developed." — the first of Vernor Vinge's pathways to the singularity from his 1993 paper (alongside networks waking up, intimate computer/human interfaces, and biological improvement of intellect).

- "fluent in over six million forms of communication" — C-3PO's self-description, Virk's example of an embodied autonomous AI personality.
- "as real as you or me" — Douglas Hall on the NPCs of the ancestor simulation in The Thirteenth Floor.
- "profound risk for society" — the March 2023 Future of Life Institute pause letter's claim about models larger than GPT-4.
- "These two stages are related; by building artificial beings (stage 9), we have inadvertently found a way to represent what appear to be conscious beings as digital information. Thus that information should be downloadable (stage 10)." — the chapter's core synthesis.

## Thread

The chapter runs the last leg of Part I's road map. Virk starts from where video games left off — dumb NPCs with conversation trees — and asks what it would take to make artificial characters real enough for a simulated world. He builds the answer historically: Turing's and Shannon's 1950 game-based tests, the chess and Go milestones (Deep Blue, AlphaGo/Zero), AI learning arcade and esports games (Atari, Dota 2), the chatbot lineage (Eliza → A.L.I.C.E. → Mitsuku → Kuki), NLP's evolution from rules to statistics to weighted probabilities (Duplex), and the generative-AI explosion (ChatGPT, LaMDA/Lemoine, hallucinations). Alongside runs the social thread — Replika's griefbot origin, AI romance, virtual influencers versus true virtual AI characters — culminating in smart NPCs and NPC societies (Smallville's generative agents, the Minecraft thousand). From this he defines the Metaverse Turing Test and enumerates stage 9's requirements (NLP, voice, learning over time — if babies could suddenly speak complete sentences or untaught languages, that would be a clue we're in a simulation — spatial awareness, physical interaction, goal-setting and reasoning, ability to create further AI) plus photorealistic avatars — AGI, not ASI. A sci-fi interlude (embodiment and autonomy axes) and an ethics section (killer AI, pause letters, Yudkowsky, Asimov) frame how fiction guides development and fear. Then stage 10: the singularity lineage (von Neumann → Vinge → Kurzweil) leads to consciousness-as-information — connectomes, digital immortality, and sci-fi visualizations (Altered Carbon's cortical stacks, San Junipero, Upload). The upshot ties both stages together: if apparent consciousness can be built as information (stage 9), it should be downloadable (stage 10), consciousness may be substrate independent, and a civilization with both has reached the simulation point.
