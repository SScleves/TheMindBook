# Traveling in Good Company

## In one paragraph
Thomas Campbell explains that he deliberately wrote My Big TOE before doing any literature search, so that its content would be uniquely his own rather than a scholarly patchwork of others' ideas; only after finishing Chapter 18 of Book 3 did he go looking for corroboration. He then presents that corroboration: quotes from Einstein and David Bohm on the illusory, field-like nature of space-time and the need to connect consciousness to reality; from Max Jammer (via his book *The Concepts of Space*, forwarded by Einstein) and mathematician/physicist Gauss on space as a mental/temporal construct; from Nobel laureate Eugene Wigner on consciousness as "the ultimate universal reality"; from futurist Willis Harmon on an emerging "science of subjective experience"; and, most importantly, from digital-physics pioneer Edward Fredkin, whose "Ultimate Computer" residing in a nonphysical "Other" Campbell equates with his own AUM/TBC and the space-time rule-set. He argues that these thinkers each glimpsed a piece of the Big Picture but got stuck applying "little picture" (space-time) logic to a problem that can only be solved by stepping outside the little picture altogether. The chapter then pivots to Western philosophy — Descartes, Kant, Leibniz, Fichte, Schelling, Hegel, Kierkegaard — as evidence that rigorous rational philosophy has also long pointed toward consciousness/mind/spirit as the ultimate ground of reality, and argues science and philosophy are really one discipline artificially split by cultural belief traps. The chapter closes with an extended reflection on truth-seeking as an individual, non-democratic, non-group-proof process, and an appeal to the reader to be willing to live at the "edge" rather than safely in the cultural "center," while still valuing what the center provides.

## Core claims
- Campbell states he wrote My Big TOE from his own experience first and researched supporting literature only afterward, and that outside validation is not what determines the work's value to the reader — the reader's own experience is the judge.
- He claims Big Truth is the same for everyone at all times, so it is unsurprising that many great thinkers throughout history independently touched on parts of it, even without seeing the whole picture.
- Gaining new knowledge automatically confers new responsibility, and this accelerated growth — not the "wild ideas" themselves — is the real danger of the Path of Knowledge, since it can make the seeker feel lonely if their circle does not share the broadened perspective.
- Campbell argues Einstein's Unified Field Theory work shows Einstein believed physical space itself is an illusion and that objects are simply denser regions of a single unified field, but that Einstein did not go so far as to identify that field with consciousness or recognize its digital/discrete nature.
- He credits David Bohm (with Bohr, Heisenberg, Wigner) with making the "consciousness connection" that Einstein didn't quite make, but says these physicists still missed the digital nature of reality and the Big Picture.
- Campbell interprets Max Jammer's and Gauss's statements (that space is a function of mind and that time is logically prior to space) as independent scientific confirmation of a conclusion he says was first derived from AUO in Book 1, Chapter 31.
- Wigner's claim that "the content of consciousness is the ultimate universal reality" is presented by Campbell as validating his own framework, though he notes the "non-objective" nature of consciousness research was not (and still is not) fully appreciated by science.
- Campbell reads Willis Harmon's 1969 paper as correctly forecasting a coming "science of subjective experience" that would dissolve the subjective/objective dichotomy — a vision Campbell says was more prescient than Harmon himself realized, but suppressed by the conservatism of the scientific mainstream (the "visionless center").
- He argues Edward Fredkin's digital-physics papers ("A New Cosmogony" and "Finite Nature") independently derived a computational, nonphysical "Ultimate Computer" residing in "Other" — which Campbell claims is functionally the same as his own concept of TBC and the space-time rule-set, except that Fredkin did not realize consciousness itself is the computer.
- Campbell asserts that four claims are now jointly supported by "the most respected scientists of our time": physical reality is a nonphysical virtual reality; the doorway to the larger reality is the subjective mind; the larger reality is founded on consciousness; and that larger reality is digital/computational, making the little-picture reality a digital simulation.
- He argues that scientists like Einstein and Bohm got stuck at the "little picture / Big Picture" boundary because they tried to derive the larger reality using little-picture (space-time) mathematics and logic, which by definition cannot reach beyond the closed logical system it describes.
- Campbell contends that My Big TOE's non-mathematical form is a logical necessity, not a scientific weakness, since the Big Picture's causality cannot be expressed or proven inside the little picture's rule-set.
- He extends the same argument to Western philosophy: Descartes, Kant, Leibniz, Fichte, Schelling, Hegel, and Kierkegaard each independently arrived at conclusions privileging mind/consciousness/spirit over matter, but philosophy became discredited once it tried to stay within the "little picture" causality demanded by objective science.
- Campbell claims science and philosophy are "two sides of the same coin" that were artificially split by the mind-matter delusion, and that they will reunite once the Big Picture is generally understood.
- He states that determining the truth of My Big TOE (or any reality model) cannot be a matter of majority opinion, peer review, or group-proof; each reader must personally test it against experience ("tasting the pudding") and either "raise it up" or "let it go."
- Campbell argues that big discoveries always come from "the edge," never from "the center," and that the center — while necessary for stability — cannot itself produce deep innovation; he encourages the reader to be willing to live near or beyond the edge while still respecting the center's stabilizing function.
- He states he deliberately omitted quotes from Eastern/spiritual/religious traditions because the book's rhetorical strategy is aimed specifically at a Western, scientific, logical mindset — not because those traditions lack wisdom; he explicitly denies that Western culture has a superior average quality of consciousness.
- Campbell frames the ongoing tension between individual consciousness evolution ("growing up," decreasing entropy, becoming one with the larger reality while retaining individuality) and ego/fear/conformity as a reflection of AUM's own struggle to evolve the quality of its consciousness.
- The chapter ends by turning the argument back on the reader, asking what their part is in this "epic struggle" and noting that "there is no end — only a beginning."

## Concepts
- **Big Truth** — the single truth Campbell says is "the same for everyone for all time"; because of this, independent thinkers throughout history who search sincerely tend to converge on similar partial insights into it.
- **My Big TOE / Big TOE** — Campbell's own name for his overall reality model/theory of everything, referenced repeatedly in this chapter as the framework being cross-checked against science and philosophy.
- **AUM** — referenced here as the source of "digital consciousness" from which our own consciousness derives ("AUM derived consciousness at our core," "actualize the potential we inherit from AUM"); the struggle to evolve our consciousness quality is described as a reflection of AUM's own struggle to evolve.
- **AUO** — named as the entity to which the chapter attributes a "high-frequency state-changing spaceless nonphysical consciousness," said to have been "very happy" that Jammer's/Gauss's findings (time prior to space) matched a conclusion "first derived" involving AUO back in Book 1, Chapter 31.
- **PMR (Physical Matter Reality)** — the little-picture, space-time-bound reality; used throughout as the frame whose "logic" cannot by itself reach the Big Picture, and as the object of the "PMR über alles" belief trap.
- **PMR über alles** — Campbell's pejorative label for the belief trap that treats physical-matter-reality as the only possible or ultimate reality, dismissing anything nonphysical (like AUM or consciousness) as automatically outside the scope of science.
- **OS (Our System)** — referenced in the line "digital consciousness simulating OS," identifying Fredkin's computational "other" concept with Campbell's own notion of a simulated reality-system.
- **TBC (The Big Computer)** — invoked implicitly when Campbell says Fredkin "is talking about TBC and the space-time rule-set" — i.e., Campbell equates Fredkin's nonphysical Ultimate Computer with his own TBC concept.
- **"Other" (Fredkin's term)** — Edward Fredkin's name, quoted directly, for the nonphysical place/engine where the "Ultimate Computer" that runs our universe's informational processes resides; Fredkin explicitly frames "Other" as necessarily nonphysical and not bound by three-dimensional space, conservation laws, symmetries, or ordinary time.
- **Ultimate Computer** — Edward Fredkin's postulated computational basis of reality: physical existence (space, time, matter, energy) as the output of an informational process run on this computer, located in "Other."
- **Digital physics** — the (per Campbell) still-fringe but serious scientific field, growing out of Fredkin's work, that treats space and time as quantized/discrete and reality as fundamentally computational, comparable in stature to relativity and quantum mechanics.
- **Cellular automaton** — used in Fredkin's quoted passage to describe how "the thing in Other" computes the universe: not by finding an analytical shortcut (which "the speedup theorem forbids") but by imagining/computing each step of a cellular automaton, which Fredkin says is identical whether done "in one's head" (consciousness) or on a computer.
- **Unified Field Theory** — Einstein's own name (per Campbell) for his effort to describe a single nonphysical field underlying both space-time and matter; Campbell treats it as Einstein's deepest intuitive glimpse of the Big Picture, though incomplete because it didn't reach consciousness or digital discreteness.
- **Little picture vs. Big Picture** — Campbell's core interpretive frame in this chapter: the "little picture" is PMR's closed, self-contained logical/mathematical system (its "physics"); the "Big Picture" is the larger digital-consciousness reality. He argues the Big Picture can never be derived from little-picture logic alone because a virtual reality's internal logic only applies within itself.
- **Space-time rule-set** — the term used for the defining logical/physical rules of our little-picture virtual reality, which Campbell says physicists (including Einstein and Bohm) still have much to learn about, and which must itself be a digital simulation nested in a larger digital reality.
- **Big Digital Bang / Big Simulation** — cross-referenced (from Book 2 Ch. 29 and Book 3 Chs. 1 and 9) as Campbell's own account of the universe's origin, which he says Fredkin's account of "Other" setting the "Ultimate Computer" in motion closely parallels.
- **Subjective science (Willis Harmon's term/vision)** — the coming discipline Harmon predicted, treating subjective experience as a legitimate object of empirical inquiry, dissolving the sharp subjective/objective divide and incorporating unified accounts of altered states, "higher self," creativity, hypnosis, mystical experience, psychedelics, and ESP/psychokinesis under one framework.
- **Group-proof** — Campbell's term for consensus/majority/peer-review-style validation, which he says is irrelevant to determining the truth of a personal, subjective reality model like My Big TOE; truth here cannot be settled by "an objective group-proof organized by the establishment (con) or the antiestablishment (pro)."
- **"Tasting the pudding"** — Campbell's recurring shorthand (used here multiple times) for personally, objectively testing subjective claims/experience against one's own life rather than accepting or rejecting them on authority or hearsay.
- **Path of Knowledge** — named as the road the reader travels by acquiring new knowledge; its "danger," per Campbell, is the accompanying new responsibility and accelerated personal growth, which can create distance ("a lonely trip") from others who haven't grown alongside you.
- **Monads (Leibniz)** — described (via Encarta-derived material, per Campbell's disclosed source) as countless conscious centers of spiritual force composing the universe in Leibniz's philosophy, each an independent microcosm mirroring the universe under a divine plan; Campbell links this directly to his own "consciousness-evolution fractal" from Book 3, Chapter 13.
- **Absolute idealism** — the philosophical lineage Campbell traces from Fichte (the world created by an absolute ego moving toward God-consciousness) through Schelling (all things reduced to a self-realizing absolute spirit identified with nature's creative impulse) to Hegel (an absolute spirit/cosmic reason developing through conflict and contradiction as the source of all reality).
- **Herd / conceptual center vs. edge** — Campbell's social-psychological model in which the "herd"/center offers safety, mutual validation ("I'm OK, you're OK"), and stability via fear and ego, while genuine breakthroughs, innovation, and "Big-thoughts" can only originate at the "edge"; he frames both as necessary and mutually dependent, and calls on the reader to be willing to live nearer the edge.
- **Consciousness-evolution fractal** — referenced (from Book 3, Chapter 13) as the "fundamental dynamic pattern of all existence and creation" that Campbell says Leibniz's monad concept parallels.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Campbell's own writing method as evidence**: he reports he wrote through Chapter 18 of Book 3 before conducting any literature search, framing this as proof that My Big TOE's content is original to his experience rather than derivative scholarship — he explicitly contrasts this with "intellectually slick term paper[s] laced with expert references."
- **Einstein quotes on space-time as illusion**: a note to the 15th edition of *Relativity* (June 9, 1952) stating space-time has no separate existence apart from physical objects and that "empty space" loses meaning; a passage from *Generalization of Gravitation Theory* ("If we think of the field as being removed, there is no 'space' which remains"); the aphorism "Reality is merely an illusion, albeit a very persistent one"; and a longer humanistic passage (sourced by Campbell via Rudolf Rucker's *Geometry, Relativity and the Fourth Dimension*, p.118, and also found in *Quantum Reality, Beyond the New Physics*, p.250) describing individual consciousness as an "optical delusion" separating a person from the rest of the universe, and urging the widening of one's "circle of compassion."
- **David Bohm quote** from the introduction to *Wholeness and the Implicate Order*, calling for cosmology/reality theory to make room for consciousness, and vice versa — used by Campbell as evidence Bohm made the "consciousness connection" Einstein didn't complete.
- **Max Jammer's *The Concepts of Space*** (endorsed by Einstein's foreword, per Campbell): quoted (p.171) that physical space is "not... anything given in nature" independent of human thought but "a function of our conceptual scheme [mind]," and that Newtonian absolute space, though illusory, remains a practically fruitful illusion; and (p.175) reporting Gauss's view that three-dimensionality of space is "a specific peculiarity of the human soul [consciousness]" rather than inherent to space itself; and (p.169) that time is logically prior to space, since "space measurements are reducible to time measurements."
- **Eugene Wigner's paper** *The Place of Consciousness in Modern Physics*, quoted as concluding that "the content of the consciousness is the ultimate universal reality" — presented by Campbell as a Nobel laureate physicist independently reaching a consciousness-centric conclusion.
- **Willis Harmon's 1969 paper** *The New Copernican Revolution* (he is identified as Director of the US Educational Policy Research Center at Stanford), quoted at length forecasting an emerging "science of subjective experience" that would map altered states (citing Bucke's "Cosmic Consciousness," Zen "enlightenment"), unify concepts of a "higher self" (citing Bugental's "I-process," Emerson's "Over-soul," Assagioli's "True Self," Brunton's "Over-self," Vedanta's Atman), and integrate creativity, hypnosis, mystical experience, psychedelics, ESP, and psychokinesis under one scientific umbrella; Harmon also references Boulding's phrase "the great transition" and the "more" of William James, the "All" of Bugental, and Aldous Huxley's "Divine Ground" (from *The Perennial Philosophy*).
- **Edward Fredkin's 1992 papers**, *A New Cosmogony* and *Finite Nature* (Fredkin identified as a physicist at MIT, Boston University, and Carnegie Mellon): quoted passages describing "Other" as necessarily nonphysical, not requiring three dimensions, local connectivity, conservation laws, symmetries, or conventional time; a passage describing the origin of the universe as the Ultimate Computer's "initial conditions" being "set into the engine" in Other, which Campbell explicitly compares to his own "Big Digital Bang"/"Big Simulation" account; and a passage arguing that computing a cellular automaton "in one's head" (with consciousness) is functionally identical to computing it on a machine — which Campbell reads as evidence that "consciousness is the computer," a step he says Fredkin himself did not take.
- **Einstein–Bohm correspondence**: a quoted October 28, 1954 letter from Einstein to Bohm, admitting he had no idea what elementary concepts could replace the "continuum" of space and time — used by Campbell as evidence Einstein sensed but couldn't cross the little-picture/Big-picture boundary.
- **The flat-earth analogy**: Campbell notes that Western thinkers continued believing the earth was flat long after it had already been demonstrated spherical by respected scientist-philosophers, using this as a case study in how "political correctness of scientific belief" can suppress correct ideas at the conceptual center (paralleling his claim that Harmon's subjective-science vision was ahead of its time).
- **Philosophers surveyed as a case-study lineage**: Descartes ("I think, therefore I am"), Kant (things-in-themselves unknowable; science grounded in mind, not the external world — cross-referenced by Campbell to Book 2, Chapter 32's experienceable/un-experienceable-reality distinction), Leibniz (monads, cross-referenced to Book 3, Chapter 13's consciousness-evolution fractal), Fichte (absolute ego), Schelling (self-realizing absolute spirit), Hegel (absolute spirit/cosmic reason developing through conflict and contradiction), and Kierkegaard (defense of subjective feeling against pure objective reason, feeding 20th-century existentialism) — Campbell explicitly discloses that the historical "factoids" here were "cut and pasted directly from Microsoft's Encarta Encyclopedia," with only the interpretive framing being his own.
- **Einstein's "Great spirits" quote**: "Great spirits have always encountered violent opposition from mediocre minds," used as the capstone example supporting Campbell's "edge vs. center" argument, alongside a list of historically ridiculed big discoveries (spherical earth, heliocentrism, the atom, quantum mechanics, relativity).
- **List of "goofiness from the center"**: racism, sexism, slavery, witch hunts, religious fundamentalism, stifling conformity, irrational fear of change, political correctness, conspicuous consumption, blue laws, tabloids, pet rocks, and beanie babies — offered as concrete examples of the center's own less-obvious irrationality, balancing the earlier claim that "goofiness" also comes from the edge.
- **Named scientific enigmas explained by the model**: Campbell cites "wave particle duality or entangled particle pairs" as examples of paranormal-adjacent scientific puzzles that, along with documented paranormal reports, can be "adequately explained" and given "a solid theoretical basis" by applying My Big TOE's reality model.
- **Four-question test for evaluating a reality model**: to judge whether a theory (including My Big TOE) is true and accurate, Campbell says to ask: does it make sense out of what was previously not understandable; does it explain all the data; does it provide new direction, perspectives, and knowledge that is useful and profitable; and does it enable significant progress in understanding yourself or the outside world — each to be scored against your own subjective and objective experience, since "you are the final judge."
- **The Ph.D. pun**: Campbell notes that a Ph.D. in physics (or anything else) is literally a "doctor (D) of Philosophy (Ph)," using this etymological fact to argue that science and philosophy were once understood as one discipline before a cultural split drove them apart.

## Thread
This chapter functions as a validation/legitimation interlude near the very end of the trilogy (Section 6, "The End is Always the Beginning"): rather than adding new theoretical machinery, it looks backward across the whole work and cross-checks its central claims — nonphysical unified field, consciousness as fundamental, digital/computational reality, the impossibility of proving the Big Picture from little-picture logic — against independently sourced physics (Einstein, Bohm, Wigner, Fredkin, Jammer/Gauss) and Western philosophy (Descartes through Kierkegaard). It explicitly ties back to earlier derivations: AUO and the time-prior-to-space conclusion from Book 1 Ch.31; the two basic assumptions (consciousness and evolution) from Book 1 Ch.24; the experienceable/un-experienceable reality split from Book 2 Ch.32; the Big Digital Bang/Big Simulation from Book 2 Ch.29 and Book 3 Chs.1 and 9; and the consciousness-evolution fractal from Book 3 Ch.13 — reinforcing that the trilogy's separate arguments cohere into one system. It also prepares the reader for the trilogy's final chapters by reframing the whole project as a personal, non-group-provable, "edge"-dwelling act of individual truth-seeking (echoed in the closing questions about the reader's own transformation and "part in this epic struggle"), setting up the concluding chapters' final push toward what the reader must now do with the model.
