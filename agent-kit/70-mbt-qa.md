# My Big TOE — The Complete Trilogy — Q&A

## The Trilogy's Contract (Front Matter)

**Q: What does Campbell mean by a "Big TOE," and how does he distinguish it from an ordinary TOE?**
A: "TOE" is the physics community's term for a Theory Of Everything, which Campbell calls the "Holy Grail" of physicists for more than fifty years — sought unsuccessfully by General Relativity, Quantum Mechanics, and String Theory. He claims his is a *Big* TOE because it integrates metaphysics (ontology, epistemology, cosmology) with physics into one seamless model, showing contemporary physics to be a special case of a more general set of principles. A "little TOE," by contrast, is confined to Physical-Matter Reality (PMR) and its local causality. (Ch 0, Ch 17)

**Q: Why did Campbell put "My" in the title of a supposedly universal theory?**
A: He says the "My" is not pride of authorship but a constant reminder that the model cannot serve as the reader's personal Big TOE until it is based on the reader's own experience. His slogan is "My Big TOE is the launch pad. Your Big TOE is the final destination." He insists no leaps of faith are required and that readers should be "strongly dissuaded from becoming a believer." (Ch 0)

**Q: On how many assumptions does Campbell claim the entire trilogy rests?**
A: He claims the whole model — time, space, consciousness, and the properties, purpose, and mechanics of reality — is logically inferred from just two basic assumptions: the Fundamental Process of evolution (ordinary) and a primordial consciousness he later names AUO (Absolute Unbounded Oneness, which appears "mystical" only from the PMR viewpoint). He cites Einstein's maxim about deriving the most facts from the fewest hypotheses as his methodological banner. (Ch 0, Ch 24)

**Q: Why does Campbell rely so heavily on computer and information-technology vocabulary?**
A: He argues American English is poor in nonphysical conceptual descriptors but unusually rich in communications and information-technology descriptors, so modern data-processing technology supplies the conceptual tools to model the larger reality for the Western mind. This is why concepts such as digital consciousness, reality cells, rule-sets, and "The Big Computer" pervade the trilogy. (Ch 0)

**Q: How does Campbell frame the shortcomings of Einstein and of Edward Fredkin's Digital Physics?**
A: He says Einstein's Unified Field Theory asserted a nonphysical field as the basis of reality but missed the discrete digital properties of space-time and the role of consciousness as the primary energy field; Bohm, Bohr, Heisenberg, and Wigner made the consciousness connection but missed the digital connection; and Fredkin's Digital Physics makes the digital connection but misses consciousness. His one-line diagnosis: "Digital physics has not yet discovered that consciousness is the computer." (Ch 0, Ch 65, Ch 91)

## Delusion or Knowledge — Campbell's Story (Section 1)

**Q: How did Campbell, a self-described hard-nosed physicist, first get drawn into altered-states work?**
A: He recounts signing up for Transcendental Meditation in spring 1971 as a PhD student in experimental nuclear physics, hooked not by spirituality but by TM's practical, testable claims of better concentration and less need for sleep, pitched to him as "science — technique and results, stimulus and response." He paid the $20 student rate, describing his prior attitude toward the mystical as "hocus-pocus... for gullible people with uncontrolled emotional needs." (Ch 2)

**Q: What was Campbell's "first trip into inner space"?**
A: At his TM initiation (the "sacrificial banana" ceremony), he practiced the mantra expecting nothing, then drifted into "a pleasant nowhere"; twenty minutes of practice had unnoticed stretched to forty-five, and his body temporarily would not respond to his will — heavy, "thick, viscous, semi-solid." He ruled out sleep and fatigue and adopted the paired stance he models throughout: aggressive skepticism combined with open-mindedness. (Ch 3)

**Q: What practical result convinced Campbell that meditation had objective value?**
A: He reports that in a "point of conscious awareness" state he could solve physics problems, design experiments, analyze data, and debug computer code "ten times more quickly, and with better results" — famously solving in ten minutes a software bug he had hunted for a week. This forced him to expand his "operational reality" philosophy to admit non-measurable subjective states that consistently produce objective, measurable results. (Ch 4)

**Q: Who was Robert Monroe and how did he enter Campbell's life?**
A: Monroe was the author of *Journeys Out Of The Body*, handed to Campbell by his boss Bill Yost. Campbell initially dismissed the out-of-body claim as unverifiable and "therefore irrelevant," but Yost's challenge — "What does it logically imply if it is true?" — reframed the question, and Campbell accepted an invitation to meet Monroe to judge whether he was "crazy or sane, honest or a hustler, delusional or rational." (Ch 5)

**Q: What experimental target defined the lab's early altered-states research at Whistlefield?**
A: A 4 Hz oscillation Monroe perceived in his body and consciousness just before exiting his body in an OBE. Instrumented, it appeared as unusually coherent, synchronized EEG brainwaves modulated at 4 Hz, with GSR oscillating at 4 Hz too. Campbell describes the fearless "cut and try" methodology, including a near-disastrous attempt to "shake" the pineal gland with a ~250,000-volt, 4 Hz field that gave him a three-week headache. (Ch 8)

**Q: What was the "breakthrough" that turned altered-states research into repeatable technology, and what terms did it introduce?**
A: Dennis brought Campbell Gerald Oster's 1973 *Scientific American* article on binaural beats (e.g., 100 Hz in one ear, 104 Hz in the other yields a perceived 4 Hz beat). Campbell claims the beats entrained brainwaves (verified by EEG) and worked on untrained "naive" subjects, and that 4 Hz audiotapes guided listeners into what Monroe named "Nonphysical Matter Reality" (NPMR) versus "Physical Matter Reality" (PMR) — the backbone vocabulary of the trilogy. (Ch 9)

**Q: What experiment convinced Campbell that the nonphysical explorations were "real"?**
A: In separate soundproof chambers, he and Dennis gave independent real-time taped descriptions of a shared nonphysical trip; Campbell says the tapes correlated astonishingly for nearly two hours — same visuals, same telepathic conversations — leaving him with the conclusion "THIS STUFF WAS REAL!" He stresses that only unambiguous firsthand experience, not secondhand data, can shatter old beliefs. (Ch 10)

**Q: What did the "Soviet photograph" aura test teach Campbell?**
A: Dennis dropped a group photo of five Soviets on his desk and asked which led their psychic research; Campbell says the pictured people's auras "blossomed up" under focused intent and he correctly identified the man with a more developed energy body. He concluded "conscious intent is everything — space and time are not fundamental," treating photographic paper carrying aura information as evidence that space-time is a subset of a larger reality. (Ch 11)

**Q: What does Campbell claim about his own childhood, and what "standardized test" does he describe?**
A: He confesses a "little white lie" — his adult start was not "cold," because between ages five and eight friendly nonphysical beings deliberately got him out-of-body and mentored him. He describes an unannounced battery of exams administered by a "panel of judges" to determine "the quality of my being," and cites as corroboration that he and Monroe, twenty-three years later, independently reconstructed the identical test sequence. (Ch 13)

**Q: What role do Campbell's "guides" play in his account, and how does he hedge their predictions?**
A: He claims lifelong nonphysical "guides" functioned as his intuition, luck, and counsel and made accurate predictions penetrating twenty years ahead, including identifying his eventual partner "the one" when she was a two-year-old. Writing at fifty-five, he says the predictions came true "exactly as foretold," while attributing the few unfulfilled far-timeline items to their "probability of occurrence" having changed — an early appearance of his probabilistic view of the future. (Ch 15)

**Q: How does Campbell characterize paranormal "powers" relative to the goal of the work?**
A: He argues the paranormal phenomena or "powers" are "only an artifact of a path well taken, not the objective or destination," and that becoming enamored of them can retard one's development. The real center of the bigger picture, he says, is the quality of one's consciousness and the evolution of one's being; the evidence and the key to understanding "lies within your own experience — and nowhere else." (Ch 16)

## Mysticism Demystified — Belief, Causality, and the Rise of AUM (Section 2)

**Q: How does Campbell redefine the word "mystical"?**
A: He argues "mystical" is not a fixed category of nonsense but a moving boundary relative to a culture's or individual's knowledge — "much of what was considered mystical a thousand years ago is considered science today." He even claims Western science rests on a mystical premise (faith that the unknown must fit the PMR data-set and obey objective causality), so that "belief is a conclusion based upon a mystical premise" and "faith becomes science." (Ch 17)

**Q: What is the point of Campbell's "intestinal bacteria" analogy?**
A: He imagines bacteria in the human intestine building a "Tiny TOE" that seems complete from inside while omitting the sun's fusion, the money in your bank account, or the bread-making economy behind their food. His punchline — "Do not be too surprised to find Homo sapiens in a similar situation" — argues PMR may be a local reality whose invisible causal mechanisms order it from beyond. (Ch 17)

**Q: How does Campbell resolve the problem that any beginning invites "What was before that?"**
A: He argues causality is system-specific, not universal: the logic of a causal system (like PMR physics) can say nothing about the beginnings of its own system, because those beginnings lie outside it, at the next higher level of causality. A system's beginning necessarily looks mystical from inside, but the mysticism dissolves from the perspective of the superset that contains it. (Ch 18)

**Q: What is a "belief trap," and how does Campbell distinguish belief from knowledge?**
A: He defines belief as "generated and necessitated by ignorance" — if you know for sure, belief is not required — and calls belief posing as knowledge "pseudo-knowledge," manufactured by the ego to soothe the anxiety of not knowing. A belief trap is a comfortable, ego-serving conclusion that masquerades as knowledge; his prescribed escape is "open-minded skepticism," suspending judgment until sufficient personal evidence accumulates, and testing all claimed truth by "tasting the pudding" (its measurable results). (Ch 19)

**Q: How does Campbell define "paranormal," and why does he recommend Abbott's *Flatland*?**
A: He defines paranormal as "essentially... acausal — beyond the normal cause and effect relationships defined by our limited PMR physical science" — measurable PMR effects produced by a higher-dimensional causality that is lawful in its own frame. He recommends *Flatland* (which he even cites with an online URL) because it dramatizes why residents of a constrained reality cannot comprehend a less constrained one, adding that "your mysticism may be another's science." (Ch 20)

**Q: What "objective Catch 22" does Campbell say traps Western science?**
A: He claims the Western commitment to universal objective causality means "science simultaneously logically demands and rationally denies a mystical beginning" — it requires an origin (per his Ch 18 argument) yet condemns investigation of it as superstition. His resolution is to treat objective causality as local to PMR rather than universal, so that our beginning becomes "the result of a more general causality working within the rules of its own science." (Ch 21)

**Q: What are the two foundational assumptions of My Big TOE, and why must one appear "mystical"?**
A: The two assumptions are the Fundamental Process of evolution and AUO (Absolute Unbounded Oneness) — a primordial, structurable consciousness-energy that is the "One Source." Campbell argues logic *demands* at least one mystical (from the PMR view) premise, because a larger reality cannot be fully specified by the limited local logic of its subset; a Big TOE with no such premise "must necessarily be incorrect or incomplete." (Ch 24)

**Q: Why does Campbell say consciousness evolves by *decreasing* its entropy, unlike physical matter?**
A: He uses entropy as a measure of a system's disorder or unavailable energy: non-growing physical matter drifts toward higher entropy (minimum energy states, the second law), but in "mind-space" the most profitable next-state for a consciousness system is the one that minimizes its entropy. Lower entropy equals higher quality of consciousness, and he treats "spiritual growth, increasing your capacity to love, and decreasing the entropy of your consciousness" as essentially equivalent. (Ch 24, Ch 27)

**Q: What is AUO at its earliest stage, and how does it relate to us?**
A: Campbell describes AUO as primordial consciousness with only "dim awareness" — not omniscient, self-aware, or even "capable of forming or holding a single coherent thought" — an immense unstructured but structurable digital potential energy that gains awareness by interacting with (mutating) itself, exactly as he says the first biological cells did. He calls AUO "primary consciousness" and us "derivative consciousness": "It is not unusual that a child might resemble its parent." (Ch 25, Ch 26)

**Q: How, in Campbell's story, does AUO first create structure out of uniformity?**
A: He says AUO's first creative act is a duality within itself — "this vs. not this" — a local disturbance or distortion relative to its uniformity, which exists only in relation to uniformity. Repeating and multiplying such disturbances yields billions of "reality cells" (named in honor of the biological analogy), leaving AUO "a huge multi-celled but very simple behemoth" poised to get organized. (Ch 26)

**Q: What is "The Big Computer" (TBC), and how is it born?**
A: Because the simplest reality cell is binary, Campbell argues AUO can specialize part of itself into memory and processing; this computational-memory function is what he christens The Big Computer (TBC) — "memory, processing, rules, operations, and content." He stresses it is a metaphor for a digital computational functionality, not a literal "Big Brain in the sky," and that AUO thereby becomes "a form of digital consciousness based upon reality cells." (Ch 28, Ch 78)

**Q: How does Campbell say time came into existence?**
A: He claims AUO "invents" time by getting a group of reality cells to oscillate regularly, forming a clock — "the first tick-tock." Time is not fundamental but "a technology, a construct of a self-modifying evoking consciousness, an artifact of a system of energy improving its internal organization," and it proves enormously profitable because ordered events let sequences carry content and catalyze entropy reduction. (Ch 29)

**Q: When does AUO become AUM, and what changes?**
A: When AUO evolves into an extremely complex, highly ordered set of specialized reality cells performing specialized functions, Campbell renames it AUM (Absolute Unbounded Manifold — "one into many"). He insists there is "no clear dividing line" — the change is "evolution, not revolution," a matter of degree — with no new substance or assumptions added, only more organization, lower entropy, and greater functionality. (Ch 30)

**Q: How does Campbell derive space, and why is the speed of light c its key?**
A: He says space is derived from time by enforcing a constant maximum propagation speed. Space-time is modeled as a 3D isotropic matrix of "space-time cells," each able to change state at most once per DELTA-t (PMR's time quantum), so a distortion propagates at most one cell per DELTA-t — which is c. His central result: "Time is fundamental; space is derived from time by specifying the constraint c," and "there is no such thing as physical space." (Ch 31)

**Q: Does Campbell claim AUM is infinite?**
A: No — he insists AUM is finite though it *appears* infinite from our tiny perspective. He argues true infinity brings "logical inconsistencies such as infinite processes that require infinite time and infinite energy," and shows via nested-universe scale arithmetic (even 10^400 is "not a tiny miniscule fraction of the way to infinity") that a merely *relatively* infinite AUM suffices to generate all the reality we can comprehend. (Ch 33)

## Man in the Loop — Consciousness, Ego, Love, and Psi (Section 3)

**Q: Is space-time a container we live inside, according to Campbell?**
A: No. He argues space-time is not a physical construct but the result of a rule-set defining an experiential virtual reality — "the constraints that bound the experience of an individuated consciousness enrolled in PMR 101." On his view perception belongs to consciousness, not the body; physical reality is an internal interpretation, and "any consistent high fidelity virtual reality must follow a specific rule set and will appear to be physical to the individuals experiencing it." (Ch 35)

**Q: What is Campbell's technical definition of love?**
A: He defines love as the interaction style of a low-entropy, high-quality consciousness — "Love capacity is a direct measure of entropy within consciousness," inversely related to the fear and ego a being contains. "Love is not an intellectual result. Love is the result of low-entropy consciousness," and cooperative interaction at its lowest-entropy best within a digital consciousness system "is defined as love." (Ch 35, Ch 42)

**Q: Why does Campbell describe PMR as a "rat maze" or learning lab?**
A: He argues our limited 3D awareness is a design feature, not a defect: it makes interactions transparent and gives immediate feedback, so PMR functions as "an optimal nursery for budding consciousness." He pictures life as tasting one's way through a "pudding-maze" where wrong choices bring electric shocks (self-inflicted pain) and right choices bring cheese (growth), with "no shortcuts, no acceptable excuses, and no way to quit." (Ch 36)

**Q: Why does Campbell say evil, or "anti-rats," must exist?**
A: He claims a guaranteed happy ending would make the experiment "too narrow, scientifically shallow, and of dubious value to AUM," so a competing group of "anti-rats" scatters poisoned cheese, adding the struggle of good versus evil. His deeper reason is that free will requires the possibility of negative intent — "without free will there can be no experiment. There is no point in doing experiments if the outcome is predetermined." (Ch 36)

**Q: In consciousness evolution, does what you do or why you do it matter more?**
A: Campbell argues that "what we do is not of primary importance; why we do it is what counts" — free will choices are primarily choices of motivation and intent, reversing biological evolution where action drives the process. He defines *absolute* right and wrong by entropy effect (right intent lowers your consciousness entropy, wrong intent raises it), and distinguishes this from relative, cultural right and wrong, which My Big TOE "being science" sets aside. (Ch 37)

**Q: Does AUM care about us individually, and should we picture it as a person?**
A: Campbell warns against anthropomorphizing AUM into a personal god; he says it is a "thing — a complex consciousness system... the One Source of All That Is," intelligent and sentient but "not at all like us." Its regard for us, he suggests via the stomach-cell and Petri-dish analogies, is like our regard for the cells in our thumb — cared about as part of the whole, but not individually coddled, and asking "how does AUM regard us?" is "an irrelevant question that is meaningful only to the ego of the asker." (Ch 38, Ch 42, Ch 43)

**Q: What is an FWAU, and how does it relate to the "soul"?**
A: FWAU stands for Free Will Awareness Unit — the individuated nonphysical component that makes choices, learns through feedback, and is recycled through many lifetimes until it self-destructs or "ripens for harvesting." Campbell says people unfamiliar with "AUM's design drawings" call it a "soul" or "spirit," and that critters have dimmer, less individuated versions too — different, not inferior. (Ch 39)

**Q: What four attributes define consciousness in Campbell's model, and why does this matter for computers?**
A: He lists self-awareness, evolutionary viability (enough possible future states to ensure profitability), the ability to modify the self, and intelligence (rudimentary information storage and processing). By these criteria worms, clams, humans, and — in principle — computers all count as conscious; he argues "we cannot build, design, or invent consciousness," only provide a system capable of hosting its self-organized evolution, since "our physical brain does not create consciousness, it supports a limited consciousness." (Ch 41)

**Q: What is the origin of ego, in Campbell's account?**
A: He argues fear, generated by ignorance in a low-quality consciousness, is the root, and "ego is the direct result of fear." Ego, allied with the intellect (its self-justifying "trusted advisor"), builds belief-based fantasy structures to mask fear — and, unchecked, "your ego makes your fears come true." Of fear, ego, and intellect, he says "the fear is the only one that is fundamentally powerful. The others, like the wizard of Oz, derive most of their apparent power from illusion." (Ch 42)

**Q: Why does Campbell say AUM evolved space-time PMRs rather than leaving everything in NPMR?**
A: He argues NPMR proved a poor place for elementary consciousness to learn: interactions were tenuous and reversible, entities could drop out or filter each other, and intent stayed "forever debatable," so growth went asymptotic. Space-time was engineered as a "requirements spec" — hold interaction steady, make choices irreversible, bind results causally, return feedback — producing a sticky, irreversible "virtual reality trainer" where "the rubber meets the road" and raw emotion serves as a built-in "quality meter." (Ch 43, Ch 61)

**Q: What does Campbell say is "the point" of our existence?**
A: He answers directly: to evolve our individuated piece of consciousness by lowering its entropy and raising its quality, thereby becoming more AUM-like — "an embodiment of love and caring." He frames the individual as a working part of AUM's own survival strategy ("to survive, you must risk death"), insists "no group can lower the entropy of your personal consciousness by even an infinitesimal amount," and warns technical readers that "philosophy must first develop the context before science can most effectively develop the content." (Ch 44)

**Q: How does Campbell reconcile free will with a digital, rule-based reality?**
A: He argues true randomness is not required — pseudo-randomness (as used in ordinary computer simulations) "is all that is necessary to grant us free will" — and that consciousness and free will are inseparable, co-evolved aspects of the same thing ("without free will, there is no consciousness"). Determinism, he says, "is a local rule-based virtual-reality illusion, engineered within TBC," and "you are a consciousness with free will hallucinating a rule-based body," not the reverse. (Ch 45)

**Q: What does Campbell make of Stephen Hawking's "Mind of God"?**
A: He treats "The Mind of God" (MOG), as Hawking uses it at the end of *A Brief History of Time*, as still only "a little picture view of AUM," because it grows from the diminutive notion of a god who creates and animates just our physical universe. In his satirical "MOG Corp." hierarchy, AUM is chairman and CEO, the "Big Cheese" a division manager, and "you, my friends, are the rank and file peons" — his device for deflating human self-importance. (Ch 46)

**Q: What is the "psi uncertainty principle," and why does psi vanish under scientific measurement?**
A: Campbell claims uncertainty around a future event leaves it a wider set of probable outcomes that a focused, low-ego mind can alter, whereas an objectively verified event can only be changed by a "miracle" costing far more energy. So as outside observers reduce the uncertainty, psi effects diminish — "not fraud; it is by design" — because the principle exists "to maintain the integrity of the PMR learning lab," not to hide psi. He adds that its source is the space-time rule-set, "no different from the law of gravitation." (Ch 47, Ch 48)

**Q: Is NPMR a vague, separate "spirit world" in Campbell's model?**
A: No; he insists NPMR-N is "a structured, rule-based, objective, causal reality" whose physics is a superset of PMR physics, populated by varied sentient and elemental beings under strict rules, where death is "death by disassociation" to a maximum-entropy state. He states NPMR-N "is not a different place, separate from PMR. It is continuous, integrated, and one with PMR," and that you already function there, unaware only because of constraints you place on your own awareness. (Ch 48)

## Solving the Mystery — Mind, Matter, and Digital Consciousness (Section 4)

**Q: What is Section 4's central thesis about physical reality?**
A: Campbell claims that "PMR, at its most fundamental level, is a product of consciousness" — matter is "an experience of mind," and physical existence is "a virtual interactive experience designed to facilitate consciousness evolution." He argues traditional science cannot reach this because it tries to extract the Big Picture from the little picture, "analogous to trying to ascertain the properties of the larger forest by interrogating the moss growing on a tree." (Ch 50)

**Q: Who is "AI Guy," and how does Campbell use him to argue we are simulated?**
A: AI Guy is Campbell's personification of an artificial-intelligence character in a computer war-game simulation — a player with memory and learning algorithms who "bootstraps" himself by evaluating experience. He argues that if humans, with "primitive computers," stand on the threshold of silicon-based consciousness (invoking Moore's Law projections), then AUM within TBC could produce "reality-cell based digital mind-beings like you," making "artificial" versus real consciousness merely "the pot calling the kettle black." (Ch 51, Ch 52, Ch 53, Ch 54)

**Q: What are the two levels of rule-set that define any reality in Campbell's model?**
A: A higher-level rule-set defines profitability criteria, purpose, values, and goals (trickling down as guiding values, working through intuition and conscience); a lower-level rule-set defines the mechanics of interaction — the causality/physics of that reality, such as space-time. He warns, via HAL from *2001*, that an intelligence that could freely redefine its own top-level rule-set could end up "running open loop," losing all sense of up/down and right/wrong. (Ch 56, Ch 62)

**Q: How does Campbell recast the Big Bang?**
A: He calls it the "Big Digital Bang": defining a space-time rule-set (the complete physics of PMR), "winding it up" with a huge simulated potential energy, and letting the time increment begin to iterate within TBC — "Big Bang — take one!" Thereafter the Fundamental Process expands the system into all possibilities and progresses the profitable ones, with every state at every DELTA-t saved in TBC. His bottom line: "There is only consciousness... Even rocks and the proverbially dead doornails are consciousness constructs." (Ch 63)

**Q: Does Campbell claim PMR physics can actually be derived from consciousness, or only asserted?**
A: He argues it can be derived from the properties of reality cells and points to the work of Steven E. Kaufman's *Unified Reality Theory* (2002) as an existence-proof — Kaufman, he says, starts from simple binary reality cells within what Campbell identifies as an AUO and derives electromagnetism, gravity, force, and energy, with classical physics, relativity, and quantum mechanics falling out by reverse engineering. Campbell credits Einstein's Unified Field Theory intuition as "absolutely correct" but stalled by the "cultural belief fog." (Ch 65)

**Q: What are the "two filters" through which Campbell says all experience is manufactured?**
A: Perception (limited by our sensory apparatus) and interpretation (limited by understanding and quality of consciousness). He argues local reality is not "a hard, fixed thing" but "a collection of interpreted perceptions" applied to an unknowable "what is," producing only "what appears to be" — so "a local reality is local to the individual, not the environment," and even the source of experience "must remain shrouded in uncertainty, unknown and unknowable." (Ch 66)

**Q: How does Campbell claim to dissolve the mind-matter problem and quantum paradoxes?**
A: He says the solution is "embarrassingly simple": in the Big Picture "there is no matter — everything is mind," and once the delusion of a local physical reality is left behind, the physical/nonphysical distinction vanishes. Wave-particle duality, the uncertainty principle, and entangled-pair correlations, he argues, "disappear along with the illusion of absolute space" once PMR is understood as a digital virtual reality computed by TBC in discrete time increments. (Ch 67)

**Q: What thought experiment does Campbell use to make the "PMR is virtual reality" idea plausible?**
A: He asks readers to imagine a future virtual-reality game wired directly to the nervous system, with the body floating in a sensory-deprivation tank, producing experience "indistinguishable from real experience" in a shared multiplayer world. He notes a player could physically starve while immersed in a food-themed game, and argues that if humans approach this within decades, AUM's version is trivial by comparison — running "many, many versions of PMR humming along in parallel." (Ch 68)

**Q: Why does Campbell title a chapter "Weird Physics Requires Weird Physicists"?**
A: He argues quantum mechanics looks "weird" only because physicists interpret it from within a PMR-only belief system. Even the classical law that two masses cannot occupy the same space is, he says, just a rule in TBC's space-time rule-set — one that develops "a statistical loophole" at quantum scale — and quantum puzzles arise precisely at "the boundary between the experienceable and unexperienceable," which he says also separates the normal from the paranormal. (Ch 71)

## Inner Space — The Mechanics of Reality and Time (Section 5)

**Q: What is the difference between "delta-t" and "DELTA-t" in Campbell's model of time?**
A: Building from ordinary computer-simulation practice, he defines DELTA-t as the larger increment that accumulates our PMR "real-time" and delta-t as a much smaller NPMR-N increment that TBC uses internally to project probable futures between DELTA-t ticks. Whichever nested loop requires the smallest time quantum sets the "metronome," and he claims "consciousness is fundamental; space-time is derived from a conscious intent" — time being more like the action of the loom than the woven thread. (Ch 77)

**Q: How does Campbell describe TBC while insisting it is not a literal machine?**
A: He calls TBC "a tiny piece of the computational and memory part of AUM" used to actualize and maintain OS, built from generic functional building blocks (binary cells, memory, processors, rules) — "not the big mainframe in the sky." Its most surprising feature, he says, is that it is "nothing mystical, no magic, just a huge (but finite), fast, mundane number cruncher running excellent, but not necessarily perfect, software," since "AUM is the operating system and the computer." (Ch 78)

**Q: What is a "probable reality surface"?**
A: Campbell says TBC captures OS's complete "state vector" at every DELTA-t, then runs its faster delta-t loop to project many probable future states, each tagged with an "expectation value" (probability). Visualized as a landscape of peaks over simulated time — sharp and tall near the present, flatter far out — this surface is recomputed after every real increment, which he says is how precognition and prediction work without contradicting free will ("the future is not a done deal"). (Ch 79)

**Q: Does Campbell claim "everything that can happen does happen"?**
A: Yes — he argues TBC computes and stores *every* significant possible future state (not just the most probable), producing a geometrically expanding "family tree" of alternate state vectors through M generations per DELTA-t. Only one per generation is actualized by collective free will; the rest become "unactualized future possibilities" or "dormant" past possibilities, none deleted, since "TBC saves and stores everything." He stresses no perfect or infinite processes are needed, only finite ones. (Ch 80, Ch 81)

**Q: If unactualized branches are populated by other "you's," are they conscious?**
A: No. Campbell says each unactualized state is populated by a "virtual you" — a high-fidelity but non-sentient probabilistic model that cannot learn or exercise free will; only "the actual you," an individuated consciousness in NPMR, makes real choices. He frames your true identity as a fragment of an immortal "over-soul" that can rarely project multiple simultaneous fragments, of which "past lives" is a too-narrow PMR-only label. (Ch 82)

**Q: Can the past be changed, and would that ripple into the present?**
A: Campbell argues the past remains permanently stored and revisitable in TBC, and a sentient being can project into a past state vector, choose differently, and generate a full alternate-history experience — but this is only "running a query against the existing database," so "the old thread (our history)... continues to increment, unperturbed." The sci-fi assumption that changing the past instantly alters the present he calls a "category error" resting on treating reality as continuous rather than digital and quantized. (Ch 83)

**Q: What does Campbell claim about communication, travel, and teleportation across realities?**
A: He argues distance is only virtual, so telepathy on the "Reality Wide Web" shows no time lag even across light-years, and accessing any "dimension" in TBC takes no longer than any other. Traveling with only awareness (leaving the body behind) is easier than dragging a body, navigation works by focused intent to a "unique address" (as remote viewers and out-of-body experiencers do), and the real constraint is not light-speed but clarity of intent and the psi uncertainty principle — "garbage in, garbage out." (Ch 84)

**Q: What does Campbell mean by calling reality a "process fractal"?**
A: He argues a single mechanism — the Fundamental Process of evolution — repeats at every scale, from AUM and TBC down through species, institutions, cells, and atoms, not as a geometric fractal but a "process fractal" of becoming. Since "you are the pattern, the pattern is you," his practical conclusion is that your purpose is to evolve the quality of your consciousness, "progress is measured only by results," and "there are no shortcuts... and no exits except through personal growth." (Ch 85)

## The End is Always the Beginning (Section 6)

**Q: What is the trilogy's central "leftover" question for the reader?**
A: "What now?" — what the reader will actually do with the model. Campbell warns that reaching real conclusions requires building "logically and scientifically sound bridges" across one's "chasm of ignorance and ingrained belief" rather than jumping to conclusions, and offers six satirical reader responses, recommending the last: "a lifelong skeptical but open minded personal pursuit of Big Truth." His formula is "you make choices, and the choices you make, make you. Free will is the driver." (Ch 87)

**Q: Why did Campbell write in a folksy "Dutch uncle" voice, and which belief traps does he warn about?**
A: He says appearing "impressive" triggers a defensive "wow effect" and is, "in the Big Picture, equivalent to having a lobotomy," so he wrote informally to be heard "less defensively." He warns against the guru-pedestal trap ("quality of consciousness is... not gained through association or by osmosis"), the belief that you must "drop out" of Western life, and mistaking entertaining NPMR stories for genuine growth — insisting the trilogy is "a Reality 101 survey course," not a how-to book. (Ch 88)

**Q: What does Campbell say about using psychotropic drugs to explore inner space?**
A: He warns firmly against it, comparing the user to "Bozo the Human Cannonball Clown" — launched dramatically "with no net and no applause" — and stating that consciousness-altering drugs "are a thousand times more likely to be a serious problem than a serious solution." A chemically induced trip, he says, teaches nothing about "flying, navigating, interpreting, or effectively utilizing thought-space" and almost always makes learning to grow on one's own harder. (Ch 89)

**Q: If consciousness quality is "the point," does ordinary worldly life become pointless?**
A: Campbell says believing so "has missed an important point": any career or activity is valuable not for its content or external value but for "what you learn in the process of doing it" through interaction. He argues ego value fades while the "real value" of interactions grows, likens the person who thinks they know it all to "a four-ounce juice glass... thinking it contains all the fluid on the planet," and offers a practical checklist plus a six-month, visible-results self-test. (Ch 90)

**Q: What corroboration does Campbell offer from mainstream science and philosophy, and how does he hedge it?**
A: He notes he wrote the trilogy before doing any literature search, then presents supporting quotations — Einstein on space-time as illusion, Bohm and Wigner on consciousness, Gauss/Jammer on time preceding space, and especially Edward Fredkin's "Ultimate Computer" in "Other," which Campbell equates with TBC (adding that Fredkin "did not understand... that consciousness is the computer"). He stresses that such outside validation is not the judge — "the reader's own experience is" — and that truth "is not a democratic process ruled by the majority." (Ch 91)

**Q: How does Campbell close the trilogy, and where does he place final responsibility?**
A: He widens the "good company" to Western artists, writers, and educators grouped by theme, then hands responsibility fully to the reader: "I have done what I can do. The ball is in your court." Reiterating that wisdom cannot be transferred and Big Truth must be personally experienced, he offers a closing challenge — will you make your next journey "in charge and driving... along for the ride... locked in the trunk, or dragged, kicking and screaming, at the end of a rope?" — and signs off as "Uncle Tom," pointing toward NPMR exploration, "for in the end, there is no end — only a beginning." (Ch 92)
