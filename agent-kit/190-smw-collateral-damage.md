# Collateral Damage

> Note: in the source file the chapter carries the printed number "6"; it is stored as unit 11 in this knowledge base's file ordering.

## In one paragraph

This chapter is DeLonge & Levenda's casualty roll of the UFO field: a proposed "memorial wall" for people who died — by crash, by suspicious death, or by suicide — as a result of investigating the Phenomenon. Opening with epigraphs from Hiram Johnson ("The first casualty when war comes is truth"), Christopher Mellon (on AFOSI's Richard Doty and the suicide of target Paul Bennewitz), and Clausewitz, the authors argue there is "something lethal about this field," and that government interference, hoaxes, and official deception — treated "almost as a spectator sport" in the United States — have ruined reputations and lives and constitute "an ethical and a moral outrage." They then work through their case list: the 1947 Maury Island affair and the deaths of Air Force investigators Davidson and Brown (plus reporter Paul Lantz), the 1948 death of Captain Thomas Mantell chasing a UFO over Kentucky, Wilhelm Reich's 1957 death in Lewisburg penitentiary after the burning of his books, Morris Jessup's 1959 suicide after the Allende letters and the "Philadelphia Experiment," and physicist James McDonald's 1971 suicide after ridicule destroyed his scientific reputation. The chapter closes with a call to restore the good names of those "sacrificed to a nebulous and ill-defined policy of denial."

## Core claims

- The authors claim there is "something lethal" about UFO research: since 1947 the field has accumulated violent deaths at a rate not seen among other arcane researchers (ESP enthusiasts, ghost hunters), with the possible exception of ritual magicians — and the very first violent deaths were not unstable saucer fanatics but seasoned military personnel killed while investigating.
- They argue that military, intelligence, and government interference with UFO researchers — hoaxes, lies, disinformation, break-ins — is a contributing factor to these deaths, is condoned by people with security clearances, and is "an ethical and a moral outrage"; they report being told "all sorts of impossible things by persons with impeccable credentials" during their own investigation.
- They argue the Maury Island incident was "a hoax within a hoax": neither a straightforward hoax by Dahl and Crisman nor a straightforward genuine sighting, but an affair whose recantation itself looks managed — per the FBI's own investigation, Dahl did not say it was a hoax but said that if anyone asked he would *say* it was a hoax.
- They claim Kenneth Arnold's honesty has never been seriously doubted (citing Frank Brown's own assessment) and that Arnold himself read Maury Island not as an extraterrestrial event but as "some kind of intelligence operation."
- They claim Maury Island and the Kennedy assassination are demonstrably linked through personnel — Fred Crisman (questioned by Jim Garrison's grand jury in 1968) and FBI agent Guy Banister (Maury Island investigator in 1947, later the New Orleans hub around Ferrie, Martin, and allegedly Oswald) — "ample proof that they are, indeed, linked" in the world of "clandestine operations and deep politics."
- On Mantell, they claim the official explanations (Venus, a Skyhook balloon) failed, leaving "the obvious one": an experienced decorated pilot reported what he saw and died in its pursuit — making three Air Force officers dead in UFO-related duty within just over five months (Aug 1, 1947–Jan 7, 1948).
- They report the rationale given them by interviewees for military disinterest — UFOs are judged not a threat to national security, therefore not worth the money — and note the interviewees never say there is nothing to the Phenomenon; "quite the contrary."
- On Reich, they argue he cannot be blamed for paranoia because the machinery of the US government really was arrayed against him (FDA destruction of his accumulators, the biggest single act of censorship in American history in the burning of his books), while the medical profession's paranoia about Reich was never recognized as such; they also cite recent scholarship that Reich was targeted by a Soviet cell and appeared on an NKVD death-list.
- They argue elaborate UFO hoaxes (MJ-12, CARET, the Allende material) share a signature: enormous effort, no income for anyone, no claimed authorship — so their purpose must have been confusion and the waste of government and private resources; "If we know the 'why' we may be able to identify the 'who.'"
- They claim Jessup's destruction was "intensely psychological": immersion in the Allende material multiplied coincidence and synchronicity in his life — an effect the authors generalize: because the Phenomenon partakes of science, religion, politics, intelligence, and the paranormal at once, cross-linking makes coincidences multiply for those who study it.
- Via Jessup's own reaction to the Varo edition, they assert the heart of "the Phenomenon" is the connection between UFOs and matters "of concern . . . to psychics, cultists and mystics" — an extension of the investigation into consciousness itself, which "has its dangers."
- They endorse (as a serious option) the Vallée-style view that the consciousness component is "where the real sekret is to be found": the standard interstellar-spacecraft model fails on light-speed grounds, and the Phenomenon may be a "mind game" played by an unseen player — an explanation they connect to the madness and suicides of otherwise sane, scientifically trained investigators.
- They claim there was a cover-up — grounded in solid evidence of denial and blocked access to classified sighting material — but characterize its motive as "a mixture of fear, ignorance, and confusion rather than a cynical desire to fool the American people": fear because the Phenomenon defied sovereignty; ignorance because leaders knew little more than anyone; confusion because credible physical-trace and radar cases over airports, airbases, nuclear facilities, and missile defenses left authorities unable to assess intent or threat.
- They argue the authorities' response to McDonald and the UFO problem was "not a scientific solution . . . but a political one" — pretending the anomaly did not exist — and that McDonald's despair came from realizing "science would always be secondary to politics"; they draw an explicit parallel to the modern treatment of climate change.
- They liken UFO skeptics to atheists: "a system of denial rather than the ability to use an alternative viewpoint to explain the available data," and write that "stupidity's Kevlar vest is made of arrogance and condescension."
- Closing claim: no other area of human experience combines such massive evidence with such massive indifference; a concerted, sober scientific/religious/anthropological/archaeological effort is owed to the dead — McDonald, Jessup, Reich, Mantell, Davidson and Brown, Dahl, "And, yes, to Paul Bennewitz."

## Concepts

- **Collateral damage (the chapter's frame)** — the researchers, witnesses, and military investigators whose lives, reputations, or sanity were destroyed in the "war" over UFO truth; the authors propose an imagined memorial wall, like the CIA's stars at Langley, for those who died as a result of investigating the Phenomenon.
- **The Phenomenon** — the authors' umbrella term for the UFO problem understood as more than spacecraft: it "partakes of" science, religion, politics, intelligence, and the paranormal at once, with a psychological/consciousness component; Jessup's phrase for the Varo material — matters "of concern . . . to psychics, cultists and mystics" — is said to form its heart.
- **Hoax within a hoax** — the authors' formula for Maury Island and for federal discrediting campaigns generally: the recantation of a sighting is itself staged, so that "hoax" becomes the cover story ("Dahl did not say the Maury Island incident was a hoax but rather insisted that if anyone asked him about it he would say it was a hoax").
- **"X" files** — the FBI's own designation for its early flying-disc reports; the August 18, 1947 Wilcox memo on Maury Island ("FLYING DISCS SIGHTED BY [redacted], TACOMA, WASHINGTON, SM-X") is called one of the earliest genuine examples.
- **Flying discs vs. flying saucers** — before Arnold's June 24, 1947 sighting coined "flying saucers" (from his description of craft bobbing like saucers skimming water), sightings were reported as "flying discs" — which is how Crisman and Dahl reported theirs, a detail the authors use to complicate the copycat-hoax theory.
- **Man in black** — the total stranger who visited Dahl the morning after the sighting, knew everything "as if he had been there," and threatened Dahl's family if he did not forget the incident; buried in Arnold's account, it became a staple narrative strain of Ufology and inspired the Will Smith movies.
- **Deros** — "degenerated robots"/"detrimental robots" (never completely translated), subterranean monsters coined by pulp writer Richard Sharpe Shaver and published as quasi-fact by Ray Palmer in Amazing Stories (June 1947); Crisman had claimed to fight them in a Burma cave — the authors note the resonance with Agarttha/Shambhala and call this fact-fiction twilight zone Crisman's congenial habitat.
- **Project Blue Book** — the US Air Force's UFO project, cited here for pre-Arnold spring-1947 disc sightings and the same-day Spokane sighting; its manager Hynek later admitted suppressing possibly genuine cases for fear of being thrown off the project.
- **Project Mogul** — the secret high-altitude balloon program (microphones to detect Soviet atomic-test sound waves) that became the Air Force's third, Cold-War-justifying explanation for the Roswell wreckage, after "flying disk" and "weather balloon."
- **Project Skyhook** — the balloon program offered (and, per the authors, eventually rejected) as an explanation for what Mantell chased; "metallic looking, kinda."
- **Orgone** — Wilhelm Reich's name (derived from "orgasm") for the free energy he found beneath the surface of the human body; blockages of its flow during orgasm were, in his theory, the cause of physical and psychological illness.
- **Armoring** — Reich's term for those blockages: armor developed from early childhood, grounded in psychological/emotional experience and manifest in the musculoskeletal structure.
- **Orgone accumulator** — Reich's phone-booth-sized layered box in which a patient sat to "accumulate" orgone and receive its healing properties; investors included William Burroughs and Norman Mailer; its promotion is what put Reich in prison.
- **Deadly Orgone Radiation (DOR)** — the waste matter Reich said UFOs secreted after consuming Orgone Radiation (OR): a fine black mist raining onto Earth, eroding the planet and causing physical and psychological sickness.
- **Energy alphas ("Ea's")** — Reich's name for the UFOs he claimed to see (cigar-shaped versions or pulsating red-and-yellow lights); he judged the OR/DOR imbalance they caused so great that the only alternative was to destroy them.
- **Cloudbuster / Spacegun** — Reich's battery of long tubes for drawing orgone from the cosmic "orgone stream" and shooting it into the atmosphere to "bust" clouds and make rain; the variation he aimed at UFOs he called the "Spacegun," claiming that targeted craft dimmed, shrank, and "winked out."
- **Philadelphia Experiment** — the claim in the Allende letters that in October 1943 the US Navy tested an invisibility-and-bilocation device — a practical application of Einstein's unified field theory (electricity, magnetism, gravity) — on the USS Eldridge, killing several sailors when it malfunctioned and psychologically damaging survivors.
- **Allende letters** — three letters sent to Jessup from January 1956 by "Carlos Miguel Allende"/"Carl M. Allen" of New Kensington, PA: weirdly formatted (underlinings, full caps), naming Einstein, Bertrand Russell, and Naval officers, mixing antigravity, ancient levitation, and the Eldridge story — with verifiable details like a recorded bar brawl lending credibility.
- **Varo edition** — the copy of Jessup's The Case for the UFO annotated by three apparent hands ("Mr. A," "Mr. B," "Jemi" — later judged by the Navy to be Allende alone), sent to the Office of Naval Research and laboriously mimeographed (reports say 127 copies, at least 12) by Varo Manufacturing of Garland, Texas.
- **Degaussing / stealth ships** — the real wartime Navy technology the ONR knew of: reducing a ship's magnetic signature (e.g., an electrical coil run around the hull) against German magnetic mines — radar/mine "invisibility," not literal invisibility, and possibly the seed of the Philadelphia confusion.
- **MJ-12 documents** — legitimate-seeming memoranda allegedly from the earliest postwar UFO period involving a presidential adviser group ("Majic"/"Majestic"); creator, motive, and income all unknown — the authors' archetype of the purposeless elaborate hoax.
- **CARET documents** — a more recent parallel: bizarre "alien" alphabets and vehicle designs with memoranda from a supposed secret California engineering firm, shown to be lifted from a computer company's advertising — again authorless and profitless.
- **Hokkani boro** — "great trick" or "great con" in the Roma language; traditionally designed to relieve a mark of money — the authors float the possibility that the Allende affair was a sophisticated hokkani boro aimed at the Navy and Jessup, except that no one made any money, which forces the question of who benefited.
- **L-Ms and S-Ms** — the two Alien races of the Allende mythos: "L-M" said to mean "little men," friendly to Earth; the S-Ms hostile, with no further information — which the authors compare to the Manichaean "angels and demons" scenario of warring spiritual forces using humans as battleground.
- **Control mechanism / mind game** — Vallée's proposal, endorsed as a live option: UFOs are intelligently controlled but are not spacecraft as we understand them — "a kind of mind game being played on the chessboard of human experience and sensation by an unseen player, like something out of an Ingmar Bergman film."
- **Cover-up (the authors' qualified sense)** — evidenced by governmental/military denial and blocked researcher access, but driven, in their reading, by fear (UFOs defy sovereignty, acting "outside the chain of command"), ignorance (leaders knew almost nothing), and confusion (credible trace/radar cases with unassessable intent) rather than cynicism.
- **Condon Committee** — Dr. Edward Condon's committee, described flatly as "designed to discredit Ufology."
- **Deep politics** — the world in which Tacoma and the French Quarter are "in the same neighborhood": clandestine-operations networks where Maury Island personnel resurface around the Kennedy assassination.

## Experiments, examples & case studies

- **Doty/Bennewitz (epigraph case)** — Christopher Mellon's 2021 statement: Richard Doty, formerly of USAF OSI, claims OSI spied on civilian UAP researchers, broke into homes, and fed them disinformation; prominent target Paul Bennewitz became unstable and committed suicide. Mellon asks why DoD oversight or Congress has not investigated. The authors use it as the emblem of officially sanctioned harm.
- **Maury Island, June 21, 1947** — Harold Dahl (lumber salvage), with his son, two crewmen, and his dog in a boat on Puget Sound near Maury Island, Tacoma, reports six donut-shaped flying discs, one in distress showering debris that damaged the boat, wounded his son, and killed the dog. Coworker/boss Fred Lee Crisman (1919–1975) "managed" the story, contacting pulp editor Ray Palmer; Crisman visited the island next day, saw the debris field, and reported his own silent, inner-tube-shaped craft with an observation window.
- **The FBI file** — fourteen-page memo dated August 18, 1947, Seattle, signed SAC Jack B. Wilcox, to Director J. Edgar Hoover, copies to Butte (run by W. Guy Banister, later of Kennedy-assassination fame), Portland, San Francisco, Chicago. It records AP newsman Elmer Vogel's account: Mrs. Dahl "in a considerable rage" ordered Dahl to recant, after which Vogel told the Seattle Post Intelligencer Dahl was "a mental case." Banister authored his own six-page memo (August 27, 1947) on Arnold and Maury Island. The authors stress the FBI finding that Dahl only promised to *call* it a hoax if asked.
- **Pre-Arnold "flying discs"** — Project Blue Book records spring-1947 disc sightings months before Arnold, plus a same-day (June 21, 1947) Spokane sighting the Air Force wrote off as "balloons" without saying how; the authors note the west-to-east sighting pattern also seen in the 1897 airship wave.
- **Kenneth Arnold's recruitment** — Palmer wrote Arnold June 26, 1947; Arnold read it July 15, sent his Air Materiel Command report (the same month Nazi General Walter Dornberger arrived at Wright Field under Operation Paperclip, and Roswell debris went to Wright Field); Palmer wired $200 for Arnold to interview Dahl and Crisman in Tacoma.
- **Davidson and Brown's first visit** — A2 intelligence officers Capt. William Davidson and Lt. Frank M. Brown (Fourth Air Force, Hamilton Field), with an A-26 bomber at their disposal, interviewed Arnold in Boise, met fellow witnesses Capt. E. J. Smith (United Airlines) and Dave Johnson (Idaho Statesman aviation editor), and removed from Arnold's fan mail the correspondence from organizations — an intelligence-gathering function tied to fears of Soviet penetration of UFO groups.
- **Roswell, on or about July 5, 1947** — wreckage first called part of a "flying disk," almost immediately re-labeled a weather balloon, decades later re-labeled Project Mogul; the authors note Roswell hosted the squadron that dropped the atomic bombs and Robert Goddard's secret rocket lab.
- **Tacoma, July 29–31, 1947** — Arnold has another UFO sighting en route; finds every hotel booked except a room mysteriously pre-reserved in his name at the Winthrop; Dahl tries to wave him off ("the most complicated thing you ever got mixed up in"), recounts the sighting, the lost camera film, the man in black's threat; Arnold IDs the slag as "lava" (noting a July 12 Mountain Home, Idaho sighting where lava fell from a craft); UP reporter Ted Morello phones to say an anonymous caller is relaying their hotel-room conversations verbatim — including talks held when Arnold and Smith were alone — yet hours of searching find no microphone.
- **The crash, August 1, 1947, 1:30 a.m.** — Davidson and Brown, leaving abruptly at midnight with Crisman's cereal box of fragments (which Arnold noticed were rougher and heavier than the machined-looking ones seen earlier), crash near Kelso, Washington after an engine fire; Brown throws the two other crewmen out at ten thousand feet; neither officer parachutes. Newspapers report classified material and "pieces" of a "flying disk" aboard, with sabotage suggested but no reported physical evidence. The authors call them "the first casualties of the modern UFO era."
- **Aftermath weirdness** — Morello knows the dead airmen's names before release; Palmer tells Arnold to keep the $200 but mail any fragments (they might be dangerous); Crisman never produces Dahl's photographs (left "at a cabin"); the boat's damage doesn't match descriptions; Crisman vanishes — Morello later hears he was flown by military transport to Alaska (never confirmed); Dahl disappears; the secretary's house that Arnold had visited days earlier stands abandoned and cobwebbed "as if no one had lived there for months, if not years."
- **Paul Lantz (or Lance)** — Tacoma Times newsman who filed the August 4, 1947 lead story "Wrecked Bomber Carried Disc Secret: Army Says Data Was on Plane"; dead ten days later, cause undetermined even after a thirty-six-hour postmortem.
- **Crisman before the Garrison grand jury, November 21, 1968** — questioned by Alcock, Sciambra, Oser, and Bertel in Jim Garrison's JFK probe; still in touch with Dahl (then running a secondhand store), who had introduced him to Thomas Beckham — a Garrison figure tied to "strange churches" (Universal Life Church; Old Roman Catholic and Eastern Orthodox splinters). Garrison suspects David Ferrie and Jack Martin belonged to the American Orthodox Catholic Church along with Tommy Jude Baumler (ex-Banister employee, incorporator of the Process Church) and founder Walter Propheta (anti-Communist friend of Hoover). Crisman also worked for the Tacoma ad agency of Michael Riconosciuto's father (Riconosciuto of Inslaw/PROMIS and Iran-Contra fame, in prison on drug charges he calls a frame-up). Banister, meanwhile, went from investigating Arnold in 1947 to the New Orleans office where Oswald's Fair Play for Cuba flyers bore his address. "As incredible as this sounds, it is all very well documented."
- **Mantell, January 7, 1948** — a large, round, metallic, glowing object seen by thousands from Kentucky to the Tennessee border; Godman AFB commander Guy Hix scrambles three P-51 Mustangs; two peel off at twenty thousand feet (no oxygen aboard; regulations required masks above fourteen thousand); Capt. Thomas F. Mantell, 25, Kentucky ANG, Normandy veteran with 2,000+ flight hours, climbs on — "I've sighted the thing... It looks metallic, and it's tremendous in size" — loses consciousness near thirty thousand feet and dies in the power dive at 3:18 p.m.; the plane belly-flops, oddly unscathed. Venus (afternoon; too dim) and a Skyhook balloon are rejected as explanations. Sidebar: Hix transferred to San Antonio, where his sons befriended young Whitley Strieber, the future famous abductee.
- **Wilhelm Reich (1897–1957), Lewisburg 1957** — the "flying saucer guy" to fellow inmates; intimate of Freud (his favorite disciple until a falling-out) and Einstein; "the Nikola Tesla of psychology and physiology." Six tons of his books burned in the Gansevoort incinerator on 25th Street, Manhattan, August 23, 1956 — "the biggest single act of censorship in American history," eleven years after the Nazis burned books. FDA destroyed his accumulators (June 1956); AMA enlisted to discredit him; suspected of being Communist, fascist, spy, terrorist, or crook. From 1954–56 he shot down "energy alphas" with the Cloudbuster/Spacegun at Orgonon (Rangeley, Maine) starting May 12, 1954, then greened parts of the Arizona desert by cloudbusting rain. Recent scholarship: target of a Soviet cell; The Sexual Revolution (1936) angered Soviet intelligence; name on an NKVD death-list of German émigrés; Character Analysis still taught, and used by the Soviets to manipulate consciousness. Imprisoned March 12, 1957 (Danbury, then Lewisburg); pardon refused by Eisenhower in May; died November 3, 1957 of an apparent heart attack days before a parole hearing. His UFO fascination was read as paranoia — he believed Eisenhower took a personal interest in his case and that the Air Force flew jets over his prison as encouragement — but the authors counter: "But is it paranoia if they really are out to get you?" The authors close by noting a "gradual restoration of his reputation as a visionary genius" in recent reevaluations of his work.
- **Morris K. Jessup (1900–1959), Miami 1959** — near-PhD astronomer turned South American archaeological digger; The Case for the UFO (Citadel, 1955) prefigured von Däniken and Pauwels & Bergier; marginalized by academia ("once you go off the reservation you can't get back in again"). Received the Allende letters from January 1956; ONR received the annotated book, had Varo Manufacturing hand-type and mimeograph it; the Navy invited Jessup to Washington in spring 1957 and gave him three copies. Supporting details: Allende's service record placed him where he claimed (but the Eldridge's Navy records were lost, and William L. Moore — later accused of being a disinformation agent — claimed the logs of the ship, by then Greece's Leon, matched Allende); a recorded bar brawl matched the letters; a photographed fire at Varo's plant on April 1, 1956. Allen was real; a friend published a memoir of him in 2012; he told Gray Barker in the 1970s the Experiment was based on reality while staying cagey. Jessup — divorced, unemployed, editing an astrological newsletter — gave his reannotated Varo copy to Ivan T. Sanderson (ex-British naval intelligence) on Halloween 1958; that copy has vanished. On April 20, 1959, ~6:30 p.m., at Matheson Hammock Park, Coral Gables, he ran a hose from his exhaust into his station wagon. The authors judge suicide likely over murder, blaming the Allende material's psychological undertow. John Keel claimed in personal correspondence that the hoax's source was Varo Manufacturing itself — and by extension the ONR.
- **James E. McDonald (1920–1971), Tucson 1971** — Duluth-born Navy WWII cryptographer, physics PhD, headed the Institute of Atmospheric Physics; saw his first UFO driving the Arizona desert in 1954 (unspectacular: unexplainable lights). His 1968 House testimony: UFOs are "probably extraterrestrial devices engaged in... 'surveillance.'" Feuded with Hynek (who admitted suppressing possibly genuine cases), Philip Klass (who once said ball lightning explained everything, and who smeared McDonald with fund-misappropriation claims), and Condon. At an SST/ozone congressional hearing, Senator Silvio Conte (R-MA) invoked "little green men" to laugh him out of credibility. Spring 1971: wife asks for divorce; April 1971: shoots himself in the head, is only blinded, wheelchair-bound as sight partially returns; June 13, 1971: second attempt succeeds, .38 revolver beside his body outdoors. The authors: his despair was the realization that "science would always be secondary to politics."
- **MJ-12 and CARET hoaxes** — cited as the pattern-cases of elaborate, authorless, profitless document hoaxes (CARET's alphabets traced to a computer company's ads), used to frame the Allende affair and the whole "why?" of UFO deception.

## Thread

This chapter is the human-cost ledger of the book's war metaphor: after chapters tracing the Phenomenon's history and the machinery of secrecy, "Collateral Damage" argues that the secrecy regime has a body count — military investigators (Davidson, Brown, Mantell), scientists (Reich, McDonald), writers (Jessup, Lantz), and civilian targets (Bennewitz, Dahl). It ties earlier threads together explicitly: the Arnold sighting and 1947 wave, Roswell and Wright Field/Paperclip, the Agarttha/Shambhala underground-cities material ("discussed previously"), the 1897 airship sightings ("discussed in this volume"), and the Doty/Bennewitz case ("mentioned previously"). It also advances the book's deeper pivot — from nuts-and-bolts craft toward consciousness (Vallée's control mechanism, the "mind game," coincidence-multiplication) — which the authors flag as the ground of the Phenomenon and, simultaneously, its danger to investigators. The closing roll-call of names converts the chapter into a moral demand: restoration of reputations and a serious, multi-disciplinary study of the Phenomenon, setting up the book's argument about what disclosure owes the past.
