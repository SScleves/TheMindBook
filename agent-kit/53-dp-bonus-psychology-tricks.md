# 10 Psychology Tricks to Influence Anyone

## In one paragraph
This bonus chapter closes the book with a compressed, practical top-ten list of "psychological tricks" the author says a reader can apply immediately, one per day, to become "a master of mental manipulation" within ten days. Unlike earlier chapters that build theoretical frameworks, this unit is presented as a quick-reference toolkit, each trick given a short rationale, a worked example or sample script, and (in a few cases) a cited experiment or numeric statistic. The tricks range from linguistic devices (the word "because," reinforced truisms, similes) to nonverbal cues (nodding) to structuring choices so the other person feels free while being steered (the simple bind and double bind) to persuasion frameworks borrowed from sales and hypnosis (sensory overload, the exaggeration model, the black/white model). The chapter closes the book on a practitioner's note, converting the preceding chapters' theory into ready-to-use scripts.

## Core claims
- The author claims giving any reason at all — signaled by the word "because" — makes people far more likely to comply with a request, regardless of how weak the reason actually is.
- The author claims mirroring a person's own frequently-used favorite words ("hot words") creates unconscious trust and affinity that makes them more likely to accept a proposal.
- The author claims that nodding slightly while asking a question activates the listener's mirror neurons, unconsciously cues agreement, and increases the odds of a "yes."
- The author claims a well-placed simile/comparison can make a customer feel a choice (e.g., upgrading a product) matters more than it initially seemed to.
- The author claims the conscious mind can only handle about seven pieces of information at once, and overloading it with fast, multi-sensory input creates about one second of a "receptive state" during which suggestions bypass critical thinking.
- The author claims deliberately exaggerating the emotional implication of someone's dismissive answer ("the exaggeration model") can trap them into re-engaging or reconsidering.
- The author claims framing a decision as only two options — one clearly bad, one favored — ("black/white model") pressures people toward the favored choice, and this is common in digital sales pages.
- The author claims stringing together 5+ statements that are strictly true (truisms), then attaching a suggestion, "installs" the suggestion because the mind is primed to keep saying yes.
- The author claims adding a verbal reinforcement tag ("isn't it?", "right?", "do you agree?") to the end of a truism increases the chance of a yes by 17%, and combining that with a slight nod adds another 23%, for roughly 40% more likelihood of agreement.
- The author claims the "simple bind," a technique attributed to Milton H. Erickson, offers an apparent choice that actually presupposes and traps the person inside the speaker's desired frame — also called the "illusion of alternative."
- The author claims the "double bind" is a stronger version of the simple bind, offering two specific alternatives so the listener chooses between them "without thinking too much," rather than considering whether to act at all.
- The author attributes the "because" experiment to Ellen Langer, a Harvard psychology professor, describing a 1978 copier-line study as proof of the word's power.
- The author cites a line from an Obama speech as a "fine example" of the truism technique in real political rhetoric.

## Concepts
- **because-justification** — Explaining a request with "because…" (even a weak or circular reason) makes a favorable response significantly more likely than making the same request with no reason given.
- **hot-words** — Using the exact words (e.g., "fantastic") that your interlocutor habitually uses to describe things, so they feel unconscious affinity and trust and perceive you as agreeable.
- **mirror-neuron-nodding** — Nodding slightly while asking a question, framed as a subliminal message that activates the listener's mirror neurons, unconsciously signaling agreement and triggering them to nod and answer "yes"; eye contact should be maintained and the nod should not be exaggerated.
- **similitude** — A rhetorical figure comparing two things that share qualities or defects (using "how," "like," etc.), used in persuasion/sales to reframe a choice as more significant than it first appeared.
- **sensory-overload** — Overloading the conscious mind (which handles about 7 pieces of information at once) with fast speech, lots of information, and simultaneous tactile/visual/auditory stimuli, producing momentary mental confusion and roughly one second of heightened suggestibility; described as underlying instantaneous hypnosis induction.
- **exaggeration-model** — Taking an interlocutor's dismissive or ambiguous answer and exaggerating its implied meaning into an emotional accusation, putting them on the back foot and under the speaker's control.
- **black-white-model** — Presenting only two alternatives, framed as opposites (one clearly good, one clearly bad/painful), so the listener feels compelled toward the favored option; the less explicit the manipulation, the more effective it is; commonly used in digital sales pages to create an "illusion of choice."
- **reinforced-truisms** — Stating a series of five or more statements that are strictly/verifiably true, then inserting a suggestion, so the mind (primed to say "yes" repeatedly) is more likely to accept the embedded suggestion; can be "strengthened" by adding tags like "isn't it?"/"right?"/"do you agree?" at sentence end, and further by pairing the tag with a slight nod.
- **simple-bind** — Also called "linguistic-presupposition" or the "illusion of alternative"; a hypnotic technique (attributed to Milton H. Erickson) that offers an apparent choice while presupposing and trapping the listener inside the speaker's intended outcome (e.g., asking "when" something will happen rather than "whether"), widely used in online sales copy. The author says a simple Bind can be built from two factors — Choice ("What will be the next product you buy?") and Time ("How fast do you think you'll get here?") — summarized as: "Don't give a choice; increase the conversions!"
- **illusion-of-alternative** — The author's alternate name for the simple bind: giving someone what feels like freedom of choice while the underlying premise (that they will act at all) is already assumed.
- **double-bind** — A stronger version of the simple bind offering two specific, concrete alternatives (e.g., "white or red wine," "one-time payment or installments") so the person chooses between them without questioning whether to engage in the first place.
- **linguistic-presupposition** — The author's technical label for the bind mechanism: language that presupposes a fact (e.g., that a purchase or action will happen) as already settled, leaving only a secondary choice open for debate.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Ellen Langer's 1978 copier-line experiment** [86] — Harvard psychology professor Ellen Langer tested requests to cut in a copier line under three conditions: (1) a plain request ("Excuse me, I only have five pages, can I make copies right away?"), (2) a request with a substantive reason ("...because I'm in a hurry"), and (3) a request with a circular/non-reason ("...because I have to make copies"). The author reports that even the weak, circular justification in group three succeeded 93% of the time, which he uses as proof that the word "because" itself triggers automatic compliance regardless of the reason's quality.
- **Cinema invitation example** — Contrasts "Come to the cinema with me tonight because they're showing a film that's going well?" with the bare "Come to the cinema with me tonight?", claiming the first is more effective purely because it contains "because," regardless of the actual motivation given.
- **"Fantastic" hot-word scenario** — A hypothetical interlocutor who frequently calls things "fantastic" (e.g., "this holiday was fantastic," "this movie is fantastic") should be invited using their own word ("would you like to take a holiday with me in a fantastic place?") rather than a synonym like "wonderful," to trigger unconscious trust.
- **Contract-signing nod scenario** — In a negotiation, saying "you need to sign the contract" while nodding slightly is offered as a way to subliminally pass the message "Ok, sign," aiming to get the actual signature.
- **"Gang of boys/pack of wolves" similitude example** — The author's generic illustration of the rhetorical figure: "a gang of boys is like a pack of wolves," given as the plain form of a similitude before moving to the sales application below.
- **Windows Home vs. Professional sales anecdote** — A "salesman friend" of the author asked a customer what changes between the Home and Professional versions of Windows; the customer replied that the Home version "is a bit small," and the author credits this similarity/comparison with leading to the sale of the Professional version.
- **Skincare cream exaggeration script** — Salesperson: "Madam, have you experienced the potential of this cream yet?" Customer: "No, I don't care." Salesperson: "Are you saying you don't care what your skin looks like?" — illustrating how a dismissal is reframed as an emotionally loaded admission.
- **Subscription-offer exaggeration script** — "Our offer will get you a year's subscription for only $67." / "I'm sorry, but I'm not interested." / "So, you're saying you don't want to save money?" — a second worked example of the exaggeration model.
- **Weight-loss pill black/white script** — "Either you buy our pills to lose weight, or you're going to keep this belly forever."
- **Running shoes black/white script** — "There are loser runners in life and winning runners... the latter wearing X shoes."
- **Slimming cream black/white script** — "I don't want to talk you into buying this slimming cream, it's your choice, but if you leave here without it, don't come back to me complaining in a year..."
- **"Red pill or blue pill" digital sales script** — A longer black/white example explicitly invoking "red pill or the blue pill" framing, contrasting ordering the product and "creating your business" against "continu[ing] to lead a miserable life forever thanks to your poor employee job," offered as typical of digital product sales pages.
- **Learning-to-walk truism script** — To motivate someone to embrace change, the author models: recalling learning to walk, then to speak, then to read and write, concluding "today you can do all these things well and many more, and now you can learn many new things!" — with the induced behavior (learning new things) embedded after a chain of undeniable truths.
- **Obama speech example** — Quoted line: "Today I stand before you to say that what we have already achieved gives us hope for what we can and must achieve tomorrow," presented as a truism sequence that "installs" the conviction that past electoral success predicts future success in other fields.
- **Gym-membership reinforced-truism script** — "We all want to stay in shape, isn't it? And we also know that exercise is important for weight maintenance, do you agree? Then joining a gym is an appropriate solution, right?" — used to illustrate stacking reinforcement tags, with the author claiming +17% from the tag, +23% more from pairing it with a nod, totaling roughly 40% more likelihood of a "yes."
- **Self-referential truism demonstration** — The author turns the technique on the reader directly: "This is one of the easiest communication techniques in the world, right?... And I bet you didn't think this technique could be that powerful, did you?... So, I guess as of tonight, you're going to enjoy putting it into practice, aren't you?" — modeling the technique live within the text.
- **Milton H. Erickson quote and bind examples** — Erickson, described as "a famous therapeutic hypnotist," is quoted: "I like to give my patients as many choices as possible to do what I want." Simple-bind sample scripts include: "Do you want to tell me now what's bothering you or would you rather wait a while?"; "After you go to buy bread, could you come by the newsstand and buy me the paper?"; "When are you going to take me to the movies?"; "Have you decided which foreign country to take me to for our anniversary?"
- **Online sales bind examples** — "When you get to the order form, be careful to enter the coupon code before buying, so that the discount is activated"; "After the purchase, you will be taken to the member's area and have immediate access to the course videos" — both presuppose the purchase will happen.
- **Two-factor simple-bind formula** — The author offers Choice ("What will be the next product you buy?") and Time ("How fast do you think you'll get here?") as the two factors usable to construct a simple Bind.
- **Double-bind sample scripts** — "Do you want to order white or red wine with pasta?"; "Do you want to move into your new house tomorrow or next month?"; "Would you prefer to sit in the front row or the middle for the VIP seat?"
- **Payment-plan double-bind script** — "Now you have two options to proceed. By purchasing the product with a one-time payment, you will pay $997 and save 20%. If you prefer a deferred payment, there are 3 installments of $333 available. You will pay slightly more, but not all at once." — closing example applying the double bind to a purchase decision.
