# Ch 8 — Traveling in the Superhologram

## In one paragraph
Having argued in the previous chapter that time is a construct, Talbot now argues that space is equally illusory: in a holographic universe location itself is an illusion, and consciousness — like everything else — is ultimately nonlocal. His evidence is the out-of-body experience (OBE) and, above all, the near-death experience (NDE). He marshals surveys, verified veridical cases (Maria's tennis shoe, Sabom's cardiac patients), and laboratory work to argue that consciousness can genuinely detach from the body, and then reads the classic features of the NDE — plastic thought-sculpted bodies, the frequency-like realm of light, the instantaneous life review, "thought balls" of total knowledge, life plans and personal flashforwards — as exactly what one would expect if dying is a shift of consciousness into the more frequency-like, implicate levels of the hologram. He then shows that mystics (Swedenborg, the Sufis, Sri Yukteswar, Sri Aurobindo) and shamanic cultures worldwide describe the same territory, and extends the same logic to Marian apparitions and UFOs, proposing that such phenomena are "omnijective" — neither purely subjective nor objective, but real, mind-created holographic manifestations. The chapter closes with the conclusion that reality at every level is a mass shared dream, a self-reference cosmology in which "the dream is dreaming itself."

## Core claims
- Talbot argues that space, like time, is a product of our mode of perception; in a holographic universe everything, including consciousness, is nonlocal, which is why consciousness can appear localized in a corner of a room as easily as in the head.
- Talbot argues that the accumulated evidence for OBEs is "unequivocal": under the right circumstances the thinking, perceiving part of us can detach from the body and exist just about anywhere it wants to.
- Talbot argues the second body of the OBEer is not a fixed "phantom double" but a hologram sculpted by beliefs and expectations (naked or clothed, male or female, ball of light, cloud, or no form at all); Monroe holds that "thought habits" create our OB forms and that at heart we are a "vibrational pattern [comprised] of many interacting and resonating frequencies."
- Talbot argues that inaccuracies in otherwise-accurate OB perception (his green-vs-black book lettering) arise because OBEers' new nonphysical senses are not yet proficient at converting the frequency domain into an accurate hologram of consensus reality; self-limiting beliefs (e.g., that we see only through eyes) further censor these senses — and possibly our physical senses too, as eyeless-sight cases suggest.
- Talbot argues that OB journeys into the future and past (Tanous, Harary, Strindberg) show that the OB realm is one of Bohm's subtler levels, closer to the implicate order where past/present/future divisions cease.
- Talbot argues NDEs are not hallucinations, citing five converging facts: their worldwide spread, the absence of any demographic profile, the cross-cultural consistency of the core experience, veridical perception (including in the blind), and their occurrence in patients with flat EEGs (hallucinations register on an EEG; these did not).
- Ring believes NDEs are ventures into the more frequency-like aspects of reality, and that the afterlife realm is created by interacting "thought structures" that form patterns like interference waves on a holographic plate; Fenske agrees, noting that near death "thought seems to be light."
- Whitton concludes (from hypnotic between-life regressions) that the shapes of the afterlife dimension are thought-forms: "There is no experience of existence without thought."
- Talbot argues the life review is "a holographic phenomenon par excellence" (Ring's phrase): three-dimensional, wraparound, instantaneous yet detailed, storing every event, emotion (including the emotions of others one affected) and every thought — an aleph-like moment containing all other moments. Grof and Joan Halifax (a Harvard medical anthropologist and coauthor, with Grof, of The Human Encounter with Death) have also commented on its holographic aspects, and per several researchers, including Moody, many NDEers themselves use the term "holographic" when describing it.
- The beings of light never judge (only self-judgment occurs) and stress two values: love (compassion as "the barometer of grace" — "Did you do it out of love?") and knowledge (one of the few things "you will be able to take with you after you have died"). Moody concludes we live in a universe far more benevolent than we realize.
- NDE research suggests lives are at least partly planned beforehand ("it is not your time yet" implies a life plan, says Ring), with the experiencer participating; personal flashforwards and alternate-history visions support Loye's proposal of parallel holographic time tracks.
- Talbot argues this reality and the afterlife differ in degree, not kind: both are hologram-like constructs established (per Jahn and Dunne) by the interaction of consciousness with its environment; ours is simply a "more frozen" version — beliefs resculpt our bodies more slowly (stigmata, synchronicities).
- Talbot argues that mystical and shamanic testimony (Swedenborg, the Sufis' alam almithal, Sri Yukteswar, Sri Aurobindo, the aboriginal dreamtime) independently corroborates the NDE picture; Kalweit holds that the shaman "should be considered as a most up-to-date and knowledgeable psychologist."
- Talbot argues that the NDE's transformative aftereffects (expanded love, spirituality over religion, psychic opening) are, per Moody and Ring, the most compelling evidence that NDEers touch a genuinely spiritual level of reality; Bohm allows that the implicate domain "could equally well be called Idealism, Spirit, Consciousness."
- Talbot argues that Marian visions and UFOs are "omnijective" (his own coinage, paralleling Corbin's "imaginal"): real yet mind-created holographic phenomena — either psychic projections of the collective unconscious (Grosso, Jung), manifestations of an extradimensional nonhuman intelligence (Vallee, Raschke), or both at once, with collective belief possibly opening the "doorway" through which such intelligences appear.
- Talbot concludes that in a holographic universe reality is a mass shared dream with the ontological status of a dream; asking whether God or the collective consciousness of all things dreams it is meaningless, because the part is the whole — a self-reference cosmology (Wheeler): "The dream is dreaming itself."

## Concepts
- **Out-of-body experience (OBE)** — an experience in which an individual's conscious awareness appears to detach from the physical body and travel to some other location; typically spontaneous, occurring during sleep, meditation, anesthesia, illness, and traumatic pain.
- **Nonlocal consciousness** — in a holographic universe location is itself an illusion (as an apple image has no specific location on holographic film), so consciousness possesses no definite location and can appear localized anywhere; Talbot's analogy is the dreamer, whose consciousness is really in everything in the dream.
- **Second body / phantom double** — the ghostlike replica body some OBEers find themselves in; recent findings (clothed vs. naked forms, balls of light, clouds, no form) show it is not a permanent energy replica but a hologram that can assume many shapes, sculpted by conscious and unconscious beliefs.
- **Holographic plasticity** — the tendency of OB/ND forms, landscapes, clothing, and even the mode of entry to the afterlife (tunnel, bridge, road, body of water) to be molded by the thoughts, expectations, and cultural symbols of the experiencer.
- **Eyeless sight** — the reported ability to see with areas of the body other than the eyes (armpits, nose, earlobe, fingertips); Talbot takes it as evidence the physical body's sensory limits are themselves a holographic construct of belief.
- **360-degree vision** — the ability, reported by seasoned OBEers and NDEers, to see in all directions at once while disembodied; initially blocked by the habit of believing one sees only through eyes.
- **Frequency domain** — Pribram's realm of pure frequency underlying appearances; the OB/ND realm is described in terms of light, "higher vibrations," and frequencies, suggesting dying shifts consciousness into this more holographic reality.
- **Thought-forms / interacting thought structures** — Ring's term for what builds the afterlife's apparent structure: thought structures combine into patterns like interference waves on a holographic plate, and the images they produce appear real the way an illuminated holographic image does.
- **Life review** — a panoramic, instantaneous, three-dimensional replay of one's entire life in complete sensory detail, including every thought and the felt emotions of everyone one interacted with; judgment is self-judgment only.
- **Being of light** — the compassionate luminous being(s) encountered in the NDE who prompt the life review and teach rather than judge; their outward appearance (Christian figure, Hindu deity) is sculpted by the experiencer's beliefs.
- **Thought balls / bundles of thought** — instantaneous, nonlinear chunks of information that burst whole into awareness during ND visions of knowledge (Monroe's term from the OB state is "thought balls"); the same form in which psychic information arrives.
- **Vision of total knowledge** — the ND experience of having direct access to all knowledge (by asking, by a being's gesture, or by suddenly "remembering" it), almost universally followed by amnesia on return.
- **Metaconsciousness** — the state of heightened awareness and lucid honesty in self-reflection NDEers enter after arriving in the realm of light.
- **Life plan** — the finding that lives are sketched out beforehand with the individual's participation; implied by "it is not your time yet" and by NDEers being given the choice to return or stay.
- **Personal flashforward** — Ring's term for glimpses of one's own future shown during the vision of knowledge; some are conditional ("only if you continue on your current path"), supporting parallel time tracks.
- **Parallel time tracks** — Loye's proposal (referenced from earlier in the book) that several holographic parallel universes exist; supported by conditional flashforwards and the alternate-history-of-Earth vision.
- **Between-life state** — the realm Whitton's hypnotized subjects reported between incarnations, with all the classic NDE features; its life review serves to refresh memory for mindfully planning the next life.
- **Book of Lives** — Swedenborg's name for the life-review process he watched newly deceased souls undergo ("the opening of the Book of Lives"); he added that the record is stored in the nervous system of the spiritual body and evoked by an angel examining the whole body starting with the fingers.
- **Wave-substance** — Swedenborg's term for the substrate surrounding a person in which angelic "portrayals" could be seen; he believed a wave-substance underlies visible reality.
- **Alam almithal** — the twelfth-century Persian Sufis' subtle matter of thought out of which the afterlife realm ("the country of the hidden Imam") is created — including space itself ("nearness," "distances," "far-off" places).
- **Imaginal realm** — Corbin's coinage for a world created by imagination yet ontologically no less real than physical reality — created by the imagination of many people but possessing its own corporeality, forests, mountains, and cities.
- **Na-Koja-Abad ("the land of nonwhere")** — Sohrawardi's name for the country of the hidden Imam, expressing its nonlocality: instead of searching for spiritual reality "in the where," the "where" is in us. Talbot adds that an OBEer may not actually travel anywhere at all, but merely alter the always-illusory hologram of reality so as to have the experience of traveling somewhere — in a holographic universe consciousness is not only already everywhere, it too is nonwhere.
- **Creative prayer** — the Sufi practice of using visualization to alter and reshape the fabric of one's destiny, following from imagination being a faculty of perception.
- **Dreamtime** — the Australian aboriginal realm shamans visit in trance, almost identical (per Thero) to Western afterlife descriptions; a dimension without time, space, or earthly boundaries where one must learn to deal with infinity — hence "survival in infinity."
- **Cities of light / halls of learning** — the vast, brilliantly luminous, architecturally sublime cities and knowledge-institutions consistently reported by NDEers, yogic adepts, and shamans; some NDEers say the buildings were literally "built out of knowledge," suggesting holograms our minds construct from clouds of pure information.
- **Omnijective** — Talbot's 1976 coinage for phenomena that are neither subjective nor objective but both: products of the psyche that are also quite real (UFOs, Marian visions); equivalent to Corbin's "imaginal."
- **Self-reference cosmology** — Wheeler's idea that we create subatomic particles (and hence the universe) while they also create us — each creates the other; Talbot's frame for the closing question of whose dream reality is.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Dean Shiels — cross-cultural OBE survey** — studied 44 non-Western societies; only 3 did not hold a belief in OBEs. Shows OBE belief is near-universal.
- **Erika Bourguignon (anthropologist) — cross-cultural survey** — examined 488 world societies (roughly 57 percent of all known societies); 437 (89 percent) had at least some OBE tradition.
- **Robert Crookall (geologist, University of Aberdeen; amateur parapsychologist)** — investigated enough OBE cases to fill nine books on the subject.
- **Celia Green (director, Institute of Psychophysical Research, Oxford), 1960s surveys** — polled 115 Southampton University students: 19 percent admitted an OBE; 380 Oxford students similarly questioned: 34 percent affirmative.
- **Haraldsson survey** — of 902 adults, 8 percent had experienced being out of their bodies at least once.
- **Harvey Irwin (University of New England, Australia), 1980 survey** — 20 percent of 177 students had experienced an OBE. Averaged with the others, roughly one in five people will have an OBE (other studies suggest one in ten).
- **Gabbard, Twemlow & Jones, 1980 study of 339 OBE cases** — Dr. Glen Gabbard (Menninger Foundation, Topeka), Dr. Stuart Twemlow (Topeka VA Medical Center), Dr. Fowler Jones (University of Kansas Medical Center): 85 percent described the experience as pleasant, over half joyful. Their psychological-profile work found OBEers normal and extremely well adjusted; at the 1980 American Psychiatric Association meeting they said reassurance and reading may be "more therapeutic" than psychiatric treatment, even hinting a yogi might help more than a psychiatrist.
- **Talbot's own teenage OBE** — spontaneous; he flew through walls and over treetops, stumbled across a library book a neighbor had lost and told her its location the next day (detailed in his Beyond the Quantum). Later used as a discrepancy example too: the lost book's title lettering looked bright green out-of-body but was actually black — evidence the new OB senses convert the frequency domain imperfectly.
- **Kimberly Clark and Maria (Seattle hospital)** — coronary patient Maria had a cardiac arrest, found herself at the ceiling watching resuscitation, "thought herself" over the emergency-room driveway, then to the third floor, ending "eyeball to shoelace" with an old tennis shoe on a ledge — noting the little toe worn through the fabric and the lace stuck under the heel. Skeptical social worker Clark searched, finally spotted the shoe through a narrow window, and could only confirm the details after retrieving it: the perspective required floating right outside at close range. Clark became a believer.
- **Michael B. Sabom (cardiologist, Emory University; Atlanta VA staff physician) — controlled comparison** — 32 seasoned cardiac patients who reported OBEs during heart attacks vs. 25 who had not. Non-experiencers describing their imagined resuscitations: 20 made major mistakes, 3 gave correct general descriptions, 2 had no idea. Experiencers: 26 correct general descriptions, 6 highly detailed and accurate, 1 a blow-by-blow account so accurate Sabom was stunned. He became an ardent believer: "The out-of-body hypothesis simply seems to fit best with the data at hand."
- **Robert Monroe (former radio/TV executive)** — first OBE in the late 1950s; feared insanity, sought medical help, then learned to control the state ("One was sedation for the rest of my life; the other was to learn something about this state so I could control it"). Kept a journal; could pass through solid objects, travel instantly by "thinking" himself there; friends verified his descriptions of their dress and activity during his invisible visits; occasionally met other disembodied travelers. Books: Journeys Out of the Body and Far Journeys. Found our core form is a "vibrational pattern [comprised] of many interacting and resonating frequencies"; says OB perception rests less on "a reflection of light waves" and more on "an impression of radiation."
- **Charles Tart — Miss Z experiment** — the parapsychologist had a skilled OBEer he identifies only as Miss Z correctly identify a five-digit number written on paper that could only be reached if she were floating out-of-body.
- **Karlis Osis & Janet Lee Mitchell (American Society for Psychical Research, New York)** — gifted subjects "flew in" from around the country and correctly described target images: objects on a table, colored geometric patterns on a free-floating shelf near the ceiling, and optical illusions visible only through a small window in a special device.
- **Robert Morris (Psychical Research Foundation, Durham, NC) — animal detector** — a kitten belonging to talented OB subject Keith Harary consistently stopped meowing and started purring whenever Harary was invisibly present.
- **J. H. M. Whiteman (mathematician), The Mystical Life (1961)** — experienced at least two OBEs a month through adult life, recording over two thousand incidents; always felt like a woman trapped in a man's body and sometimes found himself in female form during separation, also children's bodies; concluded beliefs, conscious and unconscious, determine the second body's form.
- **David Eisenberg, M.D. (Harvard Medical School) — Chinese sisters** — published an account of two school-age sisters in Beijing who can "see" well enough with the skin of their armpits to read notes and identify colors.
- **Cesare Lombroso (Italian neurologist) — blind girl** — studied a blind girl who could see with the tip of her nose and the lobe of her left ear.
- **Rosa Kuleshova (Soviet Academy of Science, 1960s)** — Russian peasant woman who could see photographs and read newspapers with her fingertips; the Academy pronounced her genuine and ruled out heat-detection: she could read a black-and-white newspaper covered with a sheet of heated glass. Life magazine published an article about her.
- **Alex Tanous (psychic, Maine) — OB into the future** — in Osis and Mitchell's fly-in tests he tended to describe target objects placed on the table days later; Talbot reads this as tuning into frequencies encoding the future. Same day, New York psychic Christine Whiting, holding vigil without knowing who would fly in or when, saw Tanous's apparition clearly and described him wearing brown corduroy pants and a white cotton shirt — the clothing he was wearing in Maine at that moment.
- **Keith Harary — OB future journeys** — describes moving through "a black, dark area that ends at some lighted future scene," sometimes seeing a silhouette of his future self; when the events later occur, he senses his time-traveling OB self in the scene with him — "meeting myself 'behind' myself as if I were two beings."
- **August Strindberg — OB into the past (Legends)** — while in a wine shop persuading a young friend not to quit his military career, he cited a shared past incident in a tavern, suddenly "lost consciousness," found himself sitting in that tavern reliving the occurrence, then snapped back to the present. Talbot adds that the previous chapter's retrocognitive clairvoyants who "floated" over historical scenes may also be OB projection into the past.
- **Historical NDE accounts** — described in the eighth-century Tibetan Book of the Dead and the 2,500-year-old Egyptian Book of the Dead; Plato's Republic Book X tells of the Greek soldier Er, revived seconds before his funeral pyre was lit, who had left his body through a "passageway" to the land of the dead; the Venerable Bede gives a similar account in A History of the English Church and People; Carol Zaleski (Harvard) shows in Otherworld Journeys that medieval literature is filled with NDEs. The life review also appears in the Tibetan and Egyptian books, Plato's Er account, and the 2,000-year-old yogic writings of Patanjali.
- **1981 Gallup poll** — eight million adult Americans had experienced an NDE — roughly one person in twenty. Gallup himself: the results "point toward a super parallel universe of some sort."
- **NDE demographics** — studies show no relationship between NDEs and age, sex, marital status, race, religion/spiritual beliefs, social class, education, income, church attendance, home-community size, or area of residence; the devout are no likelier than nonbelievers.
- **Osis & Haraldsson, 1977 deathbed-vision comparison** — compared nearly nine hundred deathbed visions reported to doctors and medical personnel in India and the United States; despite cultural differences (Americans saw the being of light as a Christian personage, Indians as Hindu), the "core" experience was substantially the same as Moody's and Kubler-Ross's NDEs.
- **Moody — mismatched plaids case** — a woman left her body during surgery, floated to the waiting room, and saw her daughter wearing mismatched plaids; the maid had dressed the girl hastily and was astounded when the mother, who never physically saw the child that day, commented on it.
- **The pallbearer case** — an NDEer went to the hospital lobby out-of-body and overheard her brother-in-law tell a friend he would have to cancel a business trip to be a pallbearer; after recovering she reprimanded her astonished brother-in-law for writing her off so quickly.
- **Kubler-Ross — blind NDEers** — patients blind for years, with no light perception, saw and accurately described their surroundings during NDEs; "To our amazement, they were able to describe the color and design of clothing and jewelry the people present wore."
- **Two-person NDE — the friend in the tunnel** — a woman moving through the tunnel toward the light passed a friend coming back; he telepathically told her he had died but was being "sent back." She too was sent back, and later learned he had suffered cardiac arrest at approximately the same time as her own experience. Numerous other cases exist of the dying knowing who awaited them beyond before news of that person's death arrived normally.
- **Flat-EEG NDEs** — talking, thinking, dreaming, and even hallucinations all register on an EEG; yet many people with flat EEGs have had NDEs — so they cannot have been hallucinations.
- **Melvin Morse (pediatrician, Seattle) — the seven-year-old drowning victim** — resuscitated profoundly comatose: fixed dilated pupils, no muscle reflexes, no corneal response (Glasgow Coma Score of three — almost no chance of recovery). She recovered fully, recognized Morse, said she had watched him work on her body, had passed through a tunnel into heaven and met "the Heavenly Father," who said it was not her time and — pointing out she would not see her mother again — persuaded her to return.
- **Morse — ten-year child study (Idaho)** — working for an air transport service carrying patients, he interviewed every child survivor of cardiac arrest at the hospital over ten years; over and over they reported leaving their bodies, watching the doctors, the tunnel, and comfort by luminous beings. After exhausting drug side effects and psychological explanations, he concluded: "NDEs are real... the soul really does travel."
- **Kenneth Ring (professor of psychology, University of Connecticut), Life at Death (1980)** — one of the first to apply statistical analysis and standardized interviews to NDEs; argues NDEs are ventures into the frequency-like aspects of reality, citing descriptions of the beyond as "light," "higher vibrations," "frequencies"; celestial music as "a combination of vibrations"; painless brilliance; and collapse of time and space ("I found myself in a space... where all space and time was negated").
- **Elizabeth W. Fenske (clinical psychologist) — 1989 IANDS keynote** — announced she too believes NDEs are journeys into a holographic realm of higher frequencies: in the NDE "thought seems to be light."
- **The jellyfish/soap-bubble man** — an NDEer emerged from his body "something like a jellyfish," fell lightly to the floor like a soap bubble, then quickly expanded into a ghostly three-dimensional image of a naked man; embarrassed at his nakedness (two women were present), his form yielded to clothing — so meticulously wrought he could make out the seams in the material.
- **The hands of light** — a man studying his hands in the ND state saw they were "composed of light with tiny structures in them," including "the delicate whorls of his fingerprints and tubes of light up his arms."
- **Thought-built ND bodies** — people confined to wheelchairs find themselves in healthy bodies that run and dance; amputees invariably have their limbs back; the elderly inhabit youthful bodies; children frequently see themselves as adults.
- **Joel Whitton — between-life hypnotic regressions** — regressed subjects reported all classic NDE features (tunnel, deceased relatives/"guides", light-filled timeless realm, luminous beings, life review); the review's main purpose was refreshing memory to plan the next life, with the beings assisting noncoercively. Subjects had no body unless thinking ("if he stopped thinking he was merely a cloud in an endless cloud... as soon as he started to think, he became himself"); forms began as the last life's body, then became a hologram-like composite of all past lives with its own unpronounceable name. Failed dreams and aspirations, not just thoughtless acts, caused remorse in the review. One subject, after several withdrawn, depressed incarnations, was urged to plan a life as an amorous, sexually active female for the balanced development of his soul.
- **The rose-cobra woman (Whitton)** — a subject introduced in the between-life state to a woman who would figure in his next life saw her as a shape half-rose, half-cobra; decoding the symbolism, he realized they had been in love in two other lifetimes and she had twice been responsible for his death — the ND realm giving the psyche's symbolic language "objective" holographic form.
- **The Arizona housewife — tones and colors** — during an NDE in childbirth she realized "each person and thing has its own musical tone range as well as its own color range"; she moved among prismatic rays of light hearing each person's musical notes harmonize with her own, met individuals manifesting only as clouds of color and sound, and believes these soul-tones are the "beautiful music" NDEers report.
- **360-degree ND vision** — one man, wondering what he looked like, suddenly found himself staring at his own back. Robert Sullivan (amateur NDE researcher, Pennsylvania, specializing in combat NDEs) interviewed a World War II veteran who retained 360-degree vision even after returning to his body: running from a German machine-gun nest he could see ahead while also seeing the gunners trying to draw a bead on him from behind.
- **Life-review testimony** — "It's like climbing right inside a movie of your life... Total, total recall. And it all happens in an instant"; another was "actually walking through" flashbacks fast yet slow enough to take in. Experiencers feel the emotions of everyone they interacted with: one woman relived snatching a toy from her sister in childhood and felt all her sister's loss and powerlessness. "Not even your thoughts are lost... Every thought was there." An NDEer in 1821 described the faculty as the ability to "simultaneously comprehend the whole and every part."
- **The boy hit by a car** — guided into the beyond by two people in "very white" robes; returned with the message: "What I learned there is that the most important thing is loving while you are alive."
- **Visions of total knowledge** — one man had only to ask a question (e.g., what would it be like to be an insect?) and the experience was instantly his; another: "You can think of a question... and immediately know the answer... the light will give you the instantaneous correct answer and make you understand it." Some knew everything without asking; others accessed it after a gesture (a wave of the being's hand); others "remembered" it — with near-universal amnesia on return. Information arrived in instantaneous "chunks" — "bundles of thought" (one NDEer) or Monroe's "thought balls."
- **Talbot's thought-ball anecdote** — meeting a stranger (or sometimes just hearing a name) triggers an instant thought ball of information about the person — psychology, health, scenes from their past — especially for people in crisis; he once instantly knew a woman he had just met was contemplating suicide and some of the reasons, steered the conversation to the psychic, confronted her, and got her to promise professional counseling.
- **Moody — negotiated returns** — a man who cried on realizing he was dead, afraid his wife couldn't raise their nephew alone, was allowed to return because he wasn't asking for himself; a woman who argued she "hadn't danced enough yet" made the being of light laugh heartily and was also permitted to return.
- **The forced-air heater flashforward** — a child NDEer was told he would marry at twenty-eight and have two children, and was shown his adult self and future children in a room of his future house — with a strange object on the wall his mind could not grasp. Decades later, every prediction fulfilled, he found himself in the very scene and recognized the object as a "forced-air heater," not yet invented at the time of his NDE.
- **The woman shown Moody's photograph** — in 1971 (before Life after Life was published) an NDEer was shown a photo of Moody, told his full name, and told she would tell him her experience "when the time was right." Four years later Moody's family unwittingly moved onto her street; on Halloween his son knocked at her door trick-or-treating, she heard his name and sent for his father, and told Moody her story.
- **The alternate-history vision (parallel time tracks)** — an NDEer was shown a completely different history of the earth that would have developed had "certain events" not occurred around the time of Pythagoras three thousand years ago: a world of peace and harmony "marked by the absence of religious wars and of a Christ figure." Supports Loye's parallel-universes proposal, as do conditional flashforwards ("only if you continue on your current path").
- **The sixty-two-year-old businessman** — after an NDE during cardiac arrest: "we are all part of one big, living universe... If we think we can hurt another person or another living thing without hurting ourselves we are sadly mistaken... I look at a forest or a flower or a bird now, and say, 'That is me, part of me.'"
- **The girl and the wished-for food** — a child NDEer said food appeared whenever she wished for it, but there was no need to eat — underscoring the illusory, hologram-like nature of afterlife reality.
- **Hazrat Inayat Khan — half-animal beings** — in mystical travels to "divine realities" he met beings occasionally appearing half-human, half-animal, and discerned the animal symbolized a quality of the being (a lion's head for strength, fox features for craft); he theorized this is why ancient cultures such as Egypt gave their afterlife gods animal heads.
- **Sai Baba and Therese Neumann (callbacks)** — Sai Baba's materializations suggest we can conjure food by wishing even here; Therese Neumann's inedia suggests eating may ultimately be as unnecessary for us as for those in the ND realm — this reality and the next differ in degree, not kind.
- **Emanuel Swedenborg — OB visitor to the afterlife** — born 1688, "the Leonardo da Vinci of his era": Sweden's leading mathematician, nine languages, engraver, politician, astronomer, businessman; built watches and microscopes; wrote on metallurgy, color theory, commerce, economics, physics, chemistry, mining, anatomy; invented airplane and submarine prototypes. In middle age his meditation deepened into trances in which he left his body and visited "heaven," conversing with "angels" and "spirits."
- **Swedenborg's veridical feats** — the queen of Sweden asked him why her deceased brother had not answered a letter; next day he returned with a message the queen confessed contained information only she and her dead brother knew. He also told a widow where to find a secret compartment in her deceased husband's desk containing desperately needed documents — an incident so famous it inspired Kant to write Dreams of a Spirit-Seer.
- **Swedenborg's afterlife reports** — dark tunnel, welcoming spirits, landscapes beyond earthly beauty where time and space no longer exist, a dazzling light emitting love, beings of light, all-encompassing peace; he watched the newly deceased undergo the life review ("the opening of the Book of Lives"), in which a person witnessed "everything they had ever been or done" — recorded, he said, in the nervous system of the spiritual body and evoked by an angel examining the body "beginning with the fingers of each hand, and proceeding through the whole." Angelic "portrayals" — a picture language so dense each image contains a thousand ideas, visible in the "wave-substance" around a person, sometimes lasting hours "in such a sequential arrangement that one can only marvel" — are supplemented by an angel speech beyond human understanding. In the spirit world food is unneeded, information taking its place as nourishment; angelic talk coalesces into 3-D symbolic images, especially animals (lambs for love and affection; tigers, bears, wolves, scorpions, snakes, and mice for evil affections). He reported spirits from other planets in heaven, claimed all humans are connected in cosmic unity and each of us is a heaven in miniature, and held heaven is the archetypal source of all earthly forms, with matter flowing "in by stages" from the Divine, growing "coarser and hazier... more viscous and colder." Filled almost twenty volumes; on his deathbed, asked if he wished to recant: "Everything that I have written is as true as you now behold me... After death you will see all."
- **George F. Dole (professor of theology, Swedenborg School of Religion, Newton, Mass.; degrees from Yale, Oxford, Harvard)** — notes Swedenborg's universe is constantly created and sustained by two wavelike flows (one from heaven, one from our soul): "We can view ourselves as interference patterns, because the inflow is a wave phenomenon, and we are where the waves meet" — a striking resemblance to the hologram.
- **The twelfth-century Persian Sufis — the country of the hidden Imam** — used deep trancelike meditation to visit the "land where spirits dwell," where one has a "subtle body" with senses not tied to specific organs; a world created solely from alam almithal (thought), including space itself, yet corporeal, with forests, mountains, and cities, created by the imagination of many people. Henry Corbin (Sorbonne professor of Islamic Religion) coined "imaginal" to convey this ontological reality ("I would undoubtedly have betrayed [the texts' meaning] had I simply contented myself with the term imaginary"). The Sufis concluded imagination is a faculty of perception and practiced "creative prayer" — visualization to reshape destiny; they marveled that the inner world "turns out to envelop, surround, or contain that which at first was outer and visible." Sohrawardi called the realm Na-Koja-Abad, "the land of nonwhere."
- **The seven-year-old boy on death** — "Death is like walking into your mind" — an NDEer's expression of the afterlife's nonlocal place in the psyche.
- **Sri Yukteswar Giri (Hindu holy man, d. Puri, India, 1936)** — met by Evans-Wentz in the 1920s ("pleasing presence and high character"); gifted at passing between this world and the next. Described the afterlife as "various subtle vibrations of light and color," "hundreds of times larger than the material cosmos," with opal lakes, bright seas, rainbow rivers, luminous white snow and rain of many-colored lights; inhabitants materialize any body or food (though "almost freed from any necessity of eating," they "feast only on the ambrosia of eternally new knowledge"), see with any body area, communicate by telepathic "light pictures," learn to give "a divine and equal love to all" when facing relatives from many incarnations; injuries "healed at once by mere willing"; we are "intelligent and coordinated images of light."
- **Sri Aurobindo Ghose (1872–…, mystic and political activist)** — English-educated prodigy fluent in English, Hindi, Russian, German, French, and Sanskrit; read a case of books a day with verbatim recall; jailed for sedition in India's nationalist movement; an atheist until he saw a wandering yogi instantaneously heal his brother of a life-threatening illness. Through years of silencing the mind he became "an explorer of the planes of consciousness," finding a realm beyond space and time of "multicolored infinity of vibrations" peopled by advanced nonphysical beings who appear to a Christian as a Christian saint and to an Indian as a Hindu one — not to deceive but to be accessible "to a particular consciousness"; in truest form they are "pure vibration" (he likened form/vibration to wave-particle duality in On Yoga). There information is absorbed "in great masses" rather than point-by-point. He said a "mental screen" hides "the veil of matter"; everything is "different intensities of luminous vibrations"; all matter is to some degree conscious (hence psychokinesis is possible); a "progressive law of fragmentation" takes over as one descends the vibrational levels; if a single point of the universe were totally unconscious the whole universe would be; he opposed religion in favor of the spiritual universe within ("flee also from the snare of the thinker, the snare of the theologian and the church-builder"); physical reality is "a mass of stable light."
- **E. Nandisvara Nayake Thero (anthropologist) — the dreamtime** — lived with an Australian aboriginal community; the dreamtime, visited by shamans in profound trance, is almost identical to Western afterlife descriptions: the realm of the dead, instant access to all knowledge, no time/space/boundaries — "survival in infinity."
- **Holger Kalweit (German ethnopsychologist, degrees in psychology and cultural anthropology)** — virtually all shamanic traditions describe this extradimensional realm (life review, teaching spiritual beings, thought-conjured food, beautiful meadows/forests/mountains); the ability to travel to the afterlife realm is the most universal requirement of shamanhood, and NDEs are often the catalyst — the Oglala Sioux, Seneca, Siberian Yakut, South American Guajiro, Zulu, Kenyan Kikuyu, Korean Mudang, Indonesian Mentawai Islanders, and Caribou Eskimo all have traditions of shamans made by life-threatening illness. Shamanic cultures navigate the realm better because psychic reality is daily reality for them; "the shaman should be considered as a most up-to-date and knowledgeable psychologist."
- **Michael Harner among the Conibo (Peruvian Amazon, 1960–61)** — sent by the American Museum of Natural History for a year-long expedition; told he must drink ayahuasca (the "soul vine") to learn the Conibo's religion. He had an out-of-body journey among the gods and devils of Conibo mythology: demons with grinning crocodilian heads, an "energy-essence" rising from his chest toward a dragon-headed ship crewed by Egyptian-style, blue-jay-headed figures, the felt approach of his own death. Winged dragonlike beings emerged from his spine and "projected" the "true" history of Earth: they created and inhabit all life as a hiding place from an undisclosed enemy in outer space (Harner notes they were "almost like DNA," though in 1961 he knew nothing of DNA). A blind Conibo shaman, hearing the account, smiled: "Oh, they're always saying that. But they are only the Masters of Outer Darkness." Two Christian missionaries recognized his descriptions as virtually identical to passages in the Book of Revelation, which Harner, an atheist, had never read. Talbot: Old and New Testament prophets' "trips to heaven" may also have been shamanic journeys.
- **NDE transformations** — NDEers become happier, more optimistic, easygoing, less materialistic; capacity to love expands enormously (aloof husbands become affectionate, workaholics relax, introverts become extroverts); criminals reform; fire-and-brimstone preachers switch to unconditional love. They return convinced of the soul's immortality and a compassionate, intelligent universe, favoring spirituality over religion, with increased openness to reincarnation and Eastern religions.
- **Ring's heavy-equipment driver** — no interest in books before his NDE; had a vision of total knowledge he could not recall, but physics terms surfaced: he blurted "quantum," announced "Max Planck—you'll be hearing about him in the near future," then fragments of equations. Neither he nor his wife knew the words until he looked them up; he then read voraciously (physics, parapsychology, metaphysics) and enrolled in college as a physics major. His wife's letter to Ring: he speaks words he has never heard "in our reality... learns... it in relationship to the 'light' theory... talks about things faster than the speed of light... When [he] picks up a book on physics he already knows the answer and seems to feel more."
- **Bruce Greyson (psychiatrist, University of Michigan; IANDS director of research), 1982** — gave 69 NDEers a questionnaire on psychic aftereffects; found an increase in virtually all psychic and psi-related phenomena assessed.
- **Phyllis Atwater (Idaho housewife turned NDE researcher after her own NDE)** — interviewed dozens of NDEers with similar findings: "Telepathy and healing gifts are common. So is 'remembering' the future. Time and space stop, and you live in a future sequence in detail. Then, when the event occurs, you recognize it."
- **1985 Georgetown University symposium (convened by U.S. Senator Claiborne Pell)** — on life beyond biological death. Physicist Paul Davies: "if mind is pattern rather than substance, then it is capable of many different representations"; psychoneuroimmunologist Candace Pert: "perhaps biological information flow cannot just disappear at death and must be transformed into another realm." Separately, Nobelist Brian Josephson, a longtime meditator, is likewise convinced there are subtler levels of reality accessible through meditation where one may travel after death.
- **Who are the beings? (Whitton and Monroe)** — Whitton's subjects who could answer said the between-life counselors were "entities who had completed their cycle of incarnations here." Monroe, after hundreds of journeys and interviews with fellow OBEers: they "radiate a warmth of friendliness that evokes complete trust," reading thoughts is "absurdly easy" for them, "the entire history of humankind and earth is available to them in the most minute detail," and they are "totally solicitous as to the well-being of the human beings with whom they are associated" — but their ultimate identity remains unknown. To the question whether a being of light is a reality or just an allegorical phantasm, Talbot answers it is a bit of both: in a holographic universe such images are illusions shaped by the consciousness present, but illusions based, as Pribram says, on something that is there; to speculate further would ignore Sri Aurobindo's warning against turning spiritual understandings into religious ones, and the question remains open.
- **Cities of light and halls of learning** — NDEers, yogic adepts, and ayahuasca-using shamans consistently describe vast, brilliantly luminous, architecturally foreign cities (the Sufis named several); Swedenborg described one "of staggering architectural design... the home and the source of the art itself." Whitton's subjects worked in vast halls of learning with libraries and seminar rooms between lives; NDEers report "schools," "libraries," "institutions of higher learning"; eleventh-century Tibetan texts mention learning-cities reachable through "the hidden depths of the mind" — Edwin Bernbaum (Sanskrit scholar, UC Berkeley) believes one such legend inspired Hilton's Shangri-La in Lost Horizon. Moody and Ring report NDEers saying the buildings were literally "built out of knowledge" — perhaps encounters with a living cloud of pure knowledge translated by the mind into a building.
- **Talbot's university dreams (footnote)** — through high school and college he had vivid recurring flying dreams of attending classes on spiritual subjects (the human energy field, reincarnation) at a beautiful otherworldly university, meeting dead acquaintances and souls about to be reborn; several psychically talented acquaintances report the same — Texas clairvoyant Jim Gordon as a child asked his mother why he had to go to school twice, once by day and once at night in his sleep. Monroe and other researchers hold flying dreams are poorly remembered OBEs, making Talbot wonder whether some of us visit these incorporeal schools while alive.
- **George Russell (turn-of-the-century Irish seer and talented OBEer)** — described the first "being of light" he met in the "inner world": a dazzle of light from the heart of a tall figure shaped of "half-transparent or opalescent air," radiant electrical fire centered on the heart, flaming wing-like auras through hair "like living strands of gold," leaving him in "extraordinary lightness, joyousness, or ecstasy."
- **Knock, Ireland, 1879 — Marian vision** — fourteen people saw three glowing, eerily motionless figures (Mary, Joseph, and St. John the Evangelist — identified because he resembled a statue in a nearby village) standing in a meadow by the local church; witnesses could read the lettering on the book St. John held, but when a woman tried to embrace the Virgin her arms closed on empty air: "The figures appeared so full and lifelike I could not understand why my hands could not feel what was so plain and distinct to my sight."
- **Zeitoun, Egypt, 1968–71 — Marian apparitions** — began when two Moslem automobile mechanics saw a luminous Mary on the ledge of the central dome of a Coptic church in a poor Cairo suburb; for three years glowing 3-D images of Mary, Joseph, and the Christ Child appeared weekly, sometimes hovering for as long as six hours, moving and waving at crowds. Holographic traits: always heralded by a brilliant flash of light; at first amorphous, slowly coalescing into human shape; accompanied by doves "formed of pure light" that soared without flapping; as interest waned the figures waned, ending as clouds of luminous fog. Seen by hundreds of thousands and extensively photographed. Grosso: witnesses sound like they are "describing some sort of holographic projection."
- **Michael Grosso, The Final Choice** — concludes such visions are not the historical Mary but psychic holographic projections of the collective unconscious; the apocalyptic warnings at Fatima and Lourdes show the collective unconscious is deeply disturbed by modern science's violent impact on human life and Earth's ecology — our collective dreams warning us of possible self-destruction.
- **D. Scott Rogo — the Zeitoun "psychic blueprint"** — in 1925, while the Zeitoun church was being built, its philanthropist patron dreamed the Virgin told him she would appear there when it was completed; she did not appear then, but the well-known forty-year-old prophecy may have gradually built "a psychic 'blueprint' of the Virgin within the church," an ever-increasing pool of psychic energy that in 1968 "became so high-pitched that an image of the Virgin Mary burst into physical reality." Talbot has offered a similar explanation in earlier writings.
- **The case against extraterrestrial UFOs (four arguments)** — (1) too many sightings — thousands of documented encounters, too many for actual interplanetary visits; (2) occupants are implausibly humanoid — breathing our air, unafraid of earthly viruses, adapted to our gravity and the sun's emissions, showing recognizable facial emotions, speaking our language; (3) they do not behave like visitors — no White House lawn landing; they appear to farmers and stranded motorists, chase jets without attacking, let hundreds see them but make no formal contact; their signature "medical examinations" make no sense for a civilization that could get the data without abductions; (4) they do not behave like physical objects — radar-tracked instant ninety-degree turns at enormous speeds that would rip a physical object apart; changing size, shape, and color; vanishing and appearing (their occupants too). Jacques Vallee (astrophysicist, model for LaCombe in Close Encounters of the Third Kind): "It is the behavior of an image, or a holographic projection."
- **Betty and Barney Hill — the "interrupted journey"** — one of the most thoroughly documented UFO abduction cases; it seems an actual alien contact in all ways except one: the UFO commander wore a Nazi uniform — senseless for genuine aliens, but consistent with a psychological, dream-like event containing obvious symbols and flaws in logic.
- **Surreal UFO cases** — the literature includes UFO entities singing absurd songs or throwing potatoes at witnesses; straightforward abductions that turn into hallucinogenic journeys through Dantesque realities; humanoid aliens shapeshifting into birds, giant insects, and other phantasmagoric creatures.
- **Carl Jung, 1959** — proposed "flying saucers" were a product of the collective human unconscious, a modern myth in the making.
- **Jacques Vallee, Passport to Magonia (1969)** — UFOs are an old phenomenon in a new guise, resembling elves and gnomes of European folklore, medieval angels, and Native American supernatural beings; the absurd behavior of UFO entities matches elves, fairies, Norse gods, and trickster figures. Beneath the archetypes lies one vast, pulsating, intelligent, timeless something that changes appearance to suit culture and era — the phenomenon on which all myths are based. Later Vallee: "an alien form of intelligence of incredible complexity is communicating with us symbolically... [it comes from] other dimensions beyond spacetime; from a multiverse which is all around us."
- **Talbot's "omnijective" proposal (1976 article)** — UFOs are hard to categorize because our picture of reality is fundamentally incorrect; since quantum physics links mind and matter, they are products of the collective psyche that are also quite real — neither subjective nor objective but "omnijective" (he was unaware Corbin had already coined "imaginal" for the same blurred status). Yet they leave physical traces: burnt circles, deep landing impressions, radar tracks, scars and incision marks from their examinations.
- **Ring's imaginal-UFO work** — argues UFO encounters are imaginal experiences akin to NDEs and shamanic journeys — evidence reality is a multilayered, mind-generated hologram; laments that shamanism, ufology, NDEs, and psychedelic research are studied by separate disciplines; has recently obtained a grant for a comparative study of UFO experiencers and NDEers.
- **Peter Rojcewicz (folklorist, Juilliard School, New York)** — concludes UFOs are omnijective; "There exists a continuum of experiences where reality and imagination imperceptibly flow into each other" — further evidence of Bohmian unity; folklorists can no longer treat such phenomena simply as beliefs.
- **Whitley Strieber (author of Communion, famous abductee)** — encounters with UFO beings "may be our first true quantum discovery in the large-scale world: The very act of observing it may be creating it as a concrete actuality, with sense, definition, and a consciousness of its own"; also suggests UFO beings may be what "the force of evolution looks like when it's applied to a conscious mind," and says our universe and the beings' are "spinning each other together" in cosmic communion.
- **Carl Raschke (Department of Religious Studies, University of Denver)** — in the omnijective cosmos the veridical-vs-hallucinatory question is moot; the real question is what ultimate aim these apparitions serve. He holds UFOs are "a holographic materialization from a conjugate dimension of the universe," an interpretation that "must take precedence over the psychic projection hypothesis," which flounders before the vivid, complex, consistent features abductees describe.
- **Talbot's own UFO verdict** — probably no single explanation accounts for all aspects of the phenomenon. The higher vibratory realms no doubt hold countless nonphysical species, and in a holographic universe — where there may be an infinity of realities occupying the same space as our own world — the sheer abundance of sightings ceases to be a sticking point and may instead be evidence of "just how unfathomably abundant with intelligent life the superhologram is"; the spaceless, timeless inner cosmos may be as rich with life as the rain forest and the coral reef, even if the physical cosmos proves an ecological Sahara. Our own holographically organized minds may be sculpting the beings' outward appearance (just as we convert beings of light into religious personages and clouds of pure information into libraries), drawing on our folk memories and mythological unconscious for the symbols — so the notorious "medical examinations" may be only symbolic representations: nonphysical intelligences probing not our bodies but the subtle anatomy of our energy selves, or our very souls.
- **Constantine's flaming cross and the Angels of Mons** — history records Constantine and his soldiers seeing an enormous flaming cross in the sky (Talbot: a psychic exteriorization of the emotions of the army about to Christianize the pagan world); hundreds of WWI British soldiers at Mons, Belgium, saw an immense apparition of Saint George and a squadron of angels during an initially losing battle — both apparent psychic projections. Talbot adds the explanations may not be exclusive: high-pitched collective belief may open a doorway, a "psychic niche," through which extradimensional intelligences can appear.
- **John Wheeler — self-reference cosmology** — the University of Texas physicist cautions that though consciousness lets a subatomic particle pop into existence, we are not the sole agents: we create the particles and hence the universe, but they also create us; each creates the other. Talbot: UFO entities may be archetypes in our collective unconscious while we are archetypes in theirs.
- **Carol Dryer and the spleen (callback)** — her ability to communicate with Talbot's spleen (upset because he had yelled at it) shows other organs possess their own mentality; psychoneuroimmunologists say the same of immune cells, and Bohm of subatomic particles — so some UFO-type phenomena may be projections of these collective mentalities (Harner's dragon beings suggesting a visual manifestation of the DNA molecule's intelligence).
- **The closing question** — the Tibetan Book of the Dead stresses the dreamlike nature of the afterlife (hence "dreamtime"); the Hindu Vedas and yogic texts say the universe is God's dream; Christianity: we are thoughts in the mind of God (Keats: God's "long immortal dream"). But in a holographic universe asking whether God or the collective consciousness of all things is the dreamer is meaningless — the part is the whole. The Kalahari Bushmen: "The dream is dreaming itself."

## Thread
Chapter 8 completes Part III's demolition of space-time as absolute: Chapter 7 argued time is constructed; this chapter argues location is too, using OBEs and NDEs as the experiential evidence that consciousness is nonlocal and that the "subtler levels" Bohm posits are literally visitable. It fuses nearly every thread of the book so far — Pribram's frequency domain, Bohm's implicate order, Whitton's between-life research, Grof's transpersonal realm, Hunt's energy field, Sai Baba's materializations, Neumann's inedia, Jahn and Dunne's consciousness-environment interaction, Loye's parallel time tracks — into the claim that this world and the afterlife are hologram-like constructs differing in degree, not kind. Its closing move (Marian visions, UFOs, the omnijective universe, "who are the beings of light?") deliberately leaves open questions — including whether NDEers' induced hunger for physics knowledge anticipates "some coming confluence between science and the spiritual," which Talbot promises to explore "a little later" in the book.
