# 18

## In one paragraph
Robert M. Pirsig braids the mountain climb with the "first wave of crystallization" of Phaedrus's Quality idea. Phaedrus, who as a student had violently despised esthetics, realizes that keeping Quality undefined wipes out that entire field and places Quality beyond the reach of the analytic process — he is turning the method of rationality against itself in defense of an undefined entity. Forced by his department (accountable only to the "God of Reason") to justify a refusal to define, he mounts a realist proof: subtract Quality from the world and the world functions abnormally, so Quality exists; the residue of that subtraction he names "squareness," and Quality becomes a cleavage term that splits the world cleanly into hip and square, classic and romantic. Meanwhile on the trail Chris falls apart — a staged fall, tears, a claimed hurt ankle, belligerence — and the narrator, ashamed of his own resentment, relays the packs, repacks the weights, and tells Chris he doesn't have to prove anything, which produces a flash of terror he can't explain. The chapter ends in rain: tent up, bouillon and pork and beans, a failed attempt to read Thoreau aloud ("no books can guide us anymore"), and Chris asking what his father thinks about all the time.

## Core claims
- Pirsig (as Phaedrus) claims that by refusing to define Quality, the whole field of esthetics is "wiped out…completely disenfranchised…kaput": if you can't define Quality, there is no way to subordinate it to any intellectual rule, and the estheticians have nothing more to say.
- The narrator says Phaedrus's real purpose was the opposite of what it appeared: he wasn't furthering rational analysis but blocking it — "turning the method of rationality against itself, turning it against his own kind, in defense of an irrational concept, an undefined entity called Quality."
- The book says the church attitude on academic freedom is not that a teacher may blather without accountability, but that accountability must be to the God of Reason, not to the idols of political power; Phaedrus could insult anyone as long as he made sense, and would be struck down only for failing to.
- Pirsig states the bind that drove Phaedrus into philosophy: "Definitions are the foundation of reason. You can't reason without them" — so a refusal to define had to be justified somehow, forcing crystallization beyond the traditional limits of rhetoric.
- Phaedrus argues, on the realist principle, that "a thing exists if a world without it can't function normally"; since a world with Quality subtracted functions abnormally, Quality exists whether it's defined or not.
- Pirsig notes the odd result of the subtraction experiment: purely intellectual pursuits — pure science, mathematics, philosophy, and particularly logic — would be unchanged; only rationality survives a Qualityless world intact.
- Phaedrus concludes the world can function without Quality "but life would be so dull as to be hardly worth living… The term worth is a Quality term."
- Phaedrus defines the enemy attitude as squareness: "Absence of Quality is the essence of squareness" — a presumptive faith that the world runs by laws of reason and man's improvement lies in discovering and applying them.
- Phaedrus claims Quality is a perfect cleavage term: tap it with the analytic knife and the world splits cleanly in two — hip and square, classic and romantic, technological and humanistic — with "no mess."
- Phaedrus turns the attack around: the patient on the table is no longer Quality but analysis itself — Quality is healthy; analysis has something wrong with it that prevents it from seeing the obvious.
- The narrator claims a term that can split a world can also unite it: "A real understanding of Quality captures the System, tames it, and puts it to work for one's own personal use, while leaving one completely free to fulfill his inner destiny."
- The narrator distinguishes himself from Phaedrus: Phaedrus wasn't interested in fusing the classic and romantic worlds — he was after his ghost and just passed through this territory; the narrator intends to stay and cultivate it.
- The narrator observes of the climb that the real goal is not the mountaintop but "putting in good minutes, one after the other" — by that measure, relaying the packs is no extra work.

## Concepts
- **esthetics** — The branch of philosophy concerned with the definition of Quality; its question, "What is meant by beautiful?", goes back to antiquity. Phaedrus recoiled violently from it, hating the idea that Quality should be subordinated to any point of view; keeping Quality undefined wipes the field out.
- **Quality (undefined)** — The entity Phaedrus defends by refusing to define it, thereby placing it "entirely outside the analytic process." He proves it exists (via the realist test) without defining it, and holds that both the hip and square worlds know what it is — the romantic leaves it alone and appreciates it, the classic tries to turn it into intellectual building blocks.
- **crystallization** — The narrator's term for the wave-like growth of Phaedrus's thought once the refusal to define Quality is in place; the "first wave of crystallization outside of rhetoric" is the existence proof, and the wave "rolled ahead" into the two-worlds cleavage insight.
- **realism** — An old philosophic school whose principle Phaedrus borrows: "A thing exists if a world without it can't function normally." Showing that a world without Quality functions abnormally shows Quality exists, defined or not.
- **the Qualityless world** — Phaedrus's thought-experiment world with Quality subtracted: no fine arts, poetry, comedy, sports, flavor, or entertainment; only rationality unchanged. He recognizes its resemblance to Sparta, Communist Russia and satellites, Communist China, Huxley's Brave New World, and Orwell's 1984.
- **squareness** — Phaedrus's name for what characterizes the Qualityless world and its endorsers; "Absence of Quality is the essence of squareness," and in his written definition, "an inability to see quality before it's been intellectually defined, that is, before it gets all chopped up into words." It rests on a faith that the world runs by laws of reason to be discovered and applied to satisfy desires.
- **hip and square** — The vocabulary Phaedrus's Negro artist friends used long before mass media gave it "national white usage": "square" was their word for all that intellectual stuff, and Quality (Soul?) was what they had been talking about all along.
- **cleavage term** — What every intellectual analyst looks for: a term you tap with the analytic knife so "the whole world splits, cleaves, right in two." Quality is such a term — "a tiny, almost unnoticeable fault line; a line of illogic in our concept of the universe" — and it splits hip/square, classic/romantic, technological/humanistic cleanly. The secret: "Hold Quality undefined."
- **analytic knife** — The analyst's cutting instrument (carried over from the earlier Chautauqua); here applied gently to the term Quality to produce the lucky, clean break. Kant, "that master diamond cutter," would have appreciated it.
- **church attitude / God of Reason** — The academic-freedom ethic of Phaedrus's department: a teacher's accountability must be to the God of Reason, not to the idols of political power; insults are irrelevant to truth or falsehood, but not making sense may be struck down "ethically and with gusto."
- **handful of sand** — The recap analogy from earlier Chautauquas: the world of which we're conscious, taken from the endless landscape of awareness; classical, square understanding concerns the piles and grains and the basis for sorting them. Phaedrus's refusal to define Quality was an attempt to break the grip of this sand-sifting mode and find common ground between the classic and romantic worlds.
- **the System** — What a real understanding of Quality doesn't just serve, beat, or escape, but "captures… tames… and puts to work for one's own personal use, while leaving one completely free to fulfill his inner destiny."
- **ego-climbing** — The climbing style (defined in the previous chapter via Chris's YMCA ego-goals training and Phaedrus's account of failing to reach Mount Kailas on a pilgrimage with a holy man) diagnosed in Chris here: "When an ego-climber has an image of himself to protect he naturally lies to protect this image" — hence the staged fall and claimed ankle injury.
- **lateral drift** — The narrator's name for the state between him and Chris: no answers ahead, none behind, "just lateral drift, waiting for something."
- **intellectual suicide** — Phaedrus's own "beginning awareness" of what he was involved in as he wrote the squareness definition — using intellect to disqualify intellect's claim on Quality.
- **the ghost** — What Phaedrus was actually after in pursuing wider meanings of Quality, which "drew him further and further to his end"; the narrator, by contrast, intends only to stay and cultivate the territory Phaedrus opened.
- **Chautauqua** — The narrator's ongoing traveling talk; today's installment fades ("I don't know if I've got any more Chautauqua left in me today") and closes with a single overview recapping the trip's intellectual arc so far.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Phaedrus's student war on esthetics** — As a philosophy student he almost deliberately failed the one esthetics course he attended and wrote papers subjecting the instructor and materials to outrageous attack, including the "peppermint bonbon" passage accusing estheticians of smacking their lips on "the putrescence of something they long ago killed." Shows his rage was not at any particular esthetician but at Quality's subordination to any point of view.
- **The three-point teaching statement** — Phaedrus wrote: (1) every instructor of English composition knows what quality is (an instructor who doesn't should conceal it, since it proves incompetence); (2) any instructor who thinks quality can and should be defined should go ahead and define it; (3) those who feel quality exists but can't be defined can use his classroom comparison methods to teach "pure quality in writing without defining it." He hoped someone would challenge him and try to define Quality; no one ever did — but the parenthetical about incompetence raised eyebrows, since he was the junior member setting standards for seniors.
- **The subtraction thought-experiment (the Qualityless world)** — Phaedrus subtracts Quality from a description of the world: fine arts go first (a bare wall looks as good as a painting; record scratches sound as good as symphonies); poetry disappears (seldom makes sense, no practical value); comedy vanishes (the difference between humor and no humor is pure Quality); sports vanish (scores become "empty statistics, like the number of stones in a pile of gravel"); supermarkets carry only rice, cornmeal, soybeans, flour, ungraded meat, weaning milk, and vitamin/mineral supplements; alcohol, tea, coffee, tobacco, movies, dances, plays, and parties vanish; everyone uses public transportation and wears G.I. shoes; mass unemployment until relocation into "essential non-Quality work"; applied science and technology drastically changed but pure science, mathematics, philosophy, and particularly logic unchanged. Outcome: the world functions abnormally, so Quality exists — and the survival of rationality alone is the odd clue he files away.
- **Real-world matches for the Qualityless world** — Ancient Sparta, Communist Russia and her satellites, Communist China, Huxley's Brave New World, Orwell's 1984 — plus people from his own life, the ones who tried to make him quit smoking, demanding rational reasons and acting superior when he had none: "his own kind. The kind he was now attacking."
- **The Negro artist friends** — Companions on an earlier trip across the United States who had always complained about exactly this Qualitylessness and called it "square," back before mass media gave the word national white usage. Their conversations with Phaedrus had "fantastically mismeshed" because he was a prime example of the squareness they meant; the more he tried to pin them down the vaguer they got. One told him: "Man, will you just please, kindly dig it… and hold up on all those wonderful seven-dollar questions? If you got to ask what is it all the time, you'll never get time to know." Now he wonders: Soul. Quality. The same?
- **Chris's staged fall and the ankle** — On the climb Chris flashes a tormented look, then falls "an awfully neat fall, not very accidental-looking," searches his father for condemnation, and gets none. Offered stop/go/back, he answers "I don't care," so the narrator traps him: "Then since you don't care, we'll keep on going." Chris says the trip is no fun; the narrator snaps back and sees "a sudden flick of fear." Later Chris falls sideways, cries, and claims a hurt ankle without looking at him — the ego-climber lying to protect his self-image. Earlier clues: before leaving, when DeWeese told a neighbor the narrator was experienced in the mountains, Chris showed a big flash of admiration.
- **Relaying the packs / repacking the weights** — The narrator carries both packs in relays, working off his resentment by reframing the goal: not the summit but "putting in good minutes, one after the other," by which measure relaying is no extra work — in fact better. Later he puts all the heavy stuff in his own pack (about forty or forty-five pounds) and the light stuff in Chris's, which visibly improves Chris's mood — "it seems to have solved something for him."
- **"You don't have to prove anything to me"** — At the stream stop, after Chris's tears and "I hate this! I'm sorry I came," the narrator finally says Chris doesn't have to prove anything to him. Result: "A real flash of terror hits his face" and he jerks away. The narrator admits he doesn't know what the situation is — it isn't just YMCA egotism — and names the state between them lateral drift.
- **Breath-per-step equilibrium and the aspen staves** — Climbing technique detail: with the pack, equilibrium settles at one breath per step, two on a rough grade, four at a near-vertical bank where the aspen staves (cut earlier) come in handy against toppling: plant a foot, plant the staff, SWING up, three breaths. When Chris gets cocky (shouting for echoes, throwing rocks), the narrator steps the pace up one-and-a-half times to sober him. He stops when his legs go rubbery around three p.m. — push past that and you pull muscles and the next day is agony.
- **Camp in the rain and the failed Thoreau reading** — On a flat knoll they pitch a shelter of snapped-together Army poncho tent halves over a rope tied between two trees, with machete-cut stakes and a drainage trench, just before the rain. They catch rainwater in pans for chicken-bouillon soup on a Sterno stove, then share pork and beans from opposite sides of the pot. The narrator tries their old ritual of reading advanced books sentence-by-sentence with questions — this time Thoreau — but after half an hour it isn't coming through: the language is wrong for the mountain forest, "tame and cloistered," talking to another time, "just discovering the evils of technology rather than discovering the solution… No books can guide us anymore."
- **The evening exchange** — Chris says, "I like camping with you better than with the Sutherlands"; the narrator answers only, "The circumstances are different." Chris asks what his father thinks about all the time; told "about what it's going to be like for you when you grow up," Chris asks what it will be like — but an ego gleam in his eyes makes the answer come out masked: "I don't know."

## Key quotes
- "By refusing to define Quality he had placed it entirely outside the analytic process." — the first step of the crystallization.
- "He was turning the method of rationality against itself, turning it against his own kind, in defense of an irrational concept, an undefined entity called Quality." — the narrator on what no one saw Phaedrus doing.
- "The church attitude is simply that the accountability must be to the God of Reason, not to the idols of political power." — the department's real ethic of academic freedom.
- "But how the hell do you ever justify, in terms of reason, a refusal to define something? Definitions are the foundation of reason." — the bind that forced Phaedrus into philosophy.
- "A thing exists if a world without it can't function normally. If we can show that a world without Quality functions abnormally, then we have shown that Quality exists, whether it's defined or not." — the realist proof.
- "The term worth is a Quality term. Life would just be living without any values or purpose at all." — the verdict on the Qualityless world.
- "Absence of Quality is the essence of squareness." — the coinage that names the enemy.
- "If you got to ask what is it all the time, you'll never get time to know." — the artist friend's rebuke of Phaedrus's seven-dollar questions.
- "You take your analytic knife, put the point directly on the term Quality and just tap, not hard, gently, and the whole world splits, cleaves, right in two." — Quality as cleavage term.
- "The subject for analysis, the patient on the table, was no longer Quality, but analysis itself." — Phaedrus turns the attack.
- "A real understanding of Quality captures the System, tames it, and puts it to work for one's own personal use, while leaving one completely free to fulfill his inner destiny." — the narrator's own overview.
- "It's just Chris and me and the forest and the rain. No books can guide us anymore." — after Thoreau fails to come through.

## Thread
This chapter delivers the payoff of Chapter 17's classroom crisis: Phaedrus's refusal to define Quality stops being a teaching expedient and becomes a philosophical position — the first wave of crystallization, with the realist existence proof, the naming of squareness, and the discovery that undefined Quality is the perfect cleavage term for the classic/romantic split the whole Chautauqua has been mapping since the Sutherlands and the "handful of sand." It marks the turn the narrator flags explicitly: the patient on the table is now analysis itself, and Phaedrus's justification problem pushes him "beyond the traditional limits of rhetoric and into the domain of philosophy" — setting up the wider meanings of Quality that drew Phaedrus toward his end, which the narrator says he will not follow, choosing instead to cultivate the territory (Quality as the term that can unite the split world). On the narrative track, the climb becomes the ego-climbing parable in action: Chris's staged fall, lies, and terror at "you don't have to prove anything to me" deepen the unexplained trouble between father and son, while the failed Thoreau reading ("no books can guide us anymore") signals they are past the reach of received answers.
