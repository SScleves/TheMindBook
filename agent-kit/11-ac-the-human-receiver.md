# The Human Receiver: Matter, Information, Energy . . . Contact

## In one paragraph
In this chapter (numbered 6 in the book's own chapter sequence) D.W. Pasulka follows the conversion-like transformation of Rey Hernandez — a Miami lawyer and self-described "pure rationalist" atheist — after a 2012 series of anomalous events shared with his devout Catholic wife, Dulce, whose identical encounters she reads as angels while Rey keeps interpreting his. Pasulka traces how Rey's experiences, "directed" reading binges, synchronicities, and a "download" mission led him to cofound FREE (the Dr. Edgar Mitchell Foundation for Research into Extraterrestrial Encounters) with Apollo astronaut Edgar Mitchell, Harvard astrophysicist Rudy Schild, and researcher Mary Rodwell — the first global, multilingual study of contact with nonhuman intelligence. The chapter develops the book's central technological metaphor: many scientist-believers hold that the phenomenon functions like a technology and the human is a receiver and transmitter of information, with quantum physics (Mitchell's quantum hologram theory and dyadic model of consciousness) as the go-to explanatory framework bridging ufology's materialist/subjectivist divide. Pasulka places Mitchell in a lineage of "esoteric cosmonauts" (Tsiolkovsky, Jack Parsons, Tyler D.). The chapter closes with Rey's betrayal by media: his MUFON report was turned, without fidelity, into a History Channel Hangar 1 episode, "UFO Home Invasion" — an object lesson in the book's "mechanisms of belief," whereby documentary-styled media manufacture the public meaning of UFO events.

## Core claims
- Pasulka argues that a UFO sighting often completely changes the direction of a person's life, "much like a religious conversion experience" — Rey Hernandez being her case in point.
- Citing Jacques Vallee, she says there are two types of UFO reports: those reported to "authorities" and those revealed to sympathetic listeners; fear of ridicule keeps many testimonies subterranean, submerged in subcultures that nevertheless grow each year.
- She argues that testimony operates as evidence in both secular (courtroom) and religious contexts, and that in both religious and UFO cultures the "credible witness" lends credibility — if not to the reality of the claims, at least to the fact that credible people experience extraordinary events.
- Pasulka reports that many of the scientist-believers she interviewed think the phenomenon functions like a technology, and that the human is a receiver and transmitter of information.
- She notes that the same event can receive divergent interpretations shaped by prior frameworks: Dulce read the encounters as angels within Catholic theology; Rey, an atheist, is "still interpreting" his.
- According to Rey (as Pasulka reports), FREE's quantum/holographic approach dissolves the dualism of spirit and matter and bridges ufology's rift between "nuts and bolts" materialists and subjectivists focused on experiencer testimony; ufology's old paradigm has revealed little in sixty years and must give way to the "Consciousness Paradigm."
- Rey and his cadre of colleagues — twelve retired physicists, neuroscientists, psychologists, and scientists — employ various methods, including quantum physics, to explain the relationship between consciousness and contact with nonhuman intelligence.
- Pasulka observes (echoing Jung) that some UFOs "are not photogenic": experiencers with cameras in hand inexplicably fail to record events, and the phenomenon "resisted being photographed by Rey" even after he bought high-end equipment.
- She reports Edgar Mitchell's beliefs: that extraterrestrials dismantled US weapons launched into space (because nuclear weapons damaged their environment too), that there are good and bad species of aliens, that some humans are in secret contact with the good ones, and that extraterrestrials left advanced technologies certain scientists can unlock — technologies humanity has already benefited from.
- Per Mitchell (and Tyler), organizations like SETI are not the preferred contact centers; humans themselves — with DNA and cellular receptors working "like mini satellites" — are the best receivers of contact information from nonhuman intelligences.
- Pasulka claims quantum physics has become, in her research circles, the explanatory framework of choice for impossible skills (remote viewing, information downloads, UFOs' appearing/disappearing) — even atheist colleagues entertain quantum theories for the abilities of saints.
- She notes that Vallee's "silencing mechanisms" had been internalized even by her academic colleagues, who, given the chance to quiz Mitchell about extraterrestrials, mostly declined to ask.
- She places Mitchell in a "hidden history of esoteric cosmonauts" — people whose ideas "might appear to be on the fringe, and are," yet "may be on the fringe of our future."
- The Hangar 1 episode supports "a central claim of this book": what one sees on television, in movies, and on the internet does not necessarily convey the actual stories of experiencers, yet the mechanisms of belief provide a convincing viewing framework that forms viewers' opinions, biases, even memories about UFOs.
- She argues that MUFON's database, though built from honest reports, gets misused: reports become MUFON's property (sellable to TV producers), and non-local, untrained researchers taking a "big data" approach lump misidentified blimps, drones, and lightning under "UFO," skewing the data and inflating apparent sightings of truly unknown phenomena.
- Her closing verdict: "The truth is potentially 'out there,' but it's unlikely to be found in media productions."

## Concepts
- **human receiver (receiver/transmitter)** — The chapter's title idea: many scientist-believers think the phenomenon functions like a technology and that the human being is a receiver and transmitter of information; per Mitchell and Tyler, humans with their DNA and cellular receptors "worked like mini satellites" and are the best receivers of contact information from nonhuman intelligences.
- **contact modalities** — Rey's (and Edgar's) term, received in Rey's "spinning wheel" download, for the various anomalous experiences — NDEs, UFO contact, out-of-body experiences — each understood as a way that nonhuman intelligence interacts with humans.
- **quantum hologram theory of physics and consciousness (QHTC)** — Theory developed by Edgar Mitchell holding that information consists of patterns of energy; information–energy packets are given off by matter, and on some level all bodies of matter contain information. Rudy Schild told Rey it explains all the contact modalities; Rey published articles arguing it retires the subjective-versus-materialist conundrum in UFO research.
- **dyadic model of consciousness** — Mitchell's model explaining how remote viewing, telepathy, and mystical experiences are possible: mystical insights are "just information that requires interpretation, not absolute and literal realities"; in samadhi the body/brain resonates with the "zero point field," the Self merging into the primordial field; the accompanying ecstasy is a feeling-signal (an evolutionary component) that the experience should be repeated.
- **quantum non-locality (as macro-scale basis for psi)** — Mitchell's claim that non-locality in quantum physics provides a physical basis for parapsychological effects: brain waves can be synchronized and information transferred between individuals across Faraday cage barriers, not obeying the inverse square rule and not time-dependent — "a macro-scale version of quantum non-locality."
- **Consciousness Paradigm** — Rey's proposed replacement for the ufology paradigm, which "has revealed very little about this phenomenon over the last sixty years"; FREE's mission is to understand the relationship between the science of consciousness and contact with nonhuman intelligence via the contact modalities.
- **disclosure / "Disclosure from the Bottom Up"** — Disclosure is a ufologists' term meaning that UFOs have revealed themselves, or that there is public awareness of their presence; FREE's motto, reflecting Edgar's conviction that study should focus on the experiencer. Mitchell also helped fund the Disclosure Movement — citizens pressing governments to declassify UFO documents.
- **high strangeness** — Term from UFO studies, quoted in the chapter epigraph from Susan Demeter-St. Clair's Ontario cases, for encounters (e.g., a family all hearing an external voice urging them to look at a UFO) marked by profound, anomalous effects.
- **testimony / credible witness** — Testimony is a form of evidence in both secular law (false testimony = perjury) and the history of religions, where traditions are built on believers' reports of miracles and supernatural beings; the "credible witness" — testimony accompanied by the witness's credentials — is an important credibility-lending feature shared by religious and UFO cultures.
- **missing time** — Idea Rey later incorporated to explain the clash between his and Dulce's timelines of the Niña event: that the nonhuman beings had taken his wife and dog, healed them, and returned them.
- **plasma-being / light being** — Rey's coinage for what he saw: not just a "plasma-object" but a being of pure energy plasma, multicolored and translucent, cylindrical without outer edges, "because it did control my mind."
- **"mind control" (during encounters)** — The experiencers' irrational passivity Rey only recognized afterward: full cognizance of everything except the significance of the event — no photos, no alerting neighbors, friends casually leaving an "event of a lifetime."
- **unphotogenic UFOs** — Pasulka's Jungian observation ("Some UFOs are not photogenic"): like Jung's friend who witnessed a UFO in South America and never thought to photograph it, experiencers fail to record events, and the phenomenon actively resists being photographed.
- **calling down (UFOs)** — Practice Rey learned from UFO literature of mentally summoning craft; he tried it on August 25, 2012, gave up after fifteen minutes, and at that moment saw the enormous object.
- **book encounter** — The intensive, seemingly orchestrated reading immersion following an experience: Rey ordered "tons" of UFO books, then pivoted to 200+ books on NDEs and consciousness in four months (up to eighteen hours a day), feeling the education was directed by the nonhuman intelligence; Mitchell had his own book encounter on consciousness after returning from the moon.
- **synchronicities** — Meaningful coincidences Rey experienced as guidance: meeting NDE experiencers just after first reading about NDEs, learning of his father's NDE, and Mary Rodwell answering his months-old email directly after his mission-download.
- **download experience / extraterrestrial telegram** — Rey's rush-hour experience of being inside a large spinning wheel whose spokes were the contact modalities, followed by a telepathic message delivered "not via a voice, but via information," charging him to inform humanity of the relationship between nonhuman intelligence, the spirit world, and consciousness — with two criteria: no money-making and minimal ego.
- **savikalpa samadhi / nirvikalpa samadhi** — Sanskrit terms Mitchell found in the literature of ancient India that exactly matched his space epiphany: seeing things in their separateness while experiencing them viscerally as unity/oneness, accompanied by ecstasy; nirvikalpa samadhi, in the dyadic model, is the Self merging with All-That-Is — the body/brain in resonance with the zero point field.
- **superposition / bilocation** — Pasulka's atheist colleague's off-the-record quantum theorizing about saints reported in two places at once (like Teresa of Avila's levitation reports): superposition suggests atoms and electrons can be in two places at once and remain connected; he noted that in saints' cases bilocation is chiefly associated with acts of charity (via Aquinas, divinely infused).
- **esoteric cosmonauts** — Pasulka's category for the hidden, unofficial history of the space program: cosmonauts and rocket scientists — Tsiolkovsky, Jack Parsons, Tyler D., Edgar Mitchell — who believed in extraterrestrial or nonhuman beings interacting with humans to help them achieve space travel (and, for Edgar, peace on Earth, which he saw as a prerequisite for deep space travel).
- **"nuts and bolts" materialists vs. subjectivists** — The two rival camps of ufology researchers: those focused on physical evidence versus those focused on experiencer testimonies; Rey's holographic/consciousness framework claims to bridge them by making experiences "both physical and subjective."
- **mechanisms of belief** — The book's earlier-established concept, applied here: media forms associated with truth (the documentary genre, the History Channel) present UFO events as real, forming viewers' opinions, biases, and memories regardless of fidelity to experiencers' actual accounts.
- **invisibles** — Pasulka's term (used here in passing) for the hidden members of her research circles, whose shared lexicon of repeated words and phrases about extraterrestrials, the space program, and technology Mitchell also spoke.
- **field investigators** — MUFON-trained volunteers who study anomalous reports on-site; exemplified by the North Carolina state director, who rules out police/military exercises, scrutinizes photos with trained videographers, and only then grants a report the status "UFO," i.e., "unidentified."
- **big data approach (to UFO reports)** — Pasulka's criticism: non-local, untrained researchers lump all database reports (including misidentified blimps, drones, lightning) under "UFO," giving the impression of more truly unknown sightings than is actually the case.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Epigraph case — Susan Demeter-St. Clair's Ontario family** — A family who all acknowledged hearing an external voice urging them to look at a UFO and were profoundly affected; one of dozens of cases she personally investigated in Ontario demonstrating "high strangeness."
- **The Niña healing event (early Sunday morning, March 2012, Hernandez home, Miami)** — The Hernandezes' sixteen-year-old Jack Russell terrier Niña was paralyzed (probable cerebral hemorrhage per a veterinarian friend, who offered to euthanize her Sunday). Dulce prayed for angels to cure her. At ~6 a.m. Dulce saw a metallic upside-down-U object floating four feet off the ground, one foot from the wall, with two ring lights; she knelt and prayed ("If you're a bad spirit, leave…"); green lights began flashing on her "like scanning her." After 10–15 minutes of screaming for Rey, she hauled him out of bed; Rey saw something different — a "plasma-being" two-to-three feet wide, 1.5–2 feet high, cylindrical, pure energy plasma, multicolored, translucent, which removed his peripheral vision. He said "Ah B.S.," went upstairs, and lay in a 15-minute hypnotic state; on "waking," he found Dulce celebrating ("The angels cured her!") and Niña running with puppy energy. The couple's recollections conflicted entirely (Rey: 45 minutes elapsed, per the clock; Dulce: Rey never went upstairs) — Rey later invoked "missing time." Rey notes the dark northwest corner ruled out an optical illusion. Figures 6.1/6.2 show Rey's "red energy being" and Dulce's "energy craft." Pasulka's use: same event, two interpretive frameworks — the event "shattered" the atheist's worldview and confirmed the Catholic's faith.
- **Dulce's subsequent sightings (May 2012 onward)** — A blimp-sized UFO outside the house with "stained glass windows just like our church" all around the craft; in Mexico, further sightings (some with family) including three eight-foot-tall human-looking beings in white monks' robes floating before her. She kept these from Rey, worried about his obsession; to her they are "her angels."
- **The "called down" craft (August 25, 2012, ~9:30 p.m.)** — Waiting outside for a friend, Rey tried "calling down" a UFO; after 15 fruitless minutes he quit ("I am freaking losing it") — and instantly saw an object two to three city blocks long hovering ~5 feet above his neighbor's house with hundreds of swirling white lights. He heard his absent daughter's voice asking to see a UFO; his ten-year-old daughter then ran out and watched. His arriving friends (conservative, college-educated Catholics with wife and 17-year-old daughter) were flabbergasted and rationalized (atmospheric conditions, car lights, lightning); Rey mentally told the beings, "You better come up with something better than this because they don't believe you," and instantly the craft burst into hundreds of stars ten times the size of Venus, flickering. Then everyone behaved irrationally: the friends left mid-event; no one (even the phone-glued teenager) took a picture; Rey never told his wife or the neighbors (who had turned on their light and were watching the watchers). Only when his daughter mentioned there were no mosquitoes did Rey "wake up," realize he'd been under "some type of 'mind control,'" fetch camera and camcorder — and the craft was gone. Pasulka's use: the Jung parallel; some UFOs are not photogenic.
- **Rey's equipment purchase (the Alison Kruse route)** — Large telescope, night-vision CCTV camera with telescope adapters, an old SONY night-vision camcorder, and a high-zoom digital camera — "Next time we are going to prove it." Pasulka: "None of this helped one bit. The phenomenon resisted being photographed by Rey."
- **The directed book encounter and synchronicities (to December 21, 2012)** — The day after the craft encounter, a YouTube video on NDEs and the quantum physics of consciousness redirected Rey from UFO books to 200+ NDE/consciousness books in four months, reading up to eighteen hours a day, neglecting job and family; he felt the curriculum was orchestrated by the nonhuman intelligence. Then synchronicities: meeting NDE experiencers and first hearing of his father's NDE.
- **Dulce's felt theology** — She told Rey he needed to believe in God, distinguishing her belief from her church's: "I can feel God. I feel these spirits in my hands when I pray…" Rey neither accepted nor rejected the Catholic interpretation.
- **The out-of-body mission and the rush-hour download** — An OBE combining visual information and a direct message convinced Rey he had a mission; driving in rush-hour traffic he experienced the spinning wheel of "contact modalities" and received the telepathic mandate (inform humanity of the nonhuman intelligence / spirit world / consciousness relationship; no money, minimal ego).
- **Founding of FREE ("over a three-day period under the guidance of some unknown non-human intelligence")** — Mary Rodwell (claims to have supported over 300,000 experiencers) answered Rey's old email right after the download; she introduced him to Rudy Schild (emeritus professor, Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics; editor-in-chief, Journal of Cosmology), who said the contact modalities are all explained by Mitchell's QHTC and offered to be science adviser; Rey met Edgar Mitchell (who lived nearby) and heard his stories for hours; Mary coined the acronym FREE. FREE undertook the first comprehensive, multilanguage, quantitative and qualitative data-driven study of the topic; motto "Disclosure from the Bottom Up." Rey stressed FREE is not a ufology organization and that many qualified researchers put in hundreds, even thousands of hours.
- **Edgar Mitchell's Roswell testimony (as told to Rey)** — Mitchell grew up in Roswell, NM; worked at a local airport as a teen (paid in flying lessons); his parents owned two farm supply stores. After returning from the moon a hero, old-timers and their children pulled him aside with intimate stories of the crashed ship; based on reliable individuals he'd known most of his life, Mitchell held the Roswell crash was real. He then described his work as a test pilot for the Navy, noting many such test pilots were admitted into the NASA astronaut program, and told of astronauts' and Russian cosmonauts' paranormal "Experiences" in space.
- **Mitchell's samadhi epiphany (returning from the moon, Apollo 14)** — Seeing Earth, Moon, Sun and the 360-degree panorama every two minutes from his cockpit window, he realized the molecules of his body, spacecraft, and partners were "manufactured in some ancient generation of stars" — overwhelming oneness, ecstasy, epiphany ("it wasn't them and us, it was 'that's me'"). Finding nothing in science literature, he found the exact description in the Sanskrit accounts of Savikalpa samadhi. He was one of six moonwalkers on Apollo 14, PhD in aeronautics and astronautics from MIT, raised Southern Baptist; founded the Institute of Noetic Sciences (IONS) and Quantrek.
- **Tyler D.'s post-launch grief** — Parallel to Mitchell's hard reentry to normal life: Tyler described launches as requiring total unity of crew and mission control, the high of success, then the surreal let-down of ordinary life ("How do we just go to the gym?… I would call it a form of grief").
- **Pasulka's Skype interview with Mitchell (California researchers' session, ~90 minutes)** — Pasulka learned beforehand that Mitchell, like her other brilliant interviewees, had been involved with the Stanford Research Institute (SRI), practiced remote viewing, and had conducted secret, non-NASA-sanctioned remote-viewing experiments in space during Apollo. At the session, despite the conference's theme, only Pasulka asked about extraterrestrials (Vallee's silencing mechanisms internalized); colleagues asked about world peace ("He was an astronaut, not a deity!"). Mitchell answered that extraterrestrials had dismantled several US space-launched weapons (nuclear weapons damage their environment too), that good and bad alien species exist, that some people on Earth have secret missions in contact with good ones, and that left-behind technologies have already benefited humanity. Pasulka noted he spoke the invisibles' shared lexicon. Edgar had returned from space with the confidence of one who has been where only twelve humans have ever gone. Mitchell died several months later, on the eve of the anniversary of his moon landing.
- **The bilocation exchange with an atheist colleague** — A colleague who scoffed at UFO cultures but was fascinated by Pasulka's work on levitating/bilocating saints (Teresa of Avila) sent an off-the-record note linking bilocation to quantum superposition, noting saints' bilocation is chiefly associated with charity; citing Aquinas, charity as divinely infused would place the saint "both in the world and not." This jogged Pasulka's memory that both Rey and Tyler said humility (not ego) matters to their "beings."
- **Mitchell's dyadic-model texts** — Quoted passages explaining nirvikalpa samadhi as resonance with the zero point field, ecstasy as evolutionary reward signaling, and Faraday-cage brainwave-synchronization studies as macro-scale quantum non-locality.
- **The Hangar 1 betrayal ("UFO Home Invasion")** — A friend emailed Rey that his family's experience was on the History Channel's Hangar 1. An entire episode was based on his MUFON report — Rey recognized his own handwriting with his attorney letterhead blacked out — but the positive encounter was morphed into a terrifying home invasion by extraterrestrials; the healing of Niña and Dulce's all-night prayers were cut. Rey blamed the production, not MUFON's volunteers ("MUFON is not the issue — the issue is my shock to find out that my story was sold"). Meanwhile FREE's first dataset — over three thousand reports — showed experiencers overwhelmingly reporting positive interactions with nonhuman intelligence.
- **MUFON as institution** — Founded 1969 in the midwestern US; nonprofit; national network of chapters; trains field investigators; hosts conferences; maintains an extensive public database whose reports become MUFON's property (sellable to television production companies); criticized both as "pseudoscientific" and by experiencers whose reports were altered. Counter-example of rigor: the North Carolina state director, whom Pasulka knows, rules out all possible explanations (police/military checks, videographer-vetted photographs) before granting "UFO"/"unidentified" status. Pasulka contrasts this with non-local researchers' data-skewing "big data" approach (blimps, drones, lightning miscounted as UFOs).
- **Hangar 1 website copy** — Quoted marketing text ("a vast archive of over 70,000 files… only by investigating the files of Hangar 1 can we find the truth about UFOs"), which Pasulka calls painful to read for those who have experienced a UFO event.

## Thread
This chapter delivers the book's fullest portrait of the "human receiver" thesis foreshadowed by earlier chapters: after establishing Tyler D. and James as scientist-experiencers, the artifact fieldwork in New Mexico, and Vallee's framework (including the media "mechanisms of belief" and the silencing/ridicule dynamics), Pasulka now shows the receiver model articulated from inside the experiencer community — Rey's conversion arc, FREE's Consciousness Paradigm, and Mitchell's quantum hologram theory and dyadic model, which recast contact, mysticism, remote viewing, and NDEs as information–energy phenomena. It knits Mitchell into the book's running "hidden history of the space program" lineage (Parsons, Tsiolkovsky, Tyler) and reprises the SRI connection shared by her brilliant interviewees. The chapter's back half closes the loop on the mechanisms of belief: the Hangar 1 fabrication demonstrates concretely how media, not experiencers, manufacture the public UFO narrative — setting up the book's concluding reflections on how belief in the phenomenon is formed and mediated. The religious-studies frame (testimony, credible witness, conversion, Dulce's angels vs. Rey's entities) continues Pasulka's core argument that the UFO phenomenon is a religion-in-the-making.
