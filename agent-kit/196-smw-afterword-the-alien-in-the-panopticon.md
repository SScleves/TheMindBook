# The Alien in the Panopticon

## In one paragraph
The Afterword closes the book by proposing Michel Foucault's concept of the panopticon — Jeremy Bentham's 1791 circular-prison design in which an unseen watcher in a central tower can observe every cell at any moment — as the authors' central interpretive lens for the entire alien/UFO phenomenon. The authors argue that alien abduction and UFO surveillance behave exactly like panopticon logic: humanity is watched, experimented on, and possibly "corrected" by an unseen power whose gaze cannot be verified but also cannot be disproven. They extend the metaphor through the Islamic concept of the jinn and Henry Corbin's "imaginal" realm, and through the mythic invisible kingdoms of Shambhala, Agarttha, Thule, and Asgard, to argue that a "bloodline"/genetic selection theme recurs across religious, occult, and abduction narratives alike — a theme they trace uncomfortably back to Theosophy and Nazi eugenics. A long central section applies Foucault's history of sexuality — the shift from ars erotica to scientia sexualis, and his theory of confession — to interpret the sexual content of abduction accounts and the hypnotic-regression process by which they are recovered. The essay ends by rejecting the "benevolent aliens" hypothesis as a dangerous coping fantasy and calling for a planetary, "total war"-style, multidisciplinary defense posture against the Phenomenon, closing on the image of panopticon prisoners banging on their cell walls until the tower falls.

## Core claims
- Tom DeLonge & Peter Levenda argue that Foucault's panopticon — a structure that is simultaneously a prison, a surveillance system, and "a laboratory... to carry out experiments, to alter behaviour, to train or correct individuals" — is a near-exact model for humanity's relationship to the "Others."
- They argue that society's dismissal of the idea that Earth is under alien observation (writing it off as paranoia or pop-conspiracy) has produced a "false sense of security" among those with responsible positions in academia, science, government, and some in the military, leaving a real potential threat unexamined.
- They argue alien abduction accounts — focused overwhelmingly on manipulation of the human body rather than consciousness — are a "ripe field" for Foucauldian/postmodern analysis that has not yet been attempted, and they explicitly frame their discussion as only "suggest[ing] avenues of approach for future research," not a completed study.
- They present two competing interpretations of abductor intent without resolving between them: benevolent "space brothers" running a hybridization program to help humanity survive an environmental catastrophe (also possibly explaining cattle mutilations as a food-source experiment), versus "demonic interlopers" with a domination agenda.
- They argue that even the "benevolent" hybridization theory amounts to a form of genocide, since human genetic and bodily material is being altered "without our permission," and they explicitly compare the rationale ("to save the human race from itself") to the social Darwinist logic used by 1930s–40s eugenicists and Nazis.
- They argue that the pattern of UFO sightings clustering near military bases/nuclear installations and the fact that abductees are relocated and re-abducted "effortlessly" implies the Others possess a surveillance capability so total it must be "coextensive with our own" environment — either a genuine extra dimension or faster-than-light travel/communication approaching "bilocation or quantum nonlocality."
- They argue the Arab/Islamic concept of the jinn — dual-dimensional beings living invisibly alongside humans — and Henry Corbin's term "imaginal" (a real but non-physical domain, not mere imagination or illusion) are the closest prescientific analogues to the alien phenomenon, and that contact experiences are categorically distinct from dreams, hallucination, or schizophrenic delusion.
- They argue mythic "invisible kingdoms" populated by superior beings (Shambhala/Agarttha in Buddhist/Theosophical lore, Thule for the Nazis, Asgard for the Norse) all encode the same requirement: some special quality (genetic, spiritual, or religious) is needed to be admitted among "the elect," and they cite Christianity's doctrine of the resurrection of the dead at the Last Judgment—paradise reserved for "those who followed Christ's teachings"—as a further instance of the same pattern. They read the abduction literature's claim of multigenerational, bloodline-based abduction as the same structure applied to the alien hybrid program.
- They describe the sexuality in abduction accounts as carrying "a strong S & M element," with the human as slave and the Alien as master, and "a strong element of bondage and discipline" in which the human subject is restrained, often paralyzed, and forced through sexual manipulation to orgasm inside the abductors' laboratory setting.
- They describe a "reverse panopticon" response, in which humanity's own proliferation of books, podcasts, and film/TV about UFOs is an attempt to put the Alien in the observed cell rather than the other way around—a stance they call possible "hubris" or a "wish-fulfillment fantasy," likening the exchange of (often unreliable) information among researchers to "prisoners tapping out signals on the bars of their cells," which the Alien in the tower still monitors and can adjust "to reinforce a message of power and superiority."
- They trace the eugenic/bloodline theme historically through Helena Blavatsky's "Aryan race" doctrine, William Q. Judge's 1883 Aryan Theosophical Society, Nazi SS racial-purity policy under Heinrich Himmler, and Imperial Japan's WWII racial ideology toward Koreans, Manchurians, and Chinese, and they explicitly liken concentration-camp medical experimentation (citing Josef Mengele) to the abduction scenario's "we mean you no harm" rationalizations.
- They argue that the historical debate over why Holocaust victims did not resist en masse applies directly to abductees: both groups face an experience so "high strangeness" and outside normal reference points that submission, not resistance, is the only available response.
- Applying Foucault's History of Sexuality framework, they argue abduction accounts represent a shift from ars erotica (pleasure-centered) to scientia sexualis (clinical, reproductive, state/science-controlled) discourse, and that the abduction/hypnosis process reenacts Foucault's history of "confession" — moving from religious confession, to state torture-confession (the "dark twins"), to 20th-century psychotherapeutic confession — noting pointedly that the DSM was created at the request of the US Department of Defense.
- They argue that hypnotic regression, despite being legally unreliable and inadmissible in US courts due to suggestibility/confabulation risk, still has investigative value for serious ufologists, and they draw a direct parallel between abduction narratives and the 1980s "satanic ritual abuse" recovered-memory phenomenon: no organized satanic cults were ever proven to exist, yet the underlying trauma was real, implying "another cause."
- They conclude that treating the Others as presumptively benevolent is an emotionally understandable but strategically dangerous position that "increases our vulnerability," and instead call for a global, "total war"-style, top-down and bottom-up, multidisciplinary defense effort regardless of the Others' true intent — ending on the image that if all the panopticon's prisoners "bang at the same rhythm at the same time," the tower can be brought down.

## Concepts
- **Panopticon** — Jeremy Bentham's 1791 architectural design for an ideal prison/asylum: a circular building with a central tower from which a hidden observer can see into every backlit cell, while inmates can never see or know if they are being watched at any given moment; the book uses it as the master metaphor for human/alien surveillance.
- **Furtive power** (Foucault) — the organizing principle behind the panopticon: power that is never directly seen, only inferred from its effects on the population, compared in the text to the hidden "Wizard of Oz... behind the curtain."
- **Laboratory-and-machine** — Foucault's added observation (quoted in the epigraph) that the panopticon is not only a surveillance prison but also an experimental apparatus "to alter behaviour, to train or correct individuals... to experiment with medicines and monitor their effects," which the authors map directly onto the alien abduction phenomenon.
- **Saucer-as-mandala** — a reference (attributed to Jung) that the classic circular flying-saucer/mothership image is a mandala symbol; the authors note Bentham's circular panopticon design as an architectural equivalent, calling the saucer and the panopticon "two ways of looking at the same Phenomenon."
- **Hybrid program** — the theory that alien-human genetic "hybrids" are being created via the abduction program, possibly to help the human species (and other Earth species, via cattle mutilation) survive an anticipated environmental catastrophe by "tinkering with human genetics."
- **Cattle mutilations** — livestock deaths, focused on the animals' sexual organs, which the authors tentatively connect to the same hybridization/genetic program, framed as possibly securing "a stable food source."
- **Social Darwinism** — the doctrine (used by 1930s–40s eugenicists and Nazis) of "saving the human race from itself" by eliminating elements that inhibit evolutionary progress; the authors note it is "jarring" to posit this sentiment among the alien Others, given how closely the hybrid-program rationale mirrors it.
- **Eugenics / Theosophy link** — the authors' claim that Nazi eugenics can be traced "back to Darwin via a detour into Theosophy," which "spiritualized" Darwinian natural selection so that some races were considered spiritually superior.
- **Jinn** — in Arab/Islamic tradition, spiritual entities described (via Amira El-Zein) as "dual dimensional," able to live and operate in both the manifest, visible world and an invisible domain; belief in them is doctrinally required of Muslims per the Koran. The authors treat the jinn as the closest prescientific parallel to the alien/UFO phenomenon.
- **Imaginal** — a term coined/promoted by French Islamicist and philosopher Henry Corbin (per El-Zein) to describe the jinn's realm: a domain with genuine, if non-physical, existence, categorically distinct from "imagination" or "imaginary" fantasy/illusion.
- **Shambhala** — an invisible mystical city/paradise in Indian and Tibetan (especially Vajrayana Buddhist) tradition, described in the Kalachakra Tantra as destined to one day "open its gates" and send forth its inhabitants to conquer unbelievers; used as a structural parallel to the hidden alien "universe."
- **Agarttha** — paired with Shambhala throughout as another mythic hidden city populated by perfected/supernormal beings, functioning in the text as a further analogue for the concealed alien domain.
- **Kalachakra Tantra** — the Buddhist text cited as the source for the Shambhala prophecy of its gates opening.
- **Thule** — the Nazis' mythic ancestral homeland, cited alongside Shambhala and Asgard as another "invisible kingdom" narrative populated by an elect people.
- **Asgard** — the Norse gods' mythic realm, listed as the third parallel "invisible kingdom" tradition.
- **Royal touch** — the historical belief that anointed English and French monarchs possessed a unique, bloodline-conferred power to heal scrofula and other diseases by laying on of hands; cited as an example of a body/bloodline-based claim to special (near-alien) power intersecting church and state.
- **Aryan Theosophical Society** — founded in 1883 in New York City by William Q. Judge, a follower of Helena Blavatsky; the authors credit Blavatsky's writings and this society with supplying "mystical inspiration and justification" later used by Nazi eugenics and racial theory.
- **Bondage and discipline / S&M framing** — the authors' characterization of abduction sexuality itself: "a strong S & M element" (human as slave, Alien as master) plus "a strong element of bondage and discipline," with the abductee restrained, often paralyzed, and forced to orgasm within the abductors' laboratory setting.
- **Reverse panopticon** — the authors' term for humanity's own response to being watched: the vast output of books, podcasts, TV, and film about UFOs and Aliens, framed as an (possibly hubristic or wish-fulfillment) attempt to observe the Alien on human terms, with researcher information-sharing likened to "prisoners tapping out signals on the bars of their cells" — communications the Alien in the tower still monitors and can adjust.
- **Resurrection of the dead (Christian)** — cited as a third instance, alongside the Nazi genetic and Buddhist spiritual versions, of the "elect" motif: Christian doctrine holds that the dead will rise at the Last Judgment to a heavenly paradise reserved for those who followed Christ's teachings, reinforcing the authors' theory that some special individual quality is required to "see" Shambhala.
- **Scientia sexualis** — Foucault's term (via the authors) for a "science of sexuality" that displaced the older ars erotica: sexuality reframed as the province of doctors, theologians, and the state, oriented around reproduction, legitimacy, and control rather than pleasure.
- **Ars erotica** — Foucault's term for the earlier "erotic art" tradition, which treated sexuality in terms of the pleasure it offered rather than clinical reproductive function; contrasted with scientia sexualis.
- **Confession (Foucauldian)** — per Foucault, the mechanism by which sexuality is extracted and controlled, existing in voluntary form (the Catholic sacrament of penance) and involuntary form (state-extracted confession under torture).
- **Dark twins** — Foucault's term (as used by the authors) for the paired, binary system of torture and confession as mechanisms of state control.
- **DSM (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders)** — described as "the Bible of the psychologist and psychiatrist," noted by the authors to have been "initially... created at the request of the US Department of Defense," which they flag as "an important point to keep in mind" regarding the therapeutic setting in which abduction narratives later emerged.
- **Hypnotic regression** — the technique used by therapists to elicit suppressed abduction memories from the unconscious; the authors describe it as legally problematic (information obtained under hypnosis is inadmissible in US courts) due to suggestibility and possible confabulation between hypnotist and subject.
- **High strangeness** — the quality of an experience being so far outside ordinary human reference points that it defeats attempts to contextualize it or organize resistance to it; applied to both alien abduction and, analogically, to the cargo-cult reaction to modern technology.
- **Cargo cult** — the phenomenon (introduced, per the text, in the "first book of the project") of Pacific Islander societies building religions around encounters with modern technology (e.g., aircraft); used here as an analogy for how "high strangeness" experiences get absorbed into belief systems, and suggested to have been "foundational[ly]" primed by earlier, possibly genuinely alien-linked, paranormal experiences.
- **Total war** — the German WWII-era military-philosophical concept of mobilizing an entire society/state toward a war effort; the authors propose "reengineering" this concept into a planetary, collaborative defense strategy against the alien Phenomenon.
- **Quantum nonlocality / bilocation** — invoked as the kind of physics that would be required for the Others to monitor and appear/disappear across Earth at will if they inhabit another physical location rather than another dimension.
- **Master of the tower** — the authors' working name for the alien/Other in its panopticon role: "unseen but eternally present," holding total informational and observational asymmetry over the human "prisoner."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Bentham's panopticon design (1791)** — described in full architectural detail: circular building, central observation tower, backlit cells on multiple floors, inmates visible to the tower but unable to see it themselves — used as the chapter's controlling image.
- **Repeated/serial abduction cases studied by Hopkins, Mack, Jacobs, and others** — cited as evidence that the same individuals are targeted repeatedly over years, "seemingly effortlessly" found even after relocating, which the authors use to argue for a surveillance capability incompatible with ordinary human notions of tracking.
- **The neutron bomb comparison** — the authors note humans have already developed a weapon that kills people while leaving structures intact, arguing that any hostile alien civilization would have far easier means of destroying humanity than running an elaborate genetic hybridization program — used to argue against a purely exterminationist interpretation of abduction.
- **1930s–40s European/American eugenics and Nazi genocide** — cited as the direct historical analogue for the "for your own good" rationale the authors attribute to hypothetical alien hybridizers.
- **William Q. Judge's Aryan Theosophical Society (New York City, 1883)** — a concrete historical founding cited as the institutional link between Blavatsky's Theosophy and later Nazi racial ideology.
- **SS sexual-purity policy under Heinrich Himmler** — SS officers were forbidden from sexual intercourse with non-Aryans, especially Jews, on the theory that such contact caused "spiritual pollution" regardless of whether a child resulted — cited as an example of bloodline-based purity ideology.
- **Imperial Japan's WWII racial policy in Korea, Manchuria, China, and Southeast Asia** — Koreans enslaved as laborers and sex workers, Chinese subjected to "the full range of inhuman treatment" — cited as a parallel case of racial-superiority ideology alongside Nazi Germany.
- **Josef Mengele and concentration-camp medical experimentation** — cited by name as the historical case of doctors experimenting on captive human subjects under a benevolent-sounding pretext, directly paralleled to the alien abduction scenario's medical/genetic framing.
- **The historical debate over Jewish non-resistance in the Holocaust** — presented as a case study in how bizarre, unprecedented cruelty produces disbelief and compliance rather than resistance, explicitly used as the model for understanding abductee passivity.
- **Amira El-Zein's book on the jinn** — cited directly with two quotations, used as the authors' primary modern scholarly source connecting Islamic cosmology to the Phenomenon.
- **Henry Corbin (1903–1978) and the Eranos conferences** — French philosopher and Islamic scholar credited (via El-Zein) with coining "imaginal"; cited as an authoritative bridge between Islamic mysticism and the book's framework.
- **Talbot Mundy's "The Nine Unknown"** — referenced as a literary example of the "perfected beings... who walk among us" motif shared by Shambhala/Agarttha lore.
- **The "royal touch" of English and French monarchs** — historical belief that coronation-anointed monarchs alone could heal scrofula by laying on of hands; paired with a broader lineage of healing traditions (ancient Sumerian charms against disease, Jesus healing the sick in the New Testament, the Rosicrucians healing without payment) as instances of body/bloodline-linked supernatural power, contrasted with the abduction literature's anatomical focus that is "rarely employed towards the healing of the sick."
- **The 1980s satanic ritual abuse ("recovered memory") panic** — cases where individuals recovered therapist-elicited memories of generational satanic-cult abuse; investigated seriously by police "occult cops," but no organized cult was ever proven to exist, though the underlying trauma was judged real — used as the direct structural parallel to alien abduction memory recovery.
- **The Franklin scandal and the Jimmy Savile affair** — cited as real-world cases of powerful political/corporate/entertainment/military figures involved in human trafficking that proved "notoriously difficult to prosecute," used to pose the rhetorical question of how one would even begin to prosecute non-human abductors.
- **The cargo cult scenario / Pacific Islander encountering a B-52 bomber** — a hypothetical (referencing the project's first volume) of an Indigenous "Stone Age" person's reaction to modern aircraft as an analogy for the "high strangeness" of alien contact, with the added claim that earlier paranormal experiences (e.g., "speaking with dead ancestors") may have primed the islanders for this reaction.
- **The panopticon-prisoner thought experiment** — an extended hypothetical of a person born and raised in a single cell, unaware of the tower's purpose until commanded or extracted for "an experiment," and then briefly shown the tower's view of all cells at once — collapsing from information overload — used to dramatize the epistemic limits of any human account of contact with the Others.

## Key quotes
- "The Panopticon is a royal menagerie... it could be used as a machine to carry out experiments, to alter behaviour, to train or correct individuals. To experiment with medicines and monitor their effects." — the Foucault epigraph that frames the entire essay.
- "the ‘aliens’ behave as if we are laboratory specimens in a panopticon, being observed, being experimented upon, and perhaps even being ‘corrected.’" — the authors' central thesis statement.
- "The abductee is in a cell of the panopticon. The Alien, the Other, is in the control tower—let's call it the master of the tower—unseen but eternally present. There is no sentence reduction, no parole." — describing the abductee's total asymmetry of power.
- "Resistance is futile." — a one-line aside underscoring the abductee's powerlessness, echoing the Alien's "monitor[ing]... to reinforce a message of power and superiority."
- "UFOlogy is not a science but a process of initiation. One starts with field investigations and ends up studying Arab mystics." — quoted (unattributed to a specific author in-text) to introduce the jinn discussion.
- "As spiritual entities, the jinn are considered dual dimensional, with the ability to live and operate in both manifest and invisible domains." — Amira El-Zein, cited on the jinn's dual nature.
- "In the imaginal world bodies become subtle shapes, and spirits like jinn don bodies and transform into visible beings.... It is mistakenly confused with illusion." — El-Zein/Corbin's concept of the imaginal realm.
- "Foucault calls the binary system of torture and confession the ‘dark twins.’" — summarizing Foucault's theory of state-extracted confession.
- "The Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM) is the Bible of the psychologist and psychiatrist and initially was created at the request of the US Department of Defense (an important point to keep in mind)." — a pointed aside on the institutional origins of the diagnostic framework used on abductees.
- "The Alien is indeed in the panopticon. The prisoners are starting to bang on the walls of their cells. If all the prisoners bang at the same rhythm at the same time, the walls of the panopticon will come tumbling down and the prisoners will rush the tower." — the book's closing lines.

## Thread
As the Afterword, this essay functions as the authors' theoretical capstone rather than another case-driven chapter: it steps back from the specific historical/military narrative built across the book's eleven chapters and proposes a single interpretive frame — Foucault's panopticon — through which to read everything that came before (nuclear/missile-base interference, abduction cases, the "war room" geopolitics of disclosure). It explicitly gestures backward to "the first volume of this project" (both its religious/spiritual argument and its cargo-cult opening example), showing continuity with the earlier Sekret Machines book(s). It ends the book not with a resolved answer about alien intent but with a call to action — a "total war" civilizational defense posture — positioning the entire preceding text as a brief for taking the Phenomenon seriously as a matter of national and international security, regardless of whether the Others turn out benevolent or hostile.

## Connections
- Within this book: builds directly on the abduction and disclosure/security material covered in the main chapters (1–11) of *Sekret Machines: War*, and explicitly references "the first pages of the first book of this project" (the cargo-cult opening) as well as "our previous books" on religion, spirituality, and mysticism — indicating this Afterword is meant to be read as the interpretive synthesis of the whole Sekret Machines series to this point.
- Cross-book (speculative/thematic only, not the authors' own reference): the panopticon's "master of the tower" who possesses total information and defines the abductee's entire reality loosely echoes themes explored under a very different framework in *the-simulation-hypothesis* and *my-big-toe* (a higher-order consciousness or system shaping an embedded observer's limited reality) — this is a thematic resonance noted for the knowledge base's own cross-referencing purposes, not a connection DeLonge & Levenda draw themselves.
