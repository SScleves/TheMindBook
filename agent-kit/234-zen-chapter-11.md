# 11

## In one paragraph
The party wakes in a mountain hotel, decides on John's suggested southern route through Red Lodge toward Yellowstone, and climbs the switchbacks of one of the highest paved roads in the world, up past the timberline into snowfields and over the summit at eleven thousand feet — a physical ascent the narrator explicitly uses as an image for "the high country of the mind," the uppermost, most abstract reaches of the hierarchy of knowledge. Interleaved with the climb is the fullest account yet of Phaedrus's intellectual biography: after his expulsion from science he pursued "lateral truths" by drifting, was taken by the drift into the Army and Korea, underwent a turning point there (the radiant wall, the yes-that-means-no picnic, the troopship reading of Northrop's *The Meeting of East and West*), and after two weeks of thinking in a Seattle hotel returned to the university to study philosophy — the discipline he saw as the highest echelon of knowledge. The chapter then stages a detailed exposition of Hume's empiricist demolition of substance and causation and Kant's a priori rescue of it, worked out concretely on the motorcycle they are riding ("the a priori motorcycle"). Kant's self-described "Copernican revolution" is presented as a model for the inversion Phaedrus himself would later perform to resolve the classic/romantic split. The chapter ends with Phaedrus's disappointment turning to anger: Kant's esthetics, the other philosophers, the whole university are pervaded by an ugliness he cannot locate — "It was reason itself that was ugly and there seemed no way to get free."

## Core claims
- The narrator claims there is a "high country of the mind": if all human knowledge is an enormous hierarchic structure, its uppermost reaches are the most general and abstract considerations of all — few people travel there, there is no profit in it, but it has an austere beauty, and one must adjust to "the thinner air of uncertainty" and the enormous magnitude of the questions.
- He claims lateral truths — knowledge arriving from a wholly unexpected direction, not even understood as a direction until it forces itself on one — "point to the falseness of axioms and postulates underlying one's existing system of getting at truth," and that drifting is what one does when looking at lateral truth, since the known methods are themselves what is screwed up.
- The narrator says Phaedrus came to see institutions — schools, churches, governments, political organizations of every sort — as directing thought for ends other than truth: for the perpetuation of their own functions and the control of individuals; his early failure was "a lucky break, an accidental escape from a trap," leaving him trap-wary about institutional truths ever after.
- Pirsig (through the narrator) claims philosophy is broader and more general than science — science is only a branch of philosophy — and that science cannot study scientific method without a bootstrap problem that destroys the validity of its answers; Phaedrus's questions were at a higher level than science goes.
- Against the romantic argument that there is no real progress, the narrator argues it "doesn't hold up": primitive tribes permitted far less individual freedom, ancient wars had less moral justification, technology can dispose of its debris, and the move from the agony of bare primitive existence to modern life "can be soberly described only as upward progress, and the sole agent for this progress is quite clearly reason itself." The romantic condemnation of rationality stems from rationality's very effectiveness — it has all but shut out everything else and now dominates man himself.
- The narrator claims civilizations have varied in the trails they have chosen through the high ranges of thought, yielding many different answers to the same question, "all of which can be thought of as true within their own context."
- Presenting Hume: if all knowledge comes exclusively from the senses, then there is no knowledge of substance (no sensory impression of substance itself exists) and no empirical basis for causation — both are "just something we imagine" — so "Nature" and "Nature's laws" are creations of our own imagination; and Hume made this an airtight case, not idle speculation.
- Presenting Kant: knowledge begins with experience but does not all arise out of experience; a priori aspects of reality (time, space, continuity) are intuitions the mind supplies as it receives sense data, screening what we will accept — thereby skirting "the abyss of solipsism" without abandoning empirical reason. Reality is "a continuous synthesis of elements from a fixed hierarchy of a priori concepts and the ever changing data of the senses."
- The narrator claims Kant's thesis was a "Copernican revolution" — turning the old Aristotelian "blank tablet" passive observer inside out — and that Phaedrus later performed a Copernican inversion of his own, which produced "a resolution of the separate worlds of classical and romantic understanding."
- The narrator claims Northrop's theoretic/esthetic distinction "probably shaped" Phaedrus's later classic/romantic terms more than he ever knew, with a crucial difference: the theoretic/esthetic split is between components of a single world; the classic/romantic split is between two separate worlds.
- The chapter's closing claim: Phaedrus found Kant's ideas about the "beautiful" themselves ugly, an ugliness woven through Kant's whole world, all the philosophers, the university, and himself — "It was reason itself that was ugly."

## Concepts
- **high country of the mind** — The narrator's coinage for the uppermost reaches of the hierarchic structure of human knowledge: the most general, most abstract considerations of all. Few travel there; it demands adjustment to "the thinner air of uncertainty" and to the enormous magnitude of its questions, but it has its own austere beauty that makes the hardship worthwhile for some.
- **lateral truth / lateral knowledge** — Phaedrus's term for truth seen "laterally, out of the corner of your eye" when a whole procedure goes haywire: knowledge that grows sideways rather than forward "like an arrow in flight" — from a wholly unexpected direction, not even understood as a direction until it forces itself upon one. Lateral truths point to the falseness of the axioms and postulates underlying one's existing system of getting at truth.
- **drifting** — What one does when looking at lateral truth: since the known methods and procedures are the very things that are screwed up, no method can uncover the cause; "So he drifted. That was all he could do." Phaedrus's drift took him into the Army and Korea and ended when he entered philosophy.
- **theoretic / esthetic** — Northrop's terms (from *The Meeting of East and West*): a theoretic component of man's existence that is primarily Western (corresponding to Phaedrus's laboratory past) and an esthetic component seen more strongly in the Orient (his Korean past), which never seem to meet. They correspond to what Phaedrus later called the classic and romantic modes — with the difference that the theoretic/esthetic split is between components of a single world, while the classic/romantic split is between two separate worlds.
- **classic and romantic modes of reality** — Phaedrus's later terms, here traced to Northrop: classic reality is primarily theoretic but has its own esthetics; romantic reality is primarily esthetic but has its theory too.
- **undifferentiated aesthetic continuum** — Northrop's phrase, cited by the narrator: that from which the theoretic arises, and of which the book suggests greater cognizance be made.
- **institutional truths** — Truths as directed by institutions (schools, churches, governments, political organizations), which Phaedrus believed tend to direct thought for ends other than truth — for perpetuating their own functions and controlling individuals in their service. He became permanently "trap-wary" of them.
- **empiricism** — The belief that all knowledge is derived exclusively from the senses; Hume's position, of which the scientific method of experimentation is "carefully controlled empiricism," and which the narrator says is today's common sense.
- **substance** — In the Hume exposition: the supposed something "out there" giving off sensory data. Since there is no sensory impression of substance itself, empiricism concludes there is no knowledge of it — it is entirely within our own minds, imagined, like the child's notion the earth is flat.
- **causation** — Likewise, on Hume's account, has no empirical basis in sensation: "just something we imagine when one thing repeatedly follows another," with no real existence in the observed world.
- **a priori** — Kant's term for aspects of reality not supplied immediately by the senses — e.g. time and space — which the mind must supply as "intuitions" while receiving sense data. A priori concepts originate in human nature, neither caused by the sensed object nor creating it, and provide "a kind of screening function" for what sense data we will accept (as when blinking is screened out by the a priori concept that the world has continuity).
- **a priori motorcycle** — The narrator's applied example: the motorcycle we have in our minds, built up over years from enormous amounts of sense data and constantly changing as new data come in — with quick changes (relation to the road), slower ones (gasoline, tire rubber, loosening bolts), and near-permanent ones (paint, bearings, even the frame). "The sense data confirm it but the sense data aren't it." It is "the main thing," believed in the way one believes in money in the bank.
- **Hume motorcycle** — The motorcycle "that makes no sense at all": what the hypothetical senseless bed patient would have if exposed to a motorcycle's sense data for one second only — providing no evidence whatsoever for concepts such as causation.
- **solipsism** — The abyss Hume's path leads to — the idea that the entire world is within one's own mind — which Kant skirts by his a priori doctrine.
- **Copernican revolution (Kant's)** — Kant's name for his thesis that our a priori thoughts are independent of sense data and screen what we see: as Copernicus posed an alternative a priori concept of the world (spherical, moving around the sun) that fit the same sensory data, Kant turned the concept of the knower inside out — the objective world did not change, but our a priori concept of it did.
- **blank tablet** — The old Aristotelian concept of scientific man as a passive observer, which Kant's inversion "truly turn[ed] inside out."
- **hierarchy of knowledge** — The image of all human knowledge as an enormous hierarchic structure, built up century after century by informal and formal processes of hypothesis, experiment and conclusion; philosophy sits at its highest echelon, and the high country of the mind at its uppermost reaches.
- **progress / reason** — The narrator's claim that the ascent from primitive agony to modern life is real upward progress whose "sole agent" is reason itself — the same reason whose all-dominating effectiveness now provokes the romantic complaint, and which Phaedrus at chapter's end finds ugly.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Morning in the mountain hotel** — The narrator wakes in a beautiful old wooden hotel room sensing mountain air, shaves in the common washroom down a creaking corridor, stands on a porch level with the treetops. Chris sleeps in; Sylvia joins them for a heavenly breakfast of eggs, hot cakes and coffee, talking intimately with Chris about school and friends — "So different now from that lonely night in South Dakota."
- **The Red Lodge decision** — Sylvia reports John has heard of another route to Bozeman, south through Yellowstone Park. The narrator recognizes it as the Red Lodge road that "goes way up above the timberline," remembers snowfields in June, warns it will be cold "But just tremendous." John agrees and it is settled.
- **The ride toward the Absarokas** — Beyond a railroad underpass they follow a twisting blacktop and a creek toward its source, water "probably snow less than an hour ago," through green stony fields under the high dark Absaroka Range — a road Phaedrus used all the time, with flashes of his memory everywhere. They play tag with a little blue Porsche, trading beeps and passes through aspen and mountain grass.
- **Phaedrus's backpacking** — He used this route to reach the high country, backpacking in from the road for three, four or five days, coming out for food and heading back in — needing the mountains "in an almost physiological way," because his train of abstractions was so long and involved that only silence and space could hold it straight.
- **Korea: the wall fragment** — From Phaedrus's Army service, a preserved memory-fragment of a wall seen from a ship's prow, "shining radiantly, like a gate of heaven, across a misty harbor." It fits nothing else but is so intense the narrator has returned to it many times; it seems to symbolize a turning point.
- **Korea: the letters** — Phaedrus's letters from Korea "explode with emotion," page after page on marketplaces, shops with sliding glass doors, slate roofs, roads, thatched huts — like a creature "that has found an exit from a cage he did not even know was around him."
- **Korea: the picnic and the yes-that-means-no** — Sitting by a footpath on a windswept hillside overlooking the Yellow Sea, brown full-grown rice in the terrace below, Phaedrus and Korean laborer friends (who traded English lessons for weekend hikes and cultural translation) discuss ideographs. He marvels that everything in the universe can be described by the twenty-six written characters they've been working with; his friends nod yes — and pleasantly say no. He repeats the statement; again the nod meaning yes and the answer no. The fragment ends there, and like the wall he thinks about it many times.
- **The troopship and Northrop** — Alone in the foremost, empty troop compartment on the way home, on a canvas-and-steel bunk (five to a tier), feeling the compartment rise and plunge with only booming steel and stomach-elevator feelings as evidence, he struggles through the hardest book he has ever read: a text on Oriental philosophy — Northrop's *The Meeting of East and West* — which sets out the Western theoretic and Oriental esthetic components of existence and the "undifferentiated aesthetic continuum."
- **Seattle: two weeks of apples** — After discharge, Phaedrus sat in a Seattle hotel room for two whole weeks "eating enormous Washington apples, and thinking," then returned to the university to study philosophy. "His lateral drift was ended. He was actively in pursuit of something now."
- **The climb above Red Lodge** — At Red Lodge they park, put on warm clothing, see wall photographs in a restaurant of the route up "one of the highest paved roads in the world." The narrator feels an irrational anxiety (a memory of stones dropping thousands of feet, somehow associated with cycle and rider) that he talks away. On the switchbacks John and Sylvia appear above them on hairpins, laughing — "It's so hard when contemplated in advance, and so easy when you do it." They stop at a turnout for "record photographs," walk to a cliff edge from which a motorcycle below could hardly be seen; broad-leafed trees give way to stunted pines, then alpine meadows of wildflowers above the timberline — "People spend their entire lives at those lower altitudes without any awareness that this high country exists" — then snowplow banks four, six, twelve feet high, a near-tunnel of snow, and the summit, beyond which lies "another country" of lakes, pines and further snow-covered ranges.
- **The carburetor experiment at eleven thousand feet** — All the way up the engine backfired, sputtered and threatened to quit from lack of oxygen but never did; the narrator deliberately left the carburetors unadjusted "out of curiosity to see what eleven thousand feet of altitude would do." At the summit turnoff, while John gets his camera, the narrator spreads his tool kit on the seat and screwdriver-adjusts the idle from "really bad loping to just slightly bad" — leaving the mixture rich on purpose, because descending toward Yellowstone a lean mixture would overheat the engine, which is dangerous.
- **The four questions of the high country** — Sample questions of the high country of the mind: What is truth and how do you know it when you have it? How do we really know anything — is there an "I," a "soul," which knows, or merely cells coordinating senses? Is reality basically changing or fixed and permanent? When something means something, what's meant by that?
- **The no-real-progress argument, refuted** — The romantic argument (mass warfare, pollution, forced mechanized existence vs. simpler prehistoric life) is stated and rejected: primitive tribes allowed less freedom, ancient wars less moral justification, technology is finding ways to dispose of debris, and schoolbook pictures omit the pain, disease, famine and hard labor of primitive life; reason is the sole agent of a genuine upward progress.
- **Phaedrus the abominable scholar** — Wandering the high country aimlessly, following the trails of Socrates, Aristotle, Newton, Einstein and obscurer names until they grew cold; barely passing academically not from laziness but from thinking too hard ("the harder you think in this high country of the mind the slower you go"); reading "in a scientific way rather than a literary way," testing each sentence, leaving a trunkful of notated volumes in which "almost everything he said years later is contained." The narrator likens watching him to watching someone handle jigsaw pieces of a puzzle whose solution you already know — wanting to say "Don't worry. Keep going!" He prejudged every philosopher, intruded his own views, was never fair, wanted each philosopher to go a certain way and became infuriated when he did not.
- **Phaedrus studying Kant at 3 and 4 a.m.** — A preserved fragment: Phaedrus with the *Critique of Pure Reason*, "studying it as a chess player studies the openings of the tournament masters," testing the line of development against his own judgment, hunting contradictions and incongruities. His respect for Kant rises not from agreement but from appreciation of Kant's "formidable logical fortification" — Kant as a superbly methodical climber of "that great snowy mountain of thought concerning what is in the mind and what is outside the mind."
- **Hume's senseless-child thought experiment** — Suppose a child born devoid of all senses, fed intravenously and kept alive for eighteen years: does this eighteen-year-old have a thought in his head? Hume would answer no — defining empiricism, the doctrine that all knowledge is derived exclusively from the senses.
- **The metal interrogation** — Applying Hume to the machine: "everything I know about this motorcycle comes to me through my senses." Asked what metal is — hard, shiny, cold, deforming without breaking under blows — Hume replies those are all sights and sounds and touch; tell me what metal is apart from these sensations, and "I'm stuck."
- **The two head-tilts** — Looking down left at handle grips, front wheel, map carrier and gas tank yields one pattern of sense data; tilting right yields another (different plane angles, different sunlight). With no logical basis for substance, there is no logical basis for concluding the same motorcycle produced both views — a "real intellectual impasse" where reason defeats its own purpose.
- **The blink example** — When our eyes blink, sense data report the world has disappeared, but the a priori concept that the world has continuity screens this out before it reaches consciousness.
- **The Hume motorcycle** — The senseless bed patient exposed to a motorcycle's sense data for one second only, then deprived again, would hold a "Hume motorcycle" providing no evidence whatsoever for causation. "But, as Kant says, we are not that person."
- **The money-in-the-bank analogy** — The a priori motorcycle is like money in the bank: the bank has no drawer of "my money," only east-west and north-south magnetic domains in iron oxide on a tape in a computer storage bin; yet one is satisfied because the checking system delivers what money enables. Likewise, though sense data have never yielded "substance," one is satisfied that the sense data will keep matching the a priori motorcycle; both "money" and "substance" are said "for the sake of convenience."
- **Copernicus and Kant's inversion** — Copernicus took the existing a priori concept (a flat world fixed in space), posed an alternative (spherical, moving round the sun), and showed both fit the existing sensory data; acceptance of that revolution is what distinguishes modern man from his medieval predecessors. Kant felt he had done the same in metaphysics, turning the Aristotelian blank-tablet observer inside out — and the narrator says this is a rehearsal for the Copernican inversion Phaedrus later performed to resolve classic and romantic understanding.
- **Kant's esthetics and the ugliness** — Kant's metaphysics thrilled Phaedrus at first, then dragged — perhaps, he decided, because of the Oriental experience: having felt "escape from a prison of intellect," this was just more of the prison. Reading Kant's esthetics with disappointment and then anger, he found the ideas about the "beautiful" themselves ugly — an ugliness deep, pervasive, woven into Kant's whole world, into all the philosophers he was reading, the classroom, the textbooks, the whole university, and into himself, with no clue where to attack it.

## Thread
This chapter opens the deep-biography phase of Part II: having established the classic/romantic split, the analytic knife, and scientific method in chapters 8–10 (and Phaedrus's exit from science via the crisis of infinite hypotheses), it now traces how Phaedrus got from that failure to philosophy — lateral drift, Korea, Northrop — and equips the reader with the Hume/Kant apparatus (empiricism, the a priori, the Copernican revolution) that the narrator explicitly flags as preparation "for what Phaedrus did later": his own Copernican inversion resolving classic and romantic understanding, i.e. the Quality metaphysics to come. The physical climb above the timberline is built as a running analogy for the "high country of the mind," and the closing discovery that "reason itself was ugly" sets the problem — the ghost the rest of the book hunts — while the Korean fragments plant the Oriental/esthetic pole against the Western/theoretic prison of intellect. The carburetor episode continues the strand of motorcycle maintenance as lived demonstration of the classic mode working alongside the romantic scenery.
