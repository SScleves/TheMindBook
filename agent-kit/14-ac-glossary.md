# Glossary

## In one paragraph
This short unit is D.W. Pasulka's own glossary for the book: six working definitions in her voice, fixing how the book's key vocabulary is to be read. She defines her human categories — experiencers, the Invisibles, and meta-experiencers (her term for the "scientist-believers" she met through experiencer interviews) — and her preferred label for the object of study, "the phenomenon," which she argues is more accurate than "UFO" because it does not presuppose extraterrestrial origin. She then states her academic frame: "religion" via James Livingston's definition centered on sacred value and transforming power, with the "contact event" as hierophany, and "technology" as applied knowledge revisited through Martin Heidegger's philosophy and her ethnographic research with biotechnologists, drawing on N. Katherine Hayles's picture of human–technology coevolution. The glossary thus compresses the book's central move — treating technology as a form of revelation for specific communities and framing the phenomenon with the vocabulary of religion — into definitional form.

## Core claims
- Pasulka claims "phenomenon" is a more accurate term than "UFO," because in common usage "UFO" is synonymous with "extraterrestrial aircraft," and many researchers featured in the book do not assume the objects are of extraterrestrial origin.
- Pasulka notes that "religion" is a modern term most people take for granted; many cultures do not use it as a meaningful category, since for them religion and cultural life ways permeate one another — but in the West and within the university the term is used, and she adopts James Livingston's definition.
- The book says that within most religions there is a "contact event," usually a "hierophany": the perceived contact between nonhuman, nonanimal intelligence — usually a god, divine being, or revealed knowledge — and humans.
- Pasulka states that in the book, technology and technological discovery are revisited through Martin Heidegger's philosophy and her own ethnographic research involving biotechnologists, and that she utilizes N. Katherine Hayles and others who place technology in a process of coevolution with humans.
- Pasulka argues that the biotechnologists reveal Heidegger's philosophy of technology and the process of "thinking": technology is "revealed," and thus functions as a form of revelation for these specific communities.
- Pasulka reports that meta-experiencers — scientists she encountered during her interviews with experiencers — took an interest in the details of experiencers' sightings and often applied that information to their own work on technologies.

## Concepts
- **Experiencers** — People who have sighted or who believe they are in contact with the phenomenon; they have been called "contactees" or UFO-abductees.
- **The Invisibles** — People whose work requires them to remain invisible, that is, completely removed from social media and the internet.
- **Meta-experiencers** — Scientists Pasulka encountered during her interviews with experiencers; they were interested in the details of the experiencers' sightings and often applied this information to their own work on technologies. She also calls them "scientist-believers."
- **The phenomenon** — Used interchangeably with "UFO," but a more accurate term in that it does not suggest an object of extraterrestrial origin; in common usage "UFO" is synonymous with "extraterrestrial aircraft," an assumption many researchers in the book do not make.
- **Religion** — Following James Livingston: "that system of activities and beliefs directed toward that which is perceived to be of sacred value and transforming power." Pasulka notes it is a modern, Western/university term that many cultures do not use as a meaningful category.
- **Contact event / hierophany** — Present within most religions; usually a hierophany in that it is the perceived contact between nonhuman, nonanimal intelligence (usually a god, divine being, or revealed knowledge) and humans.
- **Technology** — In a basic sense, applied knowledge, or knowledge used in a practical sense for specific purposes; in this book revisited through Heidegger's philosophy and Pasulka's ethnographic research with biotechnologists, and placed (via N. Katherine Hayles and others) in a coevolutionary relationship with humans. For the biotechnologist communities, technology is "revealed" and functions as a form of revelation.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- Pasulka's ethnographic research involving biotechnologists: she says these biotechnologists reveal Heidegger's philosophy of technology and the process of "thinking" — technology is "revealed" to them, functioning as a form of revelation for these specific communities.
- The meta-experiencers ("scientist-believers"): scientists Pasulka met during her interviews with experiencers, who were interested in the details of experiencers' sightings and often applied that information to their own technological work — the empirical basis for her meta-experiencer category.

## Key quotes
- "Experiencers are people who have sighted or who believe they are in contact with the phenomenon." — her definition of the book's central human category.
- "The invisibles are people whose work requires them to remain invisible, that is, completely removed from social media and the internet." — defining the anonymity-bound figures of the book.
- "I also call them 'scientist-believers.'" — her alternative name for meta-experiencers.
- "In common usage, 'UFO' is synonymous with 'extraterrestrial aircraft,' and many of the researchers featured in this book do not assume that the objects are of extraterrestrial origin." — why she prefers "the phenomenon."
- "Religion is that system of activities and beliefs directed toward that which is perceived to be of sacred value and transforming power." — the James Livingston definition she adopts.
- "Within most religions there is the 'contact event,' which is usually a 'hierophany' in that it is the perceived contact between nonhuman, nonanimal intelligence, usually in the form of a god, divine being, or revealed knowledge, and humans." — her frame for contact.
- "In a basic sense technology is applied knowledge, or knowledge used in a practical sense for specific purposes." — her baseline definition of technology.
- "The biotechnologists reveal Heidegger's philosophy of technology and the process of 'thinking' in that technology is 'revealed,' and thus functions as a form of revelation for these specific communities." — technology as revelation, the book's core equation.

## Thread
As the book's closing apparatus, the glossary distills the argument built from the Preface's Silicon Valley tour through the Conclusion (The Artifact): the study of the UFO phenomenon as a religion-in-the-making, populated by experiencers such as the pseudonymous scientist James, Invisibles and meta-experiencers such as the pseudonymous scientist Tyler D., and framed by religious-studies theory (Livingston's definition, the hierophanic contact event) and philosophy of technology (Heidegger, Hayles). It serves as the reader's reference key for the terminology used across the Introduction and the seven chapters, and locks in the terminological choice — "the phenomenon" over "UFO" — that governs the whole book.
