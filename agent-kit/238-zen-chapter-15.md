# 15
## In one paragraph
This chapter closes Part II by splitting the traveling party and delivering the origin story of the entire inquiry. After two days of loafing near an old mining town, John and Sylvia Sutherland say goodbye in Bozeman — Sylvia frightened and reassuring ("It'll be all right with you") — leaving the narrator and Chris alone. While the motorcycle's chain adjuster link is remachined, the narrator walks Chris through streets Phaedrus used as his open-air lecture-preparation academy, then enters the old college building where Phaedrus taught rhetoric. Inside, the narrator feels himself the ghost and Phaedrus the reality; a former student recognizes him, trembles, and nearly calls his quitting teaching "crazy." Behind one more door he finds Phaedrus's old office with Feininger's "Church of the Minorites" print on the wall — "the exact spot" where "the whole madness" started. There, Pirsig recounts the seed crystal: a colleague named Sarah asking "I hope you are teaching Quality to your students," which, like a seed crystal dropped into a supersaturated solution, precipitated within months an enormous, intricate mass of thought — beginning with Phaedrus's four-hour, then all-night, failure to answer "What the hell is Quality?"

## Core claims
- Pirsig claims (through Phaedrus's experience) that rhetoric, as taught, is "the most unprecise, unanalytic, amorphous area in the entire Church of Reason" — to a methodical, laboratory-trained mind, "a huge Sargasso Sea of stagnated logic."
- He claims that the rules of writing are post hoc — "pasted on to the writing after the writing was all done" — and that the writers students are told to mimic actually wrote without rules, "putting down whatever sounded right, then going back to see if it still sounded right and changing it if it didn't."
- He claims that teaching by calculated mimicry of models fails: students seldom got close to the models, and "more often their writing got worse," while every rule was riddled with exceptions, contradictions, and qualifications.
- He claims writing done with calculating premeditation looks poor — "It had a certain syrup, as Gertrude Stein once said, but it didn't pour" — and yet something unpremeditated seems impossible to teach.
- He claims the wrongness Phaedrus felt was not in applying reason to rhetoric but in "the old ghost of his dreams — rationality itself": no writer ever learned to write by the "squarish, by-the-numbers, objective, methodical approach," yet that was all rationality offered, and his mandate in the Church of Reason was to be rational.
- He claims prescriptive rhetoric (correct spelling, punctuation, grammar) is "all table manners," derived not from kindness or decency but from "an egotistic desire to look like gentlemen and ladies" — class identification that in Montana marked one instead as "a stuck-up Eastern ass."
- He claims a single sentence — Sarah's "I hope you are teaching Quality to your students" — acted as the seed crystal from which, "within a matter of a few months," grew "an enormous, intricate, highly structured mass of thought, formed as if by magic."
- He states Quality's central paradox: "you know what it is, yet you don't know what it is" — you cannot say what Quality is apart from the things that have it ("it all goes poof!"), yet for all practical purposes it really does exist, since grades, prices, and preferences all depend on some things being better than others.
- Narratively, he claims that in Phaedrus's old classroom "he is the reality and I am the ghost" — the former self is more real in that place than the present narrator.
- He says the classroom was the one place Phaedrus was "received — as himself. Not as what he could be or should be but as himself," and that it "wasn't one room, this was a thousand rooms," changing with weather, class, and student.

## Concepts
- **Quality** — the chapter's inaugural problem, not yet defined (deliberately): "some things are better than others, that is, they have more quality," but when you try to say what the quality is apart from the things that have it, "it all goes poof! There's nothing to talk about." Yet it must exist — grades and prices are based on it. The question "What is quality in thought and statement?" is where the inquiry begins.
- **Seed crystal** — from Phaedrus's organic-chemistry memory: the small starter (a crystal, a grain of dust, a scratch or tap on the glass) that a supersaturated solution needs to crystallize because "the molecules don't know how" on their own. Used as the metaphor for Sarah's one sentence precipitating the whole mass of thought.
- **Supersaturated solution** — a solution in which the saturation point (at which no more material will dissolve) has been exceeded, possible because saturation rises with temperature; dissolve hot, cool it, and the material may stay dissolved until something starts it. Phaedrus once watched such a solution crystallize before his eyes, "a star of crystalline material" growing "suddenly and radiantly until it filled the entire vessel," so solid the vessel could be turned upside down.
- **Church of Reason** — the university as Phaedrus's institution of rationality (a term used here in passing, previously introduced): rhetoric is its most hopeless corner, and Phaedrus's "clear mandate" within it "was to be rational." Feininger's semiabstract Gothic cathedral print appealed to him because it "seemed to reflect his mind's vision of the Church of Reason."
- **The ghost of rationality** — "the old ghost of his dreams — rationality itself": the wrongness Phaedrus recognized behind the rhetoric text's failure, the same wrongness "that had been troubling him for years, and for which he had no solutions."
- **Post hoc rules** — Phaedrus's conviction that writing rules are "after the fact, instead of prior to the fact": applied to finished writing, not used to produce it.
- **Prescriptive rhetoric** — "the old slap-on-the-fingers-if-your-modifiers-were-caught-dangling stuff": correct spelling, punctuation, grammar — "hundreds of itsy-bitsy rules for itsy-bitsy people," which the book says are table manners of class identification, defended in the department only as a "requirement of the college."
- **Rhetoric as a branch of reason** — the premise of the department's text: if rhetoric is taught at university level it should be taught "as a branch of reason, not as a mystic art," building from elementary logic and stimulus-response theory up to essay development. Phaedrus was fairly content with it for a year but never satisfied.
- **Peripatetic lecture preparation** — Phaedrus "prepared his lectures in the peripatetic manner, using these streets as his academy," walking Bozeman's avenues while thinking.
- **Psychic eddy current** — the narrator's phrase for what the visiting woman leaves behind in the classroom: a "backwash of her presence" that completely modifies the room and dispels what he came to see.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The Sutherlands' departure (Bozeman)** — After two days of loafing, talking, and a ride to an old mining town, John and Sylvia turn back home. Sylvia has been quiet for two days, turning around three times on the road to check on the narrator and Chris, with an apprehensive, "almost frightened" glance. Over a last round of beer the goodbyes are "perfunctory," "like casual acquaintances," which the narrator finds suddenly very sad. In the street Sylvia says, "It'll be all right with you. There's nothing to worry about"; the narrator answers, "Of course." As John waits with the motorcycle started, the narrator adds, "I believe you," and then, as she pulls into traffic, "I'll see you." She waves "as if in a movie" and their motorcycle disappears into out-of-state traffic. Chris says nothing.
- **The morning in town** — Narrator and Chris sit on a park bench marked SENIOR CITIZENS ONLY, get food, and at a filling station have the tire changed and the chain adjuster link replaced; the link must be remachined to fit, so they wait and walk. On a church lawn a transparent-winged bug settles from the heat onto a grass stalk by Chris's foot; the narrator, restless instead of sleepy, proposes walking toward the school.
- **Chris's nickel memory** — Chris remembers riding in the car with his mother looking for his father on these streets: "Whoever saw you first would get a nickel. And then we'd stop and let you in the back of the car and you wouldn't even talk to us." The narrator: "I was thinking hard then." Chris: "That's what Mom said." Shows Phaedrus's absorption and the family's experience of it.
- **Phaedrus's teaching load** — He'd been brought to Bozeman to teach rhetoric, writing, "the second of the three R's": advanced technical-writing courses and sections of freshman English. "The crushing teaching load was bad enough," but worse was his precise, analytic recognition that the subject he taught was "the most unprecise, unanalytic, amorphous area in the entire Church of Reason."
- **The freshman-rhetoric mimicry method** — The standard course: read a little essay, discuss "how the writer has done certain little things to achieve certain little effects," have students write imitative pieces. Phaedrus "tried this over and over again but it never jelled"; students' writing more often got worse. When students asked how a rule applied in special circumstances, his honest answer was that the rule was post hoc — so he abandoned it and simply commented on texts "in an unpremeditated way," which "wasn't satisfactory." Used to show why rhetoric-by-rules fails.
- **Entering the old classroom building** — Summer session; almost no one around. Old dark-brown brick with huge strange gables, stone stairs "cupped by wear from millions of footsteps," creaking wooden stairs smelling "of a hundred years of sweeping and waxing." Chris whispers "It's scary in here" and runs out; the narrator stays alone, feeling "In this place he is the reality and I am the ghost," and watches (in imagination) Phaedrus's hand turn the classroom doorknob.
- **The remembered classroom** — Flaked dark-green chalkboards, only chalk stubs in the trough, windows onto the mountains. Phaedrus would sit by the radiator with a chalk stub, staring at the mountains while students wrote, interrupted by "Do we have to do…?" It was the one place he was received as himself — "not one room… a thousand rooms," different with every storm, class, and student.
- **The unnamed former student** — A woman in her late twenties, aggressive-faced at first, enters expecting to catch someone; recognition turns to astonishment: "Oh, my God… Is it you?" The narrator doesn't recognize her at all. Her ringless hand trembles ("I really am a ghost"). She mentions "We heard you were in the hospital"; her not pursuing it suggests she knows why. Told he's stopped teaching, she protests "Not you!" and twice nearly says "crazy" before catching herself, mortified, then leaves almost at a run. Her "psychic eddy current" modifies the room and ends the visit; the narrator concludes "I'd rather fix motorcycles, and one's waiting."
- **The Feininger print and the office** — Behind one more door, opened "compulsively": a print of Feininger's "Church of the Minorites," which Phaedrus ordered from New York and which DeWeese had frowned at "because it was a print and prints are of art and not art themselves," a distinction Phaedrus didn't then recognize. Its semiabstract Gothic cathedral reflected his vision of the Church of Reason. This identifies the room as Phaedrus's office — "This is the exact spot!" where, looking through a "miserable cramped window" across the valley to the Madison Range, "the whole madness" started.
- **Sarah and the Quality question** — Sarah, "a lady in her final year before retirement," trotting by with her watering pot between corridor and office, says in a "la-de-da, singsong voice": "I hope you are teaching Quality to your students." She returns later: "Are you really teaching Quality this quarter?" — "Definitely!" Days later: "I'm so happy you're teaching Quality this quarter. Hardly anybody is these days." — "Well, I am… I'm definitely making a point of it." Her remark then interrupts his depressed work on lecture notes: "What the hell was she talking about? Quality?" This is named "the seed crystal" — "That was the moment it all started."
- **The laboratory crystallization** — In organic chemistry, Phaedrus once worked with an extremely supersaturated solution; walking to the water tap to cool it, he watched a star of crystalline material appear and grow "suddenly and radiantly until it filled the entire vessel" — the physical analogue for how Sarah's sentence produced, within a few months, "an enormous, intricate, highly structured mass of thought, formed as if by magic."
- **The all-night Quality vigil** — Irritated by the question, Phaedrus sat four hours with his feet on the window ledge staring into a darkening sky; his wife phoned to ask what had happened; he said he'd be home soon, forgot, and at three in the morning "wearily confessed to himself that he didn't have a clue as to what Quality was."
- **The 350-word essay assignment** — Next morning, on three hours' sleep, with lecture notes uncompleted, he wrote on the blackboard: "Write a 350-word essay answering the question, What is quality in thought and statement?" then sat by the radiator thinking about it himself. No one finished in the hour; papers went home; and in the two days before the class met again, students he nodded to between classes gave him "looks of anger and fear in return" — he guessed "they were having the same trouble he was."

## Thread
This is the hinge of the book and the close of Part II. Narratively it strips the trip down to father and son: the Sutherlands — the Romantic counterweights of Part I — exit, and Sylvia's frightened glances foreshadow worry about what lies ahead for the narrator and Chris. The pilgrimage into Phaedrus's building intensifies the ghost motif to its sharpest form yet (the narrator as the ghost, Phaedrus as the reality, a former student who trembles as if seeing the dead) and shows how near "crazy" sits to the surface of others' perception of him. Intellectually the chapter delivers the origin of the Chautauqua's central subject: after Part II's tour of the Classic mode, the analytic knife, scientific method, and the ghost of rationality (with Phaedrus's earlier collision with rationality left unresolved), Sarah's chance sentence crystallizes everything into the single question "What is Quality?" — posed here in its full paradoxical form and left spinning without traction. Everything that follows in Part III (the fight to define or refuse to define Quality within the Church of Reason) grows from this seed crystal; the Feininger print and the DeWeese connection also tie forward to the ongoing stay at the DeWeeses' ("I'm staying with Bob DeWeese for a few days and then heading West").
