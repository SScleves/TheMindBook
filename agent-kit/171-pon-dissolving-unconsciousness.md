# Dissolving Unconsciousness

## In one paragraph
In this chapter Eckhart Tolle turns to the practical work of detecting and dissolving "ordinary unconsciousness" — the habitual background resistance to the present moment that pervades normal life. He argues that bringing consciousness into ordinary, smooth situations grows "presence power," generating an energy field in which no unconsciousness, negativity, discord, or violence can survive, and that dissolving ordinary unconsciousness makes it much easier to deal with deep unconsciousness when its "gravitational pull" is felt. The core method is self-observation: making a habit of monitoring one's mental and emotional state with questions such as "Am I at ease at this moment?" Tolle then catalogs the habitual mind strategies for denying the Now — stress (being "here" but wanting to be "there"), dwelling on the past, worry about an imaginary future, and waiting in both small-scale and large-scale forms. He closes by contrasting these with a qualitatively different, alert kind of "waiting" that is presence itself, and by teaching that the past can only be dealt with — and dissolved — on the level of the present, since "more time cannot free you of time."

## Core claims
- Tolle claims it is essential to bring more consciousness into life in ordinary situations when things are going relatively smoothly; this grows presence power and generates a high-vibrational-frequency energy field in and around you in which no unconsciousness, negativity, discord, or violence can survive, "just as darkness cannot survive in the presence of light."
- He claims that when you learn to be the witness of your thoughts and emotions, you may be surprised to discover the background "static" of ordinary unconsciousness and realize how rarely, if ever, you are truly at ease within yourself.
- The book says this static shows up on the thinking level as judgment, discontent, and mental projection away from the Now, and on the emotional level as an undercurrent of unease, tension, boredom, or nervousness — both aspects of the mind in its habitual resistance mode.
- Tolle claims that anything unconscious dissolves when you shine the light of consciousness on it.
- He claims ordinary unconsciousness is hard to detect initially because it is so normal, but once you know how to dissolve it, dealing with deep unconsciousness's "gravitational pull" becomes much easier.
- Tolle argues you should be at least as interested in what goes on inside you as in what happens outside: "If you get the inside right, the outside will fall into place. Primary reality is within, secondary reality without."
- He claims stress is caused by being "here" but wanting to be "there" — a split that tears you apart inside.
- He argues that thought processes creating guilt, pride, resentment, anger, regret, or self-pity not only reinforce a false sense of self but help accelerate the body's aging process by creating an accumulation of past in the psyche — and invites the reader to verify this by observing people who hold on to the past.
- On worry, Tolle claims that "what if" thoughts mean you are identified with your mind projecting itself into an imaginary future and creating fear; you cannot cope with such a situation because it doesn't exist — it is a "mental phantom."
- He claims you can always cope with the Now but can never cope with the future — nor do you have to: the answer, strength, right action, or resource will be there when you need it, "not before, not after."
- He claims waiting is a state of mind meaning you want the future and reject the present, unconsciously creating inner conflict between the here-and-now (where you don't want to be) and the projected future (where you want to be), which greatly reduces the quality of your life; it is not uncommon for people to spend their whole life waiting to start living.
- Tolle claims gratitude for the present moment and the fullness of life now is true prosperity, which cannot come in the future; conversely, even making millions from a felt condition of lack leaves the inner lack and unfulfillment intact.
- He warns that the false, unhappy self, based on mind identification, "lives on time," knows the present moment is its own death, feels threatened by it, and will do all it can to keep you trapped in time.
- He claims that in the state of alert presence the personality — the "you" with a past and future — is hardly there anymore, yet nothing of value is lost; only then are you truly yourself.
- Tolle argues that delving into the past is a bottomless pit and that believing the future will eventually free you of the past is a delusion: only the present can free you of the past; "More time cannot free you of time."
- He claims the more attention you give to the past, the more you energize it and the more likely you are to make a "self" out of it; instead, watching the past as it appears in present reactions, moods, thoughts, emotions, fears, and desires — nonjudgmentally — dissolves it through the power of your presence.
- He claims: "You cannot find yourself by going into the past. You find yourself by coming into the present."

## Concepts
- **ordinary unconsciousness** — The habitual, normal-seeming background state of resistance to the present moment: judgment, discontent, and mental projection away from the Now on the thinking level; an undercurrent of unease, tension, boredom, or nervousness on the emotional level. Tolle says its habitual strategies are easy to overlook "because they are so much a part of normal living: the background static of perpetual discontent."
- **deep unconsciousness** — A more intense state with a "gravitational pull," which becomes much easier to deal with once one knows how to dissolve ordinary unconsciousness. (The chapter contrasts it with ordinary unconsciousness without elaborating it further.)
- **background static** — Tolle's image for the low-level unease of ordinary unconsciousness that the witness first becomes aware of; the "static" of perpetual discontent underneath normal living.
- **presence / presence power** — The power of your presence is "your consciousness liberated from thought forms" — identified with the power of Now itself. Practicing consciousness in ordinary situations grows presence power, generating a high-frequency energy field in which unconsciousness and negativity cannot survive.
- **the Now / the power of Now** — The present moment, the only thing you ever actually have to deal with in real life; its power is none other than the power of your presence. Only the present can free you of the past.
- **Being** — What one feels the fullness of when present ("Feel the power of this moment and the fullness of Being"); abiding in Being and feeling the fullness of life now is what alone constitutes true prosperity.
- **witnessing / being the witness of your thoughts and emotions** — An essential part of being present; observing thought and emotion rather than being them, which first reveals the background static of ordinary unconsciousness.
- **self-observation / monitoring your mental and emotional state** — The chapter's core habit-practice: frequently asking "Am I at ease at this moment?" or "What's going on inside me at this moment?", then looking inward at thoughts, feelings, and bodily tension rather than answering immediately. With practice this power of self-observation sharpens.
- **resistance to what is / denial of the Now** — The mechanism by which unease, discontent, and tension arise: unnecessary judgment, resisting, avoiding, or denying life by denying the present moment.
- **stress** — Caused by being "here" but wanting to be "there," or being in the present but wanting to be in the future — "a split that tears you apart inside," with the present reduced to a means of getting to the future.
- **mental phantom** — An imaginary future situation created by the mind's projection; it cannot be coped with because it does not exist. Worry ("what if" thoughts) is identification with this projecting mind.
- **waiting (as a state of mind)** — Wanting the future and not wanting the present: "You don't want what you've got, and you want what you haven't got." Every kind of waiting creates unconscious inner conflict between the here-and-now and the projected future, making you lose the present.
- **small-scale waiting** — Tolle's term for everyday waiting: in line at the post office, in a traffic jam, at the airport, for someone to arrive or finish work.
- **large-scale waiting** — Waiting for the next vacation, a better job, the children to grow up, a truly meaningful relationship, success, money, importance, enlightenment — up to spending one's whole life waiting to start living.
- **true prosperity** — Gratitude for the present moment and the fullness of life now; honoring, acknowledging, and fully accepting present reality. It "cannot come in the future"; then, in time, prosperity manifests in various ways.
- **dying to the past** — The instruction to "die to the past every moment": you don't need it; refer to it only when absolutely relevant to the present.
- **false self / mind identification** — The false, unhappy self based on identification with the mind; it "lives on time," knows the present moment is its own death, feels threatened by it, and works to keep you trapped in time. Thought-created guilt, pride, resentment, anger, regret, and self-pity reinforce this false sense of self.
- **the dream of time** — What one awakens out of, into the present, upon recognizing one has been trapped in past or future — which is to say, unconscious.
- **alert presence (presence as a different kind of waiting)** — A qualitatively different waiting requiring total alertness: something could happen at any moment, and all attention is in the Now — none left for daydreaming, thinking, remembering, anticipating; no tension, no fear. "You are present with your whole Being, with every cell of your body."
- **inner energy field** — What one is directed to feel (along with the breath) when coming out of worry into the present moment.
- **primary reality within** — Tolle's dictum that primary reality is within and secondary reality without; getting the inside right makes the outside fall into place.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
This chapter is built around practices (set in the source as capitalized instruction blocks) plus self-diagnostic questions and everyday illustrations:

- **Practice — observe how unease arises**: "OBSERVE THE MANY WAYS IN WHICH UNEASE, discontent, and tension arise within you through unnecessary judgment, resistance to what is, and denial of the Now." Intended effect: anything unconscious dissolves when you shine the light of consciousness on it.
- **Practice — habitual self-monitoring**: "MAKE IT A HABIT TO MONITOR YOUR MENTAL AND EMOTIONAL STATE through self-observation." Ask frequently: "Am I at ease at this moment?" or "What's going on inside me at this moment?" Crucially, don't answer immediately — direct attention inward: What kind of thoughts is your mind producing? What do you feel? Direct attention into the body — is there any tension? On detecting the low-level unease (background static), see in what way you are avoiding, resisting, or denying life by denying the Now. Intended effect: with practice, the power of self-observation sharpens, making it easier to know when you've been trapped in past or future and to awaken out of the dream of time.
- **Practice — "Wherever you are, be there totally"** (section heading functioning as the master instruction): interrogate yourself with the diagnostic questions below and return fully to the present.
- **Diagnostic — stress**: "Are you stressed? Are you so busy getting to the future that the present is reduced to a means of getting there?" Shows stress as the here/there split that tears you apart inside.
- **Diagnostic — the past**: Does the past take up a great deal of your attention — talking and thinking about it positively or negatively: great achievements, adventures, your victim story and dreadful things done to you, or what you did to someone else? Guilt, pride, resentment, anger, regret, or self-pity both reinforce a false self and (Tolle claims) accelerate bodily aging by accumulating past in the psyche. Tolle offers a verification exercise: observe people around you with a strong tendency to hold on to the past.
- **Practice — die to the past every moment**: "DIE TO THE PAST EVERY MOMENT. You don't need it. Only refer to it when it is absolutely relevant to the present. Feel the power of this moment and the fullness of Being. Feel your presence."
- **Diagnostic — worry**: "Are you worried? Do you have many 'what if' thoughts?" — mind projecting into an imaginary future and creating fear; the situation is a mental phantom you cannot cope with because it doesn't exist.
- **Practice — breathing into the Now (antidote to worry)**: "BECOME AWARE OF YOUR BREATHING. Feel the air flowing in and out of your body. Feel your inner energy field." Then ask yourself what "problem" you have right now — not next year, tomorrow, or five minutes from now: "What is wrong with this moment?" Intended effect: stops the "health- and life-corroding insanity" of worry; you can always cope with the Now, and the answer/strength/right action/resource will be there when needed.
- **Diagnostic — habitual waiting, with concrete examples**: Small-scale waiting: in line at the post office, in a traffic jam, at the airport, waiting for someone to arrive or to finish work. Large-scale waiting: for the next vacation, a better job, the children to grow up, a truly meaningful relationship, success, making money, being important, becoming enlightened. Tolle says it is not uncommon for people to spend their whole life waiting to start living.
- **Example — waiting for prosperity**: Many people wait for prosperity, but it cannot come in the future. Full acceptance of and gratitude for present reality is true prosperity, which then in time manifests in various ways. Counter-case: dissatisfaction or anger about present lack may motivate you to become rich, but "even if you do make millions" the inner condition of lack persists — money buys exciting experiences that come and go, leaving an empty feeling and the need for further physical or psychological gratification, because you don't abide in Being.
- **Practice — give up waiting**: "GIVE UP WAITING AS A STATE OF MIND. When you catch yourself slipping into waiting . . . snap out of it. Come into the present moment. Just be, and enjoy being."
- **Example — the "kept you waiting" reply**: Next time somebody says, "Sorry to have kept you waiting," you can reply, "That's all right, I wasn't waiting. I was just standing here enjoying myself — in joy in my self." Illustrates that if you are present there is never any need to wait for anything.
- **Contrast-example — presence as alert waiting**: Presence compared to a qualitatively different waiting requiring total alertness — something could happen at any moment, and if you are not absolutely awake and still, you will miss it; all attention in the Now, none left for daydreaming, thinking, remembering, anticipating; no tension or fear. In that state the personality with past and future is hardly there, yet you are more fully yourself than ever.
- **Practice — deal with the past on the level of the present** (under the section heading "The past cannot survive in your presence"): Rather than delving into the past (a bottomless pit — "There is always more"), give attention to your behavior, reactions, moods, thoughts, emotions, fears, and desires as they occur in the present: "There's the past in you." Watch them nonjudgmentally — not critically or analytically — and you are dealing with the past and dissolving it through the power of your presence. The challenges of the present will bring out whatever you need to know about the unconscious past in you.

## Thread
This chapter sits in Part One (Accessing the Power of Now) as the applied, diagnostic sequel to the earlier teachings on the mind, the Now, and watching the thinker: having established that mind identification and time are the obstacles, Tolle here supplies the day-to-day practice of catching unconsciousness in its ordinary, low-grade form — before it escalates — via habitual self-monitoring. It introduces the key ordinary/deep unconsciousness distinction (dissolving the former builds the presence power needed for the latter's "gravitational pull") and catalogs the specific mind strategies of present-moment denial: stress, past-dwelling, worry, and waiting. Its closing section — dealing with the past only on the level of the present — sets up the book's continuing program of dissolving accumulated past (and prepares the ground for the later relationship and surrender material in Parts Two and Three, where present-moment watching of one's reactions becomes the working method).
