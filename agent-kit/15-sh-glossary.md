# Glossary

Ch 0 = Foreword, Ch 1 = Introduction. Pointers give the chapters whose notes define or substantively develop each concept. Merged names in an entry title are treated as one concept in the notes. All definitions are Virk's own or the book's, never endorsed as established fact.

- **AFK / IRL / in-world** — Gamer states of play Virk maps onto incarnation: "in-world" means inside the simulated world; "IRL" ("in real life") conveys information about the player behind the character rather than the character; "AFK" ("away from keyboard") means logged in but absent, the avatar left in a special state — his analog for the subtle body leaving during sleep. Chapters: 11. Related: subtle body, avatar, the cloud.
- **AI hallucination** — The phenomenon of generative AI making up an answer that sounds reasonable but is false (e.g., fabricated academic citations attributed to real professors in real journals); an ongoing problem. Chapters: 5. Related: large language model, AI identification problem.
- **AI identification problem** — The difficulty of knowing whether text, image, or video was generated by AI; named after the Google Duplex robocall scare, and, Virk says, likely to plague us for years. Chapters: 5. Related: deepfakes, Turing Test.
- **Akashic records** — A repository outside the individual and outside the rendered world storing "not just our karma but the actual events of every single past life"; per Twitchell, soul records of past incarnations on the physical, astral, and causal planes. Chapters: 11. Related: karma, quest manifest, the cloud, subtle body.
- **Al-akhirah / al-dunya** — Islamic terms: al-akhirah is the hereafter (afterlife), al-dunya the here-and-now (physical world); earthly life is a temporary training ground/test for the day of judgment, and belief in the hereafter is one of Islam's six pillars of belief. Chapters: 12. Related: day of qiyamah, base reality.
- **Al ghouriri matau** — The Koran's Arabic term Virk glosses as "the game" or "enjoyable delusion," set alongside the Hindu maya and lila as scripture calling the world a crafted illusion. Chapters: 16. Related: maya, lila.
- **Allegory of the cave** — Plato's image (in *The Republic*, Socrates and Glaucon) of prisoners chained facing a wall who take passing shadows for reality and doubt the freed philosopher who returns; Virk maps cave-and-shadows onto pixels/renderings seen by those inside the simulation. Chapters: 1, 15. Related: maya, base reality.
- **Analog vs. digital** — The continuous view of the world versus the discrete-bits view; Virk frames twentieth-century physics as a migration from analog to digital. Chapters: 9. Related: quantization, pixel, information-based model.
- **Ancestor simulations** — Bostrom's term for detailed simulations that advanced ("posthuman") civilizations run of their own forebears (or people like them) on super-powerful computers; implies creators with DNA similar to ours. Chapters: 6, 15, 16. Related: simulation argument, base reality, posthuman.
- **Angels as functions / daemons** — Virk's reframing of recording and guardian angels: as much functions as entities — a specific purpose plus rules to follow — best modeled by a computer-science *daemon*, a self-running process that monitors and does housekeeping on a machine. Chapters: 12. Related: recording angels, guardian angels, godlike AI, daemon.
- **Animal electricity / bioelectricity** — Galvani's term for the electricity he thought animal tissue relied on (later shown to be ordinary electricity); "bioelectricity" is the modern term for electricity moving through the body, so Virk says Galvani "may have had the last laugh." Chapters: 4. Related: electroceutical, EEG.
- **Artificial general intelligence (AGI) / artificial superintelligence (ASI)** — AGI is human-level general intelligence, long predicted "within decades" and now forecast in a few years; ASI is beyond-human intelligence. Stage 9 requires a form of AGI but not ASI. Chapters: 0, 5, 6, 16. Related: the singularity, Metaverse Turing Test, killer AI.
- **Assembly language** — The native hexadecimal instruction set of a processor (put a value in a register/memory location); very fast, hence used for early graphics, but limited and time-consuming to write. Chapters: 2. Related: compilation / interpretation.
- **Associative information storage** — Vallée's idea that ordering data by space and time is "the worst possible way to store data"; modern computers retrieve associatively, and synchronicity may reveal the universe stores information the same way — "part and parcel of how the universe is assembled." Chapters: 13. Related: synchronicity, technological synchronicity.
- **Augmented reality (AR)** — Like VR but instead of full immersion it shows your physical surroundings and virtually adds things to them (chairs, spiders, snakes on a real table); still requires glasses or a lens. Chapters: 3. Related: virtual reality, mixed reality, the dial-in effect.
- **Autonomy axis** — Virk's second axis for classifying AI: autonomous AI is self-contained and doesn't learn from other instances (Data); synchronized AI is one codebase updated as it learns (ChatGPT — every user addresses the same AI); partially autonomous agents periodically sync with a server. Chapters: 5. Related: embodiment axis, generative agents.
- **Avatar** — The in-world character through which a player is represented; the term was coined by the makers of Lucasfilm's *Habitat* and popularized by Stephenson's *Snow Crash*, customizable by race, profession, gender, and clothing. Chapters: 1, 2, 3, 11. Related: player character, character sheet, metaverse.
- **Bardo / Bardo Thodol** — Tibetan Buddhist term literally "in-between": the intermediate state/resting place for souls between lives; *The Bardo Thodol* (Tibetan Book of the Dead) prepares monks for the transition at death. Virk treats it as the "other side" from which consciousness is downloaded into a body. Chapters: 10, 11. Related: reincarnation, Six Yogas of Naropa, downloadable consciousness.
- **Base reality** — The reality that is not itself a simulation — the "top of the stack" above any nested simulations; Musk put the chance we are in base reality at "one in billions." NDE-ers' "ultimate reality" is base reality in simulation-speak. Chapters: 1, 2, 6, 15, 16. Related: ancestor simulations, simulations within simulations, posthuman.
- **Basic game loop** — The cycle pioneered by text adventures and kept ever since: present the state of world and character → player issues a command → program updates the game state → repeat; Virk notes it mirrors the D&D dungeon master, and that *The Matrix*'s jacked-in rig is the same loop with faster I/O. Chapters: 2, 4. Related: game state, textual input, mind broadcast.
- **Bioprinting / bio-inks** — Printing living tissue with a 3D printer; bio-inks turn live cells (mostly plant-derived) into a material assembled into tissue or scale-model organs. Chapters: 3. Related: 3D printing / physical pixels.
- **Bitmap / raster image** — The complete set of color values for every pixel in an image, in memory or on disk; rarely stored raw today because JPEG/PNG compression avoids storing redundant pixels. Chapters: 9. Related: pixel, image compression, rendering engine.
- **Brahma / dharma (the beyond-illusion)** — What lies beyond maya: in Hinduism, Brahma, "the absolute 'real' world beyond form"; in Buddhism, the dharma, "the absolute basis of reality from which all phenomena emanate." Chapters: 10. Related: maya, nirvana, base reality.
- **Brain as black box** — The premise that if the brain is an electrical machine, any experience can be mimicked by controlling its input signals (from eyes/body) and reading its output. Chapters: 4. Related: mind broadcast, mind reading, brain–computer interface.
- **Brain–computer interface (BCI) / brain–machine interface (BMI)** — Technology reading (and writing) brain signals; the term BCI was defined by UCLA's Jacques Vidal in 1973. Noninvasive BCIs read via EEG without surgery; invasive BCIs surgically place a chip using ECoG signals (e.g., the Utah electrode array; Neuralink's N1). Chapters: 0, 4. Related: EEG, ECoG, N1 implant, neural lace, mind reading.
- **Butterfly effect** — Sensitivity to initial conditions in which a butterfly's wing-flap can cascade into a large effect (a distant tornado); a face of chaos theory. Chapters: 1. Related: chaos theory, computational irreducibility.
- **Caching** — Storing information "nearby" (on the client or a nearby server) to avoid expensive recomputation or internet round-trips; if a simulation renders only what's local and cached data from prior runs persists, "the same scene could run over and over again in a loop" — Virk's mechanism for looping, place-bound ghosts. Chapters: 13. Related: garbage memory, ghost mode, rendering engine.
- **Cellular automata** — Simple grid-based rule systems (central to Wolfram's work) that, with hypergraphs, Wolfram claims can derive fundamental physics; part of the "nature is computation" case. Chapters: 14, 16. Related: simple programs, Wolfram Physics Project, computational irreducibility.
- **CGI (computer-generated imagery)** — Today's film special-effects method, replacing physical models and puppets; unlike AR it need only be rendered once, not in real time. Chapters: 3. Related: motion capture, deepfakes, rendering engine.
- **Chaos theory** — The science (born in the 1960s–70s) recognizing that equations alone can't predict complex systems: tiny input changes cause large differences in outcomes (orbits, weather, populations, biology). Chapters: 1. Related: butterfly effect, computational irreducibility, fractal geometry.
- **Character mesh (3D model)** — A game character's non-rendered 3D model — a polygon "mesh" of (often blue/green) lines outlining the avatar, stored in memory and invisible to others yet indispensable; Virk's analog for the etheric template of the physical body. Chapters: 13. Related: etheric body, rendering engine, Kirlian photography.
- **Character sheet / leveling up** — The RPG record (from D&D onward) of a character's attributes (intelligence, strength), race, and class, updated as the character levels up and persisting across sessions and campaigns. Chapters: 11. Related: avatar, quests and achievements, the cloud.
- **Checksum** — Extra bits added to a transmission (e.g., a count of 1s) that let a receiver verify no errors crept in; Virk's simple picture of how computers detect transmission errors. Chapters: 14. Related: error-correcting codes, information entropy.
- **Classical (old) physics / determinism** — Newton's model: a mechanistic universe that runs independently of observers; knowing starting positions and forces suffices to predict outcomes, and the observer has no effect. Chapters: 7. Related: quantum indeterminacy, subject-object duality.
- **Classical vs. quantum computing / qubits** — Classical computing runs on bits (0/1) via Boolean logic gates; quantum computing uses qubits that hold 0 and 1 in superposition until observed, so a string of qubits takes every value at once — faster for some problems, but qubits "flip," requiring quantum error correction. Chapters: 7, 14, 16. Related: quantum superposition, quantum error correction, quantum entanglement.
- **Clock speed** — The maximum frequency at which a processor performs operations (e.g., 1.7 GHz); every step updates all elements of a simulation, and the logical step need bear no fixed relation to "real" time. Virk proposes the constant speed of light as our universe's clock speed. Chapters: 9. Related: quantized time, universal server time, speed of light.
- **The cloud** — Silicon Valley's term for "a server you cannot see," where character and game-state information persists outside the rendered world across sessions and server instances; Virk's model for where karma and life-records are kept. Chapters: 11. Related: akashic records, quest manifest, state-as-information.
- **CMYK color model** — The cyan/magenta/yellow/black ink model that made photorealistic digital printing possible; the 2D ancestor of 3D printing's material expansion. Chapters: 3. Related: 3D printing / physical pixels.
- **Compilation / interpretation** — The process by which higher-level programming languages are transformed down into assembly to run on a specific machine. Chapters: 2. Related: assembly language.
- **Compression and optimization** — "The history of computer graphics is the history of compression and optimization": techniques (JPEG/MPEG, reducing redundancy, exploiting the eye's limits) that fit rich worlds into limited memory and processing — a recurring bridge to how a simulated universe would economize. Chapters: 7, 8, 14. Related: rendering engine, substantially similar universes, information entropy.
- **Computational irreducibility** — Wolfram's point that for many processes you can't shortcut the answer — you must actually run every step to see the outcome (the three-body problem is the classic case); Virk's core reason simulations must be *run* and why a creator would run our world. Chapters: 1, 4, 12, 14, 15, 16. Related: chaos theory, simple programs, three-body problem.
- **Conditional rendering** — Virk's central bridge: video games render only the part of the world a player is observing, so quantum indeterminacy — the world "not rendered" unless observed — "is a form of conditional rendering for a shared simulated universe." Its subjective form is narrowcasting. Chapters: 1, 2, 7, 13, 14. Related: quantum indeterminacy, rendering engine, narrowcasting, lazy evaluation.
- **Connectome** — A complete map of a brain's neurons and their connections; the digital-afterlife idea of uploading a whole brain relies on capturing it. Chapters: 5. Related: digital afterlife, downloadable consciousness.
- **Conscious agents (Hoffman)** — Donald Hoffman's mathematical framework for how consciousness works, developed after *The Case Against Reality*. Chapters: 14. Related: desktop / VR-headset metaphor, digital physics.
- **Consciousness as information** — The commonality Virk extracts from materialist and mystical camps alike: "consciousness is really a set of information and a processing of that information (which we can call computation)"; makes consciousness portable across substrates. Chapters: 5, 6, 10, 11. Related: substrate independence, downloadable consciousness, digital vs. spiritual consciousness.
- **Conservation of information** — A modern physics principle Virk contrasts with the classical conservation laws (energy, momentum): physicists now ask how much information the world holds and whether it can be destroyed. Chapters: 16. Related: it from bit, digital physics, information entropy.
- **Conversation trees / branching** — The old technique of pre-programming an NPC's entire dialog so you must ask the right question to get the clue; the "dumb NPC" precursor to LLM-driven characters. Chapters: 5. Related: dumb vs. smart NPCs, non-player character.
- **Copenhagen interpretation** — The dominant reading of quantum mechanics (Born, Heisenberg, Bohr): probabilities are real and are collapsed by observation into a single outcome — contrasted with the many-worlds interpretation. Chapters: 7, 8. Related: quantum probability wave, many-worlds interpretation, Schrödinger's cat.
- **Core NDE experience** — Moody's common elements of the near-death experience: peace/painlessness, an out-of-body experience, a tunnel toward light, beings of light, a life review, a life decision at a barrier, and a return to the body — not all reported by everyone but common enough to be "the core." Chapters: 12. Related: near-death experience, life review, out-of-body experience.
- **Cortical stack / sleeves** — *Altered Carbon*'s fictional device that downloads consciousness at any time onto a "stack" movable into a new body ("sleeve"), backed up or beamed faster-than-light — sci-fi shorthand for downloadable consciousness. Chapters: 5. Related: downloadable consciousness, digital immortality.
- **Creation by spoken command** — All three Western religions have God speak the world into being ("Let there be light"); once pure divine magic, it now mirrors the holodeck and generative AI building 3D worlds from verbal commands. Chapters: 12. Related: holodeck, generative agents, computational irreducibility.
- **Daemon** — A computer-science self-running process that accomplishes a purpose, monitors other programs, and does housekeeping on a machine; Virk's model for how angel-programs (watchers outside the rendered world) could operate. Chapters: 10, 12. Related: angels as functions, godlike AI, server epochs.
- **Day of qiyamah** — Islam's day of judgment: an angel blows a trumpet, mountains are razed, and the dead are resurrected and judged before Allah on their good and bad deeds. Chapters: 12. Related: scroll of deeds, life review, al-akhirah.
- **De Sitter space** — The scaled-down "toy universe" the Institute for Advanced Study researchers used to show quantum error-correcting codes may define the fabric of space-time. Chapters: 14. Related: quantum error correction, error-correcting codes.
- **Deepfakes / AI image and video generation** — AI-generated realistic media of people saying or doing things they never did (Midjourney, OpenAI's Sora); convincing but not yet real-time and with frame-level flaws (hands with too many fingers). Chapters: 3. Related: CGI, AI identification problem, generative AI.
- **Déjà vu** — The sense of having seen something before; per Philip K. Dick a clue that "some variable was changed — reprogrammed" and a world branched off; depicted in *The Matrix*'s repeating cat. Chapters: 13. Related: glitch in the Matrix, many-worlds interpretation, synchronicity.
- **Delayed-choice experiment** — Wheeler's extension of the double-slit test, placing the detector *after* the particle passes the slits but *before* the screen, to ask whether the wave/particle "decision" is made at the slits or at measurement; read by some as retrocausality. Chapters: 8, 14. Related: double-slit experiment, retrocausality, multiple possible pasts.
- **The dial-in effect** — The ability (Apple Vision Pro, Meta Quest) to dial the real world in or out "like a dimmer switch," sliding between AR and VR — which Virk uses to question what really separates a "real" object from an overlaid one. Chapters: 3. Related: augmented reality, mixed reality, spatial computing.
- **Digital afterlife** — Uploading the whole of a person's brain (rather than relying on their writings), a popular topic among Silicon Valley technorati and its own cyberpunk subgenre. Chapters: 5. Related: digital immortality, connectome, griefbots.
- **Digital immortality** — Downloading human consciousness from biological hardware to silicon (or the griefbot-style continuation of a person via AI trained on their output) to extend life indefinitely. Chapters: 5. Related: downloadable consciousness, griefbots, cortical stack.
- **Digital physics** — The class of arguments (and, more controversially, the claim) that the proper subject of physics is information — that the universe *is* computing information. Chapters: 14, 15, 16. Related: it from bit, information-based model, second law of infodynamics.
- **Digital vs. spiritual consciousness** — The materialist view roots consciousness in reproducible physical processes; Eastern mystical and Western religious views hold consciousness exists independently outside the body, entering near birth and leaving at death. Virk's synthesis: both reduce to consciousness-as-information. Chapters: 6. Related: consciousness as information, substrate independence.
- **Double-slit experiment** — The canonical demonstration of particle-wave duality and observation-dependent collapse; a template for "evidence of conditional rendering," especially variants requiring an actual observer rather than a mere measuring device. Chapters: 7, 8, 14. Related: particle-wave duality, conditional rendering, delayed-choice experiment.
- **Downloadable consciousness** — Transferring consciousness from the "biological machine" (brain) to a "silicon machine" (computer) — "beaming bits from one device to the next"; Stage 10 of the road map, and, mystics argue, how we entered the body at birth in the first place. Chapters: 1, 2, 5, 6, 10, 13, 15. Related: consciousness as information, digital immortality, secret seventh yoga, life selection.
- **Doubly-even self-dual linear binary error-correcting block codes (Gates)** — Error-correcting codes of the kind used in computer transmissions that physicist James Gates found "quite unexpectedly" embedded within the equations of supersymmetry. Chapters: 14. Related: error-correcting codes, checksum, digital physics.
- **Dream within a dream / nested realities** — Waking inside a dream and thinking you've reached physical reality, showing "we can have multiple nested realities that our minds are not capable of distinguishing" while inside them — linked to simulations within simulations. Chapters: 10. Related: simulations within simulations, lucid dreaming, dream yoga.
- **Dream yoga (milam)** — Tibetan practices for recognizing the dreamlike, illusory nature of the physical world by maintaining consciousness from waking through the hypnagogic state into dreaming; unlike lucid dreaming its aim is spiritual — realizing that neither dreams *nor the physical world* are real, carried through sleep and into death. Chapters: 1, 10, 15. Related: lucid dreaming, Six Yogas of Naropa, maya.
- **Dreamtime** — The Aboriginal Australian "real world" beyond the physical, with which dreaming is a way to communicate. Chapters: 10. Related: non-ordinary reality, shared dream.
- **Dumb NPCs vs. smart NPCs** — Virk's labels for old characters limited to predefined branching dialog versus today's LLM-powered characters you can converse with about anything. Chapters: 2, 5. Related: non-player character, conversation trees, large language model.
- **Easy problem of consciousness** — Finding the material neural correlates of particular thoughts — on which science has made progress — as opposed to the hard problem. Chapters: 4. Related: hard problem of consciousness, neural correlates.
- **EEG (electroencephalogram)** — The technology, built on Hans Berger's 1924 discovery of brain electrical signals, used to read brain waves noninvasively (alpha/beta/theta/delta states). Chapters: 4. Related: brain–computer interface, alpha/beta/theta brain waves.
- **Electroceutical** — The modern revival of the 19th-century practice of treating conditions with electrical stimulation. Chapters: 4. Related: bioelectricity, EEG.
- **Electromagnetic field / Maxwell's equations** — The phenomenon studied by Faraday and Maxwell whose equations described something new — the only real challenge to classical mechanics before the twentieth century. Chapters: 7. Related: classical physics, photons.
- **Embodiment axis** — Virk's first axis for classifying AI depictions: from fully embodied (robots/androids) through partially embodied (part of a ship) and virtual (a body projected into a virtual world or hologram) to fully disembodied (pure software). Chapters: 5. Related: autonomy axis, virtual influencer.
- **Ensemble interpretation** — The statistical reading of quantum probability (100 boxed cats → 50 dead, 50 alive), which Goswami distinguishes from true superposition. Chapters: 7. Related: Schrödinger's cat, quantum superposition, Copenhagen interpretation.
- **Epistemology** — The study of how we know things; Virk argues the West wrongly treats only repeatable-experiment knowledge as valid, dismissing most human experience as subjective. Chapters: 14. Related: evidence of computation, near-death experience.
- **Error-correcting codes** — Codes that detect and fix errors in transmissions; central to Virk's "evidence of computation" (Gates's supersymmetry codes, quantum error correction, and the checksum idea). Chapters: 14. Related: checksum, quantum error correction, doubly-even self-dual codes.
- **Etheric body / astral body / causal body** — In many traditions the physical body is nested in subtler bodies: an etheric template (a mesh of light-blue lines defining the cells' shape/vitality), an astral body (which separates during an OBE or at death), and a causal body beyond it — the occult basis of astral travel. Chapters: 13. Related: koshas / three bodies, subtle body, character mesh, out-of-body experience.
- **Evaluation function** — The scoring function that ranks projected futures in a game AI; simple in chess, but hard to define for a shared online simulation, where "score" depends on the game's nature and may differ per player. Chapters: 8. Related: minimax algorithm, profitability function, Fundamental Process.
- **Evidence of computation (four categories)** — Virk's taxonomy of simulation evidence: *evidence of consciousness* (the skeptic's objection that we'd be simulated-only), *evidence of negation* (arguments that the resources needed are impossibly large), *evidence of conditional rendering* (signs the universe renders only what's observed), and *evidence of pixels/computation* (searching for the universe's coding artifacts). Chapters: 14. Related: conditional rendering, digital physics, lattice, GZK limit.
- **False memories** — Memories of events that never happened, shown to be inducible by suggestion in people and, in mice, by electrical stimulation — the basis for Virk's Stage 8, implanted memories. Chapters: 4. Related: implanted memories.
- **Fermi paradox** — Fermi's puzzle: given the galaxy's age, if alien life were common, aliens should have colonized or visited most of it — "so where are all the aliens?" A galaxy of procedurally generated, largely unvisited dead worlds, Virk argues, "might actually make the simulation hypothesis more likely." Chapters: 13. Related: procedural generation, UFOs / UAP, the grays.
- **Field of view** — The player's perspective in a first-person game; *Doom*'s key trick was adjusting the 3D scene almost instantly to it, since rendering every pixel in real time was then impossible. Chapters: 7. Related: rendering engine, conditional rendering.
- **Film projector metaphor for maya** — Yogananda's 1920s update of the ancient "illusion" metaphor into terms 20th-century Americans could grasp — Virk's exemplar of mystics re-expressing maya in each era's newest technology. Chapters: 12. Related: maya, techno-scientific metaphors, mystics' translation problem.
- **First-person perspective + 3D modeling** — The stage-3 leap: laying out worlds as 3D models and placing the player's character at a point in them, rendering only what's visible from where the character looks. Chapters: 2. Related: rendering engine, MMORPG, virtual camera.
- **Fractal geometry / fractals** — Never-ending, infinitely complex, self-similar patterns generated by repeating a simple process in a feedback loop; they reproduce coastlines, trees, rivers, and neurons better than Euclidean geometry — the "geometry of nature." Chapters: 1, 2, 14. Related: recursion, procedural generation, simple programs.
- **Fundamental Process (Campbell)** — From *My Big TOE*: the process driving evolution at every scale — an entity spreads its potential into all available possibilities and, by trial and error, keeps only the immediately "profitable" states, discarding the rest; recursive and running from the subatomic to the universe. Chapters: 8, 16. Related: profitability function, evaluation function, many-worlds interpretation.
- **Game state / player game state / world game state** — The state of a game captured as information: player metadata/artifacts/location (player state) and the world altered by player actions (world state); held in memory, then savable to disk, then to cloud servers — and, generalized, "the state of all particles in the current physical universe." Chapters: 2, 8. Related: state-as-information, persistent world, the cloud.
- **Gan Eden / Olam Ha-Ba** — Names for the Jewish heaven (Garden of Eden / the World to Come); Judaism details the afterlife less than Christianity or Islam, and the concepts are debated among scholars. Chapters: 12. Related: purgatory / Hades / Gehinnom, book of life.
- **Garbage memory** — Old graphics data left in memory after a scene update — "orphaned data that isn't supposed to be used" until reused; Virk's mechanism for accidental ghost artifacts. Chapters: 13. Related: caching, ghost mode.
- **Generative agents** — The 2023 Google/Stanford architecture combining LLMs with modules for goal-setting, memory, and self-reflection to make NPCs more believable — a possible road map to realistic virtual characters. Chapters: 5. Related: large language model, third wave of AI, smart NPCs.
- **Ghost mode / reduced avatar** — Video-game modes that let you be in a scene unseen — a transparent or single-pixel "reduced" avatar, "a 'visitor'... consciously controlled by another player"; Virk's mechanism for intentional apparitions (often "unfinished business"). Chapters: 13. Related: garbage memory, caching, downloaded consciousness, narrowcasting.
- **Glitch in the Matrix / glitches in the simulation** — Virk's informal name (from his book *Treasure Hunt*) for individual synchronicities, and, broadly, everyday attempts to spot simulation artifacts (objects vanishing, people appearing) — though real detection would be subtler than "Error: sky not found." Chapters: 10, 13, 14. Related: synchronicity, déjà vu, evidence of computation.
- **Godlike AI** — An AI omniscient about everything in the world by various rules — "a server with a very intelligent program keeping track of everything"; what a God handling billions of prayers and deed-records would require. Chapters: 12. Related: angels as functions, daemon, recording angels.
- **GPT (generative pretrained transformers)** — Technology that predicts the best "next word," chaining correct guesses into coherent sentences and pages — like solving SAT sentence-completion problems at scale. Chapters: 5. Related: large language model, natural language processing.
- **GPU vs. CPU** — Graphics processing units exist "plain and simple" to speed up rendering and moving pixels, because CPUs aren't optimized for it. Chapters: 7. Related: rendering engine, clock speed.
- **Grandfather paradox** — The time-travel objection (kill your grandfather and you'd never exist to do it), which the multiverse resolves by spawning a new timeline in which the change occurs. Chapters: 8. Related: multiverse, many-worlds interpretation, retrocausality.
- **The grays** — The most popular pop-culture conception of extraterrestrials (from *The X-Files* to *Close Encounters*), a candidate identity for "superusers." Chapters: 15. Related: UFOs / UAP, superusers.
- **The Great Simulation** — Virk's umbrella term for the hyperrealistic video game / simulation we live in — "our version of *The Matrix*" — combining MMORPG persistence, VR/AR immersion, real or simulated consciousness, and AI, so real it can't be distinguished from physical reality. Chapters: 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 11, 14, 15. Related: simulation hypothesis, simulation point, MMORPG.
- **Griefbots / deadbots** — Bots that emulate a deceased person, trained on their messages and writing; a burgeoning industry raising boundary and addiction questions. Chapters: 5. Related: digital immortality, social AI.
- **Guardian angels** — In Christianity, protectors keeping us on God's path; Virk suggests they are more than recording automatons — possibly conscious "master players" leading soul-groups, or advanced AI. Chapters: 12. Related: recording angels, angels as functions, superusers.
- **GZK (Greisen–Zatsepin–Kuzmin) limit** — A known cutoff in certain cosmic rays whose source is unknown; Beane et al. theorize it reveals the "geometry" of the simulation — constraints set by the lattice spacing of a simulated universe. Chapters: 14. Related: lattice, evidence of computation, pixel.
- **Haptic suit** — A wearable delivering kinesthetic feedback so a user "feels" the virtual world; paired with omnidirectional treadmills in *Ready Player One*'s accessory kit. Chapters: 3. Related: virtual reality, 3D sound.
- **Hard problem of consciousness** — Chalmers's term for the still-unanswered question of how/why physical processes give rise to subjective experience. Chapters: 4. Related: easy problem of consciousness, consciousness as information.
- **Holodeck** — *Star Trek*'s room that could simulate any environment; Virk's touchstone for a fully immersive simulation and for "creation by spoken command." Chapters: 1, 12. Related: the Great Simulation, creation by spoken command.
- **Hypergraphs** — The diagrams in Wolfram's 2020 Physics Project which, with cellular-automata rules, he claims can derive fundamental physics including relativity. Chapters: 14. Related: Wolfram Physics Project, cellular automata, simple programs.
- **Implanted memories** — Planting a plausible history of what happened "before" any point in the simulation; Stage 8 of the road map, tied to what time means inside a simulation. Chapters: 4, 6, 10. Related: false memories, mind broadcast, road to the simulation point.
- **Information-based model / reality** — Virk's alternate model: reality is based on information rather than physical matter; the unrendered world "exists only as information until and unless it needs to be rendered so that it may be observed." Chapters: 14, 16. Related: information entropy, mass-energy-information equivalence, digital physics.

- **Shared dream / collective dream** — The maya of the world "would have to be a shared dream that we all live in, with characters created for fulfilling our own personal karma," our true self residing outside it; shared dreams (different people reporting the same dream) are widely anecdotally reported but unvalidated. Chapters: 10. Related: maya, dreamtime, non-ordinary reality.
- **Shared-memory pixel model of entanglement** — Virk's explanation of entanglement: two pixels reading the same memory address change together when that value changes; if one starts using a different address they are "no longer entangled" (decoherence). Chapters: 14. Related: quantum entanglement, decoherence, pixel.
- **Side-scrollers** — Games whose limited worlds are viewed by scrolling left or right — an early expansion of the game world. Chapters: 2. Related: wraparound geometry.
- **Simple programs (Wolfram)** — Wolfram's proposal (*A New Kind of Science*, 2002) that nature is a collection of computer programs and that simple programs are a basic building block of all science — making science the study of computation and information. Chapters: 14. Related: cellular automata, computational irreducibility, Wolfram Physics Project.
- **Simulated artificial intelligence (SAI)** — Simulated AI placeable into android-type bodies, blurring the lines between intelligence, consciousness, and being "alive." Chapters: 15. Related: non-player character, downloadable consciousness, Metaverse Turing Test.
- **Simulated multiverse** — Virk's reconciliation of MWI and Copenhagen (from *The Simulated Multiverse*): the other probabilities are "other runs" of the simulation existing as information, not rendered unless part of the game being played — escaping the parsimony objection to many worlds. Chapters: 8. Related: many-worlds interpretation, rendering on demand, multiverse graph.
- **Simulation argument (Bostrom)** — Bostrom's 2003 trilemma: either civilizations almost never reach the simulation-capable stage, or they reach it but almost never run ancestor simulations, or we are almost certainly in a simulation — three probabilities summing to 100%, one of which must be true. Chapters: 1, 2, 6, 16. Related: simplified simulation argument, ancestor simulations, posthuman.
- **Simplified simulation argument** — Virk's name for the counting version (used by Musk) that drops most of Bostrom's math: if one base reality spawns many indistinguishable simulated worlds, the chance you're in the single physical one shrinks toward zero. Chapters: 6. Related: simulation argument, base reality.
- **Simulation game** — Another name for a resource-management game (like Virk's *Tap Fish*). Chapters: 1. Related: MMORPG.
- **Simulation hypothesis** — The book's central thesis: rather than living in a physical world of matter, we live inside a computer-generated reality built on information — thinkable as either a multiplayer video game or a computer simulation. (Bostrom coined the phrase for the third horn of his trilemma; it has since broadened.) Chapters: 0, 1, 2, 6, 7, 8, 16. Related: simulation argument, Matrix Hypothesis, the Great Simulation, simulation point.
- **Simulation point** — Virk's formal term: "a theoretical point at which a technological civilization can create virtual worlds (simulations) that are indistinguishable from physical reality (across all of our senses) and populated with virtual beings that are indistinguishable from physical beings" — a kind of technological singularity, and Stage 11 of the road map. Chapters: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 10, 15, 16. Related: technological singularity, posthuman, road to the simulation point.
- **Simulations within simulations / nested (stacked) simulations** — Simulated civilizations creating their own sub-simulations, forming a treelike stack of child/grandchild sims; infinite recursion would need near-infinite computing, so restrictions may be imposed (Bostrom) — and programming limits (stack overflow) may forbid it. Chapters: 6, 15, 16. Related: base reality, stack overflow error, dream within a dream.
- **Six Yogas of Naropa** — Tibetan esoteric practices as shortcuts out of samsara — tummo (inner heat), gyulus (illusory body), milam (dream yoga), ösel (clear light), phowa (transference of consciousness), bardo (intermediate state) — all concerning the illusory nature of waking reality and a dreaming body that can travel independent of the physical one. Chapters: 10. Related: dream yoga, secret seventh yoga, bardo.
- **Social AI** — Henry Shevlin's term for AI purpose-built for socializing (companionship), as opposed to generative AI used for work. Chapters: 5. Related: griefbots, generative agents.
- **Soul vs. "bundle of cause and effect" ("bag of karma")** — The key Hindu/Buddhist split: Hinduism posits an indivisible soul passing through lives; Buddhism says what reincarnates is not a soul but "a bundle of cause and effect" — the information of a consciousness. Virk uses "soul" to cover both. Chapters: 11. Related: reincarnation, consciousness as information, karma.
- **Space-time** — After general relativity, time treated as a fourth dimension: events located at (x, y, z, t) rather than (x, y, z). Chapters: 9. Related: time dilation, relativity of simultaneity, wormhole.
- **Spatial computing** — The term Apple popularized with the Vision Pro to cover both AR and VR. Chapters: 3. Related: augmented reality, virtual reality, the dial-in effect.
- **Stack overflow error / memory leak / garbage collection** — Programming concepts Virk uses to argue infinite nested simulations may be disallowed: recursion only exhausts memory when each run consumes memory never cleared (a memory leak with no garbage collection), causing a stack overflow. Chapters: 16. Related: simulations within simulations, recursion.
- **State-as-information** — The paramount requirement that the state of player and world be captured concisely as information, stored outside the rendered world (today on invisible cloud servers) and rendered only as needed. Chapters: 2. Related: game state, the cloud, information-based model.
- **Stereoscopy** — Presenting two images, one per eye, offset at the right distance so the mind assembles a single 3D perspective — dating to Victorian devices, and the basis of VR headsets. Chapters: 3. Related: virtual reality, rendering engine.
- **Subject-object duality / participatory universe** — Modern science's assumption that observer and observed are separate and measurement is neutral; quantum physics "was flying in the face of this," leading Wheeler to replace "observer" with "participator." Chapters: 7. Related: observation vs. measurement, materialistic worldview, conditional rendering.
- **Substantially similar universes** — The idea (tied to Hawking's finite-universe theory) that limited particle configurations and choice-outcomes make some spawned universes essentially identical (eggs today vs. eggs tomorrow), enabling compression of the multiverse. Chapters: 8. Related: multiverse graph, compression, branching.
- **Substrate independence** — Bostrom's assumption that "mental states can supervene on any of a broad class of physical substrates": the right computational structure yields conscious experience whether on carbon neurons or silicon processors — a key premise of the simulation argument. Chapters: 5, 6. Related: consciousness as information, downloadable consciousness, simulation argument.
- **Subtle body** — The nonvisible analog of the physical body (per Ashley-Farrand a candidate store of karma, a "distributed architecture"); in many traditions it leaves the body during sleep — which Virk maps to being AFK — and transmigrates with the soul across bodies. Chapters: 11, 13. Related: etheric body, koshas, akashic records, AFK / IRL / in-world.
- **Superusers** — Beings outside the simulation with power to manipulate it; what look like gods to us could be aliens or superusers (illustrated by *Star Trek*'s Q Continuum). Chapters: 15. Related: the grays, godlike AI, base reality.
- **Symbolic AI / expert systems** — AI's 1980s "first wave": hand-written rules encoding an expert's knowledge; expert systems still exist. Chapters: 5. Related: machine learning, third wave of AI.
- **Synchronicity** — Jung's "acausal connecting principle" (1951): a psychic event and an external event "fall together in time in a meaningful way" with no definable cause and effect — one of the few concepts both scientific and psychological. Chapters: 13. Related: technological synchronicity, associative information storage, glitch in the Matrix.
- **System prompts** — Developer instructions injected to guide (and constrain) what a chatbot outputs, invisible to end users. Chapters: 5. Related: large language model.
- **Techno-scientific metaphors** — Scriptural metaphors that were "technological for their time" — clothing for the soul, Buddha's wheel, the book/scroll of deeds, the messenger-angel — a process that continues today (film projectors, holograms, movie screens). Chapters: 12. Related: mystics' translation problem, film projector metaphor, Sci-Fi Feedback Loop.
- **Technological synchronicity** — Virk's Pasulka-inspired reframing of synchronicity as association and computation: like a web cookie tracking behavior to serve a targeted ad, something tracking our thoughts and actions could "put a meaningful next step in front of us inside a simulation." Chapters: 13. Related: synchronicity, associative information storage.
- **Teleportation** — Instantly de-rendering a character at one location and re-rendering it at another — setting new (x, y, z) coordinates without traversing the points between; in our physics, the wormhole is the mechanism. Chapters: 9, 13. Related: wormhole, rendering engine, physics engine.
- **Telepathy** — Sending a mental message from one person to another (remote viewing is a kind of it) — analogous to private messages between players in an MMORPG. Chapters: 13. Related: remote viewing, virtual camera.
- **Textual input** — Interaction by typed commands with a specific grammar ("go north," "drop knife"); Virk argues textual (vocal or typed) input remains essential even in sophisticated simulations. Chapters: 2. Related: basic game loop, interactive fiction, large language model.
- **Third wave of AI / three waves of AI** — First wave: rules-driven expert systems (1980s); second (current): data-driven LLMs and diffusion models that don't follow rules; third (arriving): machine learning combined with rules-based reasoning — analogous to a von Neumann architecture — yielding agents that make judgment calls; needed to truly pass Stage 9. Chapters: 5, 16. Related: symbolic AI, machine learning, generative agents.
- **Threat simulation theory** — A theory that dreaming evolved to let us "simulate" threats before they happen and hone our responses, linking dreams to natural selection. Chapters: 10. Related: overdetermined (dream science).
- **Three Laws of Robotics** — Asimov's 1942 rules (*I, Robot*), which Virk describes as fundamental — "hard coded into the operating system." Chapters: 5. Related: killer AI.
- **Three-body problem** — The famous case of computational irreducibility: the only way to know the state at step 2,000 is to simulate every step to it — why simulations must exist at all. Chapters: 16. Related: computational irreducibility, Monte Carlo simulations.
- **Time dilation** — Einstein's verified result that the closer one moves to light speed, the more time slows for the traveler (the twin paradox); evidence time is not absolute. Chapters: 9. Related: twin paradox, relativity of simultaneity, space-time.
- **Transhumanism** — Kurzweil's "merger of biology and technology" framing, which places him squarely in this camp. Chapters: 5. Related: the singularity, downloadable consciousness.
- **Tulku** — In Tibetan tradition, a higher, more evolved being who chooses to be born to help others evolve — the exception to karma-driven rebirth. Chapters: 11. Related: reincarnation, Lords of Karma.
- **Turing Test / the imitation game** — Turing's 1950 test: if an interrogator can't tell a machine from a human via messages, the machine passes; Virk stresses it's "more of a milestone than a definition" and that today it's the software (not hardware) that passes. Chapters: 1, 5, 15. Related: Metaverse Turing Test, natural language processing.
- **Twin paradox** — A twin traveling near light speed returns younger than the one who stayed on Earth; how much younger depends on speed and duration. Chapters: 9. Related: time dilation, relativity of simultaneity.
- **UFOs / UAP** — Unidentified flying (anomalous) objects: thousands of reports of craft with maneuverability that "defies our understanding of inertia and aerodynamics"; Virk explains them via customizable physics engines, partial rendering, and narrowcasting. Chapters: 13. Related: the unexplained 5 percent, interdimensional vs. extraterrestrial hypotheses, narrowcasting.
- **Uncertainty principle** — Heisenberg's finding (1927) that a particle's momentum and position can't both be known precisely: the observer must *choose* to measure the entity as particle or wave. Chapters: 7. Related: particle-wave duality, observation vs. measurement.
- **The unexplained 5 percent** — Virk's summary of UFO commissions: most sightings are explainable, but "around 5 percent" retain enough information to rule out known objects (including the Tic Tac encounter) — the residue that puzzles skeptics and believers alike. Chapters: 13. Related: UFOs / UAP, Fermi paradox.
- **Universal bridge** — Virk's characterization of the simulation hypothesis as a bridge letting techno-scientific people be more spiritual and spiritual people more techno-scientific, without either abandoning their core view of the universe. Chapters: 0. Related: simulation hypothesis, Sci-Fi Feedback Loop.
- **Universal server time** — In multiplayer games, the server-side clock against which operations are logged so their order can be determined, since individual machine clocks can't be trusted across the internet. Chapters: 9. Related: clock speed, relativity of simultaneity.
- **User-generated content** — Objects or structures players "leave" that become part of the persistent world (*Second Life*), which Virk likens to dropping a bicycle in a real park. Chapters: 2. Related: persistent world, metaverse, virtual economy.
- **Utah electrode array** — The chip (developed with the University of Utah) implanted in a paralyzed man in 2004 that let him control a cursor — an early invasive BCI. Chapters: 4. Related: brain–computer interface, N1 implant.
- **Virtual camera** — The movable camera showing what a player sees; because the client renders from its position, it can be placed anywhere (first-person, over-the-shoulder, or elsewhere in the world) — Virk's single mechanism for both OBEs and remote viewing. Chapters: 7, 9, 13. Related: rendering engine, out-of-body experience, remote viewing.
- **Virtual influencer** — Social-media characters (often posing with sponsor products) that are usually not full AI but carefully produced marketing images, typically actor-driven via motion capture and postproduction. Chapters: 5. Related: motion capture, embodiment axis.
- **Virtual reality (VR)** — Immersion via goggles with one small screen per eye, images offset by roughly the interocular distance; immersion comes from obscuring everything else so the user feels inside the scene (Stage 4). Chapters: 1, 3. Related: augmented reality, stereoscopy, room-scale VR.
- **Volumetric display** — Displays that use lasers to create holographic images placeable in the real world, appearing solid from any point in the room with light reflecting as from a physical object. Chapters: 3. Related: light-field display, real-world rendering.
- **Weighted probabilistic analysis** — The true NLP breakthrough: the AI chooses among multiple responses by weights learned from past experience, improving over time; combined with voice-to-text it powers Alexa, Google, and Siri. Chapters: 5. Related: natural language processing, large language model.
- **Western religious traditions / people of the book** — Virk's grouping of Judaism, Christianity, and Islam (the Abrahamic line, each founded on an individual — Moses, Jesus, Muhammad); he distinguishes rule-focused mainstream branches from mystical traditions (e.g., the Sufis) concerned with direct experience of God. Chapters: 12. Related: mystics' translation problem, creation by spoken command.
- **Wolfram Physics Project** — Wolfram's project to derive the laws of physics from discrete computational processes (hypergraphs plus cellular-automata rules) rather than continuous equations. Chapters: 1, 14. Related: cellular automata, simple programs, computational irreducibility.
- **Wormhole / Einstein-Rosen bridge** — A tear in space-time (from a massive gravitational singularity) letting matter enter at one point and exit at another without moving through 3D space faster than light — Virk's teleport mechanism for our physics. Chapters: 9, 13. Related: teleportation, space-time, physics engine.
- **Wraparound geometry** — The beginning of virtual-world geometry: in *Asteroids* a ship leaving the top of the screen reappears at the bottom, giving the illusion of a spherical world. Chapters: 2. Related: side-scrollers, physics engine.
- **Zeno's paradox** — Zeno of Elea's puzzle that if space is continuous you must always cover half the remaining distance and so can never reach a wall (or Achilles never catch the tortoise) — resolved if space is quantized/discrete. Chapters: 9. Related: quanta / quantization, pixel, analog vs. digital.
