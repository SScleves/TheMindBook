# Glossary

Definitions below are William Cooper's own, as reported faithfully in the chapter notes for *Dark Psychology and Manipulation* (2024); they are the book's claims, attributed to the author, not endorsed as fact. **Chapter numbers follow each note's own `chapter:` field: Chapter 0 = the Introduction (front matter); Chapters 1–36 are the book's numbered chapters, closing with the Conclusion (36).** Where the book gives a term several names, synonyms are joined with a slash. Large catalogues of body-language and anatomy cues are consolidated into grouped entries that still name each sub-signal.

- **Active listening** — Cooper's most-overlooked influence tool: really listening gives the impression you care, building the "narrow ties" of trust capital; the book's eight-step method covers attentiveness, removing distractions, positive body language, watching subtle cues, mirroring, empathizing, remembering, and open-ended follow-up questions. Chapters: 10, 26. Related: rapport, mirroring, empathy.
- **Actor-observer bias** — A cognitive bias a people-reader must avoid: we explain our own actions by external circumstances but blame others' actions on their internal character (excusing our own lateness on traffic while calling a colleague lazy). Chapters: 18. Related: cognitive bias.
- **Aggressive communication** — A style that expresses dominance and seeks to win over the other person "at whatever cost," often loud and rude; deemed impractical because listeners become distracted, frightened, or defensive. Chapters: 26. Related: assertive communication, passive-aggression.
- **Analog marking** — Identifying chosen words within a sentence by verbal or non-verbal means (pauses, intonation, pitch, gesture) so the conscious mind hears everything but only registers the marked words, granting direct access to the unconscious; strengthens hidden commands. Chapters: 11. Related: hidden/embedded commands.
- **Analytical mind** — The self-aware frame of mind that deliberately steps back from current feelings to observe and manipulate information; includes all complex reasoning. Chapters: 17. Related: engaged mind, automatic mind.
- **Anchoring (conditioning / stimulus-response)** — Associating a sound, image, feeling, or memory with a stimulus in the unconscious so the stimulus later re-evokes the state (an old song, an ice-cream truck's jingle); Cooper calls this stimulus-response behavior "the basis of manipulation." Chapters: 4, 11. Related: anchoring bias, Pavlov's dogs.
- **Anchoring bias / anchor effect** — The tendency to rely excessively on the first piece of information (the "anchor") and to judge everything afterward by difference from it, with heavy bearing on negotiations and diagnoses. Chapters: 1, 2, 4, 18. Related: first-move advantage, price anchoring.
- **Anchor of ownership** — Creating a sense of belonging so a customer feels a product is already theirs (Starbucks writing your name on the cup), raising its perceived value. Chapters: 4. Related: anchoring, price anchoring.
- **Ask (influence tactic)** — Persuading high-power individuals by withholding solutions and instead asking them how to solve the problem, letting them "persuade themselves"; used by journalists, lawyers, and car salespeople, but overusing leading questions can backfire. Chapters: 27. Related: leading questions, reasoning, inspire.
- **Assertive communication** — Expressing yourself with "I" statements while remaining mindful of the other person's feelings; Cooper calls it the most effective communication style and the book's recurring defensive tool for refusing a suspect gift or offer. Chapters: 2, 26. Related: setting boundaries, learn how to say no.
- **Aura of unpredictability** — Keeping victims off balance by being deliberately unpredictable (not moody); Cooper warns that overusing it gets you dismissed as a "whacko" no one believes. Chapters: 23. Related: keeping people off balance, moody behavior.
- **Authority (rule of) / authority principle** — We defer to authority, following orders from a uniform or advice from an authoritative figure; persuaders manufacture it via decisive posture, displayed diplomas, or expert branding, and marketers borrow credible leaders (the 20-year branded realtor over unknown "Peter"). Chapters: 2, 30. Related: weapons of influence, ethos, credibility.
- **Automatic mind** — The brain impulsively noticing environmental shifts, making snap positive/negative judgments, and categorizing experience; essential for survival and adaptation. Chapters: 17. Related: engaged mind, analytical mind.
- **Avoidance** — Not giving straight answers: rambling, dodging, or gradually changing topic to confuse the target into questioning the true version while the manipulator redirects attention to what they really want. Chapters: 3. Related: deception, gaslighting, create insecurity.
- **Bait and switch** — Dangling something the target wants, imposing conditions, then never letting them actually get it (stringing them along until exhausted); also pretending to be someone you are not until you extract the benefit, then reverting. A common salesperson tactic. Chapters: 6, 8. Related: love bombing, dark seduction.
- **Bandwagon bias** — The cognitive bias behind social proof: a widespread behavior automatically appears more valid and attractive; Cooper says "fashions work exactly like this." Chapters: 1, 2. Related: social proof, groupthink.
- **Baseline** — Two related senses: (1) the spectrum of "normal" human behavior against which a reader interprets any individual, like a judge; (2) the reference picture of someone's truthful facial expressions, tone, and eye contact, established by asking basic questions (name, address) that should elicit honest answers. Chapters: 17, 18. Related: analyzing people, tells.
- **"Because" justification** — Giving any reason at all, signaled by the word "because," makes people far more likely to comply, even when the reason is weak or circular (Langer's copier-line study). Chapters: 35. Related: reinforced truisms.
- **The big buildup** — The patient manipulator's long-horizon approach, seeking sustainable results over years rather than immediate gains, exemplified by advertisers entrenching a brand over time. Chapters: 5. Related: brand positioning, long con.
- **Big lie** — A lie told to exploit the deceived for self-interested gain (a dealer passing off a faulty TV as good); condemned across cultures, and the extreme case is a destructive societal narrative (Cooper's Dylann Roof / white-supremacy example). Chapters: 24. Related: white lie, lying, scapegoating.
- **Black/white model** — Framing a decision as only two options — one clearly bad, one favored — to pressure the listener toward the favored choice; common in digital sales pages, and more effective the less explicit it is. Chapters: 35. Related: bind, illusion of alternative.
- **Blackmail** — Holding damaging information the victim must comply to keep secret, creating a negative feedback loop; incredibly damaging in the workplace but self-limiting, since it reveals the manipulator's identity and eventually exhausts the victim. Chapters: 4. Related: extortion, emotional blackmail.
- **Blame game / blame, guilt and shame** — Using guilt to manipulate — making people feel inferior for help received or inadequate for a condition — and shifting blame for one's own failures onto others; built on the manipulator's assumption that "the best defense is a good offense." Chapters: 6, 12, 14. Related: guilt card/shame card, playing the victim, shaming.
- **Body language / kinesics** — Internal communication through physical behavior, mannerisms, and expressions, produced instinctively rather than consciously and continuing even in silence; Cooper says nonverbal cues "usually speak the loudest," and reading them gains an advantage since motions "betray their feelings." Interpretation is culture-dependent and must combine multiple signals. Chapters: 17, 18, 19, 20. Related: nonverbal communication, open/closed body language, mirroring.
- **Bond of sympathy / bond of similarity** — Two liking bonds: sympathy built through appreciation and compliments, similarity built by mirroring the interlocutor's points in common ("a confirmation technique... one does nothing but reflect the other"). Chapters: 2. Related: liking, mirroring, rapport.
- **Boogeyman** — A fictional figure (real or invented) created to give people something to hate, fear, or blame; expert manipulators can "milk a boogeyman for a long time" but must eventually move on. Chapters: 5. Related: gaslighting, scapegoating, us vs. them.
- **Boiled frog principle** — Cooper's (attributed to Chomsky) metaphor for humans' poor ability to react to harmful change introduced slowly enough to stay invisible to consciousness — a frog stays in water heated gradually until it boils; the mechanism behind the gradual strategy. Chapters: 34. Related: gradual strategy.
- **Brainwashing** — Replacing a person's "programmed" set of beliefs with one implanted by the manipulator, almost always via force or the threat of it, usually gradually inside cults, political groups, or abusive relationships; runs in three stages (breaking down of self → possibility of salvation → rebuilding of self) and aims at a fragmented psyche out of touch with reality. Chapters: 1, 16. Related: trauma-based mind control, mind control, isolation.
- **Brand positioning** — The secret of repetitive advertising: hearing a message until the conscious mind stops fighting and it seeps into the subconscious, entrenching a brand over years; legitimate when built on real quality, manipulative when built on hype alone. Chapters: 4, 5. Related: subconscious mind, the big buildup.
- **Breaking down of self** — First brainwashing stage: shattering the subject's old identity (attack on identity → guilt → self-betrayal → breaking point) so they become open to a new one; if the subject holds firm, the agent fails. Chapters: 16. Related: brainwashing, rebuilding of self.
- **Calibration / pacing** — In rapport, complete correspondence with the subject's language style and mental models before leading them. Chapters: 10, 11. Related: rapport, mirroring, leading.
- **Call to action** — A message containing elements that compel action; "call now before it's too late" packs an embedded command and a threat the target may not notice. Chapters: 11. Related: embedded command, trigger words.
- **Case study method** — Cooper's device for grounding technique in real applications — presenting real-world figures (Madoff, Manson, Hitler) or scenarios and analyzing the tactics used; also a teaching/marketing format (guidelines, context "like a movie preview," a relevant challenge). Chapters: 27, 33. Related: premeditation vs. insanity.
- **Charm and flirting** — A dark-seduction technique: pairing charm with attractiveness ("a very powerful combination") and flirting with no intent of a real relationship, since mere innuendo often secures compliance before the target is discarded. Chapters: 7. Related: dark seduction, love bombing, persona.
- **Cognitive bias** — The by-product of humans' evolved decision-making speed; the source of logical, cognitive, and above all decisional errors that expose us to being conditioned and influenced. The book names anchoring, ingroup, halo effect, bandwagon, confirmation, actor-observer, and false-consensus biases. Chapters: 1, 2, 18. Related: cognitive shortcuts, weapons of influence.
- **Cognitive shortcuts / mental shortcuts / reasoning shortcuts** — The brain's simplification mechanisms for saving cognitive energy under information overload ("laziness is deeply rooted in human nature"); the levers language can trigger, since an unconscious level of decision-making gives ~95% of choices. Chapters: 0, 1, 2. Related: conditioned responses, cognitive bias.
- **Cognitive empathy** — The "theory of mind": mindfulness of what others are thinking, built by educated guesses from what you know of the person rather than hasty assumptions. Chapters: 26. Related: emotional empathy, somatic empathy.
- **Commitment and consistency** — Once people take a position (especially publicly or in writing), they automatically maintain it because they need to appear consistent; the manipulator elicits a general judgment then attaches a request. In marketing, letting customers "have their say" builds commitment. Chapters: 2, 30. Related: foot in the door, pitfall/asterisk technique.
- **Common ground** — A sales technique of finding shared interests and being optimistic, enthusiastic, quick, polite, and likeable, since "people purchase products from people they like." Chapters: 30. Related: liking, rapport.
- **Communication as a tool** — Cooper's ethical frame: communication, like any household tool, can build or destroy — the knife is useful in a chef's hands and deadly in a murderer's; what matters is the user's intention. Chapters: 0. Related: intention-as-the-difference.
- **Comprehensibility** — The claim that customers are persuaded more by plans and ideas they can quickly understand, so generating fast understanding boosts persuasive power. Chapters: 30. Related: logos.
- **Conditioned responses** — The brain's pre-programmed, automatic reactions (jumping at a loud noise, salivating at food, dozing in a warm room) that let it resist sensory overload; Cooper argues words can trigger them too. Chapters: 0. Related: cognitive shortcuts, anchoring.
- **Confirmation bias** — Leaning toward information that confirms prior beliefs; a "polarizing pitfall" (supporters and opponents read the same candidate's act to fit their views). Chapters: 1, 18. Related: cognitive bias, false consensus effect.
- **Conversational hypnosis / covert hypnosis** — Hypnosis practiced without the target's knowledge or consent, hence part of dark psychology; accesses the subconscious to secure compliance the target "would not consciously agree" to, running rapport → secure focus → trance-like state → implanting the message. Chapters: 11. Related: hypnosis, Ericksonian hypnosis, NLP.
- **Conversational postulates** — Directing conversation so you tell the other party what you expect without overtly commanding it (offering only choices that presuppose agreement, or "you don't want that, do you?"). Chapters: 11. Related: linguistic presupposition, bind.
- **Covert deception** — Masking the dark persuader's true intentions, adapting approach to victim and circumstance; the featured version is the two-consecutive-requests script. Chapters: 5. Related: two consecutive requests, deception.
- **Covert emotional manipulation** — Also "occult"/"secret" manipulation: hidden influence over another's decisions and feelings, not necessarily immoral, that masks the manipulator's real intentions and targets the emotions because "a person's emotions are the key of their personality." Distinguished from dark psychology, which is immoral and can target whole groups. Chapters: 1, 6. Related: dark psychology, emotional blackmail.
- **Create insecurity** — Continuously questioning everything the interlocutor says until they question it themselves, exploiting weaknesses, convictions, or self-love. Chapters: 3. Related: negative criticism, gaslighting, undermining confidence.
- **Creating an illusion** — Replacing reality with a more convenient version, from fabricating a whole persona (fake name, accent, forged documents) down to flat-out lies; the skilled mix lies with truth so embedded kernels of truth make the illusion credible. Chapters: 5. Related: simulation, deception, persona.
- **Credibility** — "The most valuable currency in the persuasion business": built slowly, destroyed instantly (a single bad social-media post can collapse it). Chapters: 28. Related: trust capital, ethos, the messenger.
- **Critical guardian** — Cooper's term for the conscious filter that negative hidden commands bypass. Chapters: 4. Related: negative hidden command, subconscious mind.
- **Cult leaders / cult manipulation** — Leaders combining fringe beliefs, a propensity for violence, and charisma; their two common traits are narcissism (self-cast hero treating others as pawns) and charisma (able to "convince anyone of anything" and command attention). Manson is the case study: a messianic "savior/God" persona exploiting belonging and drug dependency. Chapters: 16, 27, 33. Related: messianic persona, exploiting the need for belonging, narcissism.
- **Dark persuader** — Cooper's recurring term for the manipulator applying persuasion techniques (the long con, covert deception, leading questions). Chapters: 5, 6. Related: manipulation, dark persuasion.
- **Dark persuasion** — Dark psychology deployed on groups: because it can change the minds of whole groups, even a society, Cooper calls it "frightening and dangerous." Chapters: 1. Related: dark psychology, mass manipulation.
- **Dark psychology** — The study of the human condition concerning man's natural unconscious and emotional predisposition to prey on and subjugate others for personal gain; also defined as the process of making people act against their self-interest ("its function is immoral"). Cooper holds this penchant is innate in everyone and, unlike covert manipulation, can target whole groups. Chapters: 0, 1, 12. Related: psychology, covert emotional manipulation, dark triad.
- **Dark seduction** — Broader than sexual conquest: using romantic overtures to extract some benefit from the victim (the sex is not the point); works via physical attraction, charm/flirting, and overt sexual advances, and hides behind a crafted persona. Chapters: 7. Related: persona, love bombing, power trip.
- **Dark triad** — A collection of character traits present in everyone but predominant in some — narcissism, Machiavellianism, and psychopathy — which Cooper calls "the hallmark of manipulators," typically fostered by childhood neglect, abuse, or trauma. Chapters: 8, 12. Related: narcissism, Machiavellianism, psychopathy.
- **Deception** — Cheating or trickery; not necessarily making someone believe a falsehood but manipulating their perception of the situation, used when the manipulator can't get something by traditional means. Its six types are lies, equivocations, concealment, exaggeration, understatement, and misrepresentation, and it forms a detectable pattern of behavior. Chapters: 1, 3, 23, 24. Related: lying, misrepresentation, simulation, tells.
- **Delivery** — How something is said, including displayed confidence; per Cooper as important as content, since "the best manipulators make blatant lies seem real by the confidence they exhibit." Chapters: 28. Related: the message, credibility.
- **Denial** — A form of lying: pretending something happened when it didn't, or hiding that it did, to avoid responsibility and punishment; a sincere-seeming denial plants a seed of doubt in onlookers. Chapters: 3. Related: gaslighting, lying.
- **Dependence / emotional dependence** — A toxic dynamic, especially when the victim sustains the victimizer's dependency (addicts); emotionally, being unable to function without a partner's attention and validation (the smothering "jealous type"). Chapters: 9, 13. Related: emotional dependency, isolation, toxic relationship.
- **Dismissing unresolved issues** — Manipulation sign: the manipulator skips over problems so they fester, especially when solving them would cost the manipulator dearly. Chapters: 9. Related: toxic relationship.
- **Divide and conquer** — A fundamental manipulator tenet; blanket statements are "great at achieving that," creating animosity among people. Chapters: 3. Related: generalizations, us vs. them.
- **Door in the face** — Making a deliberately excessive request, receiving the refusal ("door in the face"), then following with the real, modest request, which now seems reasonable by comparison and is felt as a concession — a "sneaky application of the reciprocity rule." Chapters: 4. Related: foot in the door, reciprocity.
- **Double bind** — A stronger simple bind offering two specific alternatives ("white or red wine," "one-time payment or installments") so the person chooses between them without questioning whether to act at all. Chapters: 35. Related: bind / illusion of alternative, simple bind.
- **Due diligence** — The victim-side counter-measure (e.g., investigating an investment) that might have prevented a fraud like Madoff's — though Cooper calls its effectiveness debatable against years of built-up trust capital. Chapters: 33. Related: trust capital, do your homework.
- **Echoing** — Interjecting a comment that repeats what a person just said ("the weather is nice" → "yes, really nice") to build rapport, regardless of agreement. Chapters: 10. Related: mirroring, rapport.
- **Embedded / hidden command** — A command camouflaged inside a broader statement so the unconscious receives it while the conscious mind overlooks it ("Do you want to tell me now or wait a while?"); the most common form is the call to action. Chapters: 4, 11. Related: analog marking, linguistic presupposition, negative hidden command.
- **Embellishment / name-dropping** — Making events or accomplishments seem far larger than they are ("top of their class" when middle of the pack); name-dropping is embellishment used to appear well-connected. Chapters: 5. Related: exaggeration, creating an illusion.
- **Emotional blackmail** — Using some control over the victim to extort them; the manipulator inspires empathy and remorse for themselves, keeping the target under constant risk of emotional pain so they comply while believing they act freely. Chapters: 1, 6. Related: blackmail, guilt trips, intimidation.
- **Emotional dependency** — The reliance the White Knight and rift-building tactics aim to produce: the victim depends on the manipulator's "support" because nothing else can fill the chasm the manipulator engineered. Chapters: 13, 14. Related: the white knight, isolation, dependence.
- **Emotional empathy** — Sharing others' emotions (possibly causing personal distress), built by asking "Have I felt similar to what they feel?" Chapters: 26. Related: cognitive empathy, somatic empathy.
- **Emotional flooding** — Being overwhelmed during charged conversations; a good listener prevents it by reassuring the speaker they are heard. Chapters: 26. Related: active listening.
- **Emotional insecurity / emotional fragility** — Favorite-victim traits: the insecure show it constantly and over-defend under provocation; the fragile appear normal but break down at the slightest provocation (temporarily, after breakups or grief), which opportunistic or patient manipulators exploit or induce. Chapters: 12. Related: victim selection, highly sensitive people, testing the target.
- **Empathy / empathic person** — The ability to identify, understand, and communicate with others' feelings at a meaningful level; a true "empathic" senses everyone's emotional "pulse," feels for people/animals/objects, makes a great therapist — and is a favorite target because malicious people can feign feelings empaths take as real. Chapters: 12, 26, 29. Related: highly sensitive people, guilt trip, fake empathy.
- **Enabler** — What a victim becomes when they are aware of the manipulation but do nothing to stop it — complicit by inaction. Chapters: 13. Related: masochistic response, manipulator-victim dynamic.
- **Encouragement / expecting the best / encouraging positive change** — Leadership habits: setting high but realistic expectations, praising even small progress ("goals aren't reached in a day"), and correcting flaws kindly and gently rather than dwelling on them. Chapters: 27. Related: leadership, rewards and recognition.
- **Engaged mind** — The state of being fully immersed in the present activity; Cooper says the absorbed are more satisfied and happier. Chapters: 17. Related: automatic mind, analytical mind.
- **Ethos / pathos / logos** — The classical persuasion triad: ethos (persuading through one's own character/credibility, "leading by example"), pathos (evoking emotion, especially negative), logos (logic, made to feel manageable); together they make persuasion "highly effective." Chapters: 28, 32. Related: fear-based persuasion, guru/expert persona, credibility.
- **Exaggeration** — A deception type: embellishing accomplishments, symptoms, or how long a task will take; distinct from a flat lie because a kernel of truth remains. Chapters: 23. Related: embellishment, deception, understatement.
- **Exaggeration model** — Taking an interlocutor's dismissive answer and exaggerating its implied meaning into an emotional accusation ("Are you saying you don't care what your skin looks like?"), trapping them into re-engaging. Chapters: 35. Related: black/white model, leading questions.
- **Exploiting the need for belonging** — Cooper's core explanation of cult control (Manson): recruiting people who lack community and protection — runaways, orphans, the homeless, women "saved" from prostitution — then supplying both. Chapters: 33. Related: cult manipulation, isolation, dependency through addiction.
- **Extortion** — Distinguished from blackmail: a threat of physical harm unless victims comply, with no personal information — typically "protection" payment; victims can fight or (usually best) run away. Chapters: 4. Related: blackmail, intimidation.
- **Fake empathy / humanitarian front / altruism as brand-building** — A manipulator's performance of compassion while "couldn't care less"; the tell is doing good deeds to be acknowledged, and staging charity/humanitarian activity as a front for control, wealth, or attention — "very common in narcissistic people." Chapters: 29. Related: empathy, narcissism, persona.
- **Fake news** — Lies wrapped in a credible-seeming context, "not known to be true, but also not known to be untrue," so the target can't distinguish fact from fiction; power multiplies when the source is credible. Chapters: 3. Related: lying, half-truths, rumors.
- **Fear and dependence** — A brainwashing tactic exploiting fear as a survival instinct (fight or flight): identifying what a person most fears and using it either as a persistent threat or by fabricating threats so the manipulator can "save" them and breed dependency. Chapters: 16. Related: generate fear, the white knight, problem-reaction-solution.
- **Fear-based persuasion / generate fear** — Fear as the most effective persuasive emotion, sown often imperceptibly by announcing dangers ("must"-framed warnings that only appear to appeal to reason); the four-step structure describes a problem, assigns blame, outlines consequences, then presents the solution. Chapters: 3, 16, 28. Related: pathos, problem-reaction-solution, scapegoating.
- **Financial obligations / economic dependence / working-partner status** — A cult/sect capture scheme: creating economic dependence via delayed salaries and "personal favor" advances that breed debt and gratitude, then demanding employees forfeit uncollected wages to buy "working partner" status (≥6 months' salary), which becomes the object of group thinking. Chapters: 5. Related: isolation, group thinking, dependency through addiction.
- **First impressions / the "new file"** — Meeting someone registers as a "new file" in the brain and sets the tone for later meetings — hence "first impressions are very important." Chapters: 18. Related: halo effect, personal appearance.
- **First-move advantage** — Kahneman's point (per Cooper): thanks to anchoring, whoever moves first in a negotiation sets the reference point for all subsequent proposals — refuting the "second counterpart has the advantage" theory. Chapters: 4. Related: anchoring bias, negotiation.
- **Flattery / kissing up** — The behavior love bombing is predicated on, appealing to self-esteem or its lack; manipulators test who is susceptible (narcissists especially), and "kissing up" to a boss wins special favor. Chapters: 4. Related: love bombing, positive reinforcement, positive identity.
- **Foot in the door** — Escalating from small favors to increasingly bigger ones until the manipulator gets what they want or exhausts the victim; the opposite direction of door in the face. The pitfall/asterisk technique is a typical application. Chapters: 2, 4. Related: door in the face, commitment and consistency, trust capital.
- **Fragmented psyche** — The result of successful brainwashing: a person no longer in control of their emotions and actions who loses touch with reality — "the main aim of brainwashing." Chapters: 16. Related: brainwashing.
- **Free will** — The freedom to choose; Cooper holds it can be diminished by circumstances and "negated to a certain extent, though not completely extinguished" — the partial negability that makes mind control possible. Chapters: 14. Related: mind control, blind obedience.
- **Gaslighting** — Making the victim question their own perception of reality until they can't differentiate (e.g.) abuse from non-abuse; unlike simple denial it needs a credible alternative explanation (blame games, a boogeyman) to supplant real events, and Cooper says lawyers' selective use of evidence is "a type of gaslighting." Chapters: 5, 8, 13, 16, 23. Related: misrepresentation, boogeyman, isolation.
- **Generalizations / blanket statements** — Direct attacks on a person or group ("all men are…") that misrepresent them, effective at creating a negative atmosphere and animosity; serve "divide and conquer." Chapters: 3. Related: divide and conquer.
- **Good cop, bad cop** — A confusion technique: one party is aggressive (triggering a defensive instinct), the other suddenly friendly (throwing the psyche "out of whack"); implemented randomly in brainwashing it can break a victim beyond repair, and moodiness is a version of it. Chapters: 14, 16. Related: moody behavior, keeping people off balance.
- **Gradual strategy** — Mass-manipulation tactic: getting an unacceptable measure accepted by introducing it little by little over years so the change is imperceptible (Cooper's example: eroded workers' rights). Chapters: 34. Related: boiled frog principle, strategy of deferring.
- **Groupthink / group thinking** — Individuals being strongly influenced by group opinions and behavior (demonstrated, per Cooper, by Asch's 1956 experiment); also the ideology non-conformers are isolated for failing to adopt. Chapters: 2, 5. Related: units, social proof, isolation.
- **Guilt card / shame card** — The two moral levers within the blame game: making a victim feel guilty for help received, or shaming them via morals while claiming a moral high ground. Chapters: 6. Related: blame game, shaming, guilt trips.
- **Guilt trips** — Laying guilt on the victim, especially after the manipulator provides protection ("rescued" them), invoked whenever the victim acts against the manipulator's aims; empaths are especially easy to guilt-trip. Chapters: 9, 12. Related: guilt card, blame game, empathy.
- **Guru / expert / whisperer persona** — The dark application of ethos: individuals elevated by media hype or self-fabrication who invent credentials, experiences, testimonials, and even "techniques that supposedly work" (dietary experts, cult leaders, self-help gurus). Chapters: 28. Related: ethos, persona, credibility.
- **Half-truths / mixing lies with truth** — Blending lies with truth so the whole is broadly true but details are added or omitted to twist it — "the devil is in the details"; the technique behind rumors, illusions, and (in the case studies) Hitler's rhetoric. Chapters: 3, 23, 33. Related: fake news, rumors, deception.
- **Halo effect bias / physical-attractiveness stereotype** — Letting an initial impression (especially attractiveness) color the whole judgment: "we associate attractiveness with benevolence." The cognitive bias the liking rule exploits. Chapters: 1, 2, 18. Related: liking, cognitive bias.
- **Hidden commands** — See embedded / hidden command. Chapters: 4, 11.
- **Highly sensitive people** — People who process information deeply, averse to cruelty, easily overwhelmed by others' feelings; targeted because introversion eases isolation and their politeness ("too polite to dismiss anyone") gives malicious people a way in. Chapters: 12. Related: emphatic people, victim selection.
- **Hook point** — The moment in state transference when the influencer subtly shifts their own state and the target follows — proof rapport is deep enough to move the target into any state. Chapters: 6. Related: law of state transference, state match, rapport.
- **Hot words** — Mirroring a person's own habitual favorite words (e.g., "fantastic") to create unconscious trust and affinity. Chapters: 35. Related: mirroring, rapport.
- **Humanitarian front** — See fake empathy / altruism as brand-building. Chapters: 29.
- **Humourism / the four temperaments** — The ancient concept that bodily "humors" set temperament, which Cooper says is fixed from birth and governed by the brain stem; the four are melancholic, choleric (linked to men/testosterone), phlegmatic (mostly introverted/female), and sanguine. Chapters: 22. Related: personality, nature vs. nurture.
- **Hypnosis / hypnotherapy** — Leading someone into a transformed, suggestion-receptive state via verbal instructions, accessing the subconscious to implant ideas (and, "in a manner of speaking," control willpower); its legitimate therapeutic form is hypnotherapy. Not an evil practice in itself. Chapters: 11. Related: conversational hypnosis, Ericksonian hypnosis, trance-like state.
- **Ericksonian hypnosis** — Hypnosis after Milton Erickson's indirect methods, using suggestions, metaphors, and stories (e.g., fairy tales; "sex sells") to transmit meaning to targets. Chapters: 10, 11. Related: hypnosis, metaphor, NLP.
- **Ideosensory contents** — Sensory details (a lemon's aroma, taste, juice) that activate a suggested mental representation with more energy; the more of them, the stronger a negative suggestion's effect. Chapters: 4. Related: negative hidden command, subconscious mind.
- **Identification mechanism / influencer / testimonial** — A declination of liking: followers buy a product because they identify with the known person recommending it (openly or via social-media photos). Chapters: 2. Related: liking, authority.
- **Illusion of alternative / bind / simple bind / linguistic presupposition** — A hypnotic device (attributed to Erickson) offering an apparent choice while presupposing the outcome, trapping the person inside your frame ("When are you going to take me to the movies?"); Cooper calls it one of the most powerful, easy-to-use tools. Built from Choice and Time factors. Chapters: 4, 11, 35. Related: double bind, conversational postulates, embedded command.
- **Implanting the message** — The final conversational-hypnosis step: "installing" the manipulator's message in the subconscious while the brain is in deep focus, overtly or via suggestion and imagery. Chapters: 11. Related: trance-like state, secure focus, subconscious mind.

- **Strategy of distraction** — The first media strategy: continuously shifting public attention to trivial news so vital decisions by political/economic lobbies pass unnoticed; the 1936 Olympics is Cooper's case-study distraction. Chapters: 34, 33. Related: mass manipulation, lowering the guard.
- **Subconscious mind** — The unfiltered layer beneath the conscious mind that still processes stimuli; the manipulator's true target, since ideas implanted there have a very high chance of sticking (the secret of repetitive advertising). Chapters: 4. Related: brand positioning, critical guardian, implanting the message.
- **Sympathy** — Comprehending a situation without fully appreciating what someone is going through; Cooper frames it as a lesser "precursor" to empathy. Chapters: 29. Related: empathy.
- **Tasting technique** — A reciprocity strategy: a supermarket sample makes you feel gifted and "almost obliged" to buy the package. Chapters: 2. Related: reciprocity.
- **Tells (deception)** — Individual characteristic signs of lying, read region by region: broken eye contact (shame), erratic head movement, faster breathing, folded palms/legs, trembling hands, slouching, rapid blinking (4–5 in under a minute), eye closure over two seconds, left-and-up gaze in a right-handed person, and a trembling voice — though "one person's tell may not be another's." Chapters: 18, 24. Related: deception, baseline, body language.
- **Testing the target** — A manipulator's probing approaches (over-friendliness, too-good offers, unsolicited advice, timed approaches) to see who responds; responsiveness invites continued advances, so the answer is to move away. Chapters: 12. Related: victim selection, red flags.
- **Thinking / feeling** — Jung's judging modes: thinkers decide by logic ("the mind"), appear professional but seem coldhearted; feelers weigh emotional/social impact ("the heart"), are more likable but hurt more easily. Chapters: 22. Related: personality.
- **Judging / perceiving** — Jung's fourth dichotomy: judgers prefer organization, structure, planning to completion, and deadlines; perceivers keep options open, stay flexible, and enjoy a task's start more than its finish. Chapters: 22. Related: personality.
- **Toxic relationship / toxic romantic relationship / toxic workplace** — Any relationship (romantic, professional, family, friendship) that causes physical or emotional harm to at least one party, marked by an unfavorable power balance and festering issues; romantic ones are often "broken from the beginning" or deteriorate, and a toxic workplace leaves you daily spent with no sense of purpose. Chapters: 9, 13. Related: manipulator-victim dynamic, narcissism, red flags.
- **Trance-like state** — The brainwave pattern (flattened activity, deep focus) induced after relaxation, in which the conscious mind resists little and the subconscious is exposed; required for effective implanting. Chapters: 11. Related: secure focus, implanting the message, sensory overload.
- **Transformational leadership** — Named as the leadership style that "intellectual motivation" (valuing creativity) defines. Chapters: 27. Related: leadership, valuing creativity.
- **Trauma-based mind control** — Hollywood-style brainwashing (e.g., "A Clockwork Orange") in which escalating tortures break the will and replace behaviors; Cooper notes it happens but excludes it from the book's scope. Chapters: 16. Related: brainwashing.
- **Treat people like children** — Media strategy: TV/advertising (and politicians as "father figures") addressing the public with childlike words, gestures, and intonation to neutralize critical sense via suggestibility. Chapters: 34. Related: mass manipulation, taking advantage of the emotional aspect.
- **Triangulation** — Introducing a third party (an old lover, someone from the gym) to confuse the victim about the relationship and intensify their attachment to the manipulator; the third party may become the next target. Chapters: 6. Related: insinuation, gaslighting.
- **Trigger words** — Words (including slogans) that "automatically signal the individual that it's time to do one thing or another," used by NLP practitioners and advertisers to spur action. Chapters: 11. Related: NLP, call to action, brand positioning.
- **Trust capital** — Accumulated credibility a manipulator builds (e.g., borrowing and repaying small sums, or years as a trusted fund manager) that makes people believe them and makes victim due diligence difficult; getting openly caught can destroy it entirely. Chapters: 4, 10, 23, 33. Related: foot in the door, long con, credibility, due diligence.
- **Trustworthiness** — Cooper's claim that trustworthy people are "always surrounded by loyal friends" and that honest individuals are in short supply. Chapters: 21. Related: credibility.
- **Two consecutive requests** — The covert-deception script: "people find it hard to refuse two requests in a row," so the persuader opens with an inflated ask, accepts the refusal, then makes the smaller (real) ask attached to an emotional reason. Chapters: 5. Related: covert deception, door in the face.
- **Undermining confidence** — Manipulation sign: eroding the victim's confidence to create reliance, since a confident victim who can fend for themselves relies less on the manipulator. Chapters: 9. Related: create insecurity, isolation.
- **Units (the community effect)** — Cialdini's seventh rule (from *Pre-Suasion*, 2016): sharing an identity with someone makes one more inclined to accept their requests (the ingroup bias); Facebook groups and the Apple-vs-Android identity are examples. Chapters: 2. Related: ingroup bias, groupthink, weapons of influence.
- **Universal emotions** — Facial expressions that cross all cultures and appear even in blind people — surprise, disgust, anger, fear, contempt, sadness, happiness — revealing true feelings "more than any words." Chapters: 19, 21. Related: micro-expressions, facial expressions.
- **Micro-expressions** — Emotions that appear and vanish almost instantly, registered subconsciously ("blimps on your radar") so the subconscious flags threatening behavior even when the conscious mind "would rather not know." Chapters: 21. Related: universal emotions, tells.
- **Unverifiable statement** — What follows the truisms in a yes-set: an induction, command, or demand taken for granted. Chapters: 4. Related: yes-set, rhetorical questions/truisms.
- **Us vs. them** — A "divide and conquer" brainwashing scenario making the victim believe the world is against them and only the manipulator offers solace, ensuring full control. Chapters: 16. Related: divide and conquer, boogeyman, isolation.
- **Verbal communication** — Language written and spoken (the use of words to share agreed meaning); Cooper stresses words are "meaningless" against tone, pitch, speed, and volume, which manipulators tailor to implant their intentions. Chapters: 10, 17, 19. Related: para-verbal/non-verbal communication, Mehrabian's proportions.
- **Victim / victimizer** — Cooper's recurring pair for the harmed and harming party; in mutual-abuse cases both play a "dual role" at once. Chapters: 13. Related: toxic relationship, manipulator-victim dynamic.
- **Victim selection / victim scouting** — How manipulators choose targets: generally at random ("sniffing around"), starting with family and the weak, favoring those who stand to lose more than the manipulator; strong premeditation signals a psychopath, and skilled manipulators scout places with many vulnerable people. Chapters: 12. Related: testing the target, emotional insecurity, highly sensitive people.
- **Vocal dynamics / paralinguistic communication** — Skillful use of the voice to persuade — deep breathing/projection, pitch inflections, voice quality, rate, rhythm, volume, low pitch for authority — since "what is essential is not what you say but how." Chapters: 19. Related: para-verbal communication, mirroring.
- **War of attrition** — Cooper's phrase for confronting a dark-triad individual: winning is less about skill than outlasting an opponent who won't back down, which can leave you more spent than what you'd gain ("know when to quit"). Chapters: 8, 12. Related: dark triad, Machiavellianism.
- **Weapons of influence (the 6+1 universal rules)** — Cialdini's rules of persuasion: reciprocity, scarcity, social proof, liking, authority, commitment & consistency, plus the seventh, Units — each resting on a psychological mechanism (often a named cognitive bias) and each paired in the book with a "how to defend oneself" countermeasure. Chapters: 2. Related: reciprocity, scarcity, social proof, liking, authority, commitment and consistency, units.
- **Wearing a mask** — Cooper's image for the fact that people's intentions aren't always clear, yet they leak unconscious signs of their real feelings (only FBI agents, perhaps, give no clue). Chapters: 17. Related: analyzing people, tells, micro-expressions.
- **"What is in it for me?"** — The counterpart's implicit question every negotiator must answer, aligning the proposal with the other party's interests to win consent. Chapters: 31. Related: negotiation, objection anticipation.
- **The white knight** — A mind-control game where the manipulator fabricates a problem then rushes to the rescue, becoming the only one who can solve the crises around them, to create emotional dependency; an expression of problem-reaction-solution. Chapters: 14. Related: problem-reaction-solution, emotional dependency.
- **White lie** — A lie told to motivate or please the deceived, usually without harming feelings (complimenting a haircut); acceptable in small social niceties but not when it lets harm compound or withholds a needed "wake-up call." Chapters: 24. Related: big lie, lying.
- **Yes-set** — Asking several questions the interlocutor can only answer "yes" to, creating a "light conditioning"/freezing effect so they also say yes to the real request; 4 or 5 harmless preamble questions suffice. Chapters: 4. Related: rhetorical questions/truisms, reinforced truisms, unverifiable statement.
- **Yes-man** — A low-level collaborator who always agrees with and praises the boss to keep their job or get promoted; Cooper's example of overdone positive reinforcement. Chapters: 4. Related: positive reinforcement, flattery.
