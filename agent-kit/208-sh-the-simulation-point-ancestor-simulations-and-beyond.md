# Ch 6 — The Simulation Point, Ancestor Simulations, and Beyond

## In one paragraph
This chapter caps Part I's engineering road map by defining Stage 11, the simulation point — the theoretical moment when a technological civilization can create virtual worlds and virtual beings indistinguishable from physical reality across all our senses — and then pivots from "could we build it?" to "are we already inside one?". Virk lays out a criteria table for knowing we have arrived, calls the simulation point a type of technological singularity, and then walks through Oxford philosopher Nick Bostrom's 2003 simulation argument: either no civilization ever reaches this point, or civilizations reach it but ban ancestor simulations, or civilizations reach it and run many — and in the third case, simulated beings so vastly outnumber biological ones that we are most likely simulated. Virk presents his own "simplified simulation argument" (the 9-worlds and 99-worlds counting versions behind Elon Musk's "one in billions" claim), covers the Kulczycki patch that adds pre-posthuman beings to the count, and Neil deGrasse Tyson's "simulations all the way down" extension. The chapter closes on consciousness: whether you take the materialist view (consciousness is neural activity) or the spiritual view (consciousness exists outside the body and is beamed into it), both converge on consciousness being information plus processing — computation — which, Virk argues, makes the simulation hypothesis more plausible under either worldview.

## Core claims
- **Virk argues** that the simulation point is reachable "over the next few decades and certainly within a hundred years" — a small time frame by Earth standards and minuscule by cosmic standards — just as no one a hundred years ago could have predicted today's computers and video games.
- **Virk argues** the remaining hurdles beyond the explicit stages (supporting billions of simultaneous players, storage, compression) are "mostly implementation challenges and not fundamental technology development milestones."
- **Virk argues** the simulation point is a type of technological singularity: after it, humanity will be different, able to experience any environment in a virtual world that appears real, with AI avatars passing the Metaverse Turing Test and being visually and kinesthetically indistinguishable.
- **Bostrom's argument is** that one of three possibilities must be true (probabilities summing to 100 percent): (1) no civilization ever reaches the posthuman/simulation point; (2) civilizations reach it but ancestor simulations are banned or not allowed; (3) civilizations reach it and create many ancestor simulations — and under (3), we are most likely in a simulation.
- **Bostrom's conclusion** (as Virk relays it): if any society ever makes it to the simulation point, it is more likely that we (all of us) are already inside a simulation. His statistical basis is beings, not worlds: simulated minds will vastly outnumber biological minds because creating new beings just requires additional computing power.
- **Bostrom's argument rests on** the assumption of substrate independence — consciousness can run on a biological substrate (brain) or a silicon substrate (computer) without significant difference. Virk flags this as "quite an assumption" to be revisited.
- **Bostrom's version is the NPC flavor**: in his model all (or the vast supermajority) of ancestors inside ancestor simulations are artificial consciousness — NPCs. His argument requires simulated beings to far outnumber biological ones, so there cannot be a one-to-one ratio. **Virk argues** the NPC/RPG distinction is an axis, not distinct categories — nothing in Bostrom's argument rules out some characters being players' avatars, leaving open the RPG flavor.
- **Virk's own opinion**: option three is the most likely scenario, if it is possible, "because most technologies developed by advanced civilizations end up being used, even if in limited form."
- **Virk argues** that if we are simulated beings, the characters around us are already passing the Metaverse Turing Test — so if that test will ever be passed, it has probably already been passed (in the reality "above" our simulation).
- **Virk argues** that materialist and spiritual views of consciousness share a commonality: consciousness is a set of information and a processing of that information (computation) — and if consciousness is information, it can be represented digitally and downloaded/uploaded to "servers," even if far beyond our current ability.
- **Virk claims** that, surprisingly, it is the religious views that are closest to the video game analogy: the physical world as a kind of "illusion" populated by conscious beings that exist outside the simulation.
- **Virk notes** that Bostrom shows a nonzero percent chance we are living in a simulation, though exact percentages can't be known; Virk's own updated estimate appears in the second edition's FAQ.

## Concepts
- **Simulation point** — Virk's formal definition: "a theoretical point at which a technological civilization can create virtual worlds (simulations) that are indistinguishable from physical reality (across all of our senses) and populated with virtual beings that are indistinguishable from physical beings." Reaching it is Stage 11 of the road map.
- **The Great Simulation** — a video game so real because it is based on incredibly sophisticated models and rendering techniques beamed directly into players' minds, where artificially generated players are indistinguishable from "real" biological players.
- **Ancestor simulations** — Bostrom's term for detailed simulations that advanced civilizations run of their forebears (or of people like their forebears) on super-powerful computers.
- **Base reality** — the original physical reality in which the simulating civilization exists; another term attributed to Bostrom.
- **Posthuman** — Bostrom's name for the stage at which a civilization can run ancestor simulations; analogous to, though not exactly the same as, Virk's simulation point.
- **Pre-posthuman** — Bostrom's (in Virk's word, awkward) term for civilizations that have not yet reached the simulation point; our current civilization (assuming we are not simulated) fits here.
- **Substrate independence** — the assumption that "mental states can supervene on any of a broad class of physical substrates": a system implementing the right computational structures and processes can be associated with conscious experiences, whether carbon-based neurons in a cranium or silicon processors in a computer.
- **Simulation argument** — Bostrom's trilemma (never reach the point / reach it but ban simulations / reach it and run many), with probabilities that must add up to 100 percent, one of which must be true.
- **Simulation hypothesis** — a phrase Bostrom coined to refer only to option three (that we are most likely in a simulation); the term is not in the actual paper and has since come to be used more generally for the idea of living in a Matrix-style or AI-style simulation, beyond Bostrom's original usage.
- **Simplified simulation argument** — Virk's name for the counting version used by Musk and others (Bostrom's logic without most of his math): if one base reality spawns many indistinguishable simulated worlds, the chance you are in the one physical world shrinks toward zero.
- **NPC version vs. RPG version** — the NPC flavor holds that all beings in the simulation are simulated/artificial consciousness; the RPG flavor allows characters to be avatars of conscious players outside the simulation. Virk treats these as an axis rather than distinct categories — one simulation can have both.
- **Metaverse Turing Test** — the test AI avatars would pass at the simulation point — and beyond passing it, they would also be visually and kinesthetically indistinguishable.
- **Simulations all the way down** — the idea (raised by Neil deGrasse Tyson, and others) that beings in each simulation could create sub-simulations, making the number of simulated beings almost infinite.
- **Digital consciousness vs. spiritual consciousness** — the materialist view roots consciousness in physical processes (neural activity, chemical reactions, potentially reproducible artificially); the Eastern mystical and Western religious views hold that consciousness exists independent of and outside the physical body, entering around birth and leaving at death (in some traditions, also during dreams).
- **Consciousness as information** — the commonality Virk extracts from both camps: "Consciousness is really a set of information and a processing of that information (which we can call computation)."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
1. **The billions-of-players scaling hurdle** — No MMORPG has been able to support billions of simultaneous individual players, but websites and apps like Facebook do. Counting the amount of other Earth-like planets in our galaxy alone, the number of players (NPCs and PCs) might jump into the trillions, requiring storage capability and compression algorithms for cloud servers. Virk uses this to show remaining hurdles are implementation challenges, not fundamental milestones.
2. **Table 1: Defining the Simulation Point** — nine criteria with readiness notes: (a) simulating a large game world — procedurally generated worlds partially exist, will improve with AI; (b) photorealistic 3D rendering — improvements on existing 3D models/textures, possibly AI-generated landscapes skipping current 3D modeling; (c) beaming the simulated world into eyes or minds — light-field rendering in early stages, mind broadcast yet to be invented; (d) haptics and emotions — some haptics tech exists, emotions/mind interfaces do not; (e) taking responses from our mind and reacting in the world — partially invented, early stages, long way to go; (f) capacity for very large numbers of online players — scaling beyond today but just an extension of existing tech; (g) storing player/character data outside the rendered world — very large data, needs improvements; (h) implanted memories — ability to change history and write to the brain, not yet invented; (i) NPCs — partially exists, needs improvement. Virk's point: some criteria are unmet, but reaching them within decades to a century is very likely.
3. **Von Neumann on the singularity** — Virk calls the simulation point a type of technological singularity, quoting von Neumann that after such a point "human affairs, as we know them, could not continue." Shows the simulation point as a civilizational discontinuity.
4. **The Thirteenth Floor reference** — AI beings within the simulation would appear, quoting the film again, "as real as you or me." Used to illustrate full indistinguishability of simulated beings.
5. **Bostrom's 2003 paper, "Are You Living in a Computer Simulation?"** — the groundbreaking paper by the Oxford philosopher that helped launch online debates about whether we live in a simulation; it doesn't specifically concern video games or science fiction, though it refers to both. Bostrom speculates later generations might run "a great many" detailed simulations of forebears, so "the vast majority of minds like ours" might belong not to the original race but to people simulated by its advanced descendants.
6. **The trilemma spelled out with probabilities** — (1) No civilization ever makes it (it destroys itself, doesn't develop computer technology, or realistic simulations are impossible — in which case this leg is most likely): probability we're simulated is zero or thereabouts, because it may not be possible for anyone to create such realistic simulations — if it were possible, at least one civilization would eventually get there. (2) Civilizations make it but ancestor sims are banned/not allowed (an open question, though Bostrom gives a few ideas): probability close to zero — a rogue sim might exist but not enough for the statistical argument. (3) Civilizations make it and create many: Bostrom "flat out states that we are most likely in a simulation." The three probabilities must add up to 100 percent — one must be true.
7. **Figure 19: 9 sims + 1 base reality** — Virk's simplified argument. Creating a new simulation is like winding up a new instance of a video game server (though admittedly a very powerful server by our current technology standards). Ten total worlds; if you cannot tell whether you are in a physical or simulated world, the chance you're in a simulated world is 9 out of 10 (90 percent), and the chance you're in base reality is 1 in 10 (10 percent). Virk stresses that objectors overlook the key assumption: the virtual worlds are so advanced everything inside them seems real.
8. **Figure 20: 99 sims + 1 base reality** — 100 total worlds: 99 percent chance simulated, 1 percent base reality. Expanding to a thousand, a million, or a billion sims (done simply by adding more computing power) drives the chance of not being simulated infinitesimally small, and the chance of being simulated to 99.99 percent and higher.
9. **Elon Musk's "one in billions"** — Musk and many others have used the simplified simulation argument (Bostrom's logic without a lot of his math) to state that the chance we are not in a simulation is only one in billions — the endpoint of the Figure 19/20 scaling logic.
10. **Figure 21: the basic simulation argument (beings, not worlds)** — Bostrom's actual statistics count simulated minds, not simulated worlds. Right side: civilizations that haven't reached the posthuman stage/simulation point; left side: the civilization past it. With A = simulated beings and B = "real" beings in that civilization, A >> B ("much greater than"), because spinning up a new simulation just takes computing power; taking our own civilization as an example, simulated beings could number in the billions or trillions. The likelihood we are in a simulation is the ratio of simulated to actual beings.
11. **The Kulczycki patch** — Bostrom and collaborator Marcin Kulczycki issued a patch when it was pointed out the argument counted only real beings in the posthuman civilization. Beings in pre-posthuman civilizations (like ours, assuming we're not simulated) must be added; these civilizations either pass through the simulation point (as we likely will over coming decades or centuries) or go extinct — Virk considers it possible some civilizations never build computers or video games, but unlikely a technological civilization would lack some kind of computation. Adding these beings makes the real count larger, but simulated beings "can be cranked up almost infinitely very quickly," so A >> (B + C) still holds, though the ratio may be less skewed than initially.
12. **Neil deGrasse Tyson on Larry King** — in an interview with Larry King, while discussing Bostrom's argument, the physicist raises the idea that beings in each simulation could create sub-simulations (simulations within simulations), making the number of simulated beings almost infinite; he and others call this the "simulations all the way down" argument.
13. **The Metaverse-Turing-Test-already-passed inference** — if we are simulated beings, the characters around us already pass the test; so if it will ever be passed, it probably already has been — in the reality "above" our simulation, while we try to solve it "inside our simulation" on computers that could, for all practical purposes, be much more simple simulated computers. Relatedly: if, as we interact with other individuals in the world around us, we cannot tell whether they are computer-generated beings or AI, then we can assume that whoever created the simulation was able to build powerful computers and get to the simulation point.
14. **Crick discouraging Koch** — Max Tegmark (MIT professor, author of *Life 3.0: Being Human in the Age of Artificial Intelligence*) recalls that his friend, neuroscientist and brain researcher Christof Koch, was discouraged from researching consciousness by one of his mentors, Nobel laureate Francis Crick (codiscoverer of the structure of DNA), because consciousness is a thorny issue in Western science and should be avoided. Virk uses this to show we lack even a definition of consciousness — human or digital — even as we talk of simulated characters, self-aware robots, or AIs like Data or HAL 9000.
15. **The two materialist positions** — Western materialist science attributes consciousness to either: (1) a subjective experience outside the purview of the "physical sciences," and so ignored (the perspective of many scientists over the past hundred years or so); or (2) the result of physical processes — chemical reactions, neural activity in the brain — reproducible artificially once neural activity is fully mapped (gaining favor with the emerging field of neuroscience).
16. **The Swiss brain-modeling group** — a group of researchers in Switzerland believe they have created a scaled computer model that reproduces and acts like the brain by modeling an individual's neurons; by modeling a rat's brain — "only" 10,000 neurons with an associated 30 million neural connections — they believe they could model an entire brain within a few years. The human brain comprises networks connecting 10^12 neurons through 10^15 synapses; as of the book's writing no one has modeled that many connections, "but it doesn't mean that it's as far off as we might think." Illustrates the materialist "copy of a person" path.
17. **Kurzweil's patterns** — Ray Kurzweil makes the point (as many others have) that it's not just consciousness that is information: your body's cells have been replaced many times — you are literally not the same physical person you were years ago — so some "information" (Kurzweil's "patterns") defines who you are and tells cells how to grow. This applies to diseased cells too: theoretically, if all cells are replaced, diseased cells should disappear on their own; they don't, because of patterns of information. Virk's takeaway: even biological entities, at their core, follow a computational structure.
18. **The three religious soul models** — from the spiritual viewpoint, the information takes various forms: a soul that is indestructible in Hinduism; a soul that survives death and lives in eternity in heaven or hell in the Judeo-Christian-Islamic traditions; no permanent soul at all in Buddhism, where it is seen as a "bag of karma." In all cases, whatever information is associated with the "player" is beamed down and linked through some mysterious process to the biological machine that is our body.
19. **Where consciousness would live if outside the body** — Virk notes the spiritual view (consciousness enters the body around birth, leaves at death, and in some traditions leaves during dreams) is now held by an increasing number of scientists, including Donald Hoffman (whose ideas come in Chapter 14). The materialist/spiritual debate predates Max Planck, "the grandfather of quantum physics." The simulation hypothesis would posit that consciousness outside the body is stored outside the rendered world — a world we cannot see because we are caught inside the simulation, as avatars or as NPCs.

## Key quotes

- "One thing that later generations might do with their super-powerful computers is run detailed simulations of their forebears or of people like their forebears. Because their computers would be so powerful, they could run a great many such simulations…. Then it could be the case that the vast majority of minds like ours do not belong to the original race but rather to people simulated by the advanced descendants of an original race." — Bostrom, from the 2003 paper, defining ancestor simulations.
- "The idea is that mental states can supervene on any of a broad class of physical substrates. Provided a system implements the right sort of computational structures and processes, it can be associated with conscious experiences. It is not an essential property of consciousness that it is implemented on carbon-based biological neural networks inside a cranium: silicon-based processors inside a computer could in principle do the trick as well." — Bostrom on substrate independence, the key assumption Virk flags.
- "as real as you or me." — from The Thirteenth Floor, quoted again for how AI beings within the simulation would appear.
- "You cannot tell if you are in a physical world or a virtual world." — the key assumption Virk says objectors to the simplified argument overlook, repeated "for emphasis."
- "one in billions" — Musk's stated chance (via the simplified simulation argument) that we are in base reality.
- "If the number of simulated beings in all the simulations is much larger than the number of simulated beings in all the real-world civilizations, then we are still more likely inside a simulation than not!" — Virk on why the argument survives the Kulczycki patch (A >> B + C).
- "This means that if the Metaverse Turing Test will ever be passed, then it has probably already been passed!" — Virk's inference from Bostrom's conclusion.
- "Consciousness is really a set of information and a processing of that information (which we can call computation)." — the commonality Virk identifies between the materialist and spiritual views.
- "As we start to view consciousness and biological reality as a type of information, the simulation hypothesis seems more and more likely, whether you subscribe to a materialist or a spiritual worldview!" — the chapter's consciousness-section conclusion.
- "Surprisingly, it is the religious views that are closest to the video game analogy: that our physical world is a kind of 'illusion,' populated by conscious beings that exist outside the simulation." — Virk, setting up the book's religion sections.

## Thread
Part I has been a builder's manual — the staged road map from early video games toward the Matrix. Chapter 6 is the capstone and the hinge: it names the destination (Stage 11, the simulation point), gives it a formal definition and a checklist (Table 1), and then flips the telescope around — if building this is feasible for us within decades or a century, Bostrom's argument says someone has probably already built it, and we are probably inside it. Virk carefully positions Bostrom's NPC-flavored argument within his own broader NPC/RPG axis, keeping the door open for conscious players. The chapter then surfaces the assumption everything hangs on — substrate independence — and turns it into the consciousness question, arguing that materialist and spiritual accounts both reduce to information plus computation. The closing section ("Does Simulation Explain Our World?") explicitly hands off to the rest of the book: the next two sections will show how the simulation hypothesis explains quantum-physics paradoxes and the claims of Eastern mystics and Western religions, and the final part will cover evidence of computation, skeptics, and the big philosophical implications — with the hypothesis pitched as the one model tying science, philosophy, spirituality, and religion together coherently.
