# Ch 5 — A Pocketful of Miracles

## In one paragraph
Talbot argues that miracles — from the liquefying blood of San Gennaro to the invulnerability of the eighteenth-century French convulsionaires — are not supernatural interventions but expressions of forces latent in the human mind, chiefly psychokinesis (PK). He builds an escalating case: laboratory evidence (Jahn and Dunne's PEAR experiments) shows most people can exert small PK effects; historical and eyewitness testimony shows PK-like healing of bones, immunity to torture and fire, and even materialization of blood, salt, jewelry and food out of thin air. To explain feats that PK alone cannot cover (where does the deflected energy go?), he invokes Bohm's implicate order and Grof's suggestion that certain states of consciousness can intervene directly in reality's "generative matrix," reprogramming the "cosmic motion picture projector" itself. The laws of physics thereby become habits — deeply ingrained but suspendable programs in the holomovement — and consensus reality becomes a "reality-field," a mutually constructed hologram sustained by the interconnected consciousness of all things, like Tiller's Star Trek "holodeck." The chapter closes with the most radical evidence — vanishing groves of trees, Talbot's own "umbrella incident," Sai Baba's teleportation, Indridason's dematerializing limbs — implying that even our bodies are holographic projections and that "virtually anything is possible."

## Core claims
- Talbot argues that nearly all miracles performed by saints and religious wonder-workers have been duplicated by psychics, suggesting miracles are produced by forces deep in the human mind latent in all of us — not by truly supernatural causes. He notes Thurston (priest, author of *The Physical Phenomena of Mysticism*) shared this reluctance to attribute miracles to the supernatural, and that many stigmatists (Padre Pio, Therese Neumann) were also renowned psychics.
- Talbot suggests the San Gennaro liquefaction is more likely caused by the intense devotion and belief of the witnessing crowd than by the saint himself.
- Rogo believes PK accounts for the more dramatic aspects of stigmata: ordinary biology can break small vessels for superficial bleeding, but only PK explains the rapid appearance of large wounds.
- Bohm argues meaning is simultaneously mental and physical — the body responds to meaning just as consciousness does — so meaning is the "bridge" between the two sides of reality. He holds that electrons actively use information (mindlike, highly complex entities), so responsiveness to meaning is a property of all matter, and PK could arise when mental processes focus on meanings "in harmony" with those guiding the material system. This would be noncausal — a nonlocal "resonance of meanings," requiring a "super" nonlocality deeper than ordinary quantum nonlocality (which Bohm believes cannot account for PK or telepathy).
- Jahn and Dunne believe consciousness, like all physical processes, has a wave/particle duality: particlelike consciousness is localized in the head; wavelike consciousness can produce remote influence effects, one of which is PK. They believe reality itself results from the interface of the wavelike aspects of consciousness with the wave patterns of matter, that PK is an information exchange / resonance rather than a transmitted force, and that concepts (electron, time, consciousness) are only "information-organizing categories" with no independent status — all theories being metaphors.
- Jahn and Dunne's PEAR data show, they claim, that PK is a small but real ability present in the majority of people, with stable individual "signatures."
- Jahn and Dunne do not identify themselves with the holographic model (their theory in fact differs from Bohm's thinking in several significant ways), but they recognize the overlap: Jahn sees "some commonality" in the basic reliance on wave mechanical behavior, and Dunne offers the metaphor of an individual consciousness as "the laser of a particular frequency that intersects with a specific pattern in the cosmic hologram."
- Jahn and Dunne believe (unlike Bohm) that subatomic particles do not possess distinct reality until consciousness enters the picture — physicists may be creating particles rather than discovering them (citing the anomalon and the neutrino-mass story). Jahn: "we are indeed creating reality by any reasonable definition of the term."
- Talbot argues that Tom's hypnotic perception of the watch shows our perceptions are not based solely on the five senses — we can tap the senses of others (telepathy, remote viewing) and even reality itself, and consensus reality may be a collectively constructed "reality-field," with normal waking consciousness itself a kind of hypnosis (supporting datum: unlike other altered states of consciousness, hypnosis is associated with no unusual EEG patterns — physiologically, the mental state it most closely resembles is normal waking consciousness).
- Grof believes certain unusual states of consciousness could mediate direct experience of, and intervention in, the implicate order — modifying phenomena by influencing their generative matrix; this would bypass rules like inertia and explain what PK alone cannot.
- Talbot (following Peat and the holographic idea) argues the laws of physics are habits — deeply ingrained programs in the holomovement, like Shainberg's vortices — that can under special circumstances be suspended (fire immunity being the proof case). The deeper and more emotionally charged our beliefs, the greater the changes we can make in body and reality.
- Bohm privately admits believing the universe is all "thought" and reality exists only in what we think, but prefers not to speculate on specific miracles. Pribram believes multiple potential realities exist and consciousness has some latitude in choosing which manifests ("I don't believe anything goes"). Watson goes further: reality is "in a very large part a construct of the imagination" (and he is no longer convinced any current physics, including the holographic idea, explains supernormal abilities).
- Globus thinks the mind constructs concrete reality from the raw material of the implicate, and that an infinite number of potential realities are enfolded there; dreams, ordinary reality, and Castaneda-style "separate realities" are constructed by the same holographic brain mechanisms and are fundamentally the same.
- Josephson (Nobelist) proposes objective reality is produced out of the collective memories of the human race, while anomalous events manifest the individual will.
- Bohm thinks telepathy and remote viewing are forms of psychokinesis: PK = resonance of meaning from mind to object; telepathy = mind to mind; remote viewing = object to mind ("inverse psychokinesis").
- Jahn and Dunne define consciousness liberally: anything that generates, receives, or utilizes information — animals, viruses, DNA, machines, "nonliving" objects — may take part in creating reality. This, Talbot says, would explain psychometry.
- Talbot argues materializations (blood, salt, objects, nutrients) imply mind can create matter out of nothing; and whole-reality transformations (Tia's grove, the umbrella incident) suggest miracles involve "parts" only because we are programmed to see the world in parts.
- Tiller believes the universe is a "holodeck" created by the integration of all living things: "we've created the laws that govern it... we're also creating the physics as we go along."
- Talbot proposes stable particles (electrons) are old, deeply ingrained reality-fields, while plastic ones (anomalons) are recent, inchoate reality-fields — which would also explain the aspirin (US vs. British) anomaly and why blood miracles only begin with fourteenth-century San Gennaro (Rogo's observation). Indeed, if the universe is a holodeck, everything that appears stable and eternal — from the laws of physics to the substance of galaxies — must be viewed as reality-fields, "will-o'-the-wisps no more or less real than the props in a giant, mutually shared dream"; all permanence would be illusory and only consciousness eternal, "the consciousness of the living universe."
- Talbot concedes the alternative — that only anomalous events are reality-fields and the world at large is stable — but argues it can never be proved: once two or more people can create a shared reality, consensus verification fails as a litmus test; it becomes a matter of personal philosophy (Jahn: only interaction-created reality is real; Globus: "a nice theory postulates existence," admitted as bias).
- Talbot, citing his own experiences, sides with Don Juan: "We are perceivers... we have no solidity" — no reality exists above that created by the integration of all consciousnesses; the holographic universe can be sculpted in virtually limitless ways by mind. Bilocation and dematerialization suggest even our bodies are holographic projections. Keith Floyd: it may be consciousness that creates the appearance of the brain, not the reverse.

## Concepts
- **Psychokinesis (PK)** — the psychic ability to alter physical matter with the mind; the chapter's prime suspect behind miracles, from the San Gennaro blood to gravity-defying stigmata flows and machine-jinxing.
- **Active/objective meaning** — Bohm's idea that meaning is simultaneously mental and physical (a thought is at the same time a neurophysiological, chemical, physical activity); meaning is the link or "bridge" between mind and matter, active also in computer chips and subatomic particles.
- **Quantum wave as information (ship on automatic pilot)** — Bohm's alternative to the orthodox "ocean wave pushing a Ping-Pong ball" view: the quantum wave doesn't push the electron; like radar guiding a ship on autopilot, it provides information about the environment which the electron uses to maneuver on its own — making the electron mindlike and complex, not a structureless point.
- **Resonance of meanings / super nonlocality** — Bohm's proposed mechanism for PK: not a causal force but a nonlocal resonance between the meanings in a mind and those guiding a material system; similar to but deeper than the twin-photon nonlocality of chapter 2 (mere quantum nonlocality can't account for PK or telepathy).
- **Random event generator (REG)** — a device using an unpredictable natural process (e.g., radioactive decay) to produce strings of random binary numbers; effectively an automatic coin-flipper producing enormous numbers of flips quickly; PEAR's main PK instrument.
- **PK "signatures"** — the consistently distinctive, idiosyncratic result patterns each volunteer produced in PEAR experiments, persisting across different experiments (REG and pinball), implying PK ability varies between individuals like any talent but stays stable within one person.
- **Wave/particle duality of consciousness** — Jahn and Dunne's postulate: since all known physical processes have wave/particle duality, consciousness does too; particlelike = localized in the head, wavelike = capable of remote influence (PK, remote perception) and of availing itself "of all of space and time."
- **Pauli effect** — physicists' joking name for Wolfgang Pauli's legendary propensity to make laboratory equipment malfunction by mere presence (or even by passing in a train).
- **Gremlin effect** — the tendency of carefully tested equipment to inexplicably malfunction at absurdly inopportune moments, reported by pilots, aircrew, and military operators; Jahn and Dunne suggest it may be unconscious PK.
- **Convulsionaires** — the Jansenist worshipers at Abbé Paris's tomb who fell into contagious trancelike convulsions during which they displayed invulnerability to torture, clairvoyance, eyes-bandaged reading, levitation, and fire immunity.
- **Human salamanders** — term for fire-immune convulsionaires (from the medieval "salamander," a mythological lizard believed to live in fire); the two most famous were Marie Sonnet and Gabrielle Moler.
- **Reprogramming the cosmic motion picture projector** — Talbot's image for Grof's proposal: beyond moving objects, mind may reach into the implicate order and reprogram the generative matrix that produced the objects, bypassing even rules like inertia.
- **Laws of physics as habits / material neurosis** — the holographic view that matter and physical law are constantly born anew out of the implicate (like a fountain's shape from flowing water); Peat calls repetition of pattern a "material neurosis" — chairs and tables are habits in the flux, and even physical laws are habits (vast whirlpools of inertial power, like Shainberg's vortices) that can be suspended.
- **Reality-field** — Talbot's term for a reality collectively constructed via extrasensory interconnectedness (as Anne and Bill built their shared ocean world); consensus reality, subatomic particles, laws of physics, and even our bodies may all be reality-fields — older ones (electrons) fixed, newer ones (anomalons) still plastic.
- **Remote viewing** — Puthoff and Targ's term for the ability to accurately describe what a distant test subject is seeing, simply by relaxing and reporting mental images; works even with no human observer at the target site.
- **Inverse psychokinesis** — Bohm's reading of remote viewing: a resonance of meanings from object to mind — the distant system's meanings act in the viewer to transmit an image of that system.
- **Mutual hypnosis** — Tart's procedure in which two skilled hypnotist-subjects hypnotize each other, deepening rapport until they co-construct a shared, fully sensory hallucinated reality.
- **Psychometry** — the ability to obtain information about an object's history simply by touching it; explained if the object is suffused with its own consciousness and connected, via the implicate, to everyone and everything associated with its existence.
- **Anomalon** — a recently discovered subatomic particle whose properties vary from laboratory to laboratory; Jahn and Dunne cite it as evidence that physicists may create rather than discover particles.
- **Inedia** — the supernormal ability to live without food (and even water); displayed by Therese Neumann for thirty-five years and by other stigmatists in Thurston's collection.
- **Materialization** — the creation of objects (blood, salt, stones, jewelry, ash, food, nutrients, tears) out of thin air; the miracle type that most rocks the conventional worldview.
- **Poltergeist** — a haunting typified by mysterious sounds and psychokinetic activity rather than apparitions; centering on people rather than places, so many parapsychologists (and Talbot, from personal experience) view them as manifestations of the focal person's unconscious PK.
- **Siddhis** — powers such as materialization described by Yogananda; he cautioned they are not always evidence of spiritual evolution: "whatever your powerful mind believes very intensely instantly comes to pass."
- **Holodeck** — Tiller's metaphor (from *Star Trek: The Next Generation*): an environment whose occupants can call up and alter any holographic simulation at will; the universe as a holodeck created by the integration of all living things.
- **Bilocation** — the ability, often associated with saints, to be in two places at once; Sai Baba reportedly goes one better, vanishing and instantly reappearing a hundred or more yards away.
- **Information-organizing categories** — Jahn and Dunne's status for all descriptive concepts (electron, wavelength, consciousness, time, frequency): useful organizing tools with no independent existence; all theories, their own included, are only metaphors.
- **Aura** — the energy field around living things that Talbot says he spontaneously began seeing at seventeen, whose pattern and colors let him determine things about a person's health.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **San Gennaro blood miracle (Naples, ongoing)** — Twice yearly (September and May) at the Duomo San Gennaro, a vial of brown crusty substance — allegedly the blood of St. Januarius, beheaded by Diocletian in A.D. 305 and collected as a relic by a serving woman — turns into bubbling bright-red liquid when the crowd shouts at it. In 1902 University of Naples scientists verified spectroscopically (passing light through it) that it is real blood; the fragile reliquary (blood ensconced there since the end of the thirteenth century; first written account of the public miracle, 1389) can't be opened for further tests. When the blood fails to liquefy it is a bad omen: failures preceded the eruption of Vesuvius, the Napoleonic invasion of Naples, and more recently the worst earthquake in Italian history (1976) and the election of a communist city government in Naples (1978). Talbot's view: caused not by the saint but by the crowd's intense devotion and belief — i.e., PK.
- **Therese Neumann's gravity-defying blood** — Blood from the stigmata wounds in her feet always flowed toward her toes — as it would have flowed on the cross — regardless of foot position, so when she sat upright in bed it flowed upward against gravity. Witnessed by many, including U.S. servicemen stationed in postwar Germany. Talbot: PK is clearly a factor in phenomena accompanying stigmata.
- **Bohm's shadow-in-the-street thought experiment** — Walking at night, a looming shadow read as "assailant" triggers imagined activities plus inseparable biological processes (nerve excitation, rapid heartbeat, adrenaline, muscle tensing); read as "just a shadow," a different mental-biological set follows. Shows the body also responds to meaning — meaning is simultaneously mental and physical, the bridge between the two sides of reality.
- **Computer chip and plasma electrons (Bohm's examples of active meaning)** — A chip's information actively determines how currents flow; and the coordinated "dance" of electrons in a plasma cannot be explained by waves mechanically pushing particles (Ping-Pong balls on the ocean) — the quantum wave instead informs the electron like radar informs a ship on automatic pilot.
- **Founding of PEAR (Princeton, 1979)** — Robert G. Jahn, professor of aerospace sciences and dean emeritus of Princeton's School of Engineering and Applied Science, ex-consultant to NASA and the Department of Defense, author of *Physics of Electric Propulsion*, didn't believe in the paranormal until a student asked him to oversee her independent-study PK experiment; the results were so provocative he founded the Princeton Engineering Anomalies Research (PEAR) lab in 1979, which has since gathered more PK data than anyone else in the country. PEAR is funded in good part by the McDonnell Foundation (created by James S. McDonnell III of McDonnell Douglas), and the *New York Times Magazine* devoted an article to the work.
- **PEAR REG experiments (Jahn and Dunne)** — Volunteers sat before a random event generator (randomness from radioactive decay) and concentrated on skewing its heads/tails output. Over hundreds of thousands of trials, concentration alone produced a small but statistically significant effect. Two further findings: the ability was present in the majority of volunteers (most of us have some PK), and each volunteer produced consistently distinctive results — "signatures."
- **PEAR pinball-machine experiments (Jahn and Dunne)** — A device in a ten-foot-high, six-foot-wide glass-fronted vertical frame lets 9,000 three-quarter-inch marbles cascade around 330 nylon pegs into 19 collecting bins, normally forming a bell-curve distribution. Volunteers willing more balls into the outer bins produced a small but measurable shift over many runs — extending PK from microscopic processes to everyday-scale objects — and the same individual "signatures" from the REG work reappeared. Jahn and Dunne: individually the segments might be dismissed as near-chance, but "taken in concert the entire ensemble establishes an incontrovertible aberration of substantial proportions."
- **The Pauli effect** — Physicist Wolfgang Pauli's legendary jinxing of equipment: his mere presence would make glass apparatus explode or sensitive instruments crack in half. In one famous incident a physicist wrote that at least Pauli couldn't be blamed for a complicated apparatus's mysterious disintegration since he was absent — only to learn Pauli had been passing the laboratory in a train at the precise moment. Jahn and Dunne read this, and the pilots' "Gremlin effect," as possible unconscious PK.
- **The "resonance" testimony of PEAR volunteers** — The factor most frequently associated with successful PK performance was attaining a feeling of "resonance" with the machine; one volunteer described immersion, loss of self-awareness, and "being in a canoe" flowing with the device rather than controlling it. Supports Jahn and Dunne's model of PK as resonance/information exchange, not force.
- **Philippine psychokinetic surgeon (Lyall Watson)** — Watson (biologist, author of *Supernature*) witnessed a Philippine psychic healer who, without touching patients, held his hand about ten inches over the body and pointed — an incision appearing instantaneously. Once, when the man swept his finger more broadly than usual, Watson received an incision on the back of his own hand and bears the scar to this day. Shows PK operating on larger-scale physical change.
- **Darmstadt nuns' pelvic-fracture healing (reported by Rex Gardner, 1983, British Medical Journal)** — Gardner, a physician at Sunderland District General Hospital, England, presented contemporary miracle healings side by side with virtually identical seventh-century cases collected by the Venerable Bede. A Lutheran nun in Darmstadt, Germany, building a chapel, broke through a freshly cemented floor onto a wooden beam; X rays showed a compound pelvic fracture. Rather than weeks in traction, the nuns held an all-night prayer vigil, took her home two days later, and performed a laying on of hands — after which she stood free of pain, achieved full recovery in two weeks, and presented herself to her astonished doctor. Talbot suggests the nuns' unconscious collective PK did the work (since even Michelli's miraculous pelvis regeneration took months).
- **St. Wilfrid and the mason Bothelm (Hexham, seventh century, from Gardner via Bede)** — During construction of the church at Hexham, the mason Bothelm fell from a great height, breaking both arms and legs; as he lay dying, Wilfrid (bishop of Hexham) prayed over him and asked the other workmen to join. "The breath of life returned" and he healed rapidly. Talbot wonders whether Wilfrid was catalyst or whether it was the combined unconscious PK of the whole assemblage — since healing came only after the workmen joined.
- **Kahuna instantaneous bone healing (recorded by William Tufts Brigham)** — Brigham, curator of the Bishop Museum in Honolulu and noted botanist, recorded an incident witnessed by his friend J. A. K. Combs, whose grandmother-in-law was one of the most powerful women kahunas in Hawaii. At a party at her home a guest slipped in beach sand and broke his leg so severely the bone ends pressed visibly against the skin. Refusing hospital, the elderly kahuna knelt, straightened the leg, pushed on the fracture, prayed and meditated several minutes, then announced the healing finished. The man rose, walked — completely healed, leg showing no indication of the break.
- **The Jansenist miracles at Saint-Médard (Paris, from 1727)** — Jansenism (founded early seventeenth century; puritanical, Dutch-influenced Catholicism) was at odds with Rome and Louis XV, both viewing it as masked Protestantism; Jansenist leaders' skill at miraculous healings fed its popularity. When the revered deacon François de Paris died May 1, 1727, and was interred at Saint-Médard, worshipers at his tomb reported cures of cancerous tumors, paralysis, deafness, arthritis, rheumatism, ulcerous sores, persistent fevers, prolonged hemorrhaging, and blindness — plus contagious involuntary convulsions that filled the streets with writhing "convulsionaires." Crowds thronged day and night for years, miracles still being reported two decades later; in 1733 public records noted over 3,000 volunteers were needed just to assist the convulsionaires (e.g., protecting female participants' modesty during seizures). Witnesses came from every social stratum and institution; even Roman Catholic Church investigators with a vested interest in refuting the miracles went away confirming them (the Church later conceded the miracles but attributed them to the devil).
- **Montgeron's documentation — Jeanne Maulet** — Louis-Basile Carré de Montgeron, member of the Paris Parliament, witnessed enough to fill four thick volumes, *La Vérité des Miracles* (1737). Twenty-year-old convulsionaire Jeanne Maulet, leaning against a stone wall, took one hundred blows to the stomach from a thirty-pound hammer wielded by "a very strong man" from the crowd (convulsionaires asked to be tortured because it relieved the pain of the convulsions). Montgeron then tested the hammer on the wall itself: at the twenty-fifth blow the stone came loose and fell through, leaving an aperture more than half a foot in size.
- **The arched woman and the fifty-pound stone (Montgeron)** — A convulsionaire bent backward into an arc, her lower back supported on "the sharp point of a peg," had a fifty-pound stone attached to a rope hoisted to "an extreme height" and dropped repeatedly onto her stomach. She was unaffected, held her position effortlessly, cried "Strike harder, harder!" throughout, and walked away without a mark on her back. Talbot uses this case to pose the inertia problem: where did the deflected energy go?
- **General convulsionaire invulnerability (Montgeron and others)** — They could not be hurt by metal rods, chains, or timbers; the strongest men could not choke them; some were crucified and showed no wounds afterward; they could not be cut or punctured by knives, swords, or hatchets. In one incident an iron drill's sharpened point was held against a convulsionaire's stomach and hammered so violently it seemed it would "penetrate through to the spine and rupture all the entrails" — but didn't; she maintained "an expression of perfect rapture," crying "Oh, that does me good! Courage, brother; strike twice as hard, if you can!" Talbot's PK reading: the convulsionaires may have unconsciously taken control of the chains, timbers, and knives, stopping them at the precise moment of impact (hence no marks or bruises), and the hands of would-be stranglers may have been held in place by PK — they thought they were squeezing flesh but "were really only flexing in the nothingness."
- **Other convulsionaire talents** — Some became clairvoyant ("discern hidden things"); others read with eyes closed and tightly bandaged; levitations were reported — the abbé Bescherand from Montpellier was so "forcibly lifted into the air" during convulsions that witnesses could not hold him down.
- **Intelligentsia and the Jansenist miracles** — Pascal's niece had a severe eye ulcer vanish within hours via a Jansenist miracle. When Louis XV closed the Saint-Médard cemetery, Voltaire quipped: "God was forbidden, by order of the King, to work any miracles there." Hume, in *Philosophical Essays*, wrote there was never "so great a number of miracles ascribed to one person," many "immediately proved upon the spot, before judges of unquestioned credit and distinction, in a learned age."
- **McDougall's collective-PK hypothesis (1920s)** — Harvard psychologist William McDougall suggested religious miracles might result from the collective psychic powers of large numbers of worshipers — anticipating Talbot's reading that the combined fervor of all present (not one individual) created the Jansenist effects.
- **St. Francis of Paula's fire immunity (from Thurston)** — Could hold burning embers unharmed; at his 1519 canonization hearings eight eyewitnesses testified they saw him walk unharmed through the roaring flames of a furnace to repair one of the furnace's broken walls.
- **Shadrach, Meshach, and Abednego (Old Testament)** — Refused to worship Nebuchadnezzar's statue and were thrown into a furnace so "exceeding hot" it burned up the men who threw them in; through faith they emerged unscathed — hair unsinged, clothing unharmed, no smell of fire. Talbot notes challenges to faith (like Louis XV's against the Jansenists) have engendered miracles more than once.
- **Brigham's lava walk with three kahunas (Hawaii, near erupting Kilauea)** — Three kahunas promised to demonstrate fire immunity and offered to confer it on Brigham. They chose a 150-foot-wide lava flow cooled enough to bear weight but with patches of incandescence coursing through its surface. After lengthy protective prayers, the oldest kahuna crossed unharmed. Brigham, refusing to remove his boots, was shoved and forced to run across; one boot burned off and his socks caught fire, but his feet were completely unharmed ("I had a sensation of intense heat on my face and body, but almost no sensation in my feet"). The kahunas rolled with laughter; Brigham laughed too, "never so relieved" in his life.
- **Marie Sonnet ("human salamander")** — In the presence of numerous witnesses including Montgeron, she stretched herself on two chairs over a blazing fire and remained half an hour, neither she nor her clothing showing ill effects. Another time she sat with her feet in a brazier of burning coals: shoes and stockings burned off, feet unharmed.
- **Gabrielle Moler** — Impervious to sword thrusts and shovel blows; could stick her head into a roaring hearth fire and hold it there uninjured — afterward her clothing was so hot it could barely be touched, yet hair, eyelashes, and eyebrows were never singed. (Talbot's aside: "No doubt she was great fun at parties.")
- **The Camisards (Cévennes valley, late 1600s)** — Huguenot resistors under Louis XIV's purge displayed similar invulnerabilities. The prior Abbé du Chayla complained in an official report to Rome that nothing harmed them: musket balls were found flattened between their clothing and skin; hands closed on burning coals were unharmed; wrapped head to toe in oil-soaked cotton and set alight, they did not burn. The Camisard leader Claris climbed a pyre, had it lit before six hundred witnesses, and kept delivering his ecstatic speech as flames rose above his head; when the pyre was consumed he remained unharmed, no mark on hair or clothing. Jean Cavalier, colonel of the French troops sent against them, later exiled to England, wrote *A Cry from the Desert* (1707) on the events. Du Chayla was eventually murdered in a Camisard retaliatory raid — possessing, unlike some of them, no special invulnerability.
- **Bernadette of Lourdes and the candle (timed by Dr. Dozous)** — In ecstasy, her hand dropped so close to a burning candle that flames licked around her fingers; Dr. Dozous, municipal physician of Lourdes, timed it: a full ten minutes before she came out of trance and removed her hand. Dozous: "I saw it with my own eyes. But I swear, if anyone had tried to make me believe such a story I would have laughed him to scorn."
- **Nathan Coker, blacksmith (New York Herald, September 7, 1871)** — An elderly Negro blacksmith in Easton, Maryland; before a committee including several doctors he heated an iron shovel to incandescence and held it against the soles of his feet until it cooled, licked the red-hot shovel's edge, and poured melted lead shot into his mouth, letting it run over teeth and gums until it solidified. Doctors examined him after each feat: no trace of injury.
- **The Tennessee boy (K. R. Wissen, 1927)** — On a hunting trip in the Tennessee mountains, New York physician K. R. Wissen watched a twelve-year-old boy handle red-hot irons out of a fireplace with impunity; the boy said he discovered the ability by accident, picking up a red-hot horseshoe in his uncle's blacksmith shop.
- **Mohotty's fire pit (the Grosvenors / National Geographic)** — The pit of flaming embers the Grosvenors watched Mohotty walk through was twenty feet long and measured 1,328 degrees Fahrenheit on the National Geographic team's thermometers. (Referenced back from the previous chapter's discussion.)
- **Ceylonese iron-pot ritual (Leonard Feinberg, Atlantic Monthly, May 1959)** — Dr. Feinberg of the University of Illinois reported witnessing a Ceylonese fire-walking ritual in which natives carried red-hot iron pots on their heads unharmed. Talbot: where does the heat energy go while the pot rests against hair and flesh?
- **Appalachian Pentecostals (Berthold Schwarz, Psychiatric Quarterly)** — Psychiatrist Schwarz reported watching Pentecostals hold their hands in an acetylene flame without harm — one of "literally hundreds of credible accounts of fire immunity."
- **The anomalon** — A recently discovered subatomic particle whose properties vary from laboratory to laboratory — "imagine owning a car that had a different color and different features depending on who drove it." Jahn and Dunne cite it as evidence that a particle's reality depends on who finds/creates it.
- **The neutrino's changing properties** — Pauli proposed the massless neutrino in the 1930s to solve a radioactivity problem; evidence of its existence came in 1957. When physicists later realized a *massive* neutrino would solve thornier problems, evidence of a small measurable mass duly appeared in 1980 — but only in Soviet laboratories; U.S. laboratories found none, a split persisting through the better part of the 1980s (other labs have now duplicated the Soviet findings; still unresolved). Talbot asks whether the differing properties reflect the changing expectations and cultural biases of the physicists searching.
- **Tom and the invisible daughter (Talbot's firsthand account, mid-1970s)** — At a gathering at his father's house, a professional hypnotist put Tom (a friend of Talbot's father, meeting the hypnotist for the first time) into deep trance — making him see a giraffe, eat a potato as an apple — then told him his teenage daughter Laura would be invisible on waking. With Laura standing directly before his chair, Tom looked right through her (despite her giggles). The hypnotist, hidden behind Laura, pressed a concealed object against the small of her back; Tom, "staring directly through Laura's stomach," identified it as a watch and read aloud its inscription — the owner's name (unknown to everyone present) and message — verified when the watch was passed around. Tom said afterward Laura had been absolutely invisible; he'd have never known he wasn't perceiving consensus reality. Talbot: Tom's perception wasn't coming through his five senses — possibly telepathically from the hypnotist's mind.
- **Sir William Barrett's hypnotized girl** — The British physicist bandaged a hypnotized girl's eyes, told her she would taste everything he tasted, and stood behind her: salt made her sputter ("What for are you putting salt in my mouth?"); sugar — "That's better... Sweet"; then mustard, pepper, ginger, etc., each named and apparently tasted by the girl as he put them in his own mouth.
- **German hypnosis study, 1950s (cited by Leonid Vasiliev, *Experiments in Distant Influence*)** — The hypnotized subject tasted what the hypnotist tasted, blinked when a light was flashed in the hypnotist's eyes, sneezed when he sniffed ammonia, heard the ticking of a watch held to his ear, and felt pain when he pricked himself with a needle — all safeguarded against normal sensory cues.
- **Puthoff and Targ's remote viewing (Stanford Research Institute, California)** — In a now-famous series, physicists Harold Puthoff and Russell Targ found just about everyone they tested could "remote-view" — accurately describe what a distant test subject was seeing — simply by relaxing and describing incoming mental images. Duplicated by dozens of laboratories around the world; probably a widespread latent ability in all of us.
- **Jahn's Paris remote viewing (PEAR)** — Jahn served as receiver for a colleague in Paris (a city he has never visited); besides a bustling street he saw an image of a knight in armor — the sender turned out to be standing before a government building ornamented with statuary of historical military figures, one of them a knight in armor.
- **Unconscious physiological interconnection studies** — An electric shock given to a person in one room registers in the polygraph readings of a person in another room; a light flashed in one subject's eyes registers in the EEG of a subject isolated in another room; and a test subject's finger blood volume (measured by plethysmograph, a sensitive autonomic indicator) changes when a "sender" in another room encounters a known person's name in a list of mostly unknown names.
- **Charles Tart's mutual hypnosis experiment (UC Davis)** — Tart had two graduate students, Anne and Bill — both able to enter deep trance and both skilled hypnotists — hypnotize each other, reasoning the hypnotist-subject rapport would be strengthened. Opening their eyes mutually hypnotized, everything first looked gray, then gave way to a jointly experienced beach of unearthly beauty: sand sparkling like diamonds, a sea of enormous frothing bubbles glistening like champagne, translucent crystalline rocks pulsing with internal light. They explored together; when they fell silent to Tart, they reported they *were* talking in the shared world — Tart believes some paranormal communication was involved (later they found they'd shared details for which no verbal stimuli existed on the tapes, concluding they must actually have been "in" those locales). Across sessions the constructed worlds were as real and fully sensory as waking reality; space was elastic beyond words; they often forgot to sculpt their own bodies, existing as floating faces or heads (Anne: "I had to kind of conjure up a hand"). The realness eventually frightened them; they stopped, and Bill gave up hypnosis entirely. Talbot: a perfect example of a holographic reality — a 3-D construct created out of interconnectedness, sustained by consciousness, plastic as thought.
- **Remote viewing without observers, and sealed boxes** — Experiments show people accurately describe distant locations with no human observers present, and can identify contents of a sealed box randomly selected from a group (contents unknown to anyone) — meaning we can tap not just other people's senses but reality itself for information.
- **Kamro's blood (Gardner report; Ruth Coggin, Pakistan, 1982)** — English physician Ruth Coggin was visited by Kamro, a thirty-five-year-old Pakistani woman eight months pregnant, suffering bleeding and intermittent abdominal pain most of her pregnancy; she refused hospitalization until two days later, when severe bleeding forced emergency admission. Blood loss was "very heavy," feet and abdomen pathologically swollen; after "another heavy bleed" Coggin performed a cesarean — copious dark blood flooded out, Kamro had virtually no clotting ability, and "deep pools of unclotted blood" filled her bed after a healthy daughter was delivered. Coggin obtained only two pints to transfuse, then resorted to prayer ("I had seen Jesus heal this condition before and was sure He was going to heal her"). Kamro stabilized despite hours of continued brisk bleeding; forty-eight hours after the operation her blood began to clot; ten days later she went home with her baby. Coggin — and Gardner after examining the documentation — concluded she lost more than her total blood volume; since humans cannot produce new blood that fast, Talbot draws the "unsettling conclusion" that Kamro's new blood materialized out of thin air.
- **The Indonesian poltergeist / Alin (Watson, Timor Timur, June 1974)** — Watson, en route to see a *matan do'ok* (Indonesian rainmaking wonder-worker), was diverted by reports of an unusually active *buan* (evil spirit) in a village house occupied by a couple, their two small boys, and the husband's unmarried younger half-sister Alin — lighter-complexioned, almost Chinese in feature (hence unmarried), treated with indifference, and immediately obvious to Watson as the source of the disturbance. At dinner: the eight-year-old boy screamed as the back of his hand bled — a semicircle of fresh punctures like a human bite but with a diameter larger than the boy's own (Alin was at the fire opposite); the lamp flame turned blue and flared; a shower of salt poured slowly down over the food until it was inedible, appearing to begin in midair about four feet above the table; loud rapping issued from the table, which wobbled, bucked "like the lid on a box containing some wild animal," and flipped on its side. Watson fled with the family, then returned and searched for trickery — finding none. Classic poltergeist: many parapsychologists hold these are manifestations of the focal person's unconscious PK.
- **A. R. G. Owen's poltergeist survey** — In *Can We Explain the Poltergeist*, Owen (fellow and lecturer in Mathematics at Trinity College, Cambridge) gives numerous examples of objects materializing out of thin air in poltergeist cases dating from A.D. 530 to modern times; small stones, not salt, are the most common materialized objects.
- **Talbot's own poltergeist (childhood onward)** — The new house his parents built became the site of an active haunting; the poltergeist followed Talbot to college, its activity tracking his moods (malicious when angry or low, impish when brighter): socks draped over house plants in good moods; objects hurled or broken in dark ones; his mother says pots and pans jumped from the kitchen table to the floor when he was a toddler. Materializations began at age six with inexplicable night showers of gravel on the roof; later it pelted him indoors with small polished stones and beach-worn shards of drift glass, and materialized coins, a necklace, and odder trifles. Usually he saw only aftermaths — e.g., a pile of cold sauceless spaghetti noodles falling on his chest during a nap in his New York apartment, with no open windows/doors, no one in the apartment, no sign of cooking or break-in. In 1976 he watched an actual materialization: a small brown object popped into existence in midair inches below his study ceiling, zoomed down at a sharp angle, and landed at his feet — a piece of brown drift glass, possibly from a beer bottle. (He has written about these experiences in his book *Beyond the Quantum*, and says he accepts the unconscious-PK theory of poltergeists because of them.)
- **Sathya Sai Baba's materializations (studied by Erlendur Haraldsson)** — The sixty-four-year-old Indian holy man of Andhra Pradesh plucks lockets, rings, and jewelry out of the air as gifts, materializes endless Indian delicacies and sweets, and pours volumes of vibhuti (sacred ash) from his hands — witnessed by thousands, including scientists and magicians, with no trickery ever detected. Haraldsson (psychologist, University of Iceland) studied him for over ten years, publishing *Modern Miracles: An Investigative Report on Psychic Phenomena Associated with Sathya Sai Baba*; he admits he cannot conclusively rule out sleight of hand but offers strong contrary evidence: (1) materialization on request — asked what a "double rudraksha" was, Sai Baba impatiently closed his fist, waved it a second or two, and opened it on an acorn-like double rudraksha (two seeds grown together like a twin orange); when Haraldsson asked to keep it, Sai Baba enclosed it in both hands, blew on it, and returned it capped top and bottom in two golden shields joined by a short golden chain, topped with a golden cross bearing a small ruby and a tiny opening for a neck chain. Indian botanists told Haraldsson they had never even seen a double rudraksha (extremely rare botanical anomaly); a malformed specimen in a Madras shop was priced at the Indian equivalent of almost three hundred dollars; a London goldsmith confirmed the gold was at least twenty-two carats. (2) Materialized delicacies fall from his hands sizzling hot — sometimes too hot to hold; sweet syrups and fragrant oils pour from his hands (even feet) leaving no sticky trace on his skin; he produces rice grains with tiny perfectly carved pictures of Krishna, out-of-season fruits (near-impossible in a region without electricity or refrigeration), and anomalous fruits — apples that peel to reveal an apple on one side and another fruit on the other. (3) Sacred ash pours prodigiously on every walk among the crowds — enough in a single transit of the ashram grounds to fill several drums; Haraldsson and Dr. Karlis Osis (director of research, American Society for Psychical Research) actually saw ash materializing: palm open and downturned, a few quick small circles of the hand, and a grey substance appeared in the air just below the palm — Osis, sitting closer, saw it first appear entirely as granules that crumbled to ash when touched. (4) Not mass hypnosis: Sai Baba freely allows filming of open-air demonstrations and everything still shows up on film; no credible evidence of fakery has ever surfaced; he has produced a continuous flow of objects for half a century, since age fourteen. Haraldsson's verdict: the demonstrations remind us of "enormous potentials that may lie dormant somewhere within all human beings."
- **Yogananda's ascetics** — In *Autobiography of a Yogi*, Paramahansa Yogananda (1893–1952), the first eminent holy man of India to settle permanently in the West, describes meeting several Hindu ascetics who could materialize out-of-season fruits, gold plates, and other objects — cautioning that such siddhis are not always evidence of spiritual evolution: "The world [is] nothing but an objectivized dream," and "whatever your powerful mind believes very intensely instantly comes to pass." Talbot asks whether such individuals tap a little of the enormous sea of cosmic energy Bohm says fills every cubic centimeter of empty space.
- **Therese Neumann's inedia (investigated 1927)** — Her inedia began in 1923 when she "transferred" a young priest's throat disease to her own body and subsisted solely on liquids for several years; in 1927 she gave up food and water entirely. The bishop of Regensburg sent a commission: from July 14 to July 29, 1927, under medical doctor Seidl, four Franciscan nursing sisters scrutinized her day and night, measuring and weighing even her mouth-rinsing water. Findings: she never went to the bathroom (one bowel movement after six weeks — examined by a Dr. Reismanns: only a small amount of mucus and bile, no traces of food); no signs of dehydration although the average human expels about four hundred grams (fourteen ounces) of water daily in exhaled air and a like amount through the pores; constant weight — though she lost nearly nine pounds of blood during the weekly opening of her stigmata, her weight returned to normal within a day or two. Seidl and the sisters were completely convinced she had not eaten or drunk for fourteen days — conclusive, Talbot argues, since the body can rarely survive even a week without water. She then ate and drank nothing for the next thirty-five years — implying she regularly materialized both the blood for her stigmata and the water and nutrients keeping her alive. Thurston gives several more examples of stigmatists who went years without eating or drinking.
- **Bleeding and weeping icons** — Compelling accounts abound of bleeding statues, paintings, icons, and even rocks of historical or religious significance; dozens of stories of weeping Madonnas, with a virtual epidemic of them sweeping Italy in 1953; in India, Sai Baba's followers showed Haraldsson pictures of the ascetic miraculously exuding sacred ash.
- **Tia and the vanishing kenari grove (Watson, Indonesia)** — Watson also encountered Tia, a young woman whose power (unlike Alin's unconscious gift) was consciously controlled — a shaman in the making. He saw her perform miraculous healings and, during a power struggle with the local Moslem religious leader, set the minaret of the local mosque on fire with the power of her mind. Most awesome: stumbling on her trying to communicate something to a little girl in a grove of kenari trees, he watched her frustration turn into an eerie, hypnotic dance — and the entire grove suddenly blinked out of existence: "One moment Tia danced in a grove of shady kenari; the next she was standing alone in the hard, bright light of the sun." Seconds later she made it reappear, and the girl (who clearly shared the experience, rushing to touch the trees) and Tia blinked the grove on and off several times, dancing and giggling, while Watson walked away with his head reeling. Talbot's category: transformation of the *whole* of reality, not just parts.
- **Castaneda's dying-spirit branch (Journey to Ixtlan)** — Don Juan and Castaneda, searching the desert at night for a spirit, come upon a creature like a calf with a wolf's ears and a bird's beak, screaming as if dying. Terrified Castaneda tells himself it can't be real; his vision changes and he sees a fallen tree branch trembling in the wind. Don Juan rebukes him: the branch was a dying spirit while it was alive with power and became a branch when Castaneda doubted — but *both realities were equally real*. This is the incident Talbot and his professor were discussing minutes before the umbrella incident.
- **The umbrella incident (Talbot, 1975, Michigan State University)** — As a senior, dining with a professor and discussing Castaneda's two-equally-real-realities claim, Talbot strolled with her afterward on a clear summer night behind a boisterous, apparently drunk group speaking an unrecognizable foreign language — one woman carrying a green umbrella despite a cloudless sky. After swinging it in huge erratic arcs (nearly grazing them) in a performance clearly designed to attract their attention, she held it overhead with both hands and threw it dramatically at their feet. The umbrella then "flickered" like a dying lantern flame, emitted a crackling sound like crumpled cellophane, and in a dazzling array of sparkling multicolored light its ends curled up, its color changed, and it reshaped into a gnarled brown-gray stick. Both wrote down their accounts, which matched exactly (the professor said it had "sizzled"). The group was long gone before they thought to ask. Talbot connects it to the Castaneda conversation and speculates their emotional fervor caused their unconscious minds to change the hologram of reality — perhaps the psyche (per Ullman's assertion) is programmed to produce such miracles to give us glimpses of reality's true nature.
- **Talbot's native psychic history** — From adolescence: vivid, detailed precognitive dreams of later events; knowing things about people "I had no right knowing"; at seventeen, spontaneous ability to see an energy field or "aura" around living things, from whose pattern and colors he can often determine things about a person's health. He presents himself as born with "the chemistry necessary to trigger shifts in reality."
- **Sai Baba's teleportation (per Haraldsson)** — Numerous witnesses report watching Sai Baba snap his fingers and vanish, instantly reappearing a hundred or more yards away — going bilocation (the saintly ability to be in two places at once) one better. Talbot: this suggests bodies are holographic projections that can blink "off" in one location and "on" in another like an image on a video screen.
- **Indridi Indridason's dematerializing body parts (Iceland, 1905)** — Several of Iceland's leading scientists, investigating the paranormal, chose Indridason — then a country bumpkin with no psychic experience — as a subject; he proved a spectacularly talented medium, entering trance quickly and producing dramatic PK. Most bizarre: while deep in trance, different parts of his body would completely dematerialize — an arm or hand fading out of existence as the astonished scientists watched, rematerializing before he awakened. Talbot: further evidence of the body's holographic, immaterial nature.
- **Physicists' "schizophrenia" about quantum reality (Rohrlich, Mermin)** — Quantum theorist Fritz Rohrlich (Syracuse University): most physicists accept the standard interpretation of quantum theory yet "insist on the reality of quantum systems even when these are not observed." N. David Mermin (Cornell) sorts physicists into three categories: a small minority troubled by the philosophical implications; a second group with elaborate reasons for being untroubled that "miss the point entirely"; and a third with no explanations who also refuse to say why they aren't troubled — "Their position is unassailable." Context for why the consciousness-creates-particles question goes unexamined.
- **Aspirin as a young reality-field (callback)** — Talbot suggests the finding that aspirin helps prevent heart attacks in Americans but not in the British may mark a relatively recent reality-field still in the making — same logic as electrons (old, fixed) vs. anomalons (new, plastic).
- **Rogo on the historical onset of blood miracles** — Rogo notes accounts of blood miracles began with the fourteenth-century miracle of San Gennaro; none predate it. Talbot reads this as the ability "flickering into existence" then — once established as a reality-field, it became easier for others to tap, explaining the many blood miracles since and none before.

## Thread
This chapter escalates Part II's mind-over-body argument (placebo, stigmata, faith-driven healing in the previous chapter) beyond the body into the external world: PK in the lab, mass miracles in history, then materialization and whole-reality transformation. It cashes out Bohm's implicate order and Pribram's constructed perception into the book's boldest claim so far — that physical law itself is a habit of the holomovement and consensus reality a collectively sustained "reality-field," a hologram sculpted by consciousness. The pattern Talbot names explicitly — "the deeper and more emotionally charged our beliefs, the greater the changes we can make in both our bodies and reality itself" — becomes the through-line, and the closing gallery (bilocation, dematerializing limbs, the holodeck universe) sets up the later chapters' treatment of even more radical psi, and of space, time, and the self as holographic constructs.
