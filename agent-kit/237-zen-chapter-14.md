# 14

## In one paragraph
The riders come down out of the pass and arrive at last in the Montana college town where the narrator — as Phaedrus — once lived, and ride out to the canyon home of his old friend Robert DeWeese, a painter and art instructor. The chapter is the arrival Part II has been building toward: an afternoon on the deck, an evening party with trout, wine and faculty guests, and a painful social impasse born of the fact that DeWeese believes he is welcoming back the brilliant person who once lived here, while the narrator and the Sutherlands know that person is gone. Asked to critique the instructions for a barbecue rotisserie, the narrator delivers an impromptu lecture — assembly "require great peace of mind," the test of a machine is your own serenity, rotisserie assembly is a long-lost branch of sculpture — that restates the classic–romantic argument in front of an audience of artists. After the party, alone with the DeWeeses, he lays out the fullest statement yet of the Chautauqua's program: the crisis of technological ugliness cannot be solved by rationality because rationality itself is the source of the problem, so reason must be expanded at its roots, the way Newton invented the calculus. He half-reveals Phaedrus — disguised as "an ancient Greek rhetorician" — and announces the climb he and Chris will make up the canyon.

## Core claims
- Pirsig's narrator says that "To travel is better than to arrive" returns to mind on arrival: reaching a temporary goal brings on a period of depression and forced reorientation toward the next one.
- The narrator claims that what is scary about technology is not the technology itself but "what it does to the relations between people, like callers and operators."
- He observes that he and the DeWeeses "live in entirely different time structures": to them Chris has hardly been gone; to him the intervening years are a tomb.
- The narrator argues that peace of mind is not superficial in mechanical work — "It's the whole thing." Good maintenance produces it, poor maintenance disturbs it, and a machine's "workability" is just an objectification of this peace of mind.
- He claims the test of the machine is the satisfaction it gives you — "The test of the machine's always your own mind. There isn't any other test" — since molecules have no ethical codes except those people give them.
- Against DeWeese's objection (what if the machine is wrong and I feel peaceful?), he answers that this is self-contradictory: pronouncing something wrong is itself a form of caring, and if you truly didn't care the thought would never occur to you. The commoner case is feeling unpeaceful about a machine that is right — which means it isn't checked out thoroughly enough.
- He argues that factory instructions are hard to follow because they presume there is only one right way to assemble the machine, wiping out creativity; in truth there are hundreds of ways, and the selection among them — the art of the work — depends on your own mind and spirit as much as on the material.
- He claims the craftsman never follows a single line of instruction: the material at hand determines his thoughts and motions, which simultaneously change the material, "until his mind's at rest at the same time the material's right." The divorce of art from technology is "completely unnatural"; rotisserie assembly is "actually a long-lost branch of sculpture."
- DeWeese's real trouble with the rotisserie instructions, the narrator decides, was not bad cross-referencing but the classic–romantic split: science works with chunks and pieces with continuity presumed, while DeWeese works with continuities with the chunks presumed.
- After the party he claims what's wrong with technology is a "noncoalescence between reason and feeling" — it is not connected with matters of the spirit and heart, so it does blind, ugly things by accident and gets hated for it, now that food, clothing and shelter are assured.
- His central thesis: the crisis "can't be solved by rational means because the rationality itself is the source of the problem" — but abandoning rationality for feelings alone (as John and Sylvia and millions like them do) is also a wrong direction; the solution is to *expand* the nature of rationality.
- He argues the needed expansion must be made at the roots, not the branches, on Newton's model: Newton presumed instantaneous change and invented the calculus, thereby inventing a new form of reason; a similar expansion is needed to handle technological ugliness.
- He claims real learning comes from hang-ups — stopping and drifting laterally until you find what lets you expand the roots of what you already know — and that whole civilizations undergo the same process; periods of root expansion always look topsy-turvy and purposeless at the time (the Renaissance after Columbus being his example).
- He says present-day reason is an analogue of the medieval flat earth: go too far beyond it and you're presumed to fall off, into insanity; fear of insanity is comparable to the old fear of the world's edge, or of heretics. Its growing inadequacy is driving people into occultism, mysticism, and drug changes.
- He claims classical (analytic, dialectic) reason is "completely bankrupt with regard to abstract art"; the fault lies not with the art but with the "sense" that can't grasp it — the answers are at the roots, not the branches.
- He says the ancient Greeks, who invented classical reason, knew better than to use it exclusively — they predicted the future from the wind — and that we have yet to understand the methods that produced the results our whole system of knowledge stems from.
- He tells the DeWeeses that the rhetoricians of ancient Greece were the first teachers of the Western world, vilified by Plato "to grind an axe of his own," condemned throughout history without their side ever being told; the Church of Reason "was founded on their graves," and digging into its foundations "you come across ghosts."
- On essays: the trouble is they "always have to sound like God talking for eternity," when in truth it is never anything other than one person talking from one place in time and space and circumstance.
- All of it, he confirms to DeWeese, is "the direct result" of what he was doing on "Quality."

## Concepts
**chautauqua** — The narrator's traveling lecture-essay project; he tells Gennie he's thinking about "a series of lecture-essays—a sort of Chautauqua" worked out in his mind while riding. He also jokes about its danger: "When you've got a Chautauqua in your head, it's extremely hard not to inflict it on innocent people."

**peace-of-mind** — In maintenance and assembly work, the whole point, not a superficial extra: what good maintenance produces and poor maintenance disturbs. A machine's workability is an objectification of it; "The ultimate test's always your own serenity," and without it you build your personal problems into the machine.

**quality** — Named only at the chapter's end: the subject Phaedrus was working on at the college, which DeWeese once warned no one had ever succeeded at; the narrator says everything he has been saying about art, technology, and expanded reason is "the direct result of it."

**classic-romantic-split** — The divide the rotisserie instructions "hang up" on, "like everything else about technology": science works with chunks and bits with continuity presumed, while DeWeese (the romantic artist) works with continuities with the chunks presumed — so ugly, chopped-up technical prose defeats him regardless of its accuracy.

**technology-art-divorce** — The narrator's claim that the separation of art from technology is "completely unnatural," so old "you have to be an archeologist to find out where the two separated"; hence rotisserie assembly is really a long-lost branch of sculpture.

**craftsman-harmony** — The mark of excellent work: the craftsman follows no single line of instruction but makes decisions as he goes, absorbed without contriving to be; the material determines his thoughts and motions, which change the material, in a progression ending when his mind is at rest and the material is right.

**classical-reason** — "Analytic reason, dialectic reason. Reason which at the University is sometimes considered to be the whole of understanding." The narrator says it is completely bankrupt with regard to abstract art, and likens it to the medieval flat earth.

**expansion-of-reason / root-expansion** — The proposed solution to the crisis: not abandoning rationality but expanding its nature so it can produce a solution — an expansion "at the roots, not at the branches," on the model of Newton's calculus, and therefore hard to see.

**lateral-drift** — The narrator's account of real learning: it results from hang-ups, where instead of extending the branches of what you know, you stop and "drift laterally for a while" until you find something that lets you expand the roots.

**topsy-turvy-times** — The felt confusion of a period of root expansion, caused by "inadequacy of old forms of thought to deal with new experiences"; the narrator says such periods always look confused and purposeless at the time, and cites the post-Columbus Renaissance.

**church-of-reason** — The institution the narrator once talked about at length here (DeWeese confirms it); he now says it "was founded on their graves" — the graves of the vilified Greek rhetoricians — "and supported today by their graves. And when you dig deep into its foundations you come across ghosts."

**ghosts** — Chris's bedtime question ("are there any ghosts up in the canyon?") echoed in the narrator's closing image of the ghosts in the Church of Reason's foundations — continuing the book's ghost motif.

**phaedrus** — Half-disclosed to the DeWeeses under a mask: asked who Phaedrus was, the narrator answers, "He was an ancient Greek…a rhetorician…a 'composition major' of his time. He was one of those present when reason was being invented." DeWeese confirms the narrator never spoke of him before.

**living-images** — The narrator's term for people out of the tomb-fragments of memory suddenly made present and animate: "the living image of DeWeese himself," and later "More living images" for Jack and Wylla Barsness. He realizes the authentic image is also "a brand-new person who's been renewing himself continually."

**time-structures** — The narrator's realization that he and the DeWeeses "live in entirely different time structures": what to them is a short absence is to him the sealed, tomb-like time of the person who lived here before.

**code-of-the-west** — The narrator's deflecting joke when Chris asks what he'll "stick to": "Mah guns, boy, mah guns… That's the Code of the West" — the laugh that gets his speechifying forgiven.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Arrival in the college town.** Coming down out of the pass onto a green plain ringed by snow-capped mountains, the narrator finds the main street only vaguely familiar — a fifteen-to-thirty-thousand-population town "not exactly a town, not exactly a city," with shop signs pitched at tourists and a glass-and-chrome restaurant with "no recall at all." Shows memory (Phaedrus's) failing to match the present, and the town's "lack of self-identity."
- **The phone-book panic.** DeWeese's number isn't in the book and the operator has never heard of him; for a moment the narrator panics — "Were they just in his imagination?" — until he remembers the DeWeeses answered his letter: "Imaginary people don't use the mails." Used to show both his fragile hold on Phaedrus's reality and his point that what's scary about technology is what it does to relations between people.
- **The ride to the canyon.** Under ten miles of dirt road through thick alfalfa to where light green meets dark green pine; deep gravel forces first gear at ten miles an hour, feet off the pegs; then the steep V canyon, the large grey house with an enormous abstract iron sculpture on its side, and DeWeese himself tipped back in a chair with a beer, waving — "Right out of the old photographs." The narrator, hands stuck to the grips, waves a leg back.
- **The deck afternoon.** The unfinished porch deck with grate-like gaps hangs over the canyon; a horse grazes by the stream fifty feet below. Guests: an art instructor with horn-rimmed glasses and his self-conscious wife. Gennie DeWeese arrives with a tray of beer cans and neighbors' brook trout for dinner; the narrator notes the "shared smile over the artistic economy of grabbing a can of beer instead of her hand." Sun, beer, and the marshmallow-toasted head; then the sudden chill as the sun nears the ridge — Gennie: once the sun goes over, "it gets really cold."
- **The rainstorm story retold.** Asked what happens when something goes bad mechanically, the narrator retells the rainstorm engine-failure story from earlier in the book — "a good story, but somewhat pointless" as an answer — with the out-of-gas punchline drawing the expected groan and Chris's "And I even told him to look."
- **The impasse.** John's "movie star" ribbing over the sunglasses starts a "funny stickiness": John's sarcasm chills DeWeese into respectful tones, which stoke more sarcasm. When John says the narrator "must have been really crazy, I mean really nuts to leave this place," DeWeese is shocked, then angry. The narrator anatomizes the three-way asymmetry of knowledge: John and he know "the person they're both referring to isn't much these days"; DeWeese and he know there was once someone "creatively on fire with a set of ideas no one had ever heard of before" to whom something unexplained and wrong happened — and DeWeese thinks that person is here now.
- **"He saw too much."** As a halation of sunlight flashes down through the ridge trees and catches him, the narrator blurts this about Phaedrus — a non sequitur no one registers. Privately he rebukes himself: "You don't make statements like that. You leave the hospital with the understanding that you don't." First direct reference in-scene to his hospitalization.
- **Chris's dream report.** Unpacking, Chris says the place "isn't anything like the way you told about it last night… You said it was lonely here." The narrator has no memory of saying this and decides Chris "must have been dreaming" — an unresolved, uncanny beat.
- **The campfire fragment.** DeWeese's remark that the narrator "used to spend all your time up there" summons a single memory-fragment: a small campfire above the timberline, sheltered in rock against high wind with no trees, packs and cooking gear as windbreak, a canteen of snowmelt collected early "because above the timberline the snow stops melting when the sun goes down."
- **The wolves exchange.** Chris asks if there are ghosts up the canyon; DeWeese says no, but there are wolves that "make trouble for the ranchers," killing young calves and lambs; asked if they chase people, he's never heard of it — then, seeing Chris's disappointment, adds "but they could."
- **The trout dinner.** Brook trout with Bay county Chablis by the fire; Sylvia points out the large pots and vases made by Peter Voulkas, "a student of Mr. DeWeese" — John: "Oh, for Christ's sake! I almost kicked one of those over." John's toast ("anybody with friends who can provide an evening like this can't be all bad") partially dissolves the impasse; he also glumly foresees "another eight years on Twenty-six-forty-nine Colfax Avenue."
- **The welder-sculptor.** After dinner comes a sculptor from northern Montana who herds sheep for income; suspicious of the non-artist narrator ("like I'm a detective") until he learns the narrator welds — "Motorcycle maintenance opens strange doors." The sculptor welds for the same reasons: skill brings "a tremendous feeling of power and control over the metal," and his photographs show birds and animals with flowing metal textures "not like anything else."
- **Jack Barsness and the English-department split.** Jack, "recorded in the tomb fragments as a good person," writes fiction and teaches English; he is leaving to head a department in Boise, Idaho. The department's continuing split — fiction writers who *do* it versus systematic scholars who *talk about how it's done* — "in part gave rise to, or at least accelerated" Phaedrus's wild set of ideas; Jack supported Phaedrus without quite understanding him, seeing it as something a fiction writer could use better than linguistic analysis. "It's an old split. Like the one between art and art history."
- **The rotisserie instructions.** DeWeese, having wasted an afternoon on an outdoor barbecue rotisserie, asks the narrator as a professional technical writer to damn the instructions. They look ordinary; the only flaw he finds is a page separation forcing flips between text and illustration — which he jumps on hard, while sensing DeWeese's real problem is the classic–romantic split, not cross-referencing. Then Chris folds the instructions so illustration and text sit side by side, demolishing the critique; the narrator feels "like a movie-cartoon character who has just walked beyond the edge of a cliff" and the room dissolves in laughter.
- **"Assembly of Japanese bicycle require great peace of mind."** The narrator's saved specimen instruction, which he first laughed at (memories of bicycle assembly, the unintended slur on Japanese manufacture) before seeing "a lot of wisdom in that statement" — the springboard for the peace-of-mind lecture. The sculptor and Gennie give "sharp looks of recognition."
- **The assembly-line goof-off.** To John's "But they're from the factory," the narrator replies, "I'm from the factory too": instructions are written by taking a tape recorder to whichever assembly-line worker the foreman needs least — "the biggest goof-off he's got" — while the next man, probably better, is too busy. Shows why factory instructions are unlikely to give the best way.
- **Newton and the calculus.** The narrator's model for root expansion: instantaneous change was unreasonable in Newton's time, yet zero quantities like points were accepted; Newton presumed instantaneous change existed and worked out how to determine it, producing the calculus — "Newton invented a new form of reason."
- **Columbus, the Renaissance, and the moon.** The Renaissance "is supposed to have resulted from the topsy-turvy feeling caused by Columbus' discovery of a new world" — nothing in the flat-earth Old and New Testaments predicted it, yet it couldn't be denied, forcing abandonment of the medieval outlook. By contrast, moon exploration "must be like a tea party" next to what Columbus went through: a branch extension, not a root expansion. A really new exploration would head "into realms beyond reason."
- **Abstract art as root experience.** Nonrepresentative art is offered as a root experience classical reason cannot grasp: people condemn it for not making "sense," but "what's really wrong is not the art but the 'sense.'"
- **The Greeks and the wind.** The inventors of classical reason "listened to the wind and predicted the future from that"; the narrator compares it to a painter telling the future of his painting by staring at the canvas — methods we've yet to understand though our whole system of knowledge stems from their results. As he says "I keep listening to the wind," the canyon wind batters the house all night.
- **The Phaedrus half-disclosure.** The narrator establishes (via DeWeese) that when he lived here he talked a lot about the Church of Reason but never about Phaedrus; to Gennie's "Who was he?" he gives only the ancient-Greek-rhetorician mask, and delivers the Plato indictment. He also recalls DeWeese once advising him about the Quality work — DeWeese corrects: "I said no one had ever succeeded in doing what you were trying to do" — and DeWeese now offers hope: "A lot of people are listening better these days. Particularly the kids… not just at you—to you…to you."
- **The climb announced.** The narrator declares that once the Sutherlands leave, he and Chris will climb "up to where that wind starts"; DeWeese: "You can start from right here… There's no road for seventy-five miles." He falls asleep under the heavy quilt thinking "about snowfields and winds and Christopher Columbus."

## Thread
This chapter closes Part II's journey: the destination the whole ride has pointed toward — Phaedrus's old town and the DeWeeses — is reached, and the narrative and Chautauqua layers fuse for the first time in live conversation, as the narrator delivers his ideas not to the reader but to a room of artists. It cashes out earlier material: the peace-of-mind/maintenance doctrine extends the classic–romantic analysis and the caring theme; the rainstorm story from the trip's start is retold; the ghost motif returns in Chris's question and in the rhetoricians' graves beneath the Church of Reason. It also advances the Phaedrus mystery on several fronts — the "hospital" is named for the first time in-scene, DeWeese confirms Phaedrus preached the Church of Reason here but never used that name, and the ancient-Greek-rhetorician mask points the inquiry toward Plato and the Greeks. The impasse scene dramatizes the book's central fact — that the narrator is literally "not the same person at all" — while the after-party monologue lays out the program (expand reason at the roots, on Newton's model) that the rest of the book pursues. Finally, the announced climb up the canyon with Chris sets the physical frame for what follows, and the Sutherlands' imminent departure marks the end of the four-rider configuration.
