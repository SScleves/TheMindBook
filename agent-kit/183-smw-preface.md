# Preface

## In one paragraph
The preface frames the entire volume around the tension between UFO/UAP disclosure and national security, taking as its springboard Christopher Mellon's November 22, 2023 article in the Debrief — published, the authors note, on the sixtieth anniversary of JFK's assassination. DeLonge & Levenda summarize Mellon's plea (secrecy about UAPs is a national security matter; blunt disclosure risks existential panic and exposure of US defenses) and place it alongside statements by Garry Nolan and Lue Elizondo, all three of whom were part of the original To The Stars project of 2017–2018. The authors' counter-argument is that the Phenomenon is being handled exclusively by people trained in war and worst-case scenarios, while the "soft sciences," religion, and the arts — the very disciplines used to challenging views of reality — are never "read in." They describe their own position: not scientists, not defense insiders, not adversarial to the defense establishment, but able (thanks to Tom's access) to ask questions of it. The preface closes by naming the book's theme — war — and its subject — "flying fucking saucers" — promising a survey of the US government's and other governments' "ambiguous, misleading, and even at times seemingly insane" approaches to the Phenomenon.

## Core claims
- The authors argue that the obstacle to Americans knowing about the UFO Phenomenon "is framed as national security: war, the preparation for war, and the calculations of the battlefield."
- DeLonge & Levenda summarize Mellon's claim (as his, sympathetically) that UAP craft "are here, they are testing our defenses, and they are relatively omnipotent in a technological way," representing science far beyond human capability, and that disclosure risks (a) existential panic and (b) exposure of US defense capabilities to terrestrial enemies.
- The authors say they have heard these national-security arguments since beginning this project in January 2015, when their first contacts were individuals affiliated with military, intelligence, and industrial organizations — the "war cabinet," not Congress, the courts, or the president.
- The authors argue that if the US military has specialized access to the Phenomenon, it can only be because the Alien has decided the military "is operating along the same lines as they do": the Alien reacts to weapons systems — nuclear capabilities, aircraft, submarines, missile silos — not to culture, religion, politics, museums, or art galleries.
- The book says the popular American belief — that the defense establishment holds alien bodies and craft and has some understanding with the Aliens — is loaded with so many assumptions and anthropomorphisms that unpacking it "strains our capacity for clarification."
- The authors claim (per To The Stars' consistent position across the series) that "there is more to the Phenomenon than can be measured by either science or the national security apparatus."
- They argue that Mellon, while addressing the dangers disclosure poses to religion and culture, never enlists religions, cultural institutions, the soft sciences (psychology, sociology, philosophy), or the arts as partners in preparing the public — the people "used to challenging views of reality (clergy, artists, philosophers, mystics)" are not brought into the conversation.
- The authors report that insiders in the "war room" say the Alien "has frightened and even terrified seasoned veterans of the intelligence and military communities," and that these insiders fear disclosure would confront the public with a reality questioning everything we think we know — an existential threat.
- The authors state a "catch-22" of disclosure: "We want the government to disclose; we don't trust the government's disclosure" — the UFO community greets former military and intelligence figures with suspicion and hostility.
- DeLonge & Levenda position themselves as non-adversarial toward the defense establishment: "We are reasonable fellows. We know that if we expect the government to provide disclosure it will be from government employees. Who else?"
- The authors note that some "conspiracy-minded Ufologists" believe Kennedy's November 12, 1963 initiative for US–USSR space cooperation might have contributed to his assassination, because sharing outer-space information would imply sharing UFO information and put Russia at a strategic advantage (they report this belief without endorsing it).
- They claim the twentieth century's spectacular planetary changes "may have prompted the current unprecedented response by the Phenomenon."

## Concepts
- **UAP (Unidentified Aerial Phenomena)** — the term used for the Phenomenon in official contexts (e.g., AATIP), described by the authors as "considered less whimsical and more inclusive of related experiences than the more commonly used UFO."
- **The Phenomenon** — the authors' preferred umbrella term for the UFO/UAP reality ("the Alien or the Phenomenon or however you wish to label it"); something the To The Stars project insists exceeds what science or the national security apparatus can measure, and which challenges the defense worldview built on weapons, war gaming, and worst-case scenarios.
- **The Alien** — the intelligence behind the Phenomenon, "whatever it is"; the authors argue it reacts to human weapons systems (nuclear capabilities, aircraft, submarines and surface vessels, missile silos) — explicitly not to small arms ("not to our AR-15s or AK-47s") — and not to human culture, religion, or art.
- **Disclosure** — the revelation by the government of what it knows about the UFO Phenomenon; the preface's central problem, caught between public demand and national-security resistance.
- **Existential panic** — the feared public reaction to blunt disclosure, described by Mellon, Nolan, and Elizondo; confrontation with a reality "that questions everything we know or think we know about ourselves, our planet, and our existence."
- **The "war room" / defense establishment** — the authors' shorthand for the Pentagon and Department of Homeland Security (not Congress, the Supreme Court, or the president) — "what, at other times, in other countries, might be referred to as the war cabinet"; the sole current custodian of the Phenomenon.
- **Military-industrial complex** — Eisenhower's phrase, invoked for the coalition (defense department, intelligence community, companies such as Lockheed Martin) that worked with uneasy members of Congress to gut the proposed UAP declassification mandate.
- **AATIP (Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program)** — the Pentagon program focused on UAPs that Lue Elizondo ran and whose existence he revealed.
- **SAP (Special Access Program) / CAP (Controlled Access Program)** — classified government program categories dealing with (among other things) the UFO/UAP Phenomenon; neither DeLonge nor Levenda has been read-in to any.
- **Read-in** — being formally granted access to a classified program; the authors extend the term metaphorically: clergy, artists, philosophers, and mystics are not "read in" to the conversation about the Phenomenon.
- **"Tic Tacs"** — the famous UFOs in the video footage revealed by the New York Times in December 2017.
- **Soft sciences** — psychology, sociology, and philosophy (grouped by the authors with the humanities, religion, and the arts) — disciplines excluded from the official approach to the Phenomenon despite being needed to prepare the public for disclosure.
- **To The Stars** — DeLonge's project (original team formed 2017–2018, including Mellon, Nolan, and Elizondo; the authors' research began January 2015) whose consistent position across the series' volumes is that the Phenomenon exceeds science and national security.
- **Stovepipes** — the compartmentalized information channels within government, referenced in George Knapp's question about whether the UFO problem might remain unsolvable "even after all the stovepipes are torn down."
- **The catch-22 of disclosure** — the authors' formulation of the trust bind: disclosure must come from government employees, yet the UFO community has learned to distrust the government, so "We want the government to disclose; we don't trust the government's disclosure."
- **First casualty of war is truth / fog of war** — the adage the authors adopt as the volume's framing device: the book is offered as "a flashlight to guide you through the fog of that war."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Christopher Mellon's Debrief article (November 22, 2023)** — "Disclosure and National Security: Should the U.S. Government Reveal What It Knows about UAP?", by the former deputy assistant secretary of defense for intelligence in the Clinton and George W. Bush administrations. Opens by asking the reader to imagine being a newly elected president told in a classified briefing that the US military "has recovered advanced extraterrestrial technology" with only modest headway in understanding it. Mellon argues national security takes precedence over "pure science" and urges the defense establishment to take the Phenomenon seriously immediately. The authors note the uncanny juxtaposition of the publication date (60th anniversary of the JFK assassination) with the opening line about being elected president, and playfully suppose "some weird force in the world stage-managed this little nexus of themes."
- **JFK's National Security Memorandum of November 12, 1963** — "Cooperation with the USSR on Outer Space Matters," addressed to NASA's administrator, asking for "broader cooperation between the United States and the USSR in outer space"; one of Kennedy's last such memoranda. The authors relay (without endorsing) the conspiracy-minded Ufologists' belief that this initiative might have contributed to his assassination, since space cooperation implies sharing UFO information with a possibly non-reciprocating Russia.
- **December 2017 New York Times revelations** — publication of video footage of UFOs (the "Tic Tacs") and ensuing interviews with military whistleblowers and government insiders verifying, "to one degree or another," the reality of the UFO Phenomenon; the authors cite it as reinforcing the idea that the Alien reacts to military systems.
- **The gutted congressional UAP declassification mandate (c. 2022, five years after the NYT story)** — a proposed mandate to declassify government-created UFO documents was gutted by uneasy members of Congress working with the "military-industrial complex" (defense department, intelligence community, Lockheed Martin). What passed instructed the National Archives to gather US government UFO documentation and make it available within twenty-five years, with no automatic declassification. Senators wanted a Kennedy Assassination Records–type review board; Rep. Tim Burchett (R-Tennessee), a House drafter of a similar bill, wanted to bypass a review board and simply declassify, but said his approach was blocked "by the intelligence community," with others noting Defense Department pushback. Neither chamber got what it wanted; the result was a "watered-down (some might say toothless)" directive.
- **Garry Nolan on Tucker Carlson (2022 interview)** — Nolan argues that admitting "we are not at the top of the food chain anymore" would upend all religions ("who is their God?"), with every religion looking for a mirror of its beliefs, starting "a whole new series of arguments" — one reason for government hesitancy. Carlson calls it "inherently destabilizing"; Nolan adds the populace "might . . . revolt."
- **Lue Elizondo on Coast to Coast AM with George Knapp (July 2020)** — Elizondo, former Pentagon counterintelligence specialist who ran AATIP and revealed its existence, discussed his history with AATIP and the SAPs dealing with the UFO/UAP Phenomenon (still bound by security oaths, he couldn't detail specific events). Asked by Knapp whether the problem might be unsolvable even with all data collected, Elizondo answered optimistically — citing the Moon landing achieved within ten years of the early jet age — and said "We should not be determining what America should or should not know. That's up for Americans to decide, not governments." The authors read this as implying the field should involve people outside the "war room" — a position they call "problematic" given insiders' testimony of genuine terror at the Alien.
- **Levenda's Bendix disclosure** — in the spirit of full disclosure, Levenda notes he worked c. 1973–1979 for Bendix Corporation, one of America's largest defense contractors, but in a Manhattan sales and marketing office without a security clearance; the authors add the aside that one of Bob Lazar's ID badges at Area 51 bore the Bendix logo.
- **The To The Stars project timeline** — the authors began this research project in January 2015 with contacts in military, intelligence, and industrial organizations; the original To The Stars team of 2017–2018 included Mellon, Nolan, and Elizondo.

## Thread
As the preface, this unit states the book's thesis in miniature: the UFO Phenomenon has been captured by the national-security frame — war, preparation for war, battlefield calculations — and this frame simultaneously explains the secrecy, the military's privileged access (the Alien reacts to weapons systems), and the exclusion of religion, the soft sciences, and the arts from the conversation. It follows the front matter (dedications, acknowledgments) and sets up the Introduction ("Welcome to the War Room") and the eleven chapters, promising a wild historical tour ("scientists and madmen, mystics and murderers") through the US government's and other governments' twentieth-century approaches to the Phenomenon. It also plants seeds the rest of the book will grow: the twentieth century's planetary changes as a possible trigger for the Phenomenon's "unprecedented response," the disclosure catch-22, and the authors' non-adversarial but questioning stance toward the defense establishment — themes that culminate in the Afterword's "Alien in the Panopticon" argument about secrecy and control.
