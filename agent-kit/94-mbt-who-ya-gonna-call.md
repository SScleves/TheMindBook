# Ch 23 — Who Ya Gonna Call?

## In one paragraph
Campbell pauses before introducing "the wholly new concepts of the next chapter" to consolidate what Section 2 has established about belief, knowledge, and personal growth — and then, for the first time, to give practical instructions. He argues that spiritual growth (improving the quality of your consciousness) is fundamentally an independent, individual effort: others' maps and teachers can help, but believing anyone (including Campbell) is "lazy, risky," and no substitute for your own verifiable results. Growth is a matter of "subjective science" or "personal science" — which he calls the mother of objective science — and its metric is real, obvious, measurable change in your being, not intellectual knowledge or external doing. Because Westerners are culturally paralyzed without a physical process to start with, Campbell reluctantly supplies one: a generalized, dogma-free meditation practice with custom "mantras" for auditory, visual, and kinesthetic types, practiced twenty minutes twice daily for three to six months before evaluating results. The purpose of meditation, on his account, is to quiet the ego-driven mental foreground so you can experience the invisible background of consciousness itself — like a deep-sea fish becoming aware of water — and thereby discover that you are a unit of consciousness within a larger absolute consciousness.

## Core claims
- Campbell claims that "in matters of evolution there is no free lunch": others' ideas can serve as a map, but every map must be evaluated as you travel; a map is necessarily general while each journey is individual and personal.
- Campbell argues that becoming a believer — accepting what anyone (including Campbell himself) tells you — is "lazy, risky," and substitutes someone else's belief or knowledge for your own; copying, reciting, or memorizing others' knowledge cannot produce significant spiritual or personal growth. "Talking about it all day and all night with the greatest of gurus won't produce one iota of real progress."
- Campbell asserts that personal growth is most efficiently the product of "good science" — subjective or personal science, distinct from both religion and objective science — and that "subjective science is the mother of objective science." Real personal science requires "real, verifiable, measurable, objective results."
- Campbell claims that our reality is "fundamentally nonphysical (from a PMR perspective) and is animated and driven by profitability toward states of lower entropy," and promises that by the end of the next two sections science will have logically derived the origins, nature, purpose, and mechanics of both spirituality and the physical world from consciousness evolution.
- Campbell contrasts belief systems with personal science: belief systems require only faith, produce no results, and — being assumed complete and perfect — have a logical disincentive to change; knowledge from personal scientific experience is in continual flux through open-minded skepticism and new data. "Honest truth seekers never become know-it-alls."
- Campbell holds that no particular belief, disbelief, or faith is needed to start — only grasping the possibility of a greater reality; after that, the desire to discover truth should be motivation enough.
- Campbell claims you can and should learn from others "to the greatest extent possible," but choosing who you can best learn from "is an iterative process that must constantly be reevaluated in light of your experience and your results" — those who can be most helpful "will change as you and your situation changes."
- Campbell argues change is unavoidable: "We are all constantly evolving our consciousness. Evolution forces choice and change. Remaining the same by choosing the no action option is not possible." Individual choices determine whether change is positive growth or negative deterioration.
- On teachers, Campbell claims a good teacher encourages, intensifies, and speeds learning while a bad one misdirects effort — and, unfortunately, "the more you need a good teacher, the less likely you are to be able to tell a good one from a bad one." The remedy is to stay skeptical, open minded, belief free, and to keep requiring measurable results ("taste that pudding"); six months with no obvious measurable results means buckle down or change approach.
- Campbell insists the results that count are results of the being, not the intellect: "It is about who you are, not what you know. It is about why you do what you do, not what you say, or what you do." Written tests and factual knowledge cannot help you pass a test of the quality of your being.
- Campbell claims truth is absolute but how to discover and express it must be personal; the right method is a tentative road map built by open-minded skepticism applied to others' experience and theory, then modified by your own data.
- Campbell assures scientists this discussion is "directly on the path to a scientifically legitimate, more general theory of physics"; a Big TOE requires wholly new paradigms and may wander through apparently ridiculous territory until Section 6 makes the connections clear.
- Campbell claims spiritual growth is not and cannot be an intellectual achievement; how you start matters far less than starting, and improvement does not flow automatically from any external activity or practice. What needs changing is not what you do but why you do it: "When the 'why' … is right, the 'what' will take care of itself."
- Campbell argues the Western mind is trapped in the belief that right results are products of right action (external actions for external results) and so is "effectively paralyzed" — like a deer in headlights — before internal change; but he adds it is unfair to single out Westerners: most Easterners are in the same "doing-fixated boat," since doing within a spiritual-cultural tradition is as unproductive as doing within a material-cultural one.
- Campbell claims genuine results are unsubtle and obvious to everyone: "A significant change in your capacity to love is as subtle as the healing of a badly broken leg." Self-proclaimed success means nothing.
- Campbell asserts the two most necessary ingredients are (1) a sincere desire to grow the quality of your consciousness and (2) the courage to change — to face fears, "death and personal destruction," the story the ego tells to dissuade you.
- Campbell defines the ego's main job as keeping you feeling good "by managing various systems of belief that are designed to keep your fears beyond the reach of your intellectual awareness"; growth requires facing fears and dissolving the ego, so expect it to struggle mightily. Great ego reflects great fear — not necessarily arrogance; timidity, worry, and insecurity are equally ego.
- Campbell claims consciousness must be explored from the inside: studying it objectively "is like studying biology by looking at pictures of zoo animals"; an outside approach limits you to "the facts about the shadow that consciousness projects upon the wall of PMR."
- Campbell describes our experience of others as mutual mirrors: we project consciousness into "other," define it in terms of ourselves, and see only our own reflection; "other" serves growth by "accurately reflecting the truth of you." Your conscious awareness defines your personal reality.
- Campbell claims we "project our personal consciousness onto the field of action of a multi-player interactive reality game whose point is our individual growth and learning."
- Campbell holds that in mental-awareness meditation "you do not actually have to learn how to meditate; you need only to learn how to stop blocking the meditation state from occurring naturally" — meditation is "an act of not doing."
- Campbell claims meditation works by turning down the contrast, noise, and self-referential chatter of the mind (filling it with non-operative busy-work — a generalized mantra) so you can experience the still, invisible background of consciousness; its purpose is self-discovery — a glimpse of the fundamental reality of which you are part.
- Campbell claims individual consciousness is a subset of absolute consciousness: you are not merely the "wrinkles" (clutter, ego, thoughts) you use to define yourself; "you are also consciousness, a piece of the whole."
- Campbell generalizes the mantra scientifically beyond tradition: since people take in data preferentially by ear, eye, or touch (auditory, visual, kinesthetic), mantras should match the dominant perception type rather than forcing everyone down the traditional auditory path.
- Campbell prescribes consistency: at least twenty minutes uninterrupted, twice daily, for three to six months before judging results; no expectations, no goals, no forcing — "If you ever become frustrated, you are trying too hard." Results must come to you; chasing them delays progress.
- Campbell adds a daily-choices practice: examine motivation and intent before and after (not during) each meditation, and by an act of will modify intents to be more giving, caring, loving, and less self-serving.
- Campbell claims the same meditation prescription is optimal for everyone regardless of starting point, because each person naturally extracts what they need — a "bootstrapping" operation of consciousness upon itself; that is why virtually everyone on the Path of Knowledge is instructed to begin with daily meditation.
- Campbell frames this as a life's work: progress accrues through "many unnoticeable tiny successes"; with moderate effort, significant results should become obvious within six months to a year; a teacher can only facilitate — growth is internal and "cannot be forced from the outside."

## Concepts
- **Big TOE** — Campbell's "Big Theory of Everything": the larger-perspective theory he is constructing, requiring "several wholly new paradigms," which he insists is on the path to "a scientifically legitimate, more general theory of physics" (as opposed to a "little TOE").
- **PMR** — physical-matter reality; the local physical reality. Campbell says reality is "fundamentally nonphysical (from a PMR perspective)" and that our culture believes we must do something in PMR to effect change; the objective study of consciousness only collects facts about "the shadow that consciousness projects upon the wall of PMR."
- **Big Truth** — the larger truth about reality that the individual explorer must discover personally; "discovering Big Truth and increasing the quality of your consciousness is fundamentally an independent individual effort."
- **quality of consciousness** — Campbell's core growth metric: the improvement of your conscious being — "the evolution of mind, the growing-up and maturing of spirit." Increasing it is what he means by spiritual growth; it is measured by obvious changes in attitude, awareness, fear, ego, and capacity to love.
- **consciousness evolution** — the natural process driving reality; it "forces choice and change," and both the spiritual and physical natures of our reality are "straightforwardly derived" from it. Direction (positive growth or negative deterioration) is determined by individual choices.
- **entropy / profitability** — Campbell says reality is "animated and driven by profitability toward states of lower entropy"; lower entropy corresponds to higher consciousness quality (humans have greater memory/processing capacity and "less entropy" than the average fish).
- **subjective science / personal science** — the scientific investigation of your own subjective experience; "the mother of objective science." It demands "real, verifiable, measurable, objective results" — significant continuing progress in the quality of your being — otherwise it is "only illusory." Its knowledge continually modifies itself as new data arrive.
- **objective science** — science of the external/objective; from the outside it can only study consciousness's projected shadow, not consciousness itself.
- **belief system** — any cultural, personal, religious, or scientific system requiring "a sincere belief in the assumed truth of their associated dogma, doctrine, and creed"; because correctness is assumed, results are not required and mature belief systems do not tend to change.
- **belief trap** — the trap of getting stuck in beliefs, patterns, habits, rituals, groups, or organizations that substitute for personal exploration; "Belief traps are bigger problems than most of us think they are."
- **open-minded skepticism** — Campbell's required stance: stay skeptical, open minded, and belief free; apply it to the experience and conjecture of others to build a tentative road map, then modify the map with your own data.
- **results ("taste the pudding")** — the sole valid measure of a growth process: "Actual, clear, unsubtle, measurable results" in the being (not the intellect); periodically "taste the pudding" (evaluate at three-month intervals, ask people close to you after six months). Genuine results are as unmissable as a healed broken leg.
- **being vs. doing** — Campbell's central distinction: growth is "a problem of being, not one of doing"; you "cannot modify being merely by taking physical action within the local physical reality"; internal change is primary, behavior and action are secondary results that follow on their own.
- **ego** — the function whose "main job is to keep you feeling good by managing various systems of belief that are designed to keep your fears beyond the reach of your intellectual awareness." It comes in infinite expressions (arrogance, timidity, worry, insecurity); "great ego reflects great fear." It invents the "I vs. other" delusional contrast, fears its own dissolution, and fights growth — when fundamental change begins, people run away, terrified that "the entire I-structure comes tumbling down into ruins" and that the ego will face "its own dissolution and death." (Campbell defers the full definition to an aside in Chapter 8, Book 2.)
- **intent / motivation ("the why")** — the internal variable that must change: "what you need to change or improve is why you are doing it"; when the why is right, the what takes care of itself.
- **meditation** — on Campbell's definition, "an act of not doing": a condition of inner attentiveness reached by removing the mind's contrasting clutter, in which you become aware of your personal consciousness, then of being "a unit of consciousness among many such units," and eventually regain your "fundamental identity as a spiritual (nonphysical) entity" and your oneness with all consciousness. You don't learn to meditate — you learn to stop blocking the state from occurring naturally.
- **mantra** — traditionally a sound; Campbell generalizes it "by science, not tradition" to any non-meaningful mental busy-work matched to your dominant perception channel (auditory, visual, kinesthetic — or smell/taste) that occupies the operative intellect so the still center of being can be experienced; complexity is tuned up when thoughts intrude and down as they fade, and the mantra is eventually let go.
- **absolute consciousness / individual consciousness** — "Individual consciousness is a subset of absolute consciousness"; individuals are "wrinkles in the fabric of consciousness," foregrounds of contrast against the invisible background — but also consciousness itself, "a piece of the whole."
- **source consciousness** — the "larger (source) consciousness" whose properties become accessible through scientifically probing your own subjective experience.
- **unit of consciousness** — what meditation eventually reveals you to be: one unit among many such units.
- **inner space** — the domain of the subjective exploration of consciousness; "the scientific exploration of inner space is the only logical approach that delivers results" for consciousness quality.
- **personal reality** — "Your conscious awareness defines your personal reality. There are as many different shades and levels of personal reality as there are of personal awareness."
- **multi-player interactive reality game** — Campbell's image of existence: we project our personal consciousness onto "the field of action of a multi-player interactive reality game whose point is our individual growth and learning."
- **Path of Knowledge** — the growth path toward spiritual growth / improved consciousness quality on which "virtually everyone … is instructed to begin with daily meditation."
- **bootstrapping** — each individual "pulling themselves up by their bootstraps" with his or her own consciousness; the reason one meditation prescription personalizes itself naturally to every starting point.
- **Big Picture / little picture** — the larger reality perspective to be gained vs. the "culturally conditioned false commitment to the little picture" that keeps people trapped in external doing.
- **love** — the marker capacity of growth: growth means becoming "more giving, caring, loving, and … less self-serving"; a significant change in your capacity to love is unmissable.
- **free will / choice** — implicit engine of growth here: evolution forces choice; "the individual choices you make ultimately determine the direction (positive or negative) of your growth"; the evolution of consciousness through choice is entirely personal.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The flypaper realms and the New Age carnival** — You can hope and pray for someone else to hand you enlightenment, but "that won't happen"; Campbell warns "do not expect to find shortcuts through the flypaper realms of religious, scientific, or personal dogma, or along the midway of a New Age carnival" — you must keep your mind free to change and grow.
- **The map analogy** — Others' advice is a map for your explorations: an incorrect map sends you on a wild goose chase; you can only evaluate a map's correctness as you go; a useful map is general while each journey is individual. Shows why guidance helps but cannot replace personal exploration.
- **The fish-in-water analogy (deep-sea fish)** — A fish two miles down in a four-mile-deep ocean, with no light and dim awareness, "knows nothing of water": water just is, taken for granted; the fish experiences only variations (current, temperature, salinity, viscosity), never water itself. "We swim in an ocean of consciousness" aware only of our local interactions with it. Campbell extends it: humans, with more memory/processing capacity and less entropy than the fish, can experience unvarying consciousness directly — meditation "lets us notice the water itself."
- **The sensory-deprivation tank** — Imagine a perfect sensory deprivation tank where zero input makes the local environment disappear; total immersion (in water, or in cultural belief systems) renders a thing invisible for lack of contrast. "Consciousness is like that." Campbell concedes it is "not a perfect analogy."
- **The wrinkles-in-fabric image** — We create a foreground of contrasts in the fabric of absolute consciousness and call it ourselves: "See that cute little wad of wrinkles in the fabric of consciousness? That's me!" But you are more than the wrinkles — you are also the fabric.
- **The fun-house hall of interactive mirrors** — Our objective sense of consciousness is our personal consciousness reflected "from the uniquely curvy surface of a mirror that we call 'another'"; symmetrically, we mirror others. Within this hall of mirrors, choices are made, "reality is actualized," and growth or regression is achieved; "other" grows you by accurately reflecting the truth of you.
- **Zoo-animal pictures** — Studying consciousness from the outside is "like studying biology by looking at pictures of zoo animals": consciousness is fundamentally individual and personal.
- **The shadow on the wall of PMR** — The outside approach collects only "facts about the shadow that consciousness projects upon the wall of PMR" (Campbell's own image for objective consciousness study).
- **The multi-player interactive reality game** — Existence as a game field onto which we project personal consciousness, whose point is individual growth and learning.
- **The deer in the headlights / spiritual roadkill** — Westerners, culturally certain that change requires doing something in PMR, freeze like a deer before an oncoming car asking "What should I do? Where should I start?"; later reprised comically: without something to do they become "spiritual road kill — run-aver by mindless conformity … Thump! Splat! … All the king's horses and all the king's men will have a difficult time getting that one back on the road to spiritual progress again."
- **The carpenter holding tools by the wrong ends** — Meditating for external change, paranormal abilities, or guilt-placation is like a carpenter building cabinets while holding screwdriver and hammer by the wrong ends: all the pieces are there, execution is flawed — leading to the false conclusion that the tools are useless, "that cabinets are a logical impossibility, and that all carpenters are delusional frauds and fools." Illustrates why failed meditators say "I tried spiritual exercises, and they didn't work for me."
- **The failed meditators and habitual churchgoers** — Sociological sketch: in the West meditation is acultural and individual; most churchgoers attend out of social convenience, habit, duty, or cultural expectation; most meditators decide it isn't worth the effort; a few of each pretend superiority; the honest failed meditators give up and forget. Shows external ritual without internal change produces nothing.
- **The musical-instrument analogy** — Learning to meditate is like learning an instrument: serious steady effort before music replaces "screeching noises," long dedication to master it, and most who try give up first; pretending to play, however impressive the external display, produces nothing. Also used for pacing: progress in both accrues slowly through many tiny successes.
- **The healed broken leg** — A significant change in your capacity to love is "as subtle as the healing of a badly broken leg — nobody, including you, could miss noticing the change." Genuine results are not subtle.
- **The meditation program itself (Campbell's practical model)** — moving parts:
  1. *Prerequisites*: sincere desire to change at a deeply personal level; courage to face fear (the ego will plead "death and personal destruction").
  2. *Auditory mantra*: a meaningless two-syllable sound ending in a soothing/vibratory sound — examples "sehr-ring," "da-room," "ra-zing," "ca-ouhn," "sah-roon," "sher-loom"; chant option "ah-lum-bar-dee-dum — ah-lum-baa-dee-dum" (you're on your way when "bar"/"baa" interchange effortlessly); mix-and-match first/second syllables gives "no fewer than thirty-six unique mantras." Sit comfortably, eyes closed, fill the mind with the sound (no actual sound needed), let it slow and smear into continuous background.
  3. *Visual mantra*: non-personal visualization from complexity to simplicity — a black-and-white soccer ball changing colors and rotating; then simple geometric shapes (spheres, cubes, circles, triangles, cylinders, rectangles, lines) slowly changing; imagery "clear as a watercolor painting, not … a high-resolution photograph"; or a generic beach scene narrowing to a single grain of sand inspected from every angle until "only you and the grain of sand quietly coexist within the void."
  4. *Kinesthetic mantra*: non-personal pleasant textures — mentally rubbing a velvet or fur coat, becoming tiny and crawling into a pocket; walking barefoot in squishy mud, walking in rain, "swimming in a pool filled with grape jelly"; complexity to simple rhythmic sensation.
  5. *Smell/taste*: build your own analogously ("a smelly or yummy mantra"); mixing senses allowed; no intellectual or emotional connection to the mantra.
  6. *Operating rules*: gently set intruding thoughts aside; more complexity if thoughts stream in, simplify as they fade; let the mantra go when unneeded and "drift in the quiet blankness of your consciousness." Twenty minutes uninterrupted, twice a day, three months minimum (judge only after three to six months); no expectations, no goals, no forcing, no analyzing during; results must come to you.
  7. *Companion practice*: examine motivations and intent for the day's choices immediately before and after (never during) meditation; by will, shift intent toward giving/caring/loving; thirty minutes twice a day covers both.
  8. *Troubleshooting*: if frustrated — back off, change mantra, attitude, or technique; perhaps you're trying too hard, belief/fear-limited, or not ready; "no blame … no failure"; success will "take you by surprise" when you relax.
- **The children/adults comparison** — "No one faults children for not being adults, though most children wish they were adults": there is no technique for skipping steps; accept who you are gracefully.
- **Personality-typing by data-input preference** — Campbell cites "the popular literature" of the previous decade assessing personality by preferred input channel (auditory/visual/kinesthetic) as the basis for generalizing mantra beyond the traditional auditory path.
- **The "thoughtless men" gag** — Campbell's deliberate "ego-tweak": why are most spiritual gurus men? "Because, men are born thoughtless and remain that way the rest of their lives!" — a double-entendre on the meditative state of thoughtless existence, followed by mock crowd-management ("peace and light — no violence until after I leave"). An example of his running technique of tweaking readers' egos.
- **The "try not to try" koan** — "What you are trying to do without trying is to not do. Read that sentence again"; if it makes sense you are on your way, if it sounds like gibberish restart the aside — "but don't get stuck in an endless loop — twice is enough."
- **Forward reference** — Ego will be carefully defined "in an aside in Chapter 8, Book 2. Go there now if you are seriously confused."

## Thread
This chapter closes the arc of Section 2's belief-vs-knowledge argument (it explicitly "pulls together what we have learned about the origins and consequences of belief and the requirements of personal growth") and follows directly from the preceding chapter's conclusion that you must do your own exploring and grow your own wisdom. Its title question — who ya gonna call for help? — gets Campbell's answer: helpers, maps, and teachers exist, but ultimately yourself; evaluate everything by results. It is also the trilogy's first practical how-to: the meditation aside converts the epistemology (open-minded skepticism, taste the pudding) into a concrete, dogma-free daily practice, positioned as the doorway to exploring consciousness. Structurally it is a staging chapter: Campbell tells scientists to hold on — the consciousness/spirituality material is "directly on the path" to a more general physics, with the derivation promised over the next two sections (Books 2's material) and full clarity only "through the end of Section 6." It seeds later machinery: reality as nonphysical and entropy-reduction-driven, individual consciousness as a subset of absolute/source consciousness, reality as a multi-player interactive game for growth, and a forward pointer to the full treatment of ego in Chapter 8 of Book 2.
