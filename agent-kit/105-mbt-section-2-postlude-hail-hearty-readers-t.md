# Ch 34 — Section 2 Postlude Hail! Hearty Readers, Thou Hast Shown Divine Patience and Great Tenacity

## In one paragraph
This very short postlude closes Section 2 ("Mysticism Demystified") by acknowledging that the reader has just absorbed a dense pile of new concepts and probably has little experience with which to judge their truth. Campbell says that is the nature of Big Truth — "either trivially obvious or totally opaque" — and instructs the reader not to believe anything, for or against, but to stay open-minded and skeptical this early in the journey. He argues that what seems abstruse and beyond knowing only appears that way from the limited perspective of one's culture and self-limited experience, and that overestimating one's own vision is a natural trait of high entropy consciousness constrained to an individuated unit of independent action (a concept he defers to Section 4). He justifies Section 2's abstract material (the "life and times of AUM") as necessary groundwork for the Big Picture, then previews Section 3 ("Man in the Loop"), which will connect those concepts to the reader's own physical and mental reality and be less abstract. He ends by playfully awarding tenacious readers four gold stars.

## Core claims
- Campbell claims Big Truth is by nature "either trivially obvious or totally opaque," which is why readers without relevant experience cannot yet evaluate what they have read.
- Campbell instructs the reader to make no judgments and hold no beliefs "for or against" — maintain an open mind and remain "always skeptical," because this is only the beginning of the journey.
- Campbell argues that a trilogy-length exposition is required precisely because the material could not all make sense early on; ambivalence and feeling lost are the expected state at this stage.
- Campbell claims that what he writes only *seems* abstruse, unsubstantiated, and beyond knowing to those with little experience "systematically exploring the larger reality of inner space" — it is not actually beyond knowing, it is merely out of sight of a limited vision shaped by culture and self-limited experience.
- Campbell claims that believing your vision is less limited than it actually is "reflects a natural trait of high entropy consciousness that is constrained to an individuated unit of independent action" (to be clarified in Section 4).
- Campbell states that Section 2's material, however remote from the reader's sense of reality, was necessary groundwork: it introduced basic ideas needed to bring the Big Picture into focus, and everything will fit together more smoothly later.
- Campbell promises that Section 3 ("Man in the Loop") will make the first tentative connections between Section 2's "weird off-the-wall concepts" and the reader's physical and mental reality, will be less abstract, and will offer things the reader has the experience to evaluate.

## Concepts
- **big-truth** — Campbell's term for truth of the largest scope; its nature is that "it is either trivially obvious or totally opaque," so most readers lack the experience to evaluate it on first encounter.
- **big-picture** — the overall view of reality that the trilogy is assembling; Section 2's basic ideas were introduced so the reader can eventually "bring the Big Picture into focus."
- **aum** — the entity whose "life and times" were the subject of Section 2's discussion (Campbell puns on its name with readers yawning "ahhh-ummms"). Not redefined here; referenced as the abstract subject matter just completed.
- **inner-space** — the domain of the "larger reality" that can be systematically explored; readers without such exploratory experience will find Campbell's claims seemingly abstruse and unsubstantiated.
- **larger-reality** — the greater reality beyond the reader's ordinary sense of reality, accessible via systematic exploration of inner space; things in it that seem "beyond anyone's possible knowing" are actually "only out of sight of a limited vision and understanding."
- **high-entropy-consciousness** — consciousness in a high-entropy (low-quality) state; Campbell says a natural trait of high entropy consciousness constrained to an individuated unit is believing one's vision is less limited than it actually is.
- **individuated-unit-of-independent-action** — the constrained, individuated form of consciousness the reader is; Campbell names it here and explicitly defers clarification to Section 4.
- **open-minded-skepticism** — the reader stance Campbell prescribes (though not named as a phrase here): "Do not believe anything, for or against. Maintain an open mind and… always skeptical."
- **man-in-the-loop** — the title of Section 3, previewed here as the section "all about you" that connects Section 2's concepts to the reader's physical and mental reality.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The teenage boy at the buffet analogy** — new concepts and ideas have been "piled one upon the other upon the next like food on the plate of a teenage boy at an all-you-can-eat junk food buffet"; Campbell uses it to concede the density of Section 2 and the reader's likely overload.
- **The five-year-old child / pet critter analogy** — Campbell asks the reader to consider how a five-year-old child, or "your favorite pet critter," views and understands the larger world it lives in; this shows how limited vision makes real knowledge seem beyond possible knowing — the reader stands to the larger reality as the child or pet stands to the adult world.
- **The "ahhh-ummms" yawn pun** — Campbell jokes that if the discussion of "the life and times of AUM" was tough slogging, it may have prompted the reader "to yawn a few ahhh-ummms of your own"; a wordplay tying reader fatigue to AUM's name.
- **The four gold stars award** — readers who made it this far with desire and gumption should award themselves "four beautiful gold stars" and paste them inside the front cover of the book so everyone will know they are a "dedicated, committed, serious seeker after something extraordinarily important even if you are not sure at this point exactly what it is"; a playful device to reward tenacity and frame the reader as a seeker.

## Key quotes
- "That is the nature of Big Truth. It is either trivially obvious or totally opaque." — on why readers cannot yet evaluate the material.
- "Do not believe anything, for or against. Maintain an open mind and realize that you are only at the beginning of this journey." — the prescribed reader stance.
- "you should expect (this early in the unfolding) to feel somewhat ambivalent, lost, and — please — always skeptical." — normalizing the reader's disorientation.
- "It only seems that way from the perspective of your culture and your self-limited experience." — on why the material appears abstruse and unsubstantiated.
- "Many things will seem absolutely beyond anyone's possible knowing when they are actually only out of sight of a limited vision and understanding." — the core epistemic claim of the postlude.
- "Believing that your vision is less limited than it actually is reflects a natural trait of high entropy consciousness that is constrained to an individuated unit of independent action." — linking overconfidence to entropy, with clarification deferred to Section 4.
- "this next section — Section 3 — is all about you." — the pivot to "Man in the Loop."
- "award yourself four beautiful gold stars for your intellectual tenacity and inquisitiveness." — the closing reward for persistent readers.

## Thread
This is the closing bookend of Section 2 ("Mysticism Demystified," chapters 17–34), which built the abstract metaphysics — the "life and times of AUM." Its role is pastoral and structural rather than argumentative: it tells the reader that confusion is expected, that judgment should be suspended (neither belief nor disbelief), that Section 2's abstractions were necessary groundwork for the Big Picture, and it hands off to Book 2's Section 3, "Man in the Loop" (ch 35–49), which promises to connect the cosmology to the reader's own physical and mental reality. It also plants a forward pointer to Section 4, where the concept of high entropy consciousness constrained to an individuated unit of independent action will be clarified.
