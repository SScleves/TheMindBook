# Tom's Acknowledgments

## In one paragraph
This is Tom DeLonge's brief acknowledgments note in the front matter of the book — a single paragraph of thanks. Rather than naming individuals, DeLonge thanks a "long list of collaborators on this project" whom he describes as "the ones in the shadows, from deeper places within the government that know a lot more than we do." He credits them with taking risks to bring forth "new ways of thinking, positive change and a few more breadcrumbs that point to the larger story." Even at this length, the note signals the book's premise: that the authors' project is informed by anonymous government insiders, and that what the public knows is only a fragment of a larger hidden story.

## Core claims
- DeLonge claims the project had a long list of collaborators who remain unnamed and "in the shadows."
- DeLonge claims these collaborators come "from deeper places within the government" and "know a lot more than we do" — i.e., that parts of the government hold knowledge exceeding public understanding.
- DeLonge claims these insiders took risks by collaborating, and did so to produce "new ways of thinking, positive change and a few more breadcrumbs."
- DeLonge implies there is a "larger story" to which these breadcrumbs point — a bigger picture not yet fully disclosed.

## Concepts
- **collaborators in the shadows** — DeLonge's phrase for the project's unnamed contributors: people "from deeper places within the government that know a lot more than we do," who took risks by participating.
- **breadcrumbs** — DeLonge's metaphor for the partial pieces of information the insiders provide: not the whole truth, but "a few more breadcrumbs that point to the larger story."
- **the larger story** — the implied bigger hidden narrative that the breadcrumbs point toward; DeLonge does not define it here beyond asserting its existence.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
No specific cases, events, or named individuals appear. The only concrete material is DeLonge's characterization of his sources: anonymous government insiders ("from deeper places within the government") who collaborated on the project at personal risk, which he presents as evidence that the book draws on inside knowledge beyond what the public has.

## Key quotes
- "I would like to thank the long list of collaborators on this project, the ones in the shadows, from deeper places within the government that know a lot more than we do." — the entire basis of the acknowledgment: unnamed insider sources.
- "All of them, taking risks, only to bring forth new ways of thinking, positive change and a few more breadcrumbs that point to the larger story." — DeLonge frames his sources' motive as reform and gradual revelation.

## Thread
As front matter, this note frames the book before the argument begins: it asserts insider sourcing and stakes the authors' credibility on anonymous government collaborators. It primes the reader for the book's central themes — official secrecy, partial disclosure, and a hidden "larger story" — which the Introduction ("Welcome to the War Room"), the eleven chapters, and the Afterword ("The Alien in the Panopticon") develop. It sits alongside the dedications and the co-author's acknowledgments as the personal voice of DeLonge in the front matter.

## Connections
- Within this book: pairs with the other front-matter units (dedications, Peter's acknowledgments, preface) and sets up the Introduction; the "government knows more than we do" framing anticipates the secrecy/control thesis of the Afterword essay "The Alien in the Panopticon."
- Cross-book (speculative/thematic, not the author's reference): the motif of a hidden "larger story" behind official reality loosely echoes the reality-behind-appearances themes of the-holographic-universe, the-simulation-hypothesis, and my-big-toe, but DeLonge makes no such link here.
