# Science, Truth, Knowledge, Reality, and You

## In one paragraph
This chapter is Thomas Campbell's methodological preface to Book 3's "mechanics of reality" material: he restates that My Big TOE is science in the original sense (observation, description, experimental investigation, theoretical explanation) built from thirty years of his own firsthand experience, not belief. He argues that an "objective evaluation of subjective experience" is not a contradiction, and introduces a distinction between "intellectual objects" (shared, physical, measurable) and "being objects" (personal, nonphysical, consciousness-based) as the two kinds of data any complete account of reality must include. From there he develops a parallel distinction between knowledge (mastery of intellectual objects) and wisdom (evolved, low-entropy being objects plus Big Picture caring), and argues that the "science of being" needs a broader, uncertainty-tolerant methodology than the science of objects, and cannot be settled by consensus. The chapter closes by telling the reader explicitly how to hold the material: not as dogma to believe or disbelieve, but as a model to test against one's own gathered experience, in a stance of "open minded skepticism."

## Core claims
- Thomas Campbell states that My Big TOE is a "relational logical structure" describing the forms, functions, and processes of the larger reality, built using science "in its original and most general sense," from thirty years of his personal observation and investigation.
- He argues that transforming this material into knowledge requires the reader's own experience, not faith, and that if the material doesn't resonate — or resonates only with anger — the reader should discard it rather than force the learning.
- He claims his model is based entirely on direct firsthand experience, with philosophical bias, preconceived notions, and belief minimized to the best of his ability, and that the data represents his "best objective evaluation of my subjective experience."
- He argues that calling "objective evaluation of subjective experience" an oxymoron reflects too narrow a definition of "objective" — results can be objectively measured even when the underlying motivations and intent that produced them are entirely subjective.
- He claims the model's correctness must be judged, like any scientific model (he cites the shell model of the atom), by its ability to explain existing data within a self-consistent, coherent system — not by whether it convinces the reader.
- He explicitly disclaims any effort to convince the reader of the model's "goodness-of-fit"; understanding must be personally earned through firsthand experience, not book-learned.
- He states the point of the book is, in addition to offering a coherent understanding of reality and time and explaining the purpose and significance of the reader's existence, to help the reader interpret both objective and subjective reality data, possibly nudge the reader toward theories of their own, help solve a personal puzzle, and act as a catalyst to learning by expanding the reader's set of perceived possibilities.
- He argues any complete set of data about the larger reality must include both "intellectual objects" (shared physical objects obeying PMR's space-time causality) and "being objects" (nonphysical, individuated consciousness in various states of awareness and quality).
- He claims a system capable of supporting consciousness spontaneously generates self-organizing potential so the Fundamental Process of evolution, pursuing profitability, generates synergy through recursive reorganization ("learning") to form an individuated consciousness independent of its supporting mechanism.
- He distinguishes knowledge (about intellectual objects, "good to have," required to apply wisdom) from wisdom (about being objects, requiring Big Picture understanding and deep caring); he states knowledge without wisdom is common, but wisdom without knowledge is impossible.
- He argues that applying the standards of traditional (intellectual-object) science to the science of being effectively denies the latter's existence, and that turning the scientific method into "a universal dogma (science as religion)" wrongly makes the science of being look delusional and unprovable.
- He argues a digital, quantized, statistical reality (comparable to the one quantum mechanics describes) requires a broader, generalized scientific method that tolerates uncertainty and allows truth to be derived from statistical data via inductive as well as deductive reasoning.
- He claims that in the science of being, unlike the science of objects, consensus does not define truth — functionality and results (how something helps grow the quality of one's consciousness) are the real measures of knowledge's value, and "Big Truth does not rearrange itself to fit the whims or demands of the fashionable majority."
- He states the science of being is an individual science that cannot be advanced through group effort; its significance lies in its effect on an individual's willingness and ability to profitably evolve their own consciousness.
- He recommends "open minded skepticism," neither predisposed to confirm nor deny, as the correct stance for reading the rest of the section, warning that any other stance risks missing important points.
- He states that any connections the reader draws between My Big TOE and other bodies of thought are the reader's own synthesis, not his intention, though he calls making such connections "good" and "necessary, helpful and important."

## Concepts
- **My Big TOE** — the author's "relational logical structure" defining the forms, functions, and processes that constitute the reader's larger reality; described here as science in the original, general sense (observation, identification, description, experimental investigation, theoretical explanation) built from thirty years of the author's own accumulated observations and investigations.
- **science (original sense)** — as the author uses the word: "the observation, identification, description, experimental investigation, and theoretical explanation of phenomena" — broader than the popular, narrow, materialist sense.
- **being objects** — nonphysical objects "primarily represented by units of individuated consciousness in various states of awareness and quality"; they are one's consciousness, its attributes, and its evolutionary processes; personal and primarily experienced through subjective personal awareness rather than shared.
- **intellectual objects** — physical, shared, public objects (including the body and other physical objects) that "must obey the objective causality (space-time rule-set) of PMR," and that can be probed, measured, and tabulated by the intellect and tools of others.
- **PMR** — referenced in this chapter as the domain whose "objective causality (space-time rule-set)" intellectual objects must obey; the physical, shared reality governed by space-time rules (defined more fully elsewhere in the trilogy per the chapter's cross-references).
- **Fundamental Process** — the process of evolution that, "in the pursuit of profitability," generates sufficient synergy through recursive reorganization (learning) to form an individuated consciousness independent of the mechanisms that support it, given a system capable of supporting consciousness.
- **synergy** — the outcome generated by the Fundamental Process through the recursive reorganization called learning, sufficient to form individuated consciousness.
- **learning (as recursive reorganization)** — the process by which a system's self-organizing potential is recursively reorganized, described here as the mechanism producing synergy and, ultimately, individuated consciousness.
- **consciousness creation process** — the overall process (encompassing the Fundamental Process, synergy, and learning) whose individual result is a "being object"; a digital system's potential to profitably self-organize is described as constituting a nonphysical form of energy the Fundamental Process organizes into successively lower entropy configurations.
- **entropy / low entropy (of being)** — used as a quality measure for being objects: one who has "successfully evolved low entropy being objects is said to be wise and capable of being a great person."
- **knowledge** — mastery in "acquiring, assimilating, and manipulating intellectual objects"; makes one "knowledgeable" and "capable of doing great things (has good quality ideas)"; is about facts and is required to apply wisdom, but is not the same as wisdom.
- **wisdom** — more than complete knowledge; requires "Big Picture understanding and caring at a deep level," both attributes of experience and being rather than of information; wisdom "understands how those facts relate and interact within the Big Picture"; the author states wisdom without knowledge is impossible, while knowledge without wisdom is common.
- **Big Picture** — the larger, integrated context within which facts relate and interact; understanding it (plus deep caring) is what elevates knowledge to wisdom.
- **science of being** — the (currently unrecognized) science concerned with being objects — mind, consciousness, psi, love, spirituality — which the author says Western science and philosophy are "ill-equipped and ill-prepared" to address; it is an individual science that "cannot be advanced through a group effort," its significance measured by its effect on an individual's evolution of consciousness.
- **science of objects** (traditional/Western science) — the science of intellectual/material objects, whose method (the scientific method) is described as excellent for Newton's mechanistic universe and Einstein's relative universe but of limited validity when applied, as universal dogma, outside the realm of intellectual objects.
- **self-consistent fool / invisible fool** — the author's warning about the risk of separating the wise from the foolish by applying beliefs rather than carefully evaluated firsthand experience: doing so makes you "merely a self-consistent fool — or one fool among many (an invisible fool) if your beliefs are shared by others."
- **consensus vs. truth** — the author's claim that in the science of being, unlike the science of objects, "consensus does not define the accepted truth, much less the actual truth"; functionality and results are the true measures of knowledge's value.
- **Big Truth** — capitalized in the text as the stable, majority-independent truth that "does not rearrange itself to fit the whims or demands of the fashionable majority" and "is, and has always been, the same as it is now."
- **open minded skepticism** — the recommended stance for reading the section: neither predisposed to confirm nor to deny what is read, to avoid missing important points.
- **generalized scientific method** — a broader methodology the author says is needed for a "digital (quantized) statistical reality," one that tolerates uncertainty and allows a "larger more porous truth" to be derived from uncertain statistical data using both inductive and deductive reasoning.
- **digital (quantized) statistical reality** — the author's characterization of reality as "somewhat like the one described by quantum mechanics," based on the constraints of our knowing, requiring the generalized scientific method above.
- **goodness-of-fit** — the standard (borrowed from scientific model evaluation) by which a model's correctness is judged: its ability to explain existing data within a self-consistent, coherent system; the author explicitly declines to argue for his model's goodness-of-fit, leaving that evaluation to the reader's own experience.
- **objective vs. subjective (evaluation)** — the author's argument that results can be objectively measured even when the motivations, understanding, and intent that produced them are entirely subjective, so "objective evaluation of subjective experience" is not a genuine oxymoron.
- **shell model of the atom** — cited as an example of a scientific model judged correct not by absolute truth but by its ability to explain existing data self-consistently and coherently.
- **blind men and the elephant** (proverbial reference) — invoked as a caution against jumping to conclusions about the whole of reality from the one part an individual happens to experience.
- **automobile / nutcracker analogy** — the author's illustration that evaluating My Big TOE using only public (objective) experience is like inventing a car and using it only to crack walnuts by driving over them — technically possible but missing the point of the vehicle (the Big Picture).

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The author's thirty years of firsthand observation** — presented as the empirical base of the whole model: "The observations have been accumulated and the investigations undertaken by the author during the past thirty years," offered as evidence the model is derived from direct experience rather than belief or secondhand surmise.
- **Everyday relationships as a source of subjectively-derived objective truth** — the author asks the reader to consider "correct, objective conclusions or knowledge (truth)" reached through "subjectively driven and interpreted interactions with friends, family members, lovers, spouses, children, bosses, or parents," used to argue that useful, even objective, conclusions can flow from subjective experience via inductive as well as deductive reasoning.
- **The shell model of the atom** — used as a worked example of how "correctness" is judged for any scientific model: not by absolute truth but by explanatory power over existing data within a coherent system, applied by analogy to how My Big TOE itself should be judged.
- **Classical mechanics in the realm of the very small and fast** — cited as an example of a scientific method reaching its limits ("classical mechanics makes hopelessly wrong inferences" there), used to argue by analogy that the ordinary scientific method similarly has "little validity outside its realm of intellectual objects" when applied to being.
- **The automobile-as-nutcracker thought experiment** — a deliberately absurd scenario (inventing a car and using it only to crack walnuts by driving over them one at a time) illustrating the cost of evaluating the TOE using only objective, public experience while ignoring the subjective (private) portion.
- **The blind men and the elephant** (proverbial case) — invoked as a caution against reaching for conclusions about the character of the whole "elephant" (reality) based only on the one part personally experienced.
- **Cross-references to earlier chapters as embedded "case studies" of prior arguments** — the chapter repeatedly directs the reader back to specific earlier material as already-worked demonstrations: Chapter 13, Book 2 (on deriving truth/objective knowledge from subjective experience), Chapter 14, Book 2 (twice — on judging a model's correctness, and on the "detailed solution" to separating the wise from the foolish), Chapters 32 and 33, Book 2 (on the personal/subjective nature of what counts as valid existing data), and Chapters 7 and 23, Book 2 (on the Fundamental Process and consciousness formation).

## Thread
Positioned as the second chapter of Section 5 ("Inner Space, the Final Frontier") opening Book 3, this chapter functions as a methodological gateway into the "mechanics of reality" material that follows. It reprises and consolidates epistemological groundwork the author says was laid earlier in Book 2 (the chapters on deriving objective truth from subjective experience, on judging a model's goodness-of-fit, and on separating the wise from the foolish), and uses that groundwork to introduce the being-object/intellectual-object and wisdom/knowledge distinctions that recur through the rest of the trilogy's discussion of consciousness, evolution, and the science of being. By explicitly instructing the reader to adopt "open minded skepticism" rather than belief or disbelief, it sets the interpretive stance the author wants applied to the more technical model-building chapters (covering PMR, the Fundamental Process, and individuated consciousness) that follow in Book 3.
