# Section 4 Postlude Hail! Hearty Reader, Thine Open Mind and Force of Will are Truly Extraordinary

## In one paragraph
This short postlude closes Section 4 by directly addressing the reader in two voices: those with little experience beyond PMR (Physical Matter Reality), who by this point have had their beliefs "thoroughly trashed," and those with considerable experience outside PMR, for whom the book has instead supplied a conceptual structure that finally makes sense of their unexplained experiences. Thomas Campbell then offers the reader a third, dismissive option — burn the book and write it off as "My Big Delusion" — before pivoting to frame what comes next: Section 5, which he says will build an operational model of reality on top of the conceptual foundation laid so far. He signs off encouraging the reader to press on with the same open-minded skepticism, perseverance, and stamina the book has demanded throughout.

## Core claims
- Thomas Campbell claims that readers with little to no experience beyond PMR will, by this point in the book, have had "almost everything they ever believed in thoroughly trashed or turned upside down and inside out," producing feelings of being lost, empty, or rootless.
- He claims such uncomfortable feelings are "often the doorway to a better place than you inhabited before," reframing the disorientation as a positive sign of progress rather than a problem.
- He tells the reader to "award yourself four more of those highly coveted beautiful golden stars; paste them in your book next to your name and you will feel better" — a continuation of the running gold-stars reward device used in the Section 2 and Section 3 postludes.
- He claims that readers with considerable experience outside PMR will instead find in My Big TOE a conceptual structure and context that finally makes sense of unusual experiences they always knew were significant but could not previously explain or integrate into their worldview.
- He states the book's aim for such readers is to transform "the incomprehensible into the well understood, the mystical into the mundane, the fear of being strange into the assurance of being normal, and far-out seekers searching blindly by trial and error into well-reasoned individuals with a good explanation of what they are doing and why they are doing it."
- He acknowledges, with self-deprecating humor, that the reader may instead dismiss the whole book as "My Big Delusion — the ravings of a wacko physicist who has obviously lost his way," and notes the book at least serves as a good fire-starter.
- He states that Section 5 is "the result of everything we have accomplished in the previous sections" and is where the previously introduced ideas "begin to pull together into a coherent... model of reality."
- He claims Section 5 will reveal "the mechanics of how the larger reality functions," describe its processes, explain what's "out there," and explain why unusual experiences are "rationally bounded."
- He states that up through Section 4, only the conceptual foundation has been laid, and that Section 5 will build the operational model of the larger reality upon that foundation.
- He warns that the trail ahead "gets steep in a few places" because the structure and processes of reality get complex, though he says most of the conceptual heavy lifting is already behind the reader.
- He states that Section 5 will require open-minded skepticism, perseverance, and dogged personal stamina "at least as much, if not more" than previous sections required.

## Concepts
- **PMR (Physical Matter Reality)** — used here as the baseline reality frame against which the reader's prior beliefs and "little to no experience" are measured; readers without experience beyond it are described as having had their beliefs most thoroughly upended by the book so far.
- **My Big TOE** — the author's name for his own theory/book, invoked here as the thing that either transforms the reader's understanding of anomalous experience or, alternatively, gets dismissed by the skeptical reader.
- **My Big Delusion** — Campbell's own humorous alternate name for his work, offered as the label a dismissive reader might apply instead, casting the whole book as "the ravings of a wacko physicist who has obviously lost his way."
- **Conceptual foundation vs. operational model** — a distinction the author draws between what the book has done so far (laying conceptual foundation, through Section 4) and what it is about to do (building an operational model that describes how the larger reality actually functions, in Section 5).
- **Open-minded skepticism** — named here as one of the required dispositions (alongside perseverance and "dogged personal stamina") for getting the most out of Section 5, echoing the stance demanded throughout the book.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
This unit is a short direct-address postlude and contains no field experiments, informants, or case studies. Its only "concrete" material is the author's characterization of two reader archetypes (the PMR-bound reader vs. the reader with outside-PMR experience) and a light metaphor of himself as gardener: "I simply supplied the hoe, trowel and rototiller (or was that a bulldozer?) and gave some encouragement," describing his own role in dismantling the reader's prior beliefs over the course of the book.

## Thread
This postlude functions as a hinge between Book 2's Section 4 ("Solving the Mystery," ch50-72) and Section 5 ("Inner Space, the Final Frontier," ch73-86, opening Book 3: INNER WORKINGS). It closes out the conceptual work of Sections 1-4 by acknowledging the disorienting effect that work has had on readers without outside-PMR experience, while validating readers with such experience as having finally found an explanatory framework. It explicitly reframes everything prior as "conceptual foundation" and casts Section 5 as the shift into an operational model of how the larger reality actually functions — setting reader expectations that the material ahead compiles the pieces into a working system rather than introducing new isolated concepts.
