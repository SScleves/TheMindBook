# Ch 7 — Time Out of Mind

## In one paragraph
Talbot argues that in a holographic universe linear time is not fundamental: past and future both remain "recorded" in the implicate order and are therefore accessible to consciousness. He opens with evidence for retrocognition — clairvoyants such as Ossowiecki and McMullen reading the past from artifacts and sites with archaeologically verified accuracy — and interprets it through Bohm's idea that the past does not cease to exist but re-enfolds into the implicate, where mind and the record of the past are "neighbors." He then surveys precognition (card-guessing, dream labs, remote viewing, Croiset's chair tests) and resolves the free-will paradox with Loye's picture of multiple parallel holographic futures between which we make "hololeaps," and Bohm's parallel view that premonitions perceive tendencies already implicate in the present. The chapter's second half turns to reincarnation research (Whitton, Stevenson, Wambach) as evidence that a deep, "metaconscious" stratum of the psyche actively scripts our destiny — even our bodies — an idea Talbot finds echoed in kahuna, Tibetan tantric, Sufi, and other traditions of "thought as builder." He closes with three further time anomalies: mass hypnotic progressions into four alternative futures, retroactive psychokinesis on the past, and apparent physical "walks" into past scenes such as the Moberly–Jourdain Versailles episode.

## Core claims
- Talbot argues the past is not lost but persists in the implicate order — Bohm's "cosmic storehouse" — and retrocognition is a shift of attention that accesses it; since consciousness also has its source in the implicate, mind and the record of the past "are, in a manner of speaking, already neighbors."
- Talbot claims retrocognition "has been confirmed repeatedly by researchers" and, citing Schwartz, that psychic archaeology has been "a reality" for three-quarters of a century.
- Talbot argues the retrocognitive experience is hologram-like in two ways: the accessed scenes are fully three-dimensional, and the record is nonlocal — accessible at the site or many miles away, like information in a hologram.
- Talbot argues the multiple-image "white light" hologram is a metaphor for time itself: the whole activity is always recorded, and the viewer's changing perspective creates the illusion of unfolding in time.
- Following Evans-Wentz, Talbot suggests some "fairy" sightings are afterimages of past events — "Nature's mental records" — and that belief determines whether such abilities manifest (the Manx "education" remark).
- Talbot claims an "enormous body of evidence" proves at least some future events are as easy to see as past ones (Rhine, Schmidt, dream-lab, remote-viewing, chair-test results).
- Talbot argues the future is neither frozen nor nonexistent but "substantive enough for us to perceive it, but malleable enough to be susceptible to change" — Loye's many jostling holographic futures and "hololeaps," Bohm's "shadows cast deep in the implicate order," Swann's and the kahunas' "crystallizing possibilities." Precognized disasters that were avoided (Titanic cases) show the future is plastic, so free will survives.
- Talbot presents Whitton's and Stevenson's reincarnation data as suggestive (explicitly not proof — "The evidence does not prove that reincarnation exists, nor is it the intention of this book to make such an argument") that a deeply unconscious part of us plans our life circumstances, chooses our companions, and even shapes our physical body via a nonphysical "template" body.
- Talbot argues, via Stevenson, that there is no "retributive karma" or external judge — "we are the makers of our own souls" — and that moral character, not material circumstance, carries between lives.
- Talbot claims the idea that thought creates destiny recurs across shamanic, Hawaiian, Tibetan, Sufi, biblical, kabbalistic, and modern self-help sources, and asks whether this ubiquity signals "something all human beings intuitively know is true"; in a holographic universe he judges it "not so farfetched. It even seems probable."
- Talbot argues (via Schmidt–Schlitz) that mind may even alter the past retroactively, and (via the Versailles cases) that under some conditions people may physically walk into past scenes — conclusions he frames as possibilities "we may have to become accustomed" to, since in a holographic universe time is an illusion.

## Concepts
- **Implicate order (as home of time)** — Epigraph from Larry Dossey (*Recovering the Soul*): the implicate order is the "home" of the mind and of all things, an atemporal plenum where "moments are not strung together serially like beads on a string." Bohm: the flow of time is a constant series of unfoldings and enfoldings; as the present enfolds into the past it returns to the cosmic storehouse of the implicate.
- **Retrocognition** — "the ability of certain individuals to shift the focus of their attention and literally gaze back into the past."
- **Psychometry** — handling an object (a bone fragment, a flint, a petrified foot) to tune into scenes from its past; Ossowiecki's, McMullen's, and Croiset's working method.
- **Multiple-image / white-light hologram (metaphor for the past)** — successive images of an activity recorded like frames of a movie; a "white light" hologram is visible to the naked eye without laser light, and walking past it yields a 3-D motion picture. The entire activity is always in the film; the viewer's changing perspective creates the illusion of transience.
- **Nonlocality of the past** — the record of the past is not stored at any one location; like holographic information it can be accessed from any point in the space-time framework.
- **Nature's memory** — Evans-Wentz's theory: "some indefinable psychic element in the earth's atmosphere upon which all human and physical actions or phenomena are photographed or impressed," viewable by normal persons "like moving pictures."
- **Precognition** — perceiving future events before they occur; demonstrated in "literally hundreds of studies" per Talbot.
- **Chair tests** — Croiset's protocol: an experimenter randomly picks a chair from the seating plan of an upcoming unreserved-seating public event (any city in the world); without knowing hall, city, or event, Croiset describes the person who will sit there.
- **Hololeaps** — Loye's term for intra-holographic leaps between parallel holographic universes; each hologram's future is fixed, but by leaping between holograms when acting on a premonition we change "the" future — the leaps provide "our true capacity for both insight and freedom."
- **Crystallizing possibilities** — Ingo Swann's phrase for the composition of the future; the kahunas likewise saw the future as fluid but "crystallizing," with great world events and major personal events (marriage, accidents, death) crystallized furthest in advance. Cordero expresses the same thought with a different image: the future as a hurricane beginning to form and gather momentum, becoming more concrete and unavoidable as it approaches.
- **Metaconsciousness** — Whitton's term for the intensely conscientious state of the between-life realm: acute self-awareness, heightened moral/ethical sense, no capacity to rationalize away faults, total honesty with oneself.
- **Interlife / between-life realm** — a "dazzling, light-filled realm" with "no such thing as time or space as we know it," where subjects plan the important events and circumstances of their next life.
- **Retributive karma** — cosmic punishment for sins; Stevenson found no compelling evidence of it — no external judge shifting us between lives "according to our deserts."
- **Template body / extended body** — Stevenson's inferred intermediate nonphysical body carrying attributes (wound imprints) between lives, acting "as a template for the production on a new physical body of birthmarks and deformities"; echoes Tiller's holographic energy-field template of the physical body.
- **Tondi** — the Batak (Indonesia) soul, which reincarnates and can reproduce both behavior and physical attributes of the former self; everything a person experiences is determined by it.
- **Aumakua ("high self")** — kahuna term for the unconscious portion of a person that sees the crystallized parts of the future and weaves one's destiny from thought forms.
- **Kino mea ("shadowy body stuff")** — the kahunas' subtle energetic substance of thoughts; hopes, fears, and imaginings become thought forms — "rough strands from which the high self weaves our future" — and also a template on which the physical body is molded (basis of miraculous healing).
- **Tsal** — the Tibetan tantric "stuff" of thoughts; every mental action produces waves of it, and the whole universe is created and animated by the collective tsal of all beings. The average mind functions "like a small puddle isolated from the great ocean."
- **Sadhanas** — Tibetan tantric visualization exercises for consciously creating with tsal; Kargyupa monks spent up to seven years in solitude perfecting visualization.
- **Alam almithal** — the twelfth-century Persian Sufis' subtle matter of thought, which forms into idea-images on the adjacent template plane of being, determining the course of one's life.
- **Hadarat** — the Sufi series of subtler planes of being; the plane directly adjacent to ours is a template reality.
- **Himma (heart chakra)** — for the Sufis, the agent responsible for forming idea-images; controlling it is the prerequisite for controlling one's destiny.
- **Thought as builder** — Edgar Cayce's trance dictum ("thought is the builder"): thoughts are tangible things, a finer form of matter; thinking is like a spider constantly spinning its web, every moment creating the images and patterns that give the future energy and shape.
- **Retroactive psychokinesis** — the Schmidt–Schlitz finding that subjects appear able to "reach back through time" and influence already-recorded random processes.
- **Future-life progression** — Wambach's hypnotic technique of progressing subjects to future lives and specified future time periods.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Stefan Ossowiecki — the petrified foot vision (dated "February 14, 19S5" in the OCR text; the digit is corrupt — 1935 or 1955)** — The Russian-born Polish clairvoyant, "one of the century's most gifted," handled a fragment of a petrified human foot; the room became ghostly and transparent and he saw a palace courtyard and a pretty olive-skinned Egyptian woman — gold jewelry at neck, wrists, ankles; white translucent dress; black braided hair under a high square tiara. Information flooded him: daughter of a prince (not a pharaoh), married to a slender man with many small braids framing his face. He could "fast-forward" her life like a movie: she died in childbirth; he watched the embalming, funeral procession, and sarcophagus rituals. Talbot uses it as the chapter's opening image of accessing the past.
- **Stanislaw Poniatowski's tests of Ossowiecki (University of Warsaw)** — Poniatowski, "the most eminent ethnologist in Poland at the time," tested him with flints and stone tools ("lithics") from archaeological sites worldwide, precertified by experts, provenance concealed. Ossowiecki repeatedly identified age, producing culture, and geographical origin. When his locations disagreed with Poniatowski's notes, the notes were always wrong. His method: concentrate until room and body became shadowy, then view a "three-dimensional movie of the past" he could move around in at will, eyes tracking as if the scenes were physically present; he saw vegetation, people, dwellings. Over the experiments he gave more than one hundred initially implausible details later proved correct: Magdalenian women (Stone Age France, ca. 15,000–10,000 B.C.) had very complex hairstyles — vindicated by later statue finds; Stone Age peoples used oil lamps — vindicated by Dordogne excavations uncovering lamps of the exact size and style he described; detailed drawings of hunted animals, hut styles, and burial customs all later confirmed.
- **Norman Emerson and George McMullen (University of Toronto)** — Emerson, anthropology professor and founding vice president of the Canadian Archaeological Association, studied truck driver McMullen, who psychometrizes objects or simply visits sites, pacing until oriented, then describes the vanished culture. McMullen paced out the location of an Iroquois longhouse on bare ground; Emerson marked it with survey pegs and six months later uncovered the structure exactly there. Emerson, a converted skeptic, told the 1973 conference of Canada's leading archaeologists he had received knowledge from a psychic informant "without any evidence of the conscious use of reasoning," said the work opened "a whole new vista," and urged that psychic archaeology research get "first priority."
- **Tenhaeff and Valkhoff with Gerard Croiset (1960s)** — W. H. C. Tenhaeff (director, Parapsychological Institute, State University of Utrecht) and Marius Valkhoff (dean of arts, University of Witwatersrand, Johannesburg) found the great Dutch psychic Croiset could psychometrize "even the smallest fragment of bone and accurately describe its past."
- **Lawrence LeShan and Eileen Garrett** — LeShan, a New York clinical psychologist and "another skeptic-turned-believer," ran similar psychometry experiments with the noted American psychic Garrett.
- **Clarence W. Weiant and Tres Zapotes** — At the 1961 annual meeting of the American Anthropological Association, archaeologist Weiant revealed he would not have made his famous Tres Zapotes discovery — "universally considered to be one of the most important Middle American archaeological finds ever made" — without a psychic's assistance.
- **Stephan A. Schwartz, *The Secret Vaults of Time*** — Former National Geographic editorial staffer and member of MIT's Secretary of Defense Discussion Group on Innovation, Technology, and Society; wrote a comprehensive history of clairvoyant–archaeologist partnerships and predicts retrocognition will trigger a scientific shift as profound as Copernicus and Darwin.
- **Psychic "Rich" on three-dimensionality** — The psychic Rich, who also psychometrizes objects, confirms Ossowiecki's description that the images are as real or more real than the room: "It's as if the scene takes over… It's like being in two places at once." Talbot cites this as a hologram-like feature of retrocognition.
- **Evans-Wentz and the Celtic fairy-faith** — [The OCR file is cut mid-sentence entering this section.] W. Y. He concluded these were afterimages of past events: "Nature herself has a memory."

Talbot reads this as belief-change atrophying a widespread retrocognitive ability — underscoring "the enormous power our beliefs have in determining which of our extraordinary potentials we manifest."
- **Phantom armies worldwide** — Reports of phantom soldiers in ancient Hindu costume in India; well-known Hawaiian phantom processions of warriors in feather cloaks with war clubs and torches; spectral armies fighting in ancient Assyrian texts. Shows the "hologramlike movies of the past" are not limited to Celtic countries.
- **The Puys (France) phantom raid, August 4, 1951** — At 4 a.m., two English women vacationing in the seaside village of Puys were awakened by gunfire; village and sea were calm. The British Society for Psychical Research found their chronology exactly mirrored military records of the Allied raid against the Germans at Puys on August 19, 1942 — they had "heard the sound of a slaughter that had taken place nine years earlier," a rare case where historians could identify the replayed event.
- **J. B. and Louisa Rhine (1930s)** — Volunteers guessed which cards would be randomly drawn from a deck at a success rate better than chance by odds of three million to one.
- **Helmut Schmidt's random-event machine (1970s, Boeing Aircraft, Seattle)** — Physicist Schmidt invented a device testing prediction of random subatomic events; in repeated tests with three volunteers and over sixty thousand trials, results were one billion to one against chance.
- **Maimonides Dream Laboratory (Ullman, Krippner, Honorton)** — Volunteers spent eight consecutive nights at the sleep lab, each night trying to dream about a picture that would be randomly chosen the *next day* and shown to them. Expected: one hit in eight; some subjects scored five of eight. Example: a volunteer dreamed of "a large concrete building" with a "patient" in a white doctor-like coat escaping, getting "as far as the archway"; the next day's randomly chosen painting was Van Gogh's *Hospital Corridor at St. Remy* — a lone patient at the end of a bleak massive hallway exiting through a door beneath an archway.
- **Puthoff and Targ's precognitive remote viewing (Stanford Research Institute) — Hella Hammid and the black iron triangle** — Subjects could describe locations experimenters would visit in the future, *before the locations had been decided*. Hammid, a photographer and "unusually talented subject," was asked to describe where Puthoff would be a half hour hence: she saw him entering "a black iron triangle," "bigger than a man," with a rhythmic squeak "about once a second." Puthoff had left ten minutes earlier on a half-hour drive around Menlo Park/Palo Alto; only after her report did he draw one of ten sealed envelopes by random number generator — a small park about six miles from the lab, where he found and sat in a children's swing (the black iron triangle) that squeaked rhythmically.
- **Jahn and Dunne (Princeton)** — Duplicated the precognitive remote-viewing findings (as did numerous labs worldwide): in 334 formal trials, volunteers produced accurate precognitive information 62 percent of the time.
- **Croiset's chair tests — the Eisenbud/Denver case (1969)** — Over twenty-five years, investigators in Europe and America found Croiset almost always gave accurate, detailed descriptions (gender, facial features, dress, occupation, past incidents) of whoever would occupy a randomly chosen unreserved seat. On January 6, 1969, in a study by Dr. Jule Eisenbud (clinical professor of psychiatry, University of Colorado Medical School), Croiset — in Utrecht — described the man who would sit in a chosen chair on January 23, 1969: five feet nine, black hair brushed straight back, gold tooth in his lower jaw, scar on his big toe, worked in science and industry, lab coat sometimes stained by a greenish chemical. The man who sat in the chair (in a Denver auditorium) matched in every detail but one: he was five feet nine and three-quarters.

Fahler found hypnotized subjects scored significantly higher on precognition tests than nonhypnotized subjects; other studies confirmed hypnosis enhances ESP. - **Irene Muza (from Arthur Osborn's *The Future Is Now: The Significance of Precognition*)** — The French actress, hypnotized and asked about her future, said: "My career will be short: I dare not say what my end will be: it will be terrible." Months later her hairdresser accidentally spilled mineral spirits on a lighted stove; her hair and clothing ignited and she died in hospital hours later. Talbot uses her case to pose the frozen-vs.-plastic future question — even foreknowledge could not have caused this particular death.
- **The Titanic premonitions** — Nineteen documented cases of precognitive glimpses of the sinking: some passengers heeded their premonitions and survived, some ignored their forebodings and drowned, and some experiencers were in neither category. Cited (with foreseen plane crashes avoided, and children moved before floods) as evidence the future "is not set, but is plastic and can be changed."
- **George Washington's Civil War vision** — Washington had a precognitive vision of a future civil war somehow involving "Africa," the issue that all men are "brethren," and the word "Union." Together with the many premonitions preceding the Kennedy assassination and the Civil War, it corroborates the kahuna belief that great world events crystallize furthest in advance.
- **Joel Whitton's hypnotic regression study (University of Toronto Medical School)** — Psychiatry professor (also degreed in neurobiology, expert in clinical hypnosis) quietly gathered reincarnation-suggestive evidence for decades. Core group of roughly thirty people, truck drivers to computer scientists, believers and nonbelievers; thousands of hours of individual hypnosis recordings. Background stat: over 90 percent of hypnotizable individuals recall apparent past-life memories, a phenomenon acknowledged even by skeptics (the psychiatry textbook *Trauma, Trance and Transformation* warns fledgling hypnotherapists to expect them and notes their healing potential while rejecting rebirth). Broad-stroke agreements among subjects: numerous past lives (some twenty to twenty-five, back to indistinguishable "caveman existences"); gender not specific to the soul (many lived at least one opposite-sex life); life's purpose is to evolve and learn, with multiple existences facilitating this.
- **Whitton's Canadian psychologist / British WWII pilot case** — A psychologist born and raised in Canada had an inexplicable British accent as a child, irrational fear of breaking his leg, phobia of air travel, terrible nail-biting, obsessive fascination with torture, and a brief teenage vision of being in a room with a Nazi officer (triggered shortly after operating car pedals in a driving test). Under hypnosis: he had been a British WWII pilot; over Germany bullets hit his plane, one breaking his leg, costing him control of the foot pedals and forcing a crash landing; captured by the Nazis, tortured by having his nails pulled out, died shortly after. Shows past-life memories explaining "a wide range of seemingly unrelated events" in a current life.
- **Whitton's xenoglossy case** — A thirty-seven-year-old behavioral scientist, reliving a Viking life, shouted words linguistic authorities later identified as Old Norse; regressed to an ancient Persian lifetime, he wrote a spidery Arabic-style script identified by a Near Eastern languages expert as authentic Sassanid Pahlavi, a long-extinct Mesopotamian tongue that flourished A.D. 226–651. Many subjects also had profound psychological/physical healings from unearthed traumatic memories and gave uncannily accurate historical details.
- **Whitton's interlife findings** — Regressed between lives, subjects reported a dazzling light-filled realm without time or space, where they planned the next life's important events in the "metaconscious" state — choosing rebirth with people they had wronged to make amends, planning encounters with "soul mates" (partners in loving relationships across many lifetimes), scheduling "accidental" events for lessons. One man visualized "a sort of clockwork instrument into which you could insert certain parts in order for specific consequences to follow."
- **The planned rape and the kidney-disease self-punishment** — A woman raped at thirty-seven revealed in metaconsciousness that she had planned the event before incarnating: a tragedy at that age was necessary to change her "entire soul complexion" and break through to a deeper, more positive understanding of life. A man with serious life-threatening kidney disease had chosen the illness to punish himself for a past-life transgression — but dying of it was not in his script; he had prearranged to encounter someone/something to remind him and enable healing of guilt and body. After starting sessions with Whitton he had a near-miraculous complete recovery.
- **Subjects who censored their own futures** — Several of Whitton's subjects asked for posthypnotic instructions not to remember what they said in trance, so they would not be tempted to tamper with the script their metaconscious selves had written. Talbot notes this also implies the future is only roughly outlined and still changeable.
- **William Cox's railroad study** — In a statistical study of 28 serious U.S. railroad accidents, parapsychologist Cox found significantly fewer people took trains on accident days than on the same day in previous weeks — suggesting we constantly unconsciously precognize the future and decide accordingly (some avoiding mishap, some choosing negative experiences for unconscious purposes).
- **Ian Stevenson's children (University of Virginia Medical School)** — Psychiatry professor, thirty-plus years interviewing young children who spontaneously remember apparent previous lives; thousands of cases from all over the globe (cases worth considering exceed his staff's capacity). Children typically start talking about the "other life" between ages two and four, remembering dozens of particulars — their name, family and friends' names, residence, house appearance, occupation, manner of death, even where they hid money and, in murder cases, sometimes who killed them. Memories are often detailed enough for Stevenson to identify the previous personality and verify virtually everything; taken to the former area, children navigate strange neighborhoods effortlessly and identify their former house, belongings, and past-life relatives. Six volumes published to date. Corroborates Whitton: rebirth with known individuals, guided by affection, guilt, or indebtedness; personal responsibility, not chance, arbiters fate; moral conduct, interests, aptitudes, and attitudes persist across lives (criminals drawn back to crime, the kind stay kind) — inner growths matter, not outward trappings. Found no evidence of retributive karma or an external judge.
- **Stevenson's birthmark and body-carryover cases** — A person's previous incarnation can affect the shape and structure of the current body: Burmese children remembering lives as British or American Air Force pilots shot down over Burma in WWII all have fairer hair and complexions than their siblings; distinctive facial features and foot deformities carry over; most numerous are injuries carrying over as scars or birthmarks — a boy murdered by throat-slitting bore a long reddish scarlike mark across his neck; a boy who shot himself in the head had two scarlike birthmarks aligned perfectly along the bullet's trajectory (entry and exit); a boy had a birthmark like a surgical scar complete with a line of red stitch-like marks exactly where his previous personality had surgery. Hundreds of such cases; a four-volume study in preparation; in some cases hospital/autopsy records of the deceased confirm injuries at the exact birthmark location. Stevenson infers a "template" extended body carrying wound imprints between lives; Talbot links this to Tiller's holographic energy-field template and to the idea that "we are at heart just images, holographic constructs, created by thought." Stevenson adds that our participation is nearly involuntary — "Levels of mental activity far deeper than those that regulate the digestion of our supper" govern it. His credibility: publications in the *American Journal of Psychiatry*, *Journal of Nervous and Mental Disease*, *International Journal of Comparative Sociology*; JAMA's review praised his "painstakingly and unemotionally collected" data "that cannot be ignored."

Powerful kahunas in open communication with their high selves could help remake a person's future; frequent deliberate visualization of what one wishes lets people "make their own future." The shadowy body stuff also forms the template of the physical body — attuned kahunas could resculpt another's shadowy body and thereby the physical body, effecting miraculous healings (a parallel, Talbot notes, to the book's conclusions on why thoughts and images affect health).

Paramahansa Yogananda advised visualizing the desired future and charging it with the "energy of concentration": proper visualization "enables us to materialize thoughts… as experiences in the material realm."

Mitchell: we create our reality at "a very deep metaphysical and subconscious level."
- **Helen Wambach's group regressions** — The late San Francisco psychologist hypnotized groups in small workshops, regressed them to specified time periods, and asked a fixed question list (sex, clothing, occupation, eating utensils, etc.); thousands of subjects over a twenty-nine-year investigation. Anti-fantasy findings: more than 90 percent recalled lives as peasants, laborers, farmers, and primitive food gatherers; under 10 percent aristocrats; none famous. Historical accuracy down to obscure details: subjects in the 1700s described three-pronged dinner forks, but after 1790 mostly four-pronged — correctly tracking the fork's historical evolution; equally accurate on clothing, footwear, and foods.
- **Chet Snow, *Mass Dreams of the Future* (future-life progressions)** — Wambach discovered she could also progress people to future lives and began a major future-life-progression project in France and the U.S.; she died before finishing and former colleague psychologist Chet Snow completed and published it. Of 2,500 participants: virtually all agreed Earth's population had decreased dramatically (many were not even in physical bodies in the specified future periods). Reports split neatly into four future types: (1) a joyless, sterile future of space-station dwellers in silvery suits eating synthetic food; (2) "New Agers" living happily and naturally, in harmony, dedicated to learning and spiritual development; (3) "hi-tech urbanites" in a bleak mechanical world of underground and domed/bubbled cities; (4) post-disaster survivors after a global (possibly nuclear) catastrophe, living in urban ruins, caves, or isolated farms, wearing plain handsewn (often fur) clothing and hunting for food. Snow's holographic reading (like Loye's): several potential futures/"holoverses" are forming, and the four scenarios glimpse the futures humanity is creating for itself en masse; he recommends visualizing a positive future rather than building bomb shelters, citing the Planetary Commission — millions worldwide united in prayer and meditation on world peace and healing every December 31, 12:00–1:00 p.m. GMT.
- **Schmidt and Schlitz — retroactive PK (announced at the 1988 Annual Convention of the Parapsychological Association)** — A computerized randomization process recorded 1,000 sound sequences, each of 100 tones of varying duration, some pleasing, some bursts of noise (expected ~50/50 by probability). Cassette copies were mailed to volunteers, who while listening tried to psychokinetically lengthen the pleasing sounds and shorten the noise. The original sequences, examined afterward, contained significantly longer stretches of pleasing sound than noise — subjects had apparently "reached back through time" to affect the randomization that had already produced their prerecorded tapes. A second test with random 100-tone sequences of four notes (subjects willing more high notes than low) again showed a retroactive PK effect. Regular meditators exerted a greater PK effect than non-meditators — suggesting again "contact with the unconscious is the key to accessing the reality-structuring portions of the psyche."
- **Moberly and Jourdain at Versailles, August 10, 1901** — Anne Moberly (principal) and Eleanor Jourdain (vice principal) of St. Hugh's College, Oxford, walking through the garden of the Petit Trianon, saw a shimmering pass over the landscape "not unlike the special effects in a movie"; afterward everyone wore eighteenth-century costumes and wigs and behaved agitatedly. A repulsive pockmarked man urged them to change direction; past a line of trees they heard music and saw an aristocratic lady painting a watercolor. When the vision vanished, the path they had walked was blocked by an old stone wall. Researching in England, they concluded they had been transported to the day of the sacking of the Tuileries and the massacre of the Swiss Guards (explaining the agitation) and that the painting woman was Marie Antoinette; they wrote a book-length manuscript for the British Society for Psychical Research. Talbot stresses they did not merely view the past but *walked into it*, and that the story risked their academic reputations with no benefit — hard to explain as fabrication.
- **Other time-slips** — May 1955: a London solicitor and his wife encountered several eighteenth-century figures in the same garden; on another occasion, embassy staff overlooking Versailles watched the garden revert to an earlier period. In the U.S., parapsychologist Gardner Murphy (former president of both the American Psychological Association and the American Society for Psychical Research) investigated a woman identified only as Buterbaugh who looked from her office window at Nebraska Wesleyan University and saw the campus as it was fifty years earlier — bustling streets and sorority houses gone, replaced by an open field and scattered trees "aflutter in the breeze of a summer long since passed."

## Thread
This chapter extends the holographic model from space (the preceding nonlocality material) to time: if the whole is enfolded everywhere in the implicate order, then past and future are enfolded in the present too, and psi phenomena involving time become expected rather than anomalous. It knits together earlier threads Talbot explicitly invokes — Bohm's unfolding/enfolding and his crystal analogy, Tiller's holographic energy-field template of the body, the Part II conclusions about thoughts and images shaping health, and the recurring lesson that belief determines which latent abilities manifest. It also escalates the book's central move: from mind *perceiving* the hologram to mind *authoring* it — individually (metaconscious life-scripts, birthmark carryover) and collectively (mass-created futures). The closing line ("There are still other shimmerings to cross our landscape, still deeper depths to plumb") sets up the book's remaining chapters on yet more radical reality-bending phenomena.
