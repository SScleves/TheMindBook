# Ch 4 — I Sing the Body Holographic

## In one paragraph
Talbot argues that if the brain constructs reality as an internal hologram, then the mind/body ultimately cannot distinguish between the neural holograms it uses to experience reality and those it conjures while imagining reality — and therefore mental images, beliefs, and expectations can produce dramatic, specific physiological changes. He builds the case through an escalating catalog: imagery therapy for cancer (Simonton, Achterberg), the placebo effect (sham surgery, Krebiozen, placebo LSD trips), the body-switching feats of multiple personality patients, the belief typology (cultural beliefs, attitudes, will, unconscious beliefs, faith) illustrated by human pincushions like Jack Schwarz and Mirin Dajo, hypnotic cure of the genetic Brocq's disease, the Lourdes bone regeneration of Vittorio Michelli, and stigmata as mind-generated images "ejected" onto the flesh. The chapter's stated aim is not to catalog mechanisms but to map the *range* of the mind's control over the body — which extends to the immune system, drug effects, wound healing, tumors, and even our genetic programming. A closing section offers acupuncture microsystems ("the little man in the ear"), and hand-pattern diagnostics as evidence that the body itself is holographic — every part containing an image of the whole. Talbot's two closing messages: we are all "potential wonderworkers," and the elements that build our health-shaping neural holograms are many and subtle (images, hopes, fears, doctors' attitudes, cultural beliefs, faith).

## Core claims
- Talbot argues that in a holographically operating brain, the remembered or imagined image of a thing can have as much impact on the senses — and on the body's physiology — as the thing itself, because real and imagined experiences reduce to "the same common language of holographically organized wave forms."
- Talbot argues (via Achterberg) that imagery's physiological effects are not only powerful but extremely specific — down to raising counts of one particular type of white blood cell and not another.
- Talbot argues (via Bohm) that in the implicate order, as in the brain, imagination and reality are ultimately indistinguishable, so images in the mind manifesting as bodily realities should not surprise us.
- Talbot argues (via Dossey) that health and illness cannot be truly separated; illness is not an external intruder but part of the unbroken whole of a life, and simply seeing one's condition in relation to the larger pattern of one's life (a diary, a videotape) can dissolve it.
- Talbot argues that the placebo effect shows we possess the power to cure warts, open bronchial tubes, mimic morphine, and melt tumors — but because we are unaware we possess this power, we must be fooled into using it, sometimes with tragic results when belief collapses (Wright/Krebiozen).
- Talbot raises, without fully answering, the question of whether many drugs work chiefly because we believe in them (Krebiozen, cis-platinum, pre-modern remedies, possibly many over-the-counter medications), while conceding many drugs (vitamin C for scurvy, insulin) work regardless of belief.
- Talbot argues that Multiple Personality Disorder demonstrates the body is itself a "multiple image hologram" that can switch biological states — allergies, drug responses, diabetes, epilepsy, eye color, menstrual cycles — "as rapidly as the flutter of a deck of cards," and that since integrated multiples retain the ability, all of us must have it somewhere in our psyches.
- Talbot argues that belief operates through distinct strata — cultural beliefs, attitudes, conscious will, unconscious beliefs, and spiritual faith — each offering its own window on the plasticity of the mind/body relationship.
- Talbot argues that hypnotic cure of Brocq's disease implies the mind can override even our DNA programming, "the masterplan."
- Talbot argues that belief, not divine intervention, is the prime mover in at least some so-called miracles (Mohotty, stigmata), citing evidence that stigmata copy art rather than the historical crucifixion.
- Talbot argues that stigmata — especially nail-like protuberances of flesh — and images appearing on skin and hearts fulfill Dubrov and Pushkin's criterion of "ejection of psychophysical structures outside the brain," direct evidence of brain holograms.
- Talbot argues that acupuncture microsystems, reflexology, iridology, and hand-pattern diagnostics suggest the body is holographic: every portion contains information about, or an image of, the whole.
- Talbot is careful to note that nothing in this chapter seems to violate known laws of physics (with stigmata as a partial exception) — that boundary is reserved for the next chapter.

## Concepts
- **Neural holograms / holographic imaging** — the brain's internal holograms; because all experience, real or imagined, is reduced to the same holographically organized wave forms, the brain cannot always distinguish "out there" from what it believes to be "out there" (hence phantom limbs), and images impact physiology as the thing itself would.
- **Implicate order (applied to intention)** — Bohm's deeper, nonlocal level of existence from which the universe springs; "every action starts from an intention in the implicate order," so imagination is already the creation of the form, moving through subtler levels until it manifests in the explicate.
- **Placebo** — any medical treatment that has no specific action on the body but is given either to humor a patient or as a control in a double-blind experiment; sugar pills and saline are common; even surgery has served as one. On average 35 percent of people receiving a given placebo experience a significant effect.
- **Double-blind experiment** — a study in which one group receives real treatment and another a fake, and neither researchers nor subjects know who is in which group, so the real treatment's effects can be assessed accurately.
- **Multiple Personality Disorder (MPD)** — a condition in which subpersonalities have strong psychological separation, each with own name, age, memories, abilities — often own handwriting, announced gender, cultural/racial background, artistic talents, language fluency, IQ, and different brain-wave patterns — and whose switches produce biological changes.
- **Parallel processing (Cassandra's term)** — a multiple's alternate personalities remaining aware even when not in control of the body, allowing "thinking" on many channels at once — e.g., a subpersonality doing healing imagery around the clock.
- **Cultural beliefs** — beliefs imposed by one's society that can unconsciously govern the body (Trobriand premarital non-pregnancy, TB death rates falling once the disease lost its mystery).
- **Beliefs embodied in attitudes** — passive belief expressed as outlook (hostility, fighting spirit, optimism, marital happiness) that correlates with immune strength, disease survival, and mortality.
- **Beliefs expressed through will** — conscious, "steely and unswerving" belief used to sculpt and control the body holographic (Schwarz, Dajo, Mohotty).
- **Unconscious beliefs** — the most important type of belief; the healing force can be reached by bypassing the conscious mind's "thick armor of doubt and skepticism" via placebo or via hypnosis, which reaches into the psyche like a surgeon altering an internal organ.
- **Brocq's disease** — a horribly disfiguring hereditary condition in which the skin develops a thick, horny, reptile-scale covering that cracks and bleeds at the slightest movement; many circus-sideshow "alligator-skinned people" were actually sufferers and had short lifespans due to infection risk; incurable until a 1951 hypnosis case.
- **Stigmata** — spontaneous manifestation of the wounds of the crucifixion, first in St. Francis of Assisi, in hundreds since; always on hands and feet (following art since the eighth century, not Roman wrist-nailing practice), which Talbot presents as evidence they are self-induced/autosuggested.
- **Ejection of psychophysical structures** — Dubrov and Pushkin's proposed proof of brain holograms: an instance of the brain projecting an image outside itself; Talbot counts skin images, heart imprints, and nail-like stigmata protuberances as examples.
- **Psychoneuroimmunology** — a new science studying how the mind (psycho), the nervous system (neuro), and the immune system (immunology) interact.
- **Neuropeptides** — molecules the brain uses to communicate ("the brain's telegrams"); receptors for them on immune cells imply the immune system is an extension of the brain.
- **Acupuncture / meridians** — the ancient Chinese art holding that every organ and bone connects to specific surface points (over a thousand, organized in imaginary lines called meridians); stimulating the points can alleviate disease in the connected parts.
- **Acupuncture microsystems / auriculotherapy** — Nogier's term for the two smaller acupuncture systems on each ear, whose points connect-the-dots into an anatomical map of a miniature inverted human ("the little man in the ear"); Dale has evidence for eighteen such micro-acupuncture holograms across the body.
- **Reflexology / iridology** — alternative techniques (foot massage accessing all body points; iris examination for diagnosing the body's condition) that Leviton says may indicate the body's holographic nature, though neither is experimentally vindicated.
- **Palmistry / dermatoglyphics** — the 4,500-year-old Indian hand-reading science (per Leviton) and, more scientifically, the study of hand patterns: an ulnar loop fingerprint associates with Alzheimer's, and 10 common genetic disabilities including Down's syndrome associate with hand patterns.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Frank (61-year-old throat cancer patient) / Dr. O. Carl Simonton, radiation oncologist, Cancer Counseling and Research Center, Dallas** — Frank had an almost always fatal throat cancer, under 5 percent survival odds; weight fallen 130 to 98 pounds, barely able to swallow saliva or breathe; doctors debated whether radiation was even worth the discomfort. Simonton taught him relaxation and mental imagery: three times a day Frank pictured the radiation as millions of tiny bullets of energy bombarding his cells, his cancer cells as weaker and more confused than normal cells, and his white blood cells swarming over dead cancer cells and carrying them to liver and kidneys to be flushed out. He had almost none of radiation's usual skin/mucous-membrane damage, regained weight and strength, and in two months all signs of cancer were gone. Simonton credits the visualization regimen.
- **Simonton follow-up study, 159 "medically incurable" cancer patients** — expected survival 12 months; four years later 63 were alive, of whom 14 showed no disease, 12 had regressing cancers, 17 stable disease; average survival of the whole group 24.4 months, over twice the national norm. Critics note participants sought Simonton out and thus had extraordinary fighting spirit; still, Simonton founded the Simonton Cancer Center in Pacific Palisades, California, and a survey found imagery the fourth most frequently used alternative cancer treatment.
- **Achterberg's neutrophil vs. T-cell imagery study / Jeanne Achterberg, director of research and rehabilitation science, University of Texas Health Science Center, Dallas** — one group of college students was trained to image neutrophils (the major white-blood-cell constituent), another to image T-cells. The neutrophil group significantly increased neutrophils with no T-cell change; the T-cell group significantly increased T-cells with neutrophils unchanged. Shows imagery's effects are cell-type specific.
- **Achterberg's brain-tumor patient (from her book Imagery in Healing)** — a woman admitted comatose and paralyzed with a massive brain tumor; surgery "debulked" it but she was sent home to die without radiation or chemotherapy. As her biofeedback therapist Achterberg monitored her; by sixteen months no evidence of cancer, paralysis gone, leg braces and cane discarded, even out dancing. Explanation: only moderately educated, she did not know what "tumor" meant or its death sentence, so she did not believe she would die and fought it like any other illness. Illustrates belief as critical to health.
- **Breznitz's soldiers (text fragmentary in this scan)** — Breznitz found the stress hormone levels in soldiers' blood always reflected their *estimates* of how far they had marched, not the actual distance: bodies responding not to reality but to what was imaged as reality.
- **Soviet athlete imagery study / Dr. Charles A. Garfield, former NASA researcher, president of the Performance Sciences Institute, Berkeley** — world-class Soviet athletes split into four groups: 100% physical training; 75% training/25% visualizing exact desired movements; 50/50; 25% training/75% visualizing. At the 1980 Winter Games in Lake Placid, New York, the fourth group (75% visualization) showed the greatest improvement, then groups three, two, one in that order. Garfield (book: Peak Performance) says the Soviets believe mental images act as precursors of neuromuscular impulses; he believes imagery works because movement is recorded holographically in the brain.
- **Basketball free-throw study / Alan Richardson, Australian psychologist** — three groups tested on free throws: one practiced twenty minutes a day, one did nothing, one only visualized shooting perfect baskets twenty minutes a day. No-practice group: no improvement. Practice group: +24 percent. Visualization-only group: +23 percent — almost as much as physical practice.
- **Headache diary study (cited by physician Larry Dossey)** — chronic headache sufferers asked to keep a diary of headache frequency and severity as a first step toward treatment; most found their headaches disappeared once they began the diary.
- **Epileptic children videotape experiment (cited by Dossey)** — epileptic children and families videotaped interacting; emotional outbursts were often followed by actual seizures. When the children were shown the tapes and saw the emotion-seizure relationship, they became almost seizure-free. Dossey's reading: seeing illness within the larger pattern of one's life, as part of an unbroken whole, brings health.
- **Sham angina surgery (1950s)** — angina pectoris was commonly treated by surgery tying off the mammary artery. Doctors instead cut patients open and simply sewed them back up; sham-surgery patients reported just as much relief as full-surgery patients. The full surgery was itself only a placebo — implying we all carry the ability to control angina.
- **Placebo statistics** — on average 35 percent of people receiving a placebo experience a significant effect (varying by situation). Conditions responsive to placebo: angina pectoris, migraine, allergies, fever, common cold, acne, asthma, warts, various pains, nausea/seasickness, peptic ulcers, depression and anxiety, rheumatoid and degenerative arthritis, diabetes, radiation sickness, Parkinsonism, multiple sclerosis, cancer.
- **Purple-dye wart cure / Lewis Thomas, president emeritus, Memorial Sloan-Kettering Cancer Center, New York** — Thomas tells of a physician who regularly rid patients of warts (viral growths) by painting a harmless purple dye on them; folk wart rituals generally are placebos. Thomas: calling it just "the unconscious at work" understates it — an unconscious that can orchestrate anti-viral tissue rejection "is a lot further along than I am."
- **Placebo vs. aspirin and morphine** — in nine double-blind studies placebos were 54 percent as effective as aspirin; in six double-blind studies, 56 percent as effective as morphine. The near-identical ratio against a far stronger drug shows placebo potency scales with expectation.
- **Delivery-method and pill-color studies** — injections are perceived as more potent than pills, capsules more than tablets; in a study of pills' suggestive value, people saw yellow or orange pills as mood manipulators (stimulants/depressants), dark red as sedatives, lavender as hallucinogens, white as painkillers.
- **The accidental asthma placebo / Dr. David Sobel, placebo specialist, Kaiser Hospital, California** — a doctor gave a struggling asthma patient a sample of a "potent new medicine"; within minutes, spectacular improvement. At the next attack the doctor knowingly gave a placebo — the patient complained it didn't fully work. Then a letter from the pharmaceutical company: they had accidentally sent a placebo the first time. The doctor's unwitting enthusiasm for the first placebo made it work; even subliminal information (attitude, body language) shapes the beliefs that impact health.
- **Wright and Krebiozen / psychologist Bruno Klopfer (famous reported case)** — Wright had advanced lymph-node cancer; treatments exhausted; orange-sized tumors in neck, armpits, chest, abdomen, groin; two quarts of milky fluid drained from his chest daily. He begged for the new drug Krebiozen; got one injection on a Friday; by Monday he was up walking, tumors "melted like snowballs on a hot stove" at half size — faster shrinkage than the strongest X-ray treatments could manage. Ten days after first treatment, discharged apparently cancer-free; having entered needing an oxygen mask, he left well enough to fly his own plane at 12,000 feet. Two months later articles said Krebiozen didn't work; Wright, rigidly logical, relapsed. His physician announced a (fictitious) "new highly concentrated" version and, with elaborate procedure, injected plain water: tumors melted again, chest fluid vanished, two more symptom-free months. Then the AMA announced a nationwide study finding Krebiozen worthless; Wright's faith shattered, his cancer bloomed anew, and he died two days later.
- **Cis-platinum** — the chemotherapy agent was touted as a wonder drug and 75 percent of recipients initially benefited; once its use became routine, effectiveness fell to about 25–30 percent — most of the benefit apparently placebo.
- **Historical treatments and angina reviews / Herbert Benson, Harvard Medical School, with Dr. David P. McCallie, Jr., of Harvard's Thorndike Laboratory** — most pre-twentieth-century treatments (leeching, lizard's blood) were useless yet often helped via placebo; Benson and McCallie's review of angina pectoris treatments over the years found success rates stayed high even for now-discredited remedies. The federal Office of Technology Assessment estimates over 75 percent of current medical treatments lack sufficient scientific scrutiny; Benson believes many over-the-counter medications act primarily as placebos. (Counterpoint kept by Talbot: vitamin C cures scurvy and insulin works for skeptical diabetics — not everything is placebo.)
- **Placebo LSD trips / Drs. Harriet Linton and Robert Langs, 1962** — subjects were told they were in an LSD study but given a placebo; within half an hour they experienced classic LSD symptoms (loss of control, supposed insight into the meaning of existence), with "placebo trips" lasting several hours.
- **Richard Alpert's holy men, 1966** — the Harvard psychologist went East seeking holy men to illuminate the LSD experience. One pundit said it was good but not as good as meditation; a Tibetan lama said it only gave him a headache. A wizened holy man over sixty in the Himalayan foothills asked for one of Alpert's 305-microgram pills, then another and another — 915 micrograms total (Grof's average study dose was about 200) — and behaved as if nothing had happened, serene all day. Alpert gave up LSD, changed his name to Ram Dass, and converted to mysticism. Real drug, no effect: the inverse of the placebo.
- **Amphetamine/barbiturate room experiment** — ten subjects per room: in room one, nine got a stimulant amphetamine and one a sleep-producing barbiturate; room two reversed. In each case the odd one out behaved exactly as his companions: the lone barbiturate taker became animated and speedy; the lone amphetamine taker fell asleep.
- **Ritalin-to-placebo addiction transfer** — a doctor secretly replaced a Ritalin addict's prescription with sugar pills, sparing him withdrawal — whereupon the man displayed an addiction to the placebo.
- **Everyday placebos** — even an injection of caffeine won't keep caffeine-sensitive people awake if they believe it's a sedative; any antibiotic "curing" a cold or viral sore throat is placebo (antibiotics only touch bacteria); in a study of the tranquilizer mephenesin, 10–20 percent of subjects had negative side effects (nausea, itchy rash, heart palpitations) whether given drug or placebo; in a recent chemotherapy study, 30 percent of the placebo control group lost their hair. (Talbot's footnote: not all drug side effects are placebo — consult a physician for negative reactions.)
- **The British aspirin mystery** — despite convincing US evidence (and TV commercials) that aspirin cuts heart-attack risk, a six-year study of 5,139 British doctors found no evidence of the effect in England. Talbot asks whether a flaw in someone's research or "some kind of massive placebo effect" is to blame — while advising readers to keep believing in aspirin.
- **Orange-juice allergy multiple / Dr. Bennett Braun, International Society for the Study of Multiple Personality, Chicago** — documented a patient all of whose subpersonalities were allergic to orange juice except one: drinking it in an allergic personality produced a terrible rash; switching to the nonallergic personality made the rash instantly begin to fade and he could drink freely.
- **Wasp-sting multiple / Dr. Francine Howland, Yale psychiatrist specializing in multiples** — a patient arrived with an eye swollen completely shut from a wasp sting; the ophthalmologist couldn't see him for an hour, so Howland had his "anesthetic personality" (which feels no pain) take control: pain ended, and by the appointment the swelling was gone and the eye normal — the ophthalmologist sent him home untreated. When the original personality returned, pain and swelling returned; he was treated the next day. The unbriefed ophthalmologist phoned Howland to check he hadn't imagined the previous day — "He thought time was playing tricks on him."
- **Multiples' pharmacological wizardry (Braun and others)** — a drunk multiple can instantly become sober by switching personalities; Braun records 5 milligrams of diazepam sedating one personality while 100 milligrams had little effect on another; adult doses can overdose child subpersonalities who take over; some multiples are hard to anesthetize, with accounts of waking on the operating table when an "unanesthetizable" subpersonality takes over.
- **Personality-linked bodily variation** — scars, burn marks, cysts, and handedness can differ between personalities; visual acuity differs (some multiples carry two or three pairs of glasses); color-blindness and even eye color can change; some women have two or three menstrual periods a month, one per subpersonality's cycle; speech pathologist Christy Ludlow found each personality's voice pattern differs — a physiological change beyond even the most accomplished actor; one multiple hospitalized for diabetes showed no symptoms while a nondiabetic personality was in control; epilepsy comes and goes with personality; psychologist Robert A. Phillips, Jr., reports even tumors appearing and disappearing (kind unspecified).
- **Multiples' rapid healing and slow aging** — several recorded cases of third-degree burns healing with extraordinary rapidity; Dr. Cornelia Wilbur (therapist of Sybil Dorsett, portrayed in the book Sybil) is convinced multiples age more slowly than other people.
- **Cassandra's "parallel processing" (symposium account)** — a multiple who attributes her rapid healing to visualization plus round-the-clock parallel processing: her alternates stay aware when not in control, letting her work several term papers at once and "sleep" while other personalities cook and clean; a subpersonality named Celese, with thorough knowledge of anatomy and physiology, spends twenty-four hours a day meditating and imaging the body's well-being. Other multiples make similar claims. Talbot adds: therapy-integrated multiples can still make these switches at will — so the ability must lie in all our psyches.
- **Trobriand Islands premarital pregnancy** — Trobrianders engage freely in premarital sex with no contraception and seldom abortion, yet premarital pregnancy (strongly frowned upon) is virtually unknown — suggesting unmarried women unconsciously prevent conception via cultural belief. Analogous: infertile couples who conceive shortly after adopting, suggesting an inhibition lifted.
- **Tuberculosis death rates and Koch, 1882** — 19th-century TB killed tens of thousands amid terrifying mystery; after Dr. Robert Koch discovered in 1882 that a bacterium caused TB and the knowledge spread, death rates fell from 600 to 200 per 100,000 — though an effective drug was nearly half a century away.
- **The first kidney transplant "success" (1950s)** — a Chicago doctor published a seemingly successful kidney transplant; successful transplants followed worldwide. When the first failure came, the doctor discovered his original kidney had actually been rejected from the start — but it didn't matter: once recipients believed they could survive, they did, and success rates soared.
- **Attitude studies (rapid-fire list)** — an expectant mother's attitude toward her baby and pregnancy correlates directly with childbirth complications and the newborn's medical problems; high scorers on hostility/aggression tests are seven times more likely to die from heart problems; married women have stronger immune systems than separated/divorced women, happily married women stronger still; people with AIDS who display fighting spirit live longer than passive AIDS patients, likewise cancer patients; pessimists get more colds than optimists; stress lowers immune response; the newly widowed have increased illness and disease.
- **Jack Schwarz, Dutch-born author and lecturer (1970s)** — in studies at the Menninger Foundation, the University of California's Langley Porter Neuropsychiatric Institute, and others, he stuck mammoth six-inch sailmaker's needles completely through his arms without bleeding, flinching, or producing beta brain waves (the pain waves); holes closed tightly with no bleeding on removal; he altered brain-wave rhythms at will, held burning cigarettes against his flesh, carried live coals in his hands. He says he learned the control in a Nazi concentration camp to withstand beatings, and believes anyone can learn voluntary control of the body.
- **Mirin Dajo, 1947** — another Dutchman; at Zurich's Corso Theater his assistant thrust a fencing foil completely through his body, piercing vital organs, without harm, pain, or blood — just a faint red line. After a spectator's heart attack he was legally banned from performing. On May 31, 1947, at the Zurich cantonal hospital, before Dr. Hans Naegeli-Osjord, chief of surgery Dr. Werner Brunner, doctors, students and journalists, he repeated it; walked upstairs, foil still through his abdomen, for an X-ray that proved he was genuinely impaled; the foil came out after a full twenty minutes leaving two faint scars. Later tested in Basel, where he let doctors themselves run him through. Naegeli-Osjord related the case to German physicist Alfred Stelter, who reports it in his book Psi-Healing.
- **Mohotty of Ceylon (1960s) / Gilbert Grosvenor, president of the National Geographic Society, wife Donna, and a Geographic photo team** — as a boy Mohotty promised the divinity Kataragama yearly penance if his father was cleared of a murder charge; the father was cleared. His annual penance: walking through fire and hot coals, piercing his cheeks with skewers, driving skewers into his arms from shoulder to wrist, sinking large hooks deep into his back and dragging an enormous sledge by ropes attached to them. The hooks pulled his back flesh taut with no sign of blood; when removed, no trace of wounds. Photographed and published in the April 1966 National Geographic. Talbot later argues Mohotty's protection is better explained by his deep belief in divine protection than by Kataragama's existence.
- **Indian hook-swinging ritual (Scientific American, 1967)** — annually a chosen villager has two hooks "large enough to hang a side of beef on" buried in his back; ropes through the hook-eyes are tied to an ox-cart boom and he is swung in huge arcs over the fields as an offering to fertility gods. Hooks removed: completely unharmed, no blood, literally no sign of punctures.
- **Hypnosis capabilities (summary of numerous studies)** — deeply hypnotized persons can, like multiples, control allergic reactions, blood flow patterns, and nearsightedness; also heart rate, pain, body temperature; and can will away some kinds of birthmarks.
- **Brocq's disease cured by hypnosis / A. A. Mason, hypnotherapist, Queen Victoria Hospital, London, 1951** — a sixteen-year-old boy with an advanced case (thick reptile-scale skin) was referred as a last resort. Mason found him a good hypnotic subject, put him in deep trance and told him the disease was healing. Five days later the scaly layer on his left arm fell off revealing soft healthy flesh; in ten days the arm was completely normal; they worked body area by body area until all scales were gone; symptom-free at least five years (when Mason lost touch). Because Brocq's is genetic, Talbot argues this means tapping "the masterplan, our DNA programming itself" — the mind overriding genetic makeup.
- **Vittorio Michelli's Lourdes healing, 1962–1965** — admitted to the Military Hospital of Verona, Italy, with a large cancerous tumor (malignant sarcoma) on his left hip; sent home untreated as hopeless; within ten months the hip completely disintegrated, leaving his upper leg bone floating in a mass of soft tissue (fig. 11, a 1962 X-ray). In a plaster cast he traveled to Lourdes and bathed in the spring — instant sensation of heat moving through his body, then returning appetite and energy. A month later X-rays showed the tumor smaller; doctors documented every step. After the tumor disappeared his bone regenerated — generally viewed by medicine as impossible; within two months he was walking, and over several years the bone completely reconstructed (fig. 12, 1965 X-ray; source: Michel-Marie Salmon, The Extraordinary Cure of Vittorio Michelli). The Vatican's Medical Commission ruled it a miracle, its report confirming "categorically and without doubt" an overwhelming bone reconstruction "of a type unknown in the annals of world medicine" (X-rays 1964, 1965, 1968, 1969). Talbot suggests no physical law was violated — rather an unknown natural process, likely triggered by faith accessing very deep levels of the psyche.
- **Talbot's Kauai synchronicity (footnote)** — while writing the Michelli passage, Talbot received a letter reporting that a friend in Kauai, Hawaii, whose hip had disintegrated from cancer, had experienced an "inexplicable" complete bone regeneration using chemotherapy, extensive meditation, and imagery exercises; reported in Hawaiian newspapers.
- **Stigmata follow art, not history / D. Scott Rogo, parapsychologist, graduate faculty, John F. Kennedy University, Orinda, California** — from St. Francis of Assisi (the first spontaneous stigmatist, per most church scholars) onward, all stigmatists have wounds in hands and feet; but Roman custom was to nail through the wrists (skeletal remains from Christ's time bear this out; hand-nails can't support a hanging body). Wounds appear in the hands because that is how artists have depicted the crucifixion since the eighth century. Gemma Galgani (Italian stigmatist, died 1903): her wounds precisely mirrored the stigmata on her own favorite crucifix.
- **Thurston's case for autosuggestion / Herbert Thurston, English priest, The Physical Phenomena of Mysticism (posthumous, 1952)** — stigmata are self-induced because: size/shape/location vary between stigmatists (so no common source in Christ's actual wounds); stigmatists' visions show little consistency (products of their own minds, not reenactments); and a surprisingly large percentage of stigmatists suffered from hysteria — signs of a volatile, abnormally emotional psyche rather than an enlightened one. Even some liberal Catholic leaders call stigmata products of "mystical contemplation."
- **Nail-like protuberances** — St. Francis's stigmata included flesh formed like bent nail-ends projecting from the flesh (eyewitness biographer Thomas of Celano); St. Bonaventura said the nails were so defined one could slip a finger under them into the wounds; per Celano, pressing the "nail" on one side made it instantly project on the other, like a real nail sliding through the hand. Therese Neumann (well-known Bavarian stigmatist, died 1962) had similar hardened-skin nail structures passing completely through hands and feet, examined by several doctors; her wounds opened only periodically and grew a soft membrane-like tissue when they stopped bleeding. Padre Pio (famous Italian stigmatist, died 1968): wounds passing completely through his hands; a side wound so deep doctors feared measuring it. Venerable Giovanna Maria Solimani (18th-century Italian): hand wounds deep enough to stick a key into; like all stigmatists' wounds, never decayed, infected, or inflamed. ## Thread
This is the book's first full application of the holographic model beyond brain science and physics: having established (Part I) that the brain constructs reality holographically and that the universe may be a hologram grounded in Bohm's implicate order, Talbot now argues the mind/body border dissolves — image, belief, and flesh are one holographic system. The chapter deliberately limits itself to phenomena that, however astounding, "do not seem to violate any of the known laws of physics," explicitly setting up Chapter 5, which will examine things the mind can do that current science cannot explain. It also anchors the recurring book-wide motif of part-contains-whole (microsystems, hand patterns) and of "images making images — two mirrors reflecting each other infinitely."
