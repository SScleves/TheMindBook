# Ch 15 — The Great Simulation and Its Implications

## In one paragraph
In this final chapter Virk pulls together every strand of the book — computer science, quantum physics, and Eastern (as well as Western) mysticism — into a portrait of the "Great Simulation": a hyperrealistic video game we live inside, our version of the Matrix. He frames it with Plato's allegory of the cave (the oldest Western record of the idea, deliberately saved for last), then surveys the candidate creators of the simulation — other simulations, human ancestors, time travelers from the future, nonhuman Earth life, aliens, superintelligent machines, or God/gods — noting these possibilities are not mutually exclusive. He inventories the core elements of the Great Simulation (pixelated high-resolution world, massively multiplayer structure, algorithmically generated universe, PCs/NPCs, downloadable consciousness, quantized reality, a rendering engine based on quantum indeterminacy, and a physics engine spanning classical and quantum physics), revisits the PC-vs-NPC debate (siding with the RPG version, where mystics may be closer to the truth than scientists), argues that information and computation are "eating up" all the other sciences, and closes with the book's grand claim: the simulation hypothesis may be the first plausible single framework bridging the five-hundred-year divide between science and religion.

## Core claims
- Virk argues that the evolution of computer science, how video-game simulations are built, the central mysteries of quantum physics, the messages of ancient Eastern philosophies, the descriptions of Western religions, and unexplained paranormal phenomena all suggest we are living in a simulation, like Plato's prisoners in the cave.
- Virk argues that the Great Simulation is a hyperrealistic simulation or video game — "essentially it is our version of the Matrix" — and that while inside it we cannot distinguish what is real from what is simulated; this does not make it unreal from our perspective, just as games are real to our avatars and to NPCs while we play them.
- Virk argues that we cannot know for certain who runs the simulation or how many simulations exist; he lays out candidate creators (other simulations, humans/ancestors, time travelers, nonhuman Earth life-forms, aliens, superintelligent machines, God/gods) and stresses that these possibilities are not necessarily exclusive — gods or angels could be superintelligent AI or aliens appearing to us as gods.
- Virk argues (crediting Bostrom's original paper) that infinite nested simulations would require near-infinite computing power, so restrictions may be imposed on what can be simulated within a simulation — and he flips the skeptics' resource argument: rather than proving we can't be simulated, resource limits might be evidence that we ARE in a simulation with restrictions imposed on what we can simulate.
- Virk argues that even if what is outside our simulation is another simulation, there must at some point be "a top of the stack — the base reality."
- Virk argues that the core elements of the Great Simulation, projected from video-game technology trends, map point-for-point onto features of physical reality found by physics (quantization, indeterminacy, entanglement, the constant speed of light).
- Virk argues that quantum indeterminism becomes an optimization technique: like games that render only what the player observes, the Great Simulation may render reality only when observed, with "our hardware" being our consciousness.
- Virk argues that quantum entanglement and non-locality suggest information can be transferred outside normal space-time constraints, which "suggests that there is something outside normal space-time, which means space-time is a construct, not unlike a simulation" — more explainable via the simulation hypothesis than by current physics.
- Virk argues, against Bostrom's implication that we are simulated consciousness (NPCs), for the RPG version of the hypothesis: we may be players outside the game who control or inhabit characters inside it. He chose the video-game metaphor deliberately because he believes "the Eastern mystics may be closer to the nature of the Great Simulation than many of our scientists" — though both possibilities remain and cannot be ruled out.
- Virk argues that religious concepts — karma, reincarnation, recording angels, judgment, heaven — are perfectly explained in modern computer-science terminology (quest engines, downloadable consciousness, score-keeping outside the rendered world), and that if ancient traditions had had computers and video games, they might have used similar terminology.
- Virk argues that information science is "eating up the other sciences" (paraphrasing Marc Andreessen), that computer science — like mathematics — may be a fundamental building block of the universe, and that this trend explains why scientists now take the simulation hypothesis seriously and will do so more in years to come.
- Virk argues that the simulation hypothesis may provide "a single framework, a coherent model that brings together science and religion so that we don't have to be slaves to just one way of knowing the true nature of reality" — the first plausible bridge between humanity's two great searches for truth.

## Concepts
- **The Great Simulation** — the hyperrealistic simulation or video game that we are in; "essentially it is our version of the Matrix." While inside it, we are unable to distinguish between what is real and what is part of the simulation — though it remains real from our perspective, as games are to avatars and NPCs.
- **Allegory of the cave** — Plato's story (in *The Republic*, a dialog between Socrates and Glaucon) of prisoners chained facing a wall who see only shadows of real forms, name and describe the shadows as if they were reality, and are skeptical when a freed philosopher returns to tell them reality is more. Virk maps it onto the simulation: cave and shadows = renderings of pixels visible to those inside the simulation.
- **Base reality** — the "top of the stack": even if what exists outside our simulation is another simulation, there must at some point be a non-simulated level above all nested simulations.
- **Simulations within simulations** — any simulations we create (MMORPGs, video games) if we ourselves are simulated. Infinite recursion of these would need near-infinite computing power, so (per Bostrom) restrictions might be imposed on what a simulated civilization can simulate.
- **Ancestor simulation** — a simulation run by beings to simulate their own ancestors, implying creators with DNA similar to ours; what we think of as humans could be simplified avatars of the "protohumans" at the base reality above our simulation.
- **Computational irreducibility** — the property by which the only way to find out what happens in a complex or chaotic process is to let the simulation run; invoked as a reason simulators might run our world as an all-NPC sim to see what happens when variables change.
- **Simulation point / stage 10 (downloadable consciousness)** — the technological threshold (built up in earlier chapters) at which a civilization can create simulations indistinguishable from physical reality; downloadable consciousness is called "one of the critical stages on the road to the simulation point... stage 10."
- **PCs and NPCs** — player characters (avatars controlled by players outside the game) versus non-player characters (artificial characters controlled by the game and algorithms). The central open debate: are we conscious players or unconscious simulations?
- **Turing Test / Metaverse Turing Test** — we are just reaching the point where a machine can pass the Turing Test; the Metaverse Turing Test (AI beings within fully realistic 3D simulations passing as real) has not been passed as of the second edition, but Virk judges it "probably only years away (and definitely within a decade)."
- **Simulated artificial intelligence (SAI)** — simulated AI that can be put into android-type bodies, blurring the questions of intelligence, consciousness, and what is "alive."
- **Downloadable consciousness** — capturing the trillions of neural interactions in a person's brain to simulate that person's consciousness after death; scientists think it may be mere decades away. Mystics argue downloading is how we got into the body in the first place: at birth consciousness, from a soul, is downloaded into the body.
- **Quantized, pixelated reality** — physical reality is not continuous but quantized: a practical limit to how small matter's building blocks can be, quanta of energy, mostly empty space at the atomic level, and "quantized time" implied by quantized space plus the fixed speed of light. Reality is better expressed as digital, discrete pieces of information — bits more like qubits than simple ones and zeros.
- **Rendering engine based on quantum indeterminacy** — games render only what needs to be rendered from the player's observational point of view; quantum indeterminism (the physical world may exist only when observed) is the same optimization technique, with consciousness as our hardware.
- **Physics engine** — the rules of physics within a virtual world, which may or may not define the rules outside it. In ours: the constant speed of light gives a clock speed for the simulation; wormholes (Einstein-Rosen bridges) are the teleport mechanism; entanglement/non-locality show instantaneous information transfer outside space-time constraints.
- **Algorithmically generated world / procedural generation** — games evolved from raster (bitmapped) images storing every pixel to worlds rendered only when necessary, generated as observed — short term via procedural generation, long term by generative AI. Applied to our universe's two hundred billion to two trillion galaxies (2024 estimate), many of which we may never visit because "they don't really exist, but are procedurally generated."
- **Lila and maya** — in the Hindu Vedas, the lila is the grand play we get caught up in, which is the maya or illusion that formed the basis for Buddhism.
- **Dream yoga** — whole schools of training in certain forms of Tantric Buddhism devoted to learning to recognize that you are inside a dream.
- **Karma as quest engine** — the law of cause and effect, "like an endless quest engine in video games," keeping track of achievements and goals and creating situations with other players needed to resolve previous karma; in Buddhism the endless wheel of karma drives us to keep coming back, an ongoing manifest of quests stored outside the rendered world.
- **Lords of Karma** — the metaphorical beings who would keep track of karma; Virk notes that if some kind of computer or AI tracks billions of items of karma and experience, "suddenly we do not need the metaphorical Lords of Karma."
- **Recording angels / scroll of deeds / book of life** — Western religious score-keeping: God sends angels who track your deeds (the scroll of deeds defined in Islam, the book of life described in the Bible) — consistent with a game recording players' actions to keep score.
- **Superusers** — beings outside the simulation with the power to manipulate it; what look like gods to us could be aliens or superusers (illustrated by Star Trek's Q Continuum).
- **It from bit** — John Wheeler's phrase for the conclusion that most of physics is based on information: the "it" is the physical world, the "bit" is the information.
- **Digital physics** — per PhilPapers, the phrase is used to express "the controversial idea that the proper subject matter of physics (in particular, quantum physics) is information," and sometimes "the even more controversial idea that fundamental physical reality just is information."
- **Killer-AI scenario** — the science-fiction/science-speculation idea that sufficiently intelligent machines will decide they no longer need us, or need to keep humans occupied — what happened in The Matrix; concerning to many scientists.
- **The grays** — the most popular conception of extraterrestrial aliens today, presented in science fiction and UFO lore for decades, from The X-Files to Close Encounters of the Third Kind.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Plato's allegory of the cave (The Republic)** — prisoners chained to a wall see forms passing the cave mouth only as shadows on the opposite blank wall; they can't tell if the light source is fire, sun, moon, or something else. Written as a dialog between Socrates (Plato's teacher) and Glaucon (Plato's older brother). The prisoners talk, agree on names for shadows ("a person or a book or a horse"), and build a full description of reality — but they are describing shadows. A freed prisoner (the "philosopher") finds sunlight eminently painful, perceives the real world, returns to persuade the others — who are "to put it mildly, skeptical." Virk's mapping: the cave and shadows are the renderings of pixels visible inside the simulation; without leaving the rendered world it is very difficult to conceive of the projector or light source; someone returning from outside would get the same reaction the scientific community gives mystics — and the same reaction many gave the simulation hypothesis: "that it can't possibly be true!" From a higher perspective, the prisoners would have "found a model for their reality that fit better than the old model" — exactly what mystics have said for thousands of years.
- **Ken Wilber, Quantum Questions** — Wilber said physicists have always been looking at shadows on the wall, but quantum theory forced them to acknowledge this fact. Virk's conclusion: scientists still cling to a purely materialistic view, ignoring whether the physical world is real while diving into how "our physics engine" works.
- **"Turtles all the way down" (William James anecdote)** — a woman told the famous psychologist William James that a turtle was holding up the Earth; asked what holds up the turtle, she said it was obviously "turtles all the way down!" Used to illustrate the infinite-recursion problem of simulations within simulations: without a base, a recursive algorithm never returns (each layer waits on the one beneath); in a practical finite computing environment it runs out of memory or hits a resource error.
- **Bostrom's restriction idea and the flipped resource argument** — Bostrom, in his original paper, says one way to prevent infinite recursion is imposing restrictions on what can be simulated within a simulation. Virk turns the skeptics' "too many computing resources" objection around: restrictions on simulated civilizations "might actually be turned into an argument that we are in a simulation" with limits on what we can simulate. He adds the resource argument breaks down anyway given quantum computing and optimization techniques — but concedes this possibility "doesn't really answer the question fully" since there must be a base reality at the top of the stack.
- **Humans/ancestors as creators** — our games evolved from simple stylized nonhuman characters (Pac-Man, Space Invaders) to humanlike avatars with a common "avatar editor" model (choosing gender or body type). In an ancestor simulation, "it's a pretty good bet that the creators made us look like them in some way — at least humanoid," encoding similar DNA with as much fidelity as they knew about themselves, or a subset; we may be simplified avatars of "protohumans" at base reality. Ancestor simulations also raise the specter that we are all NPCs, run to see what happens when variables change — because of computational irreducibility, the only way to find out is to let the simulation run.
- **Philip K. Dick's timeline adjustments (time travelers from the future)** — Dick's Man in the High Castle depicts a timeline where the Axis powers won WWII and the Nazis and Empire of Japan split the United States between them. According to his own words and those of his wife, Tessa, Dick actually believed this was a real timeline and that he had memories of it; beings — most likely from the future — judged it non-optimal and changed it so the Allies won. He also believed he received communication from beings outside of time who adjusted the JFK assassination many times: in alternate timelines JFK was not assassinated in Dallas in 1963 but was assassinated elsewhere, or the timeline led to nuclear war — so they took our simulation back to the Dallas assassination. Virk presents this as close to the "descendants from many millions of years in the future watching our history" scenario.
- **The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy (Douglas Adams) — nonhuman Earth-based creators** — Earth as an experiment run by hyperintelligent mice, who were here to monitor humans as the experiment proceeded. Virk calls this unlikely but notes an Earth life-form based on the creators could monitor the simulation from inside via an avatar of its original form; if closely related to the chimpanzee or dolphin, our versions would be simplified proto-chimps or proto-dolphins.
- **Star Trek IV: The Voyage Home** — the Enterprise crew returns to find an advanced alien ship awaiting signals from the only intelligent species it knows on Earth — humpback whales, now extinct — prompting Kirk and Spock to go back in time to fetch a pair of whales who communicate with the probe and save the Earth. Cited as fiction illustrating nonhuman Earth species as the "intelligent" party.
- **Aliens as creators (Musk, Hawking, the grays)** — proposed "by everyone from Elon Musk to Stephen Hawking." Extraterrestrial means beings from another planet, but "you could easily apply it to beings that came from another dimension of reality." Why would aliens create us as humans? Virk speculates: just as we create fantasy races in World of Warcraft based on our literature and imagination, humans must have played some role in their society, real or imagined. Alternatively, abduction reports commonly describe aliens harvesting our genetic material — "Is it possible the whole point of the simulation is to evolve our DNA to a point where it is compatible with or helpful to the aliens in some way?"
- **Superintelligent machines (The Matrix premise)** — machines as overlords running our simulation. AI runs as a thread through the whole hypothesis: NPCs are AIs inside games we create; some experts say we could be the AI inside a simulation (not quite the same as machines creating it, though any creator of a simulation this complex was likely assisted by AI, per Parts II and III). The killer-AI scenario: machines intelligent enough decide they don't need us, or must keep us occupied — as in The Matrix. Status report: we are just reaching the point where a machine can pass the Turing Test; AI is already creating virtual worlds; fully realistic 3D simulations with AI beings passing the Metaverse Turing Test are "not very far off (as of the writing of this second edition)."
- **God or gods and heaven — the mystical explanation** — the Great Simulation as a test playground for conscious beings. Eastern traditions: a soul goes through multiple lives (playing multiple characters). Western traditions: God, angels (including guardian angels), eternal heaven and hell. Shared concept: our actions in this world determine what happens when we leave it. Not exclusive of the other options — gods or angels may be superintelligent AI or aliens appearing as gods.
- **The Q Continuum (Star Trek: The Next Generation)** — an alien species "as close to omnipotent as we could imagine": controlling time and space, plucking the Enterprise from one part of the universe to another, creating new beings, manipulating heavenly bodies with ease, appearing as one of us to communicate. Virk's point: what look like gods could be aliens or superusers outside the simulation with power to manipulate it.
- **Element 1 — High-resolution pixelated world** — from crude 8-bit arcade pixels to millions of colors and 4K+ resolution; games and film special effects now share the same computer-modeling and texture-rendering techniques. VR gives illusion of depth and realism; AR/mixed reality fades the physical/computer-generated boundary; generative AI will grow illusory worlds "to resemble infinite worlds." Conclusion: we will render worlds indistinguishable from physical reality "in a few decades," and the likelihood that a civilization billions of years ahead of us has already done so "is very high."
- **Element 2 — Massively multiplayer online game** — games went from single-player on one screen to millions logged in; server tech doesn't yet scale to billions simultaneously, but no reason it can't soon, "considering we already have websites and apps like Facebook, Instagram, and Twitter/X with over a billion users." In an MMO there is no single rendering of the game — only information "in the cloud"; each computer renders locally as needed. Analogy: we each control our characters and the game is "shared and rendered based on perceptions in our own minds."
- **Element 3 — Seemingly infinite algorithmically generated world** — from raster images (every pixel stored) to worlds rendered only when necessary; large game universes are algorithmically generated beyond a certain point, giving arbitrarily many planets, generated as observed (procedural generation short-term, generative AI long-term); the only limit is storage, and even that can be optimized. Our universe: between two hundred billion and two trillion galaxies (best estimate as of 2024), trillions of stars, countless planets. Fractal geometry, from simple recursive fractal algorithms, has been "remarkably effective at simulating aspects of the natural world, including treelike structures, leaves, and the landscape of planets." Virk's speculation: the reason we won't visit all these worlds may be that "they don't really exist, but are procedurally generated to make it seem to us we live in an almost infinite universe."
- **Element 4 — PCs, NPCs, and AI** — many believe we have already passed the Turing Test ("we haven't," Virk writes); the Metaverse Turing Test is probably years away, definitely within a decade. If SAI can be put into android-type bodies, intelligence/consciousness blur with computer science and what is "alive" — as in Star Trek: "who is to say that we aren't, in fact, some kind of AI ourselves?" Bostrom's original argument postulates we are likely simulated consciousness rather than real beings — at odds with the mystics' model of a soul going in and out of the rendered world; both remain possibilities and cannot be ruled out.
- **Element 5 — Downloadable consciousness** — scientists believe capturing the trillions of neural interactions in a brain would let them simulate a person's consciousness after death; some think it's mere decades away. Mystics argue downloading is how we got in: at birth consciousness, from a soul, is downloaded into the body. Raises whether we are real beings or just information; hinted at in most religious and mystical traditions.
- **Element 6 — Quantized, pixelated reality** — practical limit to how small matter's building blocks can be; reality quantized, not continuous; at the atomic level most "physical objects" are empty space; quantized space + fixed speed of light suggest quantized time. Reality better expressed as digital, discrete information — bits more like qubits. The 3D objection dissolves: our pixels just aren't on a 2D screen — 3D printers show 3D objects rendered from 3D models using some minimum pixel or "object."
- **Element 7 — Rendering engine based on quantum indeterminacy** — games optimize by rendering only what the player observes; quantum indeterminism (the physical world may exist only when observed) becomes an optimization technique. As there is no single rendered world in games (rendered on everyone's hardware), "it's possible that in the Great Simulation our hardware is our consciousness" — a shared reality of which each of us sees only the needed parts.
- **Element 8 — Physics engine based on classical and quantum physics** — the constant speed of light in pixelated space-time yields quantized time, "a clock speed of the simulation." Games restrict travel time from A to B unless you teleport; Einstein-Rosen bridges (wormholes) show the teleport mechanism in our simulated space-time. Entanglement and non-locality show information transfer faster than light — instantaneous — implying transmission that bypasses normal space-time constraints, hence "space-time is a construct, not unlike a simulation"; entanglement is "more explainable via the simulation hypothesis than by our current understanding of physics."
- **PCs vs. NPCs — Virk's stance** — Bostrom's argument implies we are all NPCs; Virk deliberately chose the RPG metaphor because it opens the possibility we are players outside the game controlling or inhabiting characters, and because he believes the Eastern mystics may be closer to the nature of the Great Simulation than many scientists — while insisting this doesn't make the Great Simulation non-technological (stage 10, downloadable consciousness, is critical to the simulation point). The mystics who "peeked outside the simulation": Buddha, the mysterious rishis who wrote the Vedas, and the founders of Western religions — Moses, Jesus, Muhammad, "and others in the Abrahamic line of prophets."
- **Dreamlike nature of reality** — strongest in Hindu and Buddhist traditions: the lila (grand play) in the Vedas, the maya (illusion) foundational to Buddhism, and dream yoga in certain Tantric Buddhist schools (training to recognize you are inside a dream). In a dream we are unaware another part of us is asleep in bed, and dream elements seem real while inside — and dreams "pretty much already exhibit all the technology we have laid out in the road to the simulation point."
- **Karma, reincarnation, and quests** — Eastern traditions: a soul/consciousness downloaded into a body for the dreamlike state called life, multiple times — multiple lives, "not unlike what happens in video games." Part of us is outside the simulation and everything is accounted for, like XP, levels, and quests. Karma = an endless quest engine, tracking achievements and creating situations with other players to resolve previous karma; the Buddhist wheel of karma is an ongoing manifest of quests we create through our actions. Quests are stored outside the rendered world; a computer or AI is the most likely record-keeper of billions of karma items — removing the need for "the metaphorical Lords of Karma."
- **A godlike AI, angels, and the afterlife** — Western traditions: prayer to God, angels tracking deeds (Islam's scroll of deeds; the Bible's book of life) — pointing to a godlike entity outside the Great Simulation tracking billions of players. Angelic recording and judgment of the eternal soul based on actions in the physical world are consistent with a game keeping score. Conclusion: religious traditions describe the physical world as a place we are "downloaded to," where actions are recorded, and from which we return elsewhere — "the Great Simulation explains this perfectly," and with computers in ancient times the traditions "might have used similar terminology."
- **Information science eating the other sciences (Andreessen paraphrase)** — Marc Andreessen, founder of Netscape, said software was "eating the world"; Virk says information science is eating up the other sciences — physics, chemistry, biology. Evidence chain: communications went from physical signals over wires (telegraph, telephone) to bits bundled in layers of algorithms; entertainment from physical film frames to packets, away from TV broadcast; 3D printing (starting from CAD) turns information into "physical pixels in the 3D world," more efficiently than industrial-revolution machines; biology runs on DNA instructions, machine learning is based on neural networks ("biological algorithms"), and bioinformatics has made information and computation integral to the organic world; physics itself — no such thing as a physical object, mostly empty space and electrons, quantized space and time resembling digital pixels and clock speed, with a particle's "state" (used in quantum computing) possibly the only real way to define it.
- **John Wheeler's "it from bit"** — the legendary physicist behind many quantum concepts in the book concluded most of physics is based on information. In his autobiography he summarized three phases of his career: "Everything Is Particles" evolved to "Everything Is Fields," which eventually evolved to "Everything Is Information." The "it" is the physical world; the "bit" is the information. Virk notes Wheeler wrote this before practical quantum computers, but referenced information in particle spin — later the basis for qubits.
- **PhilPapers on digital physics** — the well-known philosophy site's definition, quoted verbatim in the chapter, capturing the field's two claims (physics' subject matter is information; physical reality just IS information).
- **MIT's Schwarzman College of Computing** — MIT "had only five colleges until recently"; a new college created in 2020 with a billion-dollar endowment, dedicated to the idea that computer science in general and AI in particular will impact every other science and industry rather than being the province of computer scientists alone. Cited as institutional proof that computation/information now underlie all fields — and a reason the simulation hypothesis is no longer "better material for a Philip K. Dick novel than for serious study."
- **The five-hundred-year divide** — physical reality (science) vs. spiritual — "a euphemism for nonphysical" — reality (religion/mysticism). Religion dominated for most of history; in the 19th and 20th centuries science became dominant and religion was relegated to morality and spirituality. Today the study of consciousness "is reduced to chemicals" and the study of religion to a social science about what people believe; most academics have given up building scientific models consistent with the mystical worldview. Against this: Einstein ("Science without religion is lame, religion without science is blind" — Virk: "You might say he rejected the idea that there is a conflict"), Max Planck ("I regard consciousness as fundamental" — a worldview abandoned by most mainstream scientists, "with a few courageous exceptions"), and Heisenberg's remark (from this part's epigraph) that when two different lines of thought from different cultures meet, "one may hope that new and interesting developments may follow." Virk: the simulation hypothesis is one of those developments.

## Thread
The book lands on synthesis: every earlier strand — the video-game technology roadmap to the simulation point (Part I/II), quantum physics' quantization, indeterminacy, and entanglement, and the mystics' millennia-old testimony that the world is a dreamlike illusion with score-keeping beyond it — describes the same object, the Great Simulation, with Plato's cave as its oldest Western sketch. Virk declines to settle who runs it (nested simulators, ancestors, time travelers, aliens, machines, or God — possibly the same thing seen from inside) and keeps both the NPC and PC answers alive while personally betting on the mystics' player-character version. What he leaves the reader with is the big claim: information and computation are becoming the substrate of all the sciences ("it from bit"), and the simulation hypothesis is therefore the first framework plausible to both camps — a comprehensible mystery that could reunite science and religion after five hundred years of divorce.
