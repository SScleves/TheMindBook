# 32

## In one paragraph
This final chapter of the main narrative closes the road trip as the narrator and Chris ride the last stretch of coastline and then freeway toward the San Francisco Bay. The narrator dwells on Chris's earlier line "I knew it," realizing Chris has known about Phaedrus for years and has been carrying that knowledge — and, the narrator now sees, has in some sense been carrying his father rather than the reverse. The two nap at a picnic table, then ride on with helmets off, talking in ordinary voices for the first time in the whole trip; Chris stands on the foot pegs and, for the first time, sees over his father's shoulders instead of just staring into his back. Their dialogue turns to Chris someday having his own motorcycle and to whether he will have "the right attitudes." The chapter ends as they pass through named wine-country towns toward the Bay, with the narrator declaring, in effect, that the family/self has been made whole: "We've won it."

## Core claims
- The author (through the narrator) suggests that Chris's repeated statement "I knew it" is not a small, throwaway remark but the surfacing of something Chris has held inside for years, formed from a childish misunderstanding of something he overheard long ago about Phaedrus.
- The narrator claims a reversal of his prior self-understanding: he had believed he was carrying Chris through the trip and through his troubles, but concludes instead, "I haven't been carrying him at all. He's been carrying me!"
- The narrator frames his own psychological task now as relieving Chris of that burden and "being one person again" — i.e., completing the reintegration of the narrator with his former self, Phaedrus.
- The narrator reflects that he and Chris "are related to each other in ways we never fully understand, maybe hardly understand at all," and states that Chris "was always the real reason for coming out of the hospital," adding that to have let him grow up alone "would have been really wrong" — fatherhood, not abstract argument, is named as the actual motive for recovery.
- The narrator notes that the same dream recurs: "In the dream too he was the one who was always trying to open the door," tying Chris's role in that shared dream to his role in the "I knew it" realization.
- The narrator presents the removal of the motorcycle helmets — no longer legally required in this state, and no longer needed once real conversation becomes possible — as marking a shift in the relationship: after many days of the trip, father and son can finally talk in a normal conversational voice.
- The narrator treats Chris's ability, once standing on the foot pegs, to "see over your shoulders" for the first time as symbolically significant — Chris has spent the whole trip staring at his father's back, and now sees a different, wider view.
- In the closing dialogue, the narrator tells Chris that having a motorcycle "when he's old enough" depends on having "the right attitudes," and that having the right attitudes "is the hard part" — and the narrator states he believes Chris will have them, "no problem at all."
- The book's final passage claims that although "trials never end" and unhappiness and misfortune are inevitable, there is now a feeling — not superficial but penetrating "all the way through" — that "We've won it. It's going to get better now."

## Concepts
- **"I knew it" motif** — Chris's repeated phrase from earlier in the book, which the narrator here reinterprets as evidence that Chris has secretly known about Phaedrus (his father's former, hospitalized identity) for years, having overheard something long ago and misunderstood it "in his childish" way, then kept it hidden.
- **Carrying the burden** — the narrator's reversed realization that rather than himself supporting/carrying Chris through the trip and through the family's troubles, it is Chris who "has been carrying" him; resolving this reversal (relieving Chris of the burden) is framed as the remaining task.
- **The right attitudes** — the narrator's answer, in dialogue with Chris, to what makes motorcycle ownership/care "hard": not technical skill but having "the right attitudes," which he calls the actually difficult part.
- **Reintegration of self / "being one person again"** — the narrator's stated aim, prompted by the realization about Chris carrying him: to stop the split (implicitly between the narrator and his former self, Phaedrus) and become whole.
- **Chris as the reason for recovery** — the narrator's claim that Chris, not any abstract goal, "was always the real reason for coming out of the hospital," and that failing to raise him "would have been really wrong"; paired with the admission that the narrator and Chris "are related to each other in ways we never fully understand."
- **The door in the dream** — a one-line callback stating that in the recurring dream between narrator and Chris, "he was the one who was always trying to open the door," linking this chapter's realization to the shared hospital-door dream raised earlier in the book.
- **Helmets off** — a small narrative device marking the end of the guarded, distanced mode of the trip: once helmets come off (permitted by the state's law, prompted by heat), Chris and the narrator can speak normally, "after all these days," instead of the muffled/shouted exchanges under helmets.
- **Seeing over the shoulder** — Chris standing on the foot pegs and, for the first time in the whole trip, seeing the road and landscape over his father's shoulders rather than only the father's back; the narrator marks this as something Chris had "never" done before and treats it as revealing "everything" as "different."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The nap at the picnic table**: riding through the heat inland, the narrator feels drowsy, sees a turnoff and picnic table in a small ravine, stops, tells Chris "I'm sleepy... I'm going to take a nap," and Chris says "Me too." They sleep and wake more rested "than for a long time" — presented as a concrete marker of relief/resolution rather than lingering exhaustion.
- **Removing the helmets**: the narrator notes helmets aren't legally required in this state, takes his off and fastens it to the pack; Chris asks for his off too; the narrator initially refuses ("You need it for safety"), Chris points out "You're not wearing yours," and the narrator relents and stows Chris's helmet too — a small concrete exchange illustrating the loosening of the narrator's protective caution.
- **The "Beautiful!" exchange**: with helmets off, the narrator hollers "Beautiful!" to Chris, who replies "You don't need to shout" — the narrator laughs, realizing after many days that a normal conversational voice is now possible.
- **Chris standing on the foot pegs**: Chris hangs onto his father's shoulders and stands up to see over them; the narrator calls it "a little dangerous," Chris insists "No, it isn't. I can tell," and is allowed to continue carefully. Riding under low branches through a grove, Chris reacts with "Oh," "Ah," "Wow," and explains, "I never could see over your shoulders before" — offered as the concrete occasion for the "seeing over the shoulder" theme; the narrator himself registers the reversal ("That's true. I never realized it. All this time he's been staring into my back."), and heading into another grove Chris asks, "Don't you get scared?", to which the narrator answers, "No, you get used to it."
- **Dialogue about a future motorcycle**: Chris asks whether he can have a motorcycle "when I get old enough," what he'd have to do to take care of it, whether his father will show him, and whether it's hard; the narrator answers that it depends on "the right attitudes," which are "hard," and, when Chris asks "Will I have the right attitudes?", answers "I think so... I don't think that will be any problem at all" — used as the chapter's closing statement of confidence in Chris's future.
- **The final ride through named towns**: the route runs down through Ukiah, Hopland, Cloverdale, into the wine country, then Asti, Santa Rosa, Petaluma, Novato, on a freeway that widens and fills with cars, trucks, and buses, until houses, boats, and the water of the Bay appear — the concrete geography marking the trip's literal end. Along the way, the narrator notes the engine itself, "which has carried us halfway across a continent," droning on "in its continuing oblivion to everything but its own internal forces."

## Thread
This chapter is the narrative resolution of the whole book: the split between the narrator and his former self, Phaedrus — pursued across the preceding chapters through the Chautauqua on Quality, the analytic knife, and the recovered story of Phaedrus's breakdown — is here resolved not through further argument but through a small domestic realization about fatherhood (Chris carrying him, not the other way around) and a literal, physical change (helmets off, Chris seeing over his shoulders). It follows directly from the buildup of Chris's distress and the narrator's growing awareness of Phaedrus across the book's second half, and it closes the road-trip frame narrative with arrival near the Bay and a declared, if guarded, sense of victory ("We've won it"), setting up only the Afterword to follow.
