# Glossary

*All definitions are the book's own, attributed to Robert M. Pirsig, his narrator, or the narrator's former self "Phaedrus"; nothing is corrected toward outside knowledge. Chapter numbers are the source-note numbers, not the book's printed chapter numbers: **0** = Dedication & Epigraph (front matter); **1** = Introduction to the Twenty-fifth Anniversary Edition; **2–33** = the narrative/Chautauqua chapters (source ch. N is roughly the book's printed Chapter N−1; the count repeats once, so both a source ch. 21 and a source ch. 30 exist); **34** = Afterword; **35** = Author's Note.*

- **A priori (Kant's)** — Aspects of reality not supplied immediately by the senses (time, space, continuity) that the mind must supply as "intuitions" while receiving sense data, providing "a kind of screening function" for which sense data we accept. Chapters: 12. Related: a-priori-motorcycle, empiricism, Copernican-revolution.
- **A priori analogues** — Phaedrus's term for the store of prior images/experience each person brings to a Quality event, which determines how differently people perceive and name the same underlying Quality. Chapters: 21. Related: preintellectual-awareness, Quality.
- **A priori motorcycle / a priori continuity of a motorcycle** — The Kantian "motorcycle we have in our minds," built up from years of sense data and constantly changing; "the sense data confirm it but the sense data aren't it," believed the way one believes money is in the bank. Chapters: 12, 13. Related: Hume-motorcycle, substance.
- **Accreditation (as excommunication)** — In Phaedrus's Church of Reason lecture, loss of accreditation carries no legal penalty; it is only official recognition of an existing condition, like excommunication — the real University declaring the place no longer "holy ground." Chapters: 14. Related: Church-of-Reason, desanctified-church.
- **Amorphic fragments** — The debris of Phaedrus's thought — thousands of notes jotted "without regard to form," many supplied by friends — from which the narrator reconstructs the Chautauqua, so that errors may lie in the reconstruction rather than in Phaedrus. Chapters: 17. Related: impersonation-of-Phaedrus.
- **Analogy (vs. literal truth)** — Phaedrus's decisive classroom move: catching the Chairman treating Socrates' chariot-and-horses image as sworn literal Truth when Socrates himself calls it "just an analogy." Chapters: 30. Related: chariot-and-horses-allegory, dialectic.
- **Analytic description** — The classical mode of describing a thing (e.g., the motorcycle) by its component assemblies and functions; "another name of the classic platform from which one discusses things in terms of their underlying form." Chapters: 7. Related: analytic-knife, classical-understanding.
- **Analytic knife / Phaedrus's knife** — The "intellectual scalpel so swift and so sharp you sometimes don't see it moving" by which analysis divides the world; its cuts are the analyst's choices, not features found in nature, and Phaedrus was "a master with this knife." Applied to experience "something is always killed... and something is always created too." Chapters: 7, 8, 19, 20, 30. Related: handful-of-sand, discrimination, cleavage-term.
- **Annihilation ECS** — The court-ordered electroconvulsive shock ("high-voltage alternating current through the lobes of his brain... twenty-eight consecutive occasions") that destroyed Phaedrus's personality, liquidating "a whole personality... without a trace in a technologically faultless act." Chapters: 8. Related: personality-vs-person, insanity-onset.
- **Ancient Greek view of time** — The metaphor (offered in the Afterword) that the future comes up from behind unseen while the past recedes visibly ahead — "more accurate," Pirsig says, than the Western picture, since no one truly faces the future. Chapters: 34.
- **Anxiety (gumption trap)** — Being so sure you'll do everything wrong that you're afraid to start; a self-stoking cycle of over-fussy overmotivation mistaken for laziness. Chapters: 27. Related: gumption-trap, ego, boredom.
- **Aretê / excellence** — The ancient Greek word usually mistranslated "virtue," meaning "excellence," a duty toward self implying wholeness of life; Phaedrus's "lightning" insight that aretê, the Sophists' teaching, the Hindu dharma, and his own Quality are one and the same thing. Chapters: 30. Related: Quality, dharma, Sophists, the-Good.
- **Aristotelian tradition** — The scholastic (via Aquinas) and Hutchins/Adler-revived view of man as "a rational animal... capable of seeking and defining the good life," the framework Phaedrus comes to see as Quality's enemy. Chapters: 29. Related: substance, Great-Books-program.
- **Art as high-quality endeavor / the Godhead revealed in the works of man** — Two "very differently sounding" statements the narrator calls identical; Art's relation to Quality he considers already shown through rhetoric. Chapters: 21. Related: Quality, introconversion.
- **Aspects of Quality** — Qualities of writing Phaedrus singled out (unity, vividness, authority, economy, sensitivity, clarity, emphasis, flow, suspense, brilliance, precision, proportion, depth), each kept as undefined as Quality itself but demonstrated by comparison. Chapters: 18. Related: unity, authority, refusal-to-define.
- **Authority (aspect of Quality) / footnotes** — An aspect of Quality of an argument that "could be jacked up with a technique called footnotes, which gives authoritative reference." Chapters: 18. Related: aspects-of-quality, outline.
- **Awareness vs. consciousness** — We are aware of millions of things but conscious only of a selection; consciousness is a selection from awareness, and "the process of selection mutates it," so the two are never the same. Chapters: 8. Related: handful-of-sand, discrimination.
- **Axioms as conventions / "geometry is not true, it is advantageous" / conventionalism** — Poincaré's conclusion that geometric axioms are neither a priori truths nor experimental facts but freely chosen "disguised definitions"; asking which geometry is true is as meaningless as asking whether the metric system is true. His critics called this "conventionalism." Chapters: 23. Related: Euclids-fifth-postulate, non-Euclidian-geometry, principle-of-objectivity.
- **Bad surroundings (muscle trap)** — A psychomotor gumption trap: poor lighting causing preventable mistakes, and discomfort from working too hot or too cold, which distorts judgment. Chapters: 27. Related: inadequate-tools, muscle-traps.
- **Beat and hip / hip and square** — Cultural names the narrator (and Phaedrus's Negro artist friends) attach to the meaning/being, romantic/classic divide; "square" was their word for the Qualityless intellectual stuff before mass media took the terms. Chapters: 6, 19. Related: groovy-dimension, squareness, generation-gap.
- **Beginner's mind** — The fresh, non-jaded, "hollow-flexible" way of seeing that boredom destroys and that Zen cultivates; its loss means "you're off the Quality track." Chapters: 25, 27. Related: boredom, egoless-acceptance-of-stuckness, koans.
- **Being in the scene / motorcycle-vs-car perception** — On a cycle "the frame is gone," you are "in the scene, not just watching it," where in a car everything through the window "is just more TV." Chapters: 2, 3. Related: secondary-roads.
- **Blank tablet** — The old Aristotelian picture of the scientist as a passive observer whom nature fills, which Kant's Copernican inversion "truly turn[ed] inside out." Chapters: 12. Related: Copernican-revolution, objectivity.
- **Boredom (gumption trap)** — Signals you've lost your beginner's mind and are off the Quality track; the correct response is to stop (sleep, coffee, a break), not push through. Chapters: 27. Related: beginners-mind, anxiety.
- **Buddha in the machine / Buddha in analytic thought** — The book's announced thesis: "the Buddha, the Godhead, resides quite as comfortably in the circuits of a digital computer or the gears of a cycle transmission as he does at the top of a mountain"; to reject the Buddha within the analysis of motorcycles "is to miss the Buddha entirely." Chapters: 2, 8. Related: Quality, death-birth-continuity.
- **Bunk-bedders dream / episode** — Phaedrus, his mind "all but gone," driving six-year-old Chris through a ruined grey city hunting a nonexistent place; "that, for Chris, is where it started." Chapters: 29. Related: insanity-onset, glass-door.
- **Care / caring** — "The real business of motorcycle maintenance, the most important aspect of all," which no manual deals with; later defined as a feeling of identification with what one is doing, "the inverse side" of which is Quality itself. Chapters: 3, 5, 25, 26. Related: Quality, spectator-attitude, just-fixing.
- **Carrying the burden / reintegration of self** — The narrator's closing reversal — "I haven't been carrying him at all. He's been carrying me!" — and the resulting task of relieving Chris and "being one person again." Chapters: 33. Related: I-knew-it-motif, mind-divided-against-itself.
- **Casuist answer** — Phaedrus's early classroom dodge (Quality is the same for everyone but the objects it inheres in vary), which "had the smell of falseness about it." Chapters: 20. Related: Quality, dilemma.
- **Causation (Hume)** — On Hume's empiricist account, causation has no basis in sensation — "just something we imagine when one thing repeatedly follows another." Chapters: 12. Related: substance, empiricism, Hume-motorcycle.
- **Chariot-and-horses allegory** — Plato's image (in the *Phaedrus*) of the soul as a charioteer drawn by a noble white horse and a passionate black horse; the Chairman's literalizing of it gives Phaedrus his opening. Chapters: 30. Related: analogy, dialectic.
- **Chautauqua** — The narrator's name for the traveling talk-series he weaves through the trip: "like the traveling tent-show Chautauquas... intended to edify and entertain," aimed not at cutting new channels of thought but at digging deeper into silted-in old ones. Chapters: 2, 3, 5, 7, 9, 13, 14, 15, 16, 17, 18, 21, 23, 27. Related: what-is-best-vs-whats-new.
- **Church attitude / God of Reason** — The academic-freedom ethic that a teacher's accountability "must be to the God of Reason, not to the idols of political power," and (in the grading argument) that civilization is best served by free men, not mules. Chapters: 17, 19. Related: Church-of-Reason, academic-freedom.
- **Church of Reason** — The real University understood as a church whose primary goal "is always Socrates' old goal of truth... revealed by the process of rationality"; later, the whole academic-rational establishment, said to run "not on individual strength but upon individual weakness." Chapters: 14, 16, 18, 19, 20, 25, 29, 30. Related: real-University, rhetoric, dialectic.
- **Church-versus-location split / the real University / the second university** — Phaedrus's cleavage distinguishing the real University ("a state of mind... the continuing body of reason itself," locationless) from the legal corporation of "the same name" that owns buildings and pays salaries but "cannot teach." Chapters: 14. Related: Church-of-Reason, teaching-college, desanctified-church.
- **Classic beauty vs. romantic beauty (Poincaré)** — Poincaré's distinction between "classic beauty" (harmonious order of the parts, grasped by pure intelligence) and "romantic beauty" (the beauty of appearances that strikes the senses); classic beauty gives structure to romantic beauty. Chapters: 23. Related: mathematical-beauty, classic-romantic-split.
- **Classic esthetic** — A subtle esthetic within the classic mode that romantics miss: "straightforward, unadorned, unemotional, economical," whose purpose is "to bring order out of chaos and make the unknown known." Chapters: 7. Related: classical-understanding.
- **Classic formalism** — The claim that "what isn't understood intellectually isn't understood at all," so Quality is unimportant as a merely emotional understanding; the argument that split Quality into a classic and a romantic kind. Chapters: 20. Related: scientific-materialism, romantic-quality-vs-classic-quality.
- **Classic Knowledge / Romantic Knowledge (train analogy)** — Classic Knowledge is the whole train (engine plus 120 boxcars) taught by the Church of Reason; Romantic Quality is the undivided "leading edge" of the engine that keeps the train moving "on a track called Quality." Chapters: 25. Related: track-of-Quality, dynamic-vs-static-reality.
- **Classical understanding / classic mode** — Seeing the world "primarily as underlying form itself," proceeding by reason and laws; motorcycle maintenance is "purely classic." Chapters: 7, 8, 12. Related: romantic-understanding, underlying-form.
- **Classical reason** — "Analytic reason, dialectic reason... sometimes considered to be the whole of understanding," which the narrator says is "completely bankrupt with regard to abstract art" and resembles the medieval flat earth. Chapters: 15. Related: expansion-of-reason, classic-romantic-split.
- **Classic–Romantic split / two realities / conflict of visions of reality** — "Two realities, one of immediate artistic appearance and one of underlying scientific explanation... they don't match and they don't fit"; the cultural fracture into "a classic culture and a romantic counterculture." Chapters: 6, 7, 11, 15, 19, 30. Related: underlying-form-vs-immediate-appearance, classic-romantic-barrier.
- **Classic-romantic barrier** — The divide between seeing the cycle "as it appears immediately" and seeing it "as a mechanic does in terms of underlying form"; a wrench has romantic beauty but "its purpose is always purely classical." Chapters: 9. Related: classic-romantic-split, precision-measuring-instruments.
- **Cleavage term** — A term you "tap" with the analytic knife so "the whole world splits, cleaves, right in two"; undefined Quality is a perfect one, splitting hip/square and classic/romantic cleanly. Chapters: 19. Related: analytic-knife, squareness.
- **Climbing without desire / ego-climbing / selfless climbing** — Mountains "should be climbed with as little effort as possible and without desire"; the ego-climber "is here but he's not here," imagining the goal as external, while selfless climbers (the Kailas pilgrims) make each step an act of devotion. Chapters: 17, 18, 19. Related: spiritual-mountain-allegory.
- **Cold front / warm front** — Rider's storm lore: cold fronts (a dark streak with no preceding cirrus) are violent but pass quickly; warm fronts are "the worst" because they can last for days. Chapters: 3. Related: quick-assumptions.
- **Common sense** — Redefined by the narrator as "nothing more than the voices of thousands and thousands of these ghosts from the past" — Moses, Christ, Buddha, Plato, Newton and the rest. Chapters: 4. Related: ghosts, context-of-thought.
- **Conservation-laws puzzle** — The Afterword's reasoning that if the "real" Chris simply vanished, physics is in trouble; if the laws hold, then what vanished (Chris-as-object) was not the real Chris — a step toward the pattern concept. Chapters: 34. Related: pattern-vs-object.
- **Context of thought** — Why belief in ghosts isn't stupidity: earlier peoples were as intelligent as moderns, but "the context in which they thought was completely different," and within it ghosts were "quite as real as atoms... to a modern man." Chapters: 4. Related: ghosts, mass-hypnosis.
- **Copernican revolution (Kant's) / Copernican inversion of Quality** — Kant's turning of the passive-observer picture "inside out" (our a priori concepts screen what we see); Phaedrus's parallel inversion later making Quality the parent, not a property, of mind and matter. Chapters: 12, 21. Related: a-priori, metaphysical-trinity, quality-as-parent.
- **Cosmologists** — The pre-Socratics (Thales, Anaximenes, Pythagoreans, Heraclitus, Anaxagoras, Parmenides) who each sought a single unchanging "Immortal Principle" behind the world. Chapters: 29. Related: Immortal-Principle, the-One-and-the-Many.
- **Craftsman-harmony** — The mark of excellent work: the craftsman follows no single line of instruction; the material determines his thoughts and motions, which change the material, "until his mind's at rest at the same time the material's right." Chapters: 15. Related: peace-of-mind, just-fixing.
- **Crisis in the foundations of science** — Poincaré's era when "absolute space, absolute time, absolute substance" collapsed, set off by the unprovability of Euclid's fifth postulate and phenomena classical physics couldn't absorb. Chapters: 23. Related: Euclids-fifth-postulate, non-Euclidian-geometry.
- **Crossing the lonesome valley** — The hymn image ("You've got to cross it by yourself") the narrator uses for Phaedrus's breakdown — a dissolution of self one must pass through alone, out of the mythos. Chapters: 30. Related: insanity-onset, mental-avalanche.
- **Crystallization / waves of crystallization** — The narrator's image for Phaedrus's thought expanding concentrically outward from Quality "like waves in all directions," not hierarchically; a first classroom wave, a metaphysical wave, and a final mystical wave "from which he never recovered." Chapters: 18, 20, 21, 23. Related: seed-crystal, supersaturated-solution.
- **Culture-bearing book (kulturbärer)** — A book that, "like a mule, bears the culture on its back," succeeding because it challenges cultural values just as the culture is shifting to embrace the challenge (his example: *Uncle Tom's Cabin*). Chapters: 34. Related: expanded-success.
- **Cut-and-try technique** — A reassembly process driven by lack of information, where a "failed" attempt still yields real progress — distinguished from a setback. Chapters: 27. Related: setback, gumption-trap.
- **Dead reckoning** — The narrator's navigation method for unposted county roads: pocket compass, map on the tank, tracking miles from the last junction, and deduction from clues. Chapters: 2. Related: secondary-roads.
- **Death-birth continuity** — What the analytic knife does to experience: "something is always killed... and something is always created too," a process "neither good nor bad, but just is." Chapters: 8. Related: analytic-knife.
- **Death force** — The romantic's name for how the classic mode appears from outside — "Oppressive. Heavy. Endlessly grey" — and for the undefined "inhuman, mechanical, lifeless" force behind technology that the Sutherlands flee. Chapters: 2, 7, 21. Related: the-system, value-freedom.
- **Dedication / epigraph** — The front matter: "For my family," and the three-line address to Phaedrus asking "what is good... Need we ask anyone to tell us these things?" — planting the question of the good before the narrative begins. Chapters: 0. Related: the-good, Phaedrus.
- **Degree-and-grading system / demonstrator / mule vs. free man / school of hard knocks** — The institution Phaedrus argued to abolish; grades condition students into "mules" ("If you don't whip me, I won't work"), and abolishing them would let a mule "flunk himself out" harmlessly and possibly return knowledge-motivated as a "free man." Chapters: 17. Related: grade-motivation, goalless-vacuum, imitation.
- **Demonstration (vs. experiment)** — What romantics confuse with experiment because both look alike; a show whose outcome is known beforehand "is not doing anything scientific." Chapters: 10. Related: experiment, scientific-method.
- **Desanctified church** — The newspaper story opening Phaedrus's lecture: a former church sold as a bar, its priest noting the building "had been desanctified" — bricks and glass are not a church. Chapters: 14. Related: church-versus-location-split.
- **Dhyana / Chan / Zen** — A discipline for removing the illusion of subject–object separation; the Sanskrit *dhyana*, "mispronounced in Chinese as 'Chan' and again mispronounced in Japanese as 'Zen.'" Chapters: 13. Related: subject-object-separation, just-sitting, tat-tvam-asi.
- **Dialectic** — The question-and-answer method of arriving at truth; Plato's sole road to truth, demoted by Aristotle to "a counterpart of rhetoric." The narrator counters that "dialectic... came itself from rhetoric," which came from myth and poetry. Chapters: 29, 30. Related: rhetoric, Sophists, mythos-over-logos.
- **Dilemma / horns of the dilemma / going between the horns** — "Greek for 'two premises,'" likened to a charging bull; the faculty's "is Quality objective or subjective?" question, which Phaedrus escapes by "going between the horns" (Quality is a third entity). Chapters: 20. Related: throwing-sand-in-the-bulls-eyes, quality-event.
- **Dimensional precision / precision measuring instruments** — Instruments (the tappet gauge among them) designed to achieve dimensional precision; their classical beauty is "not what you see, but what they mean" — control of the enormous forces inside an engine. Chapters: 9. Related: classic-romantic-barrier.
- **Direct seeing** — The cure for the blocked writer: looking and seeing "freshly for herself... without primary regard for what had been said before," as with the girl told to start with "the upper left-hand brick." Chapters: 17. Related: imitation.
- **Discrimination** — The knife that divides the handful of sand into parts ("This and that. Here and there") in a subdivision that "never comes to an end." Chapters: 8. Related: analytic-knife, handful-of-sand.
- **Divided personality / schizophrenia / a mind divided against itself** — The book's core condition: "two minds fighting for the same body, a condition that inspired the original meaning of 'schizophrenia,'" holding different values — the socially conforming narrator versus the intellectually driven Phaedrus. Chapters: 1, 28, 31. Related: social-values, intellectual-values, Phaedrus.
- **Drifting / lateral drift** — What one does when looking at lateral truth, since the known methods are themselves what is screwed up: "So he drifted. That was all he could do." Chapters: 12, 15. Related: lateral-truth, hang-up.
- **Dualistic thought / subject–object duality / nondualism** — The habitual split between person and work (or scientist and object) that "shuts out Quality"; an artificial interpretation "superimposed on reality," never reality itself, which Phaedrus's Quality dissolves. Chapters: 25, 26, 29, 31. Related: objectivity, tat-tvam-asi, mu.
- **Dynamic vs. static reality** — With Quality as a central undefined term, reality "is not static but dynamic," with forms capable of change; "when you really understand dynamic reality you never get stuck." Chapters: 25. Related: train-of-knowledge-analogy, stuckness.
- **Ego (gumption trap)** — A high self-evaluation that blocks recognition of new facts and of one's own mistakes; deflated reliably by mechanical work, and counterable by faking modesty. Chapters: 27. Related: value-traps, anxiety.
- **Egoless acceptance of stuckness** — Not fighting or fearing being stuck but accepting it as "the psychic predecessor of all real understanding," the state Zen induces through koans. Chapters: 25. Related: stuckness, beginners-mind, koans.
- **Empiricism** — The belief "that all knowledge is derived exclusively from the senses" (Hume's position); the scientific method of experiment is "carefully controlled empiricism." Chapters: 12. Related: substance, causation, a-priori.
- **Enthousiasmos** — The Greek root of "enthusiasm," "filled with theos" (God/Quality); the etymological source of gumption. Chapters: 27. Related: gumption.
- **Esthetics** — The branch of philosophy that would define Quality ("What is meant by beautiful?"); Phaedrus recoiled from it, and keeping Quality undefined "wipes out" the field. Chapters: 19. Related: Quality, refusal-to-define.
- **Euclid's fifth postulate / non-Euclidian geometry / Riemann geometry** — The parallel postulate that could never be proved; Bolyai and Lobachevski built consistent geometries by reversing it, Riemann by discarding more, and Relativity says Riemann's "best describes the world we live in." Chapters: 23. Related: axioms-as-conventions, crisis-in-foundations-of-science.
- **European vs. Indian ghosts** — The distinction the narrator retreats to when caught out over Tom White Bear, before conceding he can't sustain it: "Science isn't part of the Indian tradition." Chapters: 4. Related: ghosts, context-of-thought.
- **Expansion of reason / root expansion** — The proposed cure for technological ugliness: not abandoning rationality but expanding it "at the roots, not at the branches," on the model of Newton inventing the calculus. Chapters: 15. Related: classical-reason, topsy-turvy-times, lateral-drift.
- **Expanded success** — What the book offers in place of both material success and mere freedom: an enlargement of "success" to something "larger than a good job and staying out of trouble." Chapters: 34. Related: culture-bearing-book, freedom-as-negative-goal.
- **Fanaticism born of doubt** — "You are never dedicated to something you have complete confidence in"; Phaedrus's fanatic defense of reason grew precisely from his lost faith in it, as Jesuit zeal grew from the Church's weakness before the Reformation. Chapters: 14. Related: Church-of-Reason, ghost-of-rationality.
- **Factual basis / rhetorical alteration** — The Author's Note: the book "is based on actual occurrences" and "must be regarded in its essence as fact," though "much has been changed for rhetorical purposes." Chapters: 35. Related: Zen-Buddhist-practice-disclaimer.
- **Felt need** — The precondition of accepting help: "until he had a real felt need he was just going to resent help," so the narrator lets John struggle rather than rescue him unbidden. Chapters: 2. Related: care.
- **First-person narrator** — Per Allen Tate, the hardest form because the writer is locked inside the narrator's head — "But so is the reader!" — which lets a writer "hide a villainous narrator in plain sight." Chapters: 1. Related: Hollywood-ending, divided-personality.
- **Freedom as a negative goal** — Pirsig's diagnosis of what the hippie movement offered in place of material success: "a purely negative goal," a claim that something is bad without a positive alternative. Chapters: 34. Related: expanded-success.
- **Fulcrum word** — Phaedrus's term for a word (like "dialectic") whose precise placement can tip an entire argument, making the Professor of Philosophy uneasy. Chapters: 30. Related: dialectic, rhetoric.
- **Functional questions** — The "what does it do / why" questions that replace categorical "what is it" questions once you are truly engaged with a stuck problem, turning an object into "a collection of functions." Chapters: 25. Related: stuckness, screw-as-functions.
- **Funeral procession** — Sylvia's phrase for the "dead"-looking commuters, recast as the hurried, ego-driven modern way of living — "the biggest gumption trap of all." Chapters: 21, 27. Related: gumption-trap, loneliness. (First voiced Chapters: 2.)
- **Generation gap** — A cultural result, the narrator says, of the dimensional difference between grooving and rational form — not a fad but "here to stay." Chapters: 6. Related: groovy-dimension, beat-and-hip.
- **Genetic defect within the nature of reason** — Phaedrus's diagnosis of current social crises: a defect internal to reason itself, so that applying inherited rationality moves society away from a better world, not toward it. Chapters: 11. Related: scientifically-produced-antiscience, ghost-of-rationality.
- **Ghost (Phaedrus / the ghost)** — First a bedtime story ("a fellow who... hunted for a ghost... thrashed him good... Then he became a ghost himself"), then the narrator's own former self haunting the trip; "an evil spirit. Insane. From a world without life or death." Chapters: 4, 6, 19, 31. Related: Phaedrus, ghost-of-rationality, doppelganger.
- **Ghosts (laws of science, common sense as ghosts)** — Anything with "no matter and no energy" that exists only in people's minds — including the laws of physics and logic, the number system, and (via the machinist's steel) even "potential." "We just believe in them so thoroughly they seem real." Chapters: 4, 9, 15. Related: laws-of-science, common-sense, motorcycle-as-mental-phenomenon.
- **Ghost of rationality / ghost of reason** — The ghost Phaedrus pursued: "the ghost that underlies all of technology, all of modern science, all of Western thought... rationality itself," which he "found... and thrashed it good." Chapters: 8, 9, 10, 11, 13, 16, 30. Related: ghosts, genetic-defect-in-reason.
- **Glass door** — The recurring dream image separating the narrator from Chris (and his family) — later revealed as the hospital glass door Chris remembers; Chris's unprompted recall of it triggers the narrator's full self-recognition as Phaedrus. Chapters: 24, 28, 31, 33. Related: nightmare-as-revelation, sarcophagus-as-coffin.
- **Goalless vacuum** — What withholding grades exposed: a "Kafkaesque" punishment for failing to do something no one would name; "You cannot withhold grades and sit there and create a goalless vacuum" — a goal (Quality) must fill it. Chapters: 17. Related: degree-and-grading-system, Quality.
- **The good / the Good** — The epigraph's opening question ("what is good... Need we ask anyone to tell us these things?"); later, in Phaedrus's Greek history, "the reality of the Good" that the Sophists represented, defeated by "the True." Chapters: 0, 29. Related: Quality, aretê, the-True.
- **Gordian knot** — Phaedrus's self-image of having "cut a Gordian knot of dualistic thought with one word, Quality" — later judged by the narrator as fanatic certainty. Chapters: 29. Related: Quality, subject-object-duality.
- **Grade motivation vs. knowledge motivation** — The demonstrator's central contrast: the student conditioned to work for a grade rather than for the knowledge it was to represent, versus the returned student who "would need no external pushing to learn." Chapters: 17. Related: degree-and-grading-system, free-man.
- **Great Books program** — The University of Chicago's Hutchins-era curriculum requiring fifteen-year-olds to read the classics, built on the premise that ancient wisdom remains centrally relevant. Chapters: 28. Related: Aristotelian-tradition, Committee-on-Analysis-of-Ideas.
- **Groovy dimension** — John Sutherland's mode: responding "immediately and intuitively, grooving on it," without rational premeditation — "the way he thinks about drumming... he just does it." Chapters: 6. Related: immediate-appearance, generation-gap.
- **Gumption / gumption trap / gumptionology** — An old Scottish word for the enthusiasm that fills someone connected with Quality — "the psychic gasoline that keeps the whole thing going"; a gumption trap is "anything that causes one to lose sight of Quality," and "gumptionology" is the mock-science of cataloguing them. Chapters: 27, 30. Related: enthousiasmos, setback, value-traps, truth-traps, muscle-traps.
- **Gumption desperation** — The trap-within-a-trap of hurrying wildly to "make up for lost time" after a setback, which only breeds more mistakes. Chapters: 27. Related: out-of-sequence-reassembly-setback, impatience.
- **Handful of sand** — The governing image for consciousness: "We take a handful of sand from the endless landscape of awareness... and call that handful of sand the world," which the knife then endlessly subdivides. Chapters: 8, 19. Related: awareness-vs-consciousness, discrimination, classical-understanding.
- **Hang-up** — A work impasse where "you just sit and stare and think, and search randomly for new information... and after a while the unseen factors start to emerge"; also the internal class of gumption traps. Chapters: 6, 27. Related: stuckness, drifting.

Chapters: 26. Related: technological-ugliness, classic-and-romantic-fusion.
- **Subject–object separation / duality** — The dualistic split (of self from object, subject from predicate) that logic presumes; "logic presumes a separation of subject from object; therefore logic is not final wisdom," best removed by disciplines like dhyana. - **Subliminal self / subliminal Quality discrimination** — Poincaré's faculty that sifts huge numbers of possible combinations and lets only the beautiful ones surface, which the narrator equates "exactly" with Phaedrus's preintellectual awareness and with the Quality-based selection of good facts. Chapters: 23, 25. Related: preintellectual-awareness, mathematical-beauty, hierarchy-of-facts.
- **Substance (Hume / Aristotle)** — On Hume, the supposed "something out there" behind sense data, of which there is no sensory impression, hence imagined; on Aristotle, the unchanging "something" that changing Appearances cling to — "the moment... our modern scientific understanding of reality was born." Chapters: 12, 29. Related: causation, Ideas-and-Appearance, substantive-field.
- **Substantive field / methodological field** — The Aristotelian distinction (fields of "what is" vs. "how things are done") that the Chairman's dismissal — "English composition... is a methodological field!" — throws at Phaedrus; he answers that Quality is neither, but "the goal toward which method is aimed." Chapters: 29. Related: substance, procrustean-bed, Quality.
- **Supertruth laugh** — The narrator's reading of DeWeese's laughter at Phaedrus's failing-students insight: laughter that "destroys tensions produced by impossible situations" — "The best students always are flunking." Chapters: 13. Related: teachers-grade-up-students-who-resemble-them.
- **Suppressed anger at what you hate** — The rule that decodes the dripping faucet: "You always suppress momentary anger at something you deeply and permanently hate," which is why Sylvia "deliberately does not blame the faucet." Chapters: 2. Related: technology-hatred, the-system.

- **The system / "it"** — The Sutherlands' shorthand ("The whole thing," "The system") for what the narrator decodes as "a kind of force that gives rise to technology... a blind monster, a death force"; later, government and institutions rest on the same conceptual structure as a motorcycle, so "the true system... is rationality itself." Chapters: 2, 9, 19. Related: death-force, rationality-as-true-system, mass-person.
- **Tat tvam asi ("Thou art that")** — The Sanskrit/Upanishadic doctrine that everything you think you are and everything you think you perceive are undivided; realizing this fully is enlightenment, and it names the moment of pure Quality when subject and object are identical. Chapters: 13, 26. Related: subject-object-separation, dhyana, quality-event.
- **Teachers grade up students who resemble them** — Phaedrus's theory, and his puzzlement that the students he most identified with were all failing — met by DeWeese's "supertruth laugh." Chapters: 13. Related: supertruth-laugh, degree-and-grading-system.

- **Techne** — The Greek root of "technology," which "originally meant 'art'"; evidence the technology/art split is historically constructed, not intrinsic. Chapters: 26. Related: technology-art-divorce, technological-ugliness.
- **Technological ugliness** — The "stylized" dullness of modern technology, located not in materials or objects but in "the relationship between the people who produce the technology and the things they produce." Chapters: 26. Related: stylization, techne, dualistic-thought.
- **Technology-art divorce / transcendence of technology** — The claim that the separation of art from technology is "completely unnatural" (rotisserie assembly "a long-lost branch of sculpture"), and that technology, rightly seen, is "a fusion of nature and the human spirit... that transcends both." Chapters: 15, 26. Related: techne, craftsman-harmony, classic-and-romantic-fusion.

- **Temple of science** — Einstein's image (the 1918 speech Phaedrus knew by heart) of science as a temple whose true dwellers are the odd, solitary few escaping "the painful crudity and hopeless dreariness" of everyday life. Chapters: 11. Related: molecular-biology, intuition.
- **Theoretic / esthetic (Northrop) / undifferentiated aesthetic continuum** — Northrop's terms for the Western "theoretic" and Oriental "esthetic" components of existence (the seed of Phaedrus's classic/romantic terms), and the "undifferentiated aesthetic continuum" from which the theoretic arises. Chapters: 12. Related: classic-romantic-split, latent-image.
- **Theory of Relativity** — Cited as the physical theory (understood by few in Poincaré's time) whose foundations show Riemann geometry, not Euclid's, "best describes the world we live in." Chapters: 23. Related: non-Euclidian-geometry.

Chapters: 23. Related: subliminal-self, crystallization.
- **Throwing sand in the bull's eyes / singing the bull to sleep / refusing to enter the arena** — The three rhetorical (non-logical) ways out of a dilemma: attack the questioner, plead humble inability, or refuse the classification altogether (DeWeese's advice, which Phaedrus disregarded). Chapters: 20. Related: dilemma, philosophical-mysticism.
- **Time-lag** — The interval Phaedrus insists is real and important between the moment of (preintellectual) vision and the moment of intellectual awareness; anything past the lag is "already past" and so, he argues, "unreal." Chapters: 21. Related: preintellectual-awareness, quality-event.

- **Topsy-turvy times** — The felt confusion of "a period of root expansion," caused by "inadequacy of old forms of thought to deal with new experiences" (his example: the post-Columbus Renaissance). Chapters: 15. Related: expansion-of-reason, lateral-drift.
- **Truth-teller** — A quality attributed to both Chris and Phaedrus ("He is a truth-teller, like Phaedrus"), in contrast to the narrator's survival "by pleasing others." Chapters: 31. Related: heretic-who-has-recanted, divided-personality.
- **The True / the Good** — Phaedrus's framing of the ancient struggle: the dialecticians (Socrates/Plato) represented "the reality of the True," the Sophists "the reality of the Good"; Truth won, the Good lost, which is why Quality is hard to accept today. Chapters: 29. Related: the-Good, Sophists, aretê.
- **Twentieth-century lunacy** — The narrator's barb that his ghost talk was "the first normal thing I've said in weeks... The rest of the time I'm feigning twentieth-century lunacy... So as not to draw attention to myself." Chapters: 4. Related: scientific-point-of-view.
- **Twenty Questions dialectic** — The narrator's name for Socrates' cross-examination method in the *Gorgias*, by which Gorgias's plain answers about rhetoric are subtly rendered into "an object" with parts. Chapters: 29. Related: dialectic, analytic-knife, pandering.
- **Underlying form** — The reality beneath immediate appearance; what the working mechanic really attends to (mental images and hierarchies), and one of the two modes of discussion. Chapters: 6, 7, 10. Related: immediate-appearance, classical-understanding.
- **Underlying form vs. immediate appearance / what things mean vs. what things are** — The core distinction arrived at through the shim episode: "I was seeing what the shim meant. - **Unity (aspect of Quality) / outline** — "The hanging-togetherness of a story," an aspect of Quality improvable with the technique of an outline. Chapters: 18. Related: aspects-of-quality, authority.
- **Value freedom / value-free science / "quality free"** — The dictum that science and technology are "value free," which the narrator says "has got to go" and links to the "death-force effect"; Hutchins's premise that empirical science can't ground a "good" education. Chapters: 21, 29. Related: death-force, introconversion, Quality.
- **Value rigidity** — "The most widespread and pernicious" value trap: an inability to revalue what one sees because of commitment to prior values; "the facts do not exist until value has created them," so rigid values literally block new facts (the South Indian Monkey Trap). Chapters: 27. Related: value-traps, south-indian-monkey-trap.
- **Valve clatter ("nickels-and-dimes sound")** — The old engine's normal sound, "as if there were a lot of loose change flying around inside"; once you learn to expect it, "you automatically hear any difference." Chapters: 6. Related: hang-up.

- **The Jesuits / Loyola** — Invoked as historical proof that fanaticism marks doubt: Jesuit zeal "stems... from [the Church's] weakness in the face of the Reformation," and Phaedrus is "a pre-Reformation Loyola." Chapters: 14.
- **John (Dayville gas-station owner)** — The generous Oregon station owner who lets the travelers rest under his Chinaman's-Ditch cottonwoods; distinct from John Sutherland. Chapters: 27.
- **The John Birch Society** — Invoked via the governor's campaign manager (its state coordinator) as part of the right-wing siege on the Montana college. Chapters: 14.
- **Joshu** — The Zen monk whose "Mu" answer to whether a dog has Buddha-nature illustrates the third logical value outside yes/no. Chapters: 27.
- **Kant, Immanuel** — The philosopher whose a priori doctrine and "Copernican revolution" rescue reason from Hume's solipsism and model Phaedrus's own inversion — but whose esthetics Phaedrus finds "ugly," a turning point. Chapters: 12, 19, 20.
- **Kennedy, President John F.** — Invoked by analogy: the Montana right-wing atmosphere is compared to "Dallas, Texas, just prior to President Kennedy's assassination." Chapters: 14.
- **Kitto, H. D. F.** — Author of *The Greeks*, whose gloss on aretê as "excellence" (mistranslated "virtue") and portrait of Odysseus as the "all-rounder" trigger Phaedrus's central identification of aretê with Quality. Chapters: 29.
- **The Korean laborers** — Phaedrus's English-lesson friends on the Yellow Sea hillside whose "yes that means no" and cultural difference plant the esthetic/Oriental pole of his thought. Chapters: 12.
- **Krupp, Baron Alfred** — Invoked in the narrator's revenge-fantasy of passing the beer-can shim off as imported "from the private stock of Baron Alfred Krupp" so John "would have gone gaga over it." Chapters: 6.
- **Lao Tzu** — Author of the *Tao Te Ching*, which Phaedrus rereads with "Quality" substituted for "Tao" and finds a line-for-line match — "He had broken the code." Chapters: 21.
- **Lewis and Clark** — Invoked via a roadside marker as the explorers whose Northwest Passage side-excursion supplies the trip's metaphor. Chapters: 10.
- **Lincoln, Abraham** — Named among civilization's "ghosts" of the past that make up common sense. Chapters: 4.
- **Lobachevski, Nikolai** — Russian who, with Bolyai, proved Euclid's fifth postulate unprovable via a consistent non-Euclidian geometry. Chapters: 23.
- **Locke, John** — Whose truth that no object is knowable except through its qualities briefly seems to smash the "objective" horn of the dilemma before failing. Chapters: 20.
- **Lysias / Lycias** — The ancient orator Plato likened to a wolf (Greek *lykos*) — the real source of the "wolf" that Pirsig's minor error misattached to Phaedrus — and the author of the bad speech the dialogue-Phaedrus admires. Chapters: 1, 29, 30.
- **Mark Twain** — Invoked as the case of what analysis kills: having mastered the knowledge to pilot the Mississippi, "he discovered the river had lost its beauty." Chapters: 8.
- **The mechanics (motorcycle shop) / the mechanic** — The radio-blasting, "spectator" technologists who butchered three overhauls while a sheared 25-cent pin went unfound — the generating case of the spectator diagnosis; also the generic diagnosing mechanic of the scientific-method chapter. Chapters: 3, 10.
- **Michelangelo** — Cited among the great artists the "just what you like" reading would trivialize. Chapters: 20.
- **The motel/cabin builder and his wife** — The Gardiner craftsman who made all his cabins himself ("just well done"), whose noticed quality of work answers Phaedrus's "fakey" museum-park. Chapters: 13.
- **Moses** — Named among civilization's "ghosts" and among the makers of Western religious "routes over the mountain" now washed out by science. Chapters: 4, 17.
- **The narrator (unnamed "I")** — The book's first-person voice: a computer-manual editor and motorcyclist who, since court-ordered shock treatment, is the successor personality to "Phaedrus," reconstructing and finally reintegrating his former self. Chapters: 1–34 (throughout).
- **The Negro artist friends** — Phaedrus's cross-country companions who had long complained of Qualitylessness and called it "square," prompting his "Soul. Quality. The same?" Chapters: 19.
- **Nell** — Pirsig's daughter, born of a pregnancy he nearly ended, in whom he feels "the larger pattern of Chris" reassert itself; her typed scribble closes the Afterword. Chapters: 34.
- **Nell's mother (Pirsig's second wife)** — Pirsig's second wife, whose unexpected pregnancy and the reversed decision to end it are recounted in the Afterword. Chapters: 34.
- **Newton, Isaac** — Invoked as "a very good ghost" (the law of gravity as mind-made) and as the model of "root expansion" — presuming instantaneous change to invent the calculus, "a new form of reason." Chapters: 4, 15.
- **Northrop, F. S. C.** — Author of *The Meeting of East and West*, whose "theoretic/esthetic" distinction and "undifferentiated aesthetic continuum" shaped Phaedrus's later classic/romantic terms. Chapters: 12.
- **The Northwest Regional Accrediting Association** — The accrediting body Phaedrus corresponded with about the Montana college's violations, calling for an investigation. Chapters: 14.
- **Omar Khayyam** — Whose *Rubáiyat* the narrator recites at a cold dawn, then sets aside its fatalism ("guzzle the wine") for the Chautauqua on gumption. Chapters: 27.
- **Orwell, George** — Cited (with *1984*) as a real-world match for the Qualityless world. Chapters: 19.
- **Parmenides** — Pre-Socratic whose disciple Zeno "proved" motion illusory, making the Immortal Principle motionless. Chapters: 29.
- **Phaedrus** — The narrator's destroyed former self: a hyperintellectual (IQ 170) who pursued "the ghost of rationality" and the meaning of Quality until court-ordered shock treatment liquidated him; the "ghost" haunting the trip, whose story the whole book reconstructs and who "wins" at the end. Chapters: 0, 1, 4, 6, and throughout Parts II–IV.
- **Plato** — Invoked as the dialectician who, with Socrates, defeated the Sophists and "encapsulated" aretê as a fixed Idea — vilifying the rhetoricians "to grind an axe of his own"; his dialogue *Phaedrus* is the Chicago battleground. Chapters: 1, 4, 15, 20, 28, 29, 30.
- **Poincaré, Jules Henri** — The eminent French mathematician-physicist whose account of fact-selection, the "subliminal self," and mathematical "harmony" the narrator finds is the exact complementary fit to Phaedrus's philosophy, uniting Science and Art. Chapters: 23, 25, 30.
- **Polus** — The young, over-talkative "colt" of Plato's *Gorgias*, contrasted with the "wolf" Phaedrus in the naming discussion. Chapters: 30.
- **Pirsig, Robert M.** — The author, who in the Introduction confesses the book's two "failures" and in the Afterword and Author's Note frames its success (a "culture-bearing book"), Chris's death, and its factual basis. Chapters: 1, 34, 35.
- **The professor of philosophy at Benares** — The teacher expounding the world's illusory nature whom Phaedrus stopped cold by asking whether the Hiroshima and Nagasaki bombs were illusory — the break that ended his India years. Chapters: 13.
- **The Professor of Philosophy** — The Chicago instructor of "Rhetoric 251," Phaedrus's silent classroom antagonist, uneasy over the "fulcrum word" dialectic, who flees the room after the cookery exchange. Chapters: 29, 30.
- **Professor Lamm (or Lamb)** — The University of Chicago philosophy professor whose 1960 remark that Plato likened a *Phaedrus*-character to a wolf seeded Pirsig's naming error. Chapters: 1.
- **The psychology professor** — Phaedrus's next-door colleague who endorsed abolishing the degree-and-grading system: "then you'll get real education." Chapters: 17.
- **Raphael** — Cited among the great artists the "just what you like" reading would reduce to sensation-titillators. Chapters: 20.
- **The retired stockman in Bowman** — The old man who, in 102-degree heat, talks "with slow dignity" about the Henderson cycle he once owned — an emblem of "hard country" stamina and unrecognized care. Chapters: 8.
- **The ancient Greek rhetoricians** — Collectively invoked as "the first teachers of the Western world," vilified by Plato, on whose "graves" the Church of Reason was founded — "when you dig deep... you come across ghosts." Chapters: 15.
- **Rich, Ocee** — Author of *Chilton's Motorcycle Troubleshooting Guide* (sold by Sears), one of the three books the narrator carries. Chapters: 5.
- **Roosevelt, Eleanor** — Invoked to show what "radical" meant in Gallatin County: the President's wife was banned from the college as "too controversial." Chapters: 9.
- **Rousseau, Jean-Jacques** — Named among civilization's "ghosts" of the past that make up common sense. Chapters: 4.
- **Russell, Bertrand** — Quoted describing Poincaré as "by general agreement, the most eminent scientific man of his generation." Chapters: 23.
- **Sarah** — The retiring classics scholar whose singsong "I hope you are teaching Quality to your students" is "the seed crystal" of the whole inquiry; later insists "Quality is every part of Greek thought." Chapters: 16, 29.
- **Schelling, Friedrich** — Named in the roll of philosophic idealists. Chapters: 20.
- **The sculptor (welder from northern Montana)** — The suspicious sheep-herding sculptor at the DeWeese party won over on learning the narrator welds — "Motorcycle maintenance opens strange doors." Chapters: 15.
- **Shiva** — The Hindu god whose Himalayan abode (Mount Kailas) is the goal of Phaedrus's failed pilgrimage. Chapters: 18.
- **Socrates** — Invoked as the martyr of truth and the "ancient enemy of rhetorical argument" whose dialectic (in the *Gorgias* and *Phaedrus*) Phaedrus judges "the usurper" of the Good — yet whose founding of the idea of truth the narrator credits with making Western man. Chapters: 12, 14, 20, 29, 30.
- **Stein, Gertrude** — Quoted for the line that premeditated writing "had a certain syrup... but it didn't pour." Chapters: 16.
- **The Sutherlands (John and Sylvia)** — The narrator's friends and Part I riding companions: John, a drummer who "grooves on" things and refuses to maintain his BMW, and Sylvia, who perceives the commuters as "a funeral procession" — the book's romantic counterweights, whose flight from technology sets the central puzzle. Chapters: 1, 2, and through Part II.
- **Tate, Allen** — "A distinguished poet and literary critic" whose seminar on *The Turn of the Screw* taught Pirsig that a first-person narrator can be the hidden villain — the interpretive key to the whole book. Chapters: 1.
- **Thales** — Pre-Socratic who held the Immortal Principle to be water. Chapters: 29.
- **Thoreau, Henry David** — Author of *Walden* (read to Chris by question-and-answer), later found "tame and cloistered" for the mountain, and quoted: "You never gain something but that you lose something." Chapters: 5, 18, 19, 30.
- **The three farmers** — In a brand-new pickup at Ellendale, the counter-case to the Sutherlands: people who value technology, have the know-how, and "need it the least" — turning the couple's attitude into visible inconsistency. Chapters: 5.
- **Tom White Bear** — The Indian boy at Chris's YMCA camp whose grandmother told him ghosts come from those "not buried right"; his being Indian forces the narrator's reversal into the full ghost argument, and the saying later frames Phaedrus as "never buried right." Chapters: 4.
- **Toulouse-Lautrec, Henri** — A Paris contemporary invoked only to picture Poincaré's beard and pince-nez. Chapters: 23.
- **Twain, Mark** — *See Mark Twain.*
- **Voulkas, Peter** — DeWeese's former student, whose large pots and vases John nearly kicks over — a marker of the DeWeese circle's artistic standing. Chapters: 15.
- **The waitress / woman at the restaurant / truck driver** — Coastal walk-on figures: the waitress whose "steady gaze" exemplifies the loneliness the narrator sees everywhere, the woman who disapproves of Chris's crying, and the truck driver who offers a ride during Chris's breakdown. Chapters: 31.
- **The welder (old craftsman)** — The taciturn coastal welder who makes the narrator clean the chain guard himself, then welds it flawlessly for "One dollar" — an emblem of unrecognized Quality work. Chapters: 30.
- **Whitehead, Alfred North** — Quoted for the line that all philosophy is "footnotes to Plato," supporting Phaedrus's trace of Western confusion about Quality to ancient Greece. Chapters: 28.
- **The woman in white (nurse) / the man at the hospital** — Hospital figures in the narrator's "awakening" who greet the new personality ("It's very early for this to be happening") and tell him "You have a new personality now." Chapters: 8.
- **The YMCA camp counselors** — Invoked as the makers of the "big ego thing" out of the outdoors — proof-of-manhood accomplishments the narrator blames for Chris's ego-climbing trouble. Chapters: 18.
- **Zeno** — Parmenides' disciple whose paradoxes "proved" all motion and change illusory. Chapters: 29.
- **Zeus** — Named in the Homeric *Iliad* scene (via Kitto) where Hector prays to the gods for his son. Chapters: 29.
