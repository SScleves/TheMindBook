# Ch 8 — Parallel Universes, Future Selves, and Video Games

## In one paragraph
Building on the quantum indeterminacy (QI) of the previous chapter, Virk takes on two of quantum physics' most baffling results — Wheeler's delayed-choice experiment (a measurement in the future appears to "select" what a particle did in the past) and Everett's many-worlds interpretation (every quantum choice splits the universe into parallel copies) — and argues that neither makes practical sense in a purely physical world, but both become almost trivial if reality is a computed simulation. He maps the branching multiverse onto a "Multiverse Graph" (a directed graph of possible futures, from his book *The Simulated Multiverse*) and onto the AI he built as a video game developer: game AIs project possible future moves, score them with an evaluation function (e.g., the minimax algorithm), and bring the values back to the present to choose a path — exactly the structure of Fred Alan Wolf's "offer waves" from the future and Thomas Campbell's "Fundamental Process" with its profitability function. Because branching, copying, compression, and render-on-demand are cheap and native to computation but absurd to do with physical particles, Virk concludes the simulation hypothesis may be the only practical implementation of MWI and retrocausality — and it even offers a middle path reconciling the Copenhagen interpretation with the multiverse: the other probabilities are simply "other runs" of the simulation, existing as information unless rendered.

## Core claims
- Virk argues that quantum indeterminacy must have some impact on macro reality because subatomic particles ultimately make up our physical reality, even though this is hard to visualize at the macro level.
- Virk presents Wheeler's delayed-choice experiment as showing that quantum particles are undefined until the moment they are measured — even when the measurement happens *after* the particle "needs to choose" which slit to go through — meaning neither the cat's state nor its prior history is fully decided until observation.
- Virk reports that some physicists call this **retrocausality** (cause in the future, effect in the past), while noting it remains an area of debate and that Wheeler himself was hesitant to use the term.
- Virk argues (relaying Fred Alan Wolf's view) that one interpretation is that possible futures send information back to the present, and which future we navigate to depends on our choices and how "offer waves" from present and future superpose or cancel.
- Virk relays one answer physicists have come up with for the collapse mystery: that consciousness somehow determines the collapse, with observation and measurement part of this process — whether the observation happens in the present, past, or future, there didn't seem to be a way around requiring some type of observation, "until an ever stranger interpretation took hold" (MWI), "and Wheeler played a role in that, too."
- Virk argues that under the many-worlds interpretation (MWI), all the probabilities are true — in different universes — so Schrödinger's cat is both alive and dead, just in two different universes; we only observe one outcome because we happen to be in that timeline.
- Virk argues (citing one of Stephen Hawking's final papers) that the number of parallel universes may be finite rather than infinite — limited by the number of particle configurations and outcomes of choices — meaning some universes end up "substantially similar," which suggests an easy path to computational optimization.
- Virk argues that physically copying an entire universe at every quantum decision is not efficient or parsimonious and fits no known physical, biological, or chemical process — but branching and copying are common, cheap structures in computation.
- Virk argues that the simulation hypothesis "might be the only practical way" that MWI, the delayed-choice experiment, and future probable scenarios could actually be implemented: probable futures are generated as *information*, not physical worlds, and evaluated through a branching tree with some evaluation function.
- Virk argues that in a simulated universe the definition of "real" changes: parallel worlds are real only when rendered and exist as information otherwise.
- Virk argues the simulated multiverse offers a middle path between the Copenhagen interpretation and MWI: the "other" probabilities (the other 99 cats) are neither nonexistent (Copenhagen) nor other physical universes (MWI) but "other runs" of the simulation that need not be rendered.
- Virk raises, without resolving, the open metaphysical questions: Do the parallel universes actually exist or are they just probabilities? Are future selves physical versions of us or probable selves sending back information? What evaluation function governs a multiplayer shared online simulation? He notes these are exactly the questions Everett and Wheeler hoped to avoid, and flags that the chapters on Eastern mystics, karma, and reincarnation will offer "an elegant answer from well beyond the realm of physics."
- Claims stay claims: the chapter is explicit that how the probability wave collapses "is still one of the biggest mysteries in quantum physics," that retrocausality is debated, and that whether MWI worlds are real or probabilities is contested among physicists and philosophers.

## Concepts
- **Quantum indeterminacy (QI)** — carried over from the previous chapter: particles exist as a probability wave corresponding to a set of positions a particle might occupy; only through observation do we get a single outcome. Until then, all possible values exist in a probability wave or set of probable worlds.
- **Many-worlds interpretation (MWI)** — an interpretation of quantum physics in which every time a choice is made (including an observation of a single particle), the universe breaks off into multiple parallel universes; rather than a probability wave collapsing into one measurable reality, all the probabilities are true in different universes. Hugh Everett, a student of John Wheeler at Princeton, is the man most responsible for it; the term "many-worlds interpretation" was popularized by Bryce DeWitt.
- **Delayed-choice experiment (delayed-measurement experiment)** — Wheeler's extension of the double-slit experiment in which the detection device is placed *after* the particles have gone through the slits but *before* they reach the final screen, testing whether the wave/particle decision is really made at the slits or at measurement.
- **Retrocausality** — the concept, dubbed by some physicists, that the cause is in the future and the effect is in the past; the delayed-choice result read as the future observation influencing the particle's past behavior. Debated; Wheeler hesitated to endorse the term.
- **Offer wave (Fred Alan Wolf)** — Wolf's idea that we send an "offer wave" into the future which interacts with offer waves coming from possible futures back to the present; which future we reach depends on our choices (consciousness and observation) and how the waves superpose or cancel.
- **Multiverse** — the modern term for the totality of parallel universes spawned by quantum decisions; taken seriously because it offers an alternative to the subjective universe quantum physics seems to imply, and explains paradoxes like Schrödinger's cat (alive and dead, in two universes).
- **Grandfather paradox** — the classic time-travel objection (kill your grandfather before your parents are conceived and you'd never be born to do it); the multiverse resolves it by having the change spawn a new timeline — you were born in the first timeline, not the second.
- **Multiverse Graph** — Virk's term (defined in his book *The Simulated Multiverse*) for the directed graph of branching possibilities: a "future map" for an entity or particle starting at point A, where each choice branches universes but some paths reconverge to substantially similar states (you can reach point C from A by two different paths).
- **Substantially similar universes** — the idea (linked to Hawking's finite-universe theory) that a limited number of particle configurations and choice outcomes means some spawned universes are essentially the same (eggs today vs. eggs tomorrow), enabling compression.
- **Minimax algorithm** — a simple game AI: project possible future moves, rank those futures with an evaluation function, bring the values back to the present, and choose the path with the best value. Virk calls it a generalized version of the algorithm Claude Shannon used for one of the first chess-playing computers (Chapter 5).
- **Evaluation function** — the scoring function used to rank projected futures. Simple in chess or checkers; hard to define for a multiplayer shared online simulation, where it depends on the nature of the "game," how score is kept, what the runners define as optimal, and possibly differs per player.
- **Game state** — the representation of the state of the universe as information; if defined as the state of all particles in the current physical universe, it "can be varied and evaluated by a computer program very easily."
- **Monte Carlo simulations** — simulations that run a large number of random possibilities in order to understand which are the most likely scenarios to emerge; Virk's example of computers being good at spinning up many possibilities quickly.
- **Fundamental Process (Thomas Campbell)** — from *My Big TOE* (2003): the process driving evolution of consciousness, biology, and even inanimate objects — an entity spreads out its potentiality into all available possibilities and, by trial and error, populates only the states that are immediately "profitable," letting others go. Recursive; continues as long as profitable states exist; operates at all levels from subatomic to cells to consciousness to the universe.
- **Profitability function** — Campbell's evaluation function: for carbon-based biological entities, profit = survival (aligning with natural evolution); for non-biological entities, the state requiring the least energy (easiest/cheapest); for conscious entities, the state that reduces entropy and moves toward greater order.
- **Branching (simulation mechanism)** — spinning off multiple probable realities: extremely difficult for physical reality, "almost a trivial matter" in a simulated reality — multiple simulations can branch on the same computing power without additional physical resources.
- **Optimization / compression (simulation mechanism)** — since the number of universes is less than infinite and many are similar, information can be compressed the way images are (in a night-sky picture most pixels are black); pruning the tree avoids infinite branching and infinite computation.
- **Rendering on demand** — the copied/branched universes are information in "some kind of cyberspace"; particles (or pixels) are rendered only when necessary — real only when rendered.
- **Copenhagen interpretation** — the collapse theory from the previous chapter, contrasted here with MWI; on Copenhagen, the other probabilities (other 99 cats) "never really existed."
- **Simulated multiverse (middle path)** — Virk's reconciliation: the other probabilities are "other runs" of the simulation existing as information, not rendered unless that possibility is part of the game being played — escaping the parsimony objection to MWI.
- **Multiple possible pasts** — a "weird side effect" of the delayed-choice results Virk flags for *The Simulated Multiverse*: there may be not only multiple possible futures but multiple possible pasts.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
1. **Wheeler's delayed-choice double-slit experiment (theory + implementations)** — John Wheeler, a prominent theoretical physicist involved in many of the biggest developments in the new physics (office just down the hall from Einstein at Princeton), added a set of choices *after* the two slits. Motivation: a backdoor to pacify physicists who doubted that wave collapse occurs at observation — they held the particle was already either solid particle or wave at the slits and measurement merely revealed it after the fact (akin to insisting Schrödinger's cat must already be dead or alive). Setup (Figure 25): a lens replaces the slits and diverts particles to telescope 1 or telescope 2 (equivalent to "which slit"); the detection device is placed after the particles pass the slits but before they reach the final screen. Result — two paradoxical findings: (a) an interference screen inserted for an instant between lens and telescopes shows an interference pattern, which can occur only if the particle goes through both slits (a wave); (b) each particle nonetheless arrives at an individual telescope, which requires it to be a discrete particle that went through one slit. Wheeler's conclusion: particles retain wave-particle duality even after the lens; the matter isn't decided until measurement at the telescopes — the particle is undefined until measured, even when measurement happens after the "choice" of slit. Virk's gloss: the cat isn't dead or alive until someone looks, *nor is the cat's history before entering the box fully decided* until observation.
2. **The 2017 Italian satellite experiment** — a team of Italian scientists ran the delayed-choice experiment over a much larger distance by bouncing lasers off satellites; the particle travels about 2,200 miles to the satellite shortly after passing the slits (or lens equivalent). The fraction of a second between slit passage and measurement is a significant, measurable time for subatomic particles. The photon still exhibited both wave and particle properties until measurement — results consistent with the smaller-scale experiments. Virk: this means something in the future (measurement) was influencing something in the past (the particle/wave choice at the slits).
3. **The cross-country driving analogy** — Virk's intuition pump for delayed choice: driving from Washington, DC, to California, you must decide well before the coast whether to take I-80 north to San Francisco or I-40 south toward Los Angeles, since the freeways are hundreds of miles apart. If you were a subatomic particle, the decision of which freeway you took wouldn't actually be made until someone *sees* you in LA or SF. "Weird, isn't it?"
4. **Fred Alan Wolf's offer waves (Figure 26)** — physicist Fred Alan Wolf says information from possible futures comes to us in the present; we send an offer wave into the future that interacts with offer waves coming back; which future we navigate to depends on our choices (consciousness and observation) and how the waves superpose or cancel. Figure 26 depicts multiple probable futures sending back information we use to make decisions. Virk calls the implication — future probable selves sending information back, us consciously choosing the path — startling and common-sense-defying, and notes the mathematics doesn't answer whether these futures really exist.
5. **Schrödinger's cat under MWI** — the paradox dissolves: the cat is now both alive and dead, "just as the experiments confirm, but in two different universes." We observe only one state (Virk: "let's be nice and say it's alive") because we happen to be in the universe/timeline with that outcome.
6. **DeWitt popularizes "many worlds"** — neither Everett nor Wheeler used the phrase; theoretical physicist Bryce DeWitt popularized "many-worlds interpretation" in popular articles in the 1960s and 1970s, with a famous explanation Virk says could have been written by a science fiction writer (see Key quotes).
7. **Multiverse in superhero cinema** — evidence the idea went fully mainstream: *Doctor Strange in the Multiverse of Madness* and the series *Loki* in the Marvel cinematic universe; *Spider-Man: No Way Home* putting the three most recent Spider-Man actors (Tobey Maguire, Andrew Garfield, Tom Holland) in one movie; *The Flash* bringing Michael Keaton back as the "original" Batman. Virk: the multiverse is "the ultimate storytelling device," allowing almost infinite variations of timelines and histories.
8. **The grandfather paradox and its multiverse escape** — travel back and kill your grandfather before your parents are conceived and you'd never be born — so how did you go back? The multiverse interpretation: the act creates a new timeline (a parallel universe); you're never born in the second timeline, but you were in the first.
9. **The Terminator / Terminator 2 timelines** — in *The Terminator*, the general-purpose killer AI Skynet sends back an independent embodied autonomous AI (Arnold Schwarzenegger as the Terminator) to kill Sarah Connor so her son John Connor, future resistance leader, is never born — an even weirder grandfather paradox because Kyle Reese, whom the future John sends back to protect his mother, turns out to be John's father. In *Terminator 2: Judgment Day*, if Sarah succeeds in changing the future, a new timeline is spawned: Skynet doesn't start the war, doesn't send the Terminator, John never leads the resistance and never sends his father back — "a matrix of possibilities rather than any single timeline."
10. **Hawking's finite-universe theory** — the subject of one of Stephen Hawking's final papers: the number of parallel universes may not be infinite but limited to a smaller, finite number, based on a limited number of particle configurations and choice outcomes — implying some spawned universes are similar. Virk later uses this as "an easy path to optimization" in the computational picture.
11. **Eggs vs. toast** — Virk's macro-level illustration of "substantially similar" universes: deciding on eggs rather than toast today, then eggs tomorrow, yields universes similar enough from your perspective — especially compared with the universe where you married someone else, lived in another country, or were never born.
12. **The Multiverse Graph (Figure 27)** — branching parallel universes form a directed graph, a "future map" for an entity starting at point A with two possible outcomes (two, because of quantum superposition; generalizable). Each choice branches two universes, and so on — but some paths reconverge: you can get from point A to point C by two different paths and different sets of choices. Defined as the "Multiverse Graph" in Virk's book *The Simulated Multiverse* — a way to think about possible futures *and possible pasts*. At a higher level it raises free will vs. predetermination and whether the parallel universes/future selves actually exist or are just probabilities — exactly the metaphysical questions Everett and Wheeler hoped MWI would avoid.
13. **Fringe (TV, 2008–2013)** — the best-known recent fictional parallel world. FBI agent Olivia Dunham enlists mad scientist Walter Bishop (who begins the series in a mental institution) and his son Peter. Walter and his partner Dr. William Bell (Leonard Nimoy, in one of his last roles before his death in 2015) discovered how to *view* an alternate universe via a screen functioning as a window. When Walter's son Peter dies of a rare disease, Walter crosses over to cure the "other" Peter and return him — but fails to complete the return trip; Peter's absence from his home universe becomes a major plot point in both universes. Virk praises Fringe for depicting a world like ours with subtle and not-so-subtle differences: the alternates (fan-named Walternate and Fauxlivia) look identical but have very different personalities from different experiences and histories.
14. **Monte Carlo simulations** — a computer can simulate a large number of possibilities very quickly — one of the major ideas behind running simulations; Monte Carlo runs many random possibilities to find the most likely emergent scenarios. Frames the question "Is there a limit to the number of physical universes?" as more palatable if reality is simulated.
15. **Virk's MIT class-project game AI and minimax (Figure 28)** — the Multiverse Graph reminded Virk of the AI he built for a class project video game back at MIT: the computer projects possible futures, ranks them with an evaluation function, brings the values back to the present, and chooses the path with the best value — the minimax algorithm, a generalized version of Claude Shannon's algorithm for one of the first chess-playing computers (described in Chapter 5). Virk's punchline questions: Did those calculated futures actually exist (spun up, then spun down) or were they just probabilities? "Does the question even matter in a simulated universe—is there any difference?"
16. **The evaluation-function problem for our universe** — simple in chess/checkers, but: is some AI or part of us spinning off possible futures in the quantum or macro realm and evaluating them? Doing so requires representing the state of the universe as information and computing it — "the more we delve into the quantum realm, the more we are reminded of information and computation." If the "game state" is the state of all particles, a computer program can vary and evaluate it easily. For a multiplayer shared online simulation, the evaluation depends on the nature of the "game," how score is kept, what the runners define as optimal — and different players may have different evaluation functions. Virk defers "an elegant answer from well beyond the realm of physics" to the chapters on Eastern mystics, karma, and reincarnation.
17. **Campbell's Fundamental Process and profitability function** — physicist and former NASA scientist Thomas Campbell has advocated that we live in a virtual reality as part of his "big TOE" since *My Big TOE* (2003). His Fundamental Process drives evolution of consciousness, biology, and inanimate objects: try every possibility by trial and error, gravitate to the "most profitable" states. Profit criteria: biological (carbon-based) entities = survival (aligned with natural evolution); non-biological = least energy (easiest/cheapest state); conscious entities = reducing entropy, moving toward greater order. The process is recursive, continuing while profitable states remain, at all levels — subatomic, cellular, consciousness, universe. Virk: Campbell's universe that spins up possible outcomes, evaluates them with a profitability function, and picks the most profitable "reminded me of the minimax algorithm" and how simple video game AIs choose paths.
18. **Modern neural-net game AIs** — today's game AIs are more sophisticated: rather than projecting futures in real time, they are trained on large numbers of games — effectively trained on existing configurations of all the possible futures, assigning weights to measure the best possible future. The more games neural net–based AIs play, the better they get — "as if they are projecting timelines across the Multiverse Graph as part of their training."
19. **Why physical copying fails: the cloning analogy** — for MWI copies to be physically real, something would have to copy every particle in the universe at each decision and let the new universe run — the main scientific objection: not efficient, not parsimonious. Biology's closest analogy, cloning, doesn't help: full clones of a sheep or a tree, unlike single cells, take time and must be grown, not made instantaneously. The complexity of cloning a universe with every particle in the exact same position is astronomical and "doesn't fit well into any known physical, biological, or chemical process."
20. **Why computational copying succeeds: bitmaps and GPUs** — branching and copying are common structures in computation. Early video games defined the world as pixels laid out as bitmaps (per the previous chapter); showing part of the world on screen was just copying pixels from disk to memory (per Chapter 2) — near-instantaneous. GPUs and CPUs evolved functions optimized for the task; it's "not at all unreasonable" that universe-copying has optimizations too — and that what's copied is information, not physical particles, existing digitally in "some kind of cyberspace," rendered only when necessary.
21. **Image compression / night sky** — because many particles in copied universes share similar configurations, storage shrinks — the same optimization used in computer graphics: most images compress because many pixels are the same (in a night-sky photo, most pixels are black). Analogously, the only way to have so many universes may be optimizing on the *sameness of universes* — which are near each other, which branch out — pruning the tree so infinite branching and infinite computation are never required.
22. **The three-mechanism argument (Branching / Optimization / Retrocausation)** — Virk's synthesis of how simulation better models reality: **Branching** — spinning off probable realities is trivial for a simulation, true under both the "real" and "unreal" readings of many worlds; in a simulated universe things are "real only when rendered, and exist as information otherwise." **Optimization** — with fewer-than-infinite universes, creation and storage become a computation problem already solved for video games (sameness of pixels, tiling, reducing duplication, caching). **Retrocausation** — future-influences-past is nonintuitive and allows for paradoxes, but becomes "a more understandable process" if the futures are *possible* rather than actual, sending back information based on an evaluation function.
23. **Goswami's 100 cats and the meaning of probability** — probabilities can exist only if you run a process more than once: a 50 percent coin or a 1-in-52 ace of spades means nothing for a single toss or draw. Physicist Amit Goswami's example: with 100 cats, 50 alive and 50 dead, you can assign probability. So where are the other 99 cats, coins, cards? Copenhagen: they never really existed. Standard multiverse: they're in other physical universes. Simulated multiverse: they are "other runs" of the simulation — information, not necessarily rendered, not "physical" until that possibility is part of the game being played. This escapes the parsimony objection and offers a track toward reconciling the two interpretations.
24. **Pointer to *The Simulated Multiverse*** — Virk notes he explores time, multiple timelines, and universes in far more depth in *The Simulated Multiverse: An MIT Computer Scientist Explores Parallel Worlds, the Simulation Hypothesis, Quantum Computing and the Mandela Effect*, including what computational processes a simulated multiverse would require and the "weird side effect" that there may be multiple possible *pasts*, not just futures.

## Thread
Chapter 7 established quantum indeterminacy and the Copenhagen collapse; this chapter escalates to the two stranger consequences — delayed choice (future selecting past) and many worlds (every choice spawning universes) — and shows both are physically awkward but computationally natural. The pivot is Virk's video-game-developer lens: the branching Multiverse Graph *is* the game AI's move tree, Wolf's offer waves and Campbell's Fundamental Process *are* evaluation/profitability functions, and Hawking's finite universes *are* a compression opportunity. The chapter lands on the recurring Part II pattern: "what seem like unexplainable findings of quantum physics start to make more sense when we consider them in the context of the simulation hypothesis." It closes by handing off to the next chapter — the remaining physics topics that make more sense "when we view reality as a pixelated simulation rather than a continuous physical reality" — while explicitly deferring the *whose evaluation function?* question to the later chapters on Eastern mystics, karma, and reincarnation.
