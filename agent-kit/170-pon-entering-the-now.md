# Entering the Now

## In one paragraph

In this chapter Eckhart Tolle turns from describing the mind and the Now to the practical work of actually entering the Now. He instructs the reader to break the habit of present-moment denial by withdrawing attention from past and future whenever they are not practically needed, and — when direct entry is hard — to observe the mind's habitual escape into imagined futures, since the very act of observation brings in a "witnessing presence" that is not of the mind. He then draws his central distinction between "clock time" (the practical use of past and future) and "psychological time" (identification with the past and compulsive projection into the future), and between one's "life situation" (mind-stuff, existing in time) and one's "life" (real, existing now). Tolle argues that all problems are illusions of the mind — it is impossible to have a problem when attention is fully in the Now — and offers the joy, ease, and lightness of what one is doing as the test of whether psychological time has taken over. The chapter closes by describing the timeless state of consciousness and the back-and-forth process by which presence gradually becomes one's predominant state.

## Core claims

- Tolle claims that with the timeless dimension comes a different kind of knowing that does not "kill" the spirit that lives within every creature and thing, and that does not destroy the sacredness and mystery of life but contains a deep love and reverence for all that is — a knowing of which the mind knows nothing.
- Tolle claims the future is usually imagined as either better than the present (producing hope or pleasurable anticipation) or worse (producing anxiety), and that both are illusory.
- He claims that "the moment you realize you are not present, you are present": whenever you can observe your mind, you are no longer trapped in it, because a witnessing presence — something not of the mind — has come in.
- He claims identification with the mind gives it more energy and creates more time, while observation of the mind withdraws energy from it and opens the dimension of the timeless; the withdrawn energy turns into presence.
- He claims presence does not impair the practical use of time or of the mind — it enhances it, making the mind sharper and more focused; the enlightened person's main focus is always the Now while remaining peripherally aware of time — they continue to use clock time but are free of psychological time.
- He claims clock time turns into psychological time when a goal is used as a source of happiness, fulfillment, or a more complete sense of self, reducing the Now to a mere stepping-stone with no intrinsic value.
- He claims that in the normal, mind-identified state, life loses its vibrancy, freshness, and sense of wonder: old patterns of thought, emotion, behavior, reaction, and desire are acted out in "endless repeat performances," a script in the mind that gives an identity of sorts but distorts or covers up the reality of the Now — and the mind then creates an obsession with the future as an escape from the unsatisfactory present.
- He claims that what you perceive as future is an intrinsic part of your state of consciousness now; the past perpetuates itself through lack of presence, and the quality of your consciousness at this moment — determined by your degree of presence — shapes the future. The Now is therefore the only place where true change can occur and where the past can be dissolved.
- He claims that until the mind's basic problem-making dysfunction — attachment to past and future and denial of the Now — is dealt with, problems are interchangeable: remove them all miraculously today and, without more presence, a similar set would soon reappear "like a shadow that follows you wherever you go." Ultimately there is only one problem: the time-bound mind itself.
- He claims "there is no salvation in time. You cannot be free in the future" — presence is the key to freedom, so you can only be free now.
- He claims what people call their "life" is really their "life situation" (psychological time: past and future): certain things in the past didn't go the way you wanted them to go, you are still resisting what happened in the past, and now you are resisting what is. Hope keeps you going but keeps you focused on the future, perpetuating denial of the Now and therefore unhappiness.
- He claims it is impossible to have a problem when your attention is fully in the Now: a situation needs to be either dealt with or accepted, and the mind unconsciously loves problems because they give you an identity of sorts — "this is normal, and it is insane."
- He claims that if you create no more pain for yourself, you create no more pain for others, and no longer contaminate the Earth, your inner space, and the collective human psyche with the negativity of problem making.
- He claims action arising out of present-moment awareness is clear, incisive, and more likely to be effective — an intuitive response rather than a reaction from past conditioning — and that in other instances it is more effective to do nothing and stay centered in the Now.
- He claims "how" is always more important than "what": full attention to the doing implies complete acceptance of what is, because you cannot give full attention to something and resist it at the same time.
- He claims that in the absence of psychological time the sense of self derives from Being, not the personal past, so the need to become anything other than who you already are disappears — in the deeper dimension of Being you are complete and whole now, and neither failure nor success can change your inner state.
- He claims that being free of time — free of the psychological need of the past for identity and the future for fulfillment — represents the most profound transformation of consciousness imaginable, and that after first glimpses one shifts back and forth between presence and mind identification until presence eventually becomes the predominant state.

## Concepts

- **the Now** — the present moment, which Tolle calls the "narrow gate that leads to life"; the only place where true change can occur, where the past can be dissolved, and where freedom is possible. Its power and infinite creative potential are obscured by psychological time in the ordinary mind-identified state.
- **witnessing presence** — the factor "that is not of the mind" which comes in whenever you are able to observe your mind; the still, observing presence itself behind the content of the mind, also called "the silent watcher." Its arrival means you are no longer trapped in the mind.
- **watching the thinker / self-observation** — the practice of being present as the watcher of your mind — thoughts, emotions, and reactions — without judging or analyzing what you observe. Through self-observation, more presence comes into your life automatically.
- **unconsciousness** — the state in which a reaction or emotion takes you over so that you "become" it and act it out (justifying, making wrong, attacking, defending); Tolle says it isn't you but the reactive pattern, "the mind in its habitual survival mode." The tendency arises especially when the self-image is threatened, fear is triggered, things "go wrong," or an emotional complex from the past is brought up.
- **mind identification** — identification with the mind, which gives the mind more energy and creates more time; contrasted with observation of the mind, which withdraws energy from it and opens the timeless dimension.
- **clock time** — the practical use of time: setting goals, working toward them, referring to past or future for practical matters, while giving fullest attention to the present step and returning to present-moment awareness once practical matters are dealt with.
- **psychological time** — identification with the past and continuous compulsive projection into the future; what clock time becomes when the goal is sought as a source of happiness, fulfillment, or self. Tolle presents it as the cause of suffering and the substance of the "life situation."
- **time-bound mind** — the mind attached to past and future and in denial of the Now; Tolle calls it "the basic problem-making dysfunction of the mind" and says ultimately it is the only problem.
- **life situation vs. life** — "life situation" is what people mistakenly call their life: psychological time, past and future, mind-stuff, usually full of problems. "Life" is what is underneath it: real, and now. "Your life situation exists in time. Your life is now."
- **problem** — in Tolle's definition, "dwelling on a situation mentally without there being a true intention or possibility of taking action now" while unconsciously making it part of one's sense of self. The mind loves problems because they give an identity of sorts; a situation, by contrast, needs only to be dealt with or accepted.
- **the "no more pain" choice** — the simple but radical decision: "No matter what happens, I will create no more pain for myself. I will create no more problems." Tolle says you won't make it unless truly fed up with suffering, and can't go through with it without accessing the power of the Now.
- **intuitive response** — action arising out of present-moment awareness rather than as a reaction from the mind's past conditioning; clear, incisive, and more likely to be effective.
- **joy of Being** — the joy that flows into everything you do when compulsive striving away from the Now ceases; felt as presence, stillness, and peace, with no dependence on the future for fulfillment or salvation.
- **the joy, ease, and lightness criterion** — a simple test for whether psychological time has taken over: "Is there joy, ease, and lightness in what I am doing?" If not, time is covering up the present moment and life is perceived as a burden or a struggle — but the remedy may be changing the "how," not the "what."
- **non-attachment to the fruit of action** — the practice of not being concerned with the fruit of your action, giving attention only to the action itself; "the fruit will come of its own accord." Tolle calls this a powerful spiritual practice.
- **Being** — the deeper dimension of self from which identity derives in the absence of psychological time; in Being you are complete and whole now, whatever you become on the level of your life situation.
- **isness** — the quality of things simply being as they are; the sensory practice culminates in allowing "the 'isness' of all things," within and without.
- **timeless state of consciousness / freedom from time** — the state in which every cell of the body is so present it feels vibrant with life, felt each moment as the joy of Being; freedom from the psychological need of the past for identity and the future for fulfillment. Tolle calls it the most profound transformation of consciousness imaginable.
- **the timeless dimension** — the dimension that brings "a different kind of knowing," one that does not destroy the sacredness and mystery of life but contains deep love and reverence for all that is; a knowing of which the mind knows nothing.

## Experiments, examples & case studies

- **Practice: break the old pattern.** Instruction: withdraw attention from past and future whenever they are not needed; step out of the time dimension as much as possible in everyday life. Intended effect: ends present-moment denial and resistance.
- **Practice: observe the mind's escape from the Now.** For those who find it hard to enter the Now directly: observe the habitual tendency of the mind to want to escape from the Now, noticing that the imagined future is pictured as better (hope, pleasurable anticipation) or worse (anxiety) than the present — both illusory. Intended effect: self-observation automatically brings more presence; "the moment you realize you are not present, you are present."
- **Practice: be the watcher of your mind.** Instruction: be present as the watcher of thoughts, emotions, and reactions in various situations; be at least as interested in your reactions as in the situation or person that causes them; notice how often attention is in past or future; don't judge or analyze — watch the thought, feel the emotion, observe the reaction, without making a personal problem of them. Intended effect: you come to feel the still, observing presence behind the mind's content — the silent watcher.
- **Practice: intense presence under emotional charge.** When situations trigger a strong emotional reaction — threatened self-image, fear-triggering challenge, things "going wrong," an old emotional complex surfacing — the tendency is to become "unconscious" and be taken over by the reactive pattern. Intense presence is needed precisely then, so the reaction does not take you over.
- **Practice: clock time with full attention to the present step.** Example of right goal-use: you set a goal and work toward it, aware of where you want to go, but honoring and giving fullest attention to the step you are taking at this moment. Counter-example: becoming excessively focused on the goal as a source of happiness or self — the Now becomes a mere stepping-stone, the journey "an obsessive need to arrive," and "you no longer see or smell the flowers by the wayside."
- **Self-diagnostic questions on psychological time.** A battery of questions Tolle poses: Are you always trying to get somewhere other than where you are? Is most of your doing just a means to an end? Is fulfillment always just around the corner or confined to short-lived pleasures such as sex, food, drink, drugs, or thrills? Are you always focused on becoming, achieving, and attaining, or alternatively chasing some new thrill or pleasure? Do you believe acquiring more things will make you fulfilled, good enough, or psychologically complete? Are you waiting for a man or woman to give meaning to your life? Intended effect: exposing one's own entrapment in psychological time.
- **Thought experiment: the shadow of problems.** If all your problems or perceived causes of suffering were miraculously removed today but you had not become more present, a similar set would soon reappear, "like a shadow that follows you wherever you go" — showing that problems are interchangeable and the one real problem is the time-bound mind itself.
- **Practice/inquiry: "Do you have a problem now?"** Instruction: forget your life situation for a while and pay attention to your life; narrow your life down to this moment and find out whether you have any problem at this moment — not tomorrow or in ten minutes, but now. Intended effect: reveals the life underneath the life situation and makes room for something new — "when you are full of problems, there is no room for anything new to enter, no room for a solution."
- **Practice: use your senses fully.** A step-by-step sensory meditation: Be where you are. Look around — just look, don't interpret; see the light, shapes, colors, textures; be aware of the silent presence of each thing and of the space that allows everything to be. Listen to the sounds without judging them; listen to the silence underneath the sounds. Touch something — anything — and feel and acknowledge its Being. Observe the rhythm of your breathing; feel the air flowing in and out and the life energy inside your body. Allow everything to be, within and without; allow the "isness" of all things. Intended effect: moving deeply into the Now, leaving "the deadening world of mental abstraction, of time," and awakening "out of the dream of time into the present."
- **Q&A exchange: no problem in the Now.** Tolle challenges a questioner: "Focus your attention on the Now and tell me what problem you have at this moment." He notes, "I am not getting any answer because it is impossible to have a problem when your attention is fully in the Now" — a situation needs to be either dealt with or accepted; why make it into a problem? The silence demonstrates his claim that all problems are illusions of the mind.
- **Practice: the radical choice to create no more pain.** Instruction: make the simple decision "No matter what happens, I will create no more pain for myself. I will create no more problems." Conditions: it requires being truly fed up with suffering and accessing the power of the Now. Intended effect: no more pain for yourself means no more pain for others, and no further contamination of the Earth, your inner space, and the collective human psyche with the negativity of problem making.
- **Practice: the joy, ease, lightness check.** Instruction: ask yourself, "Is there joy, ease, and lightness in what I am doing?" If not, psychological time is covering up the present moment. Follow-up: you may not need to change what you are doing, only the how — give more attention to the doing than to the desired result, and full attention to whatever the moment presents. Intended effect: unhappiness and struggle dissolve; even the simplest action becomes imbued with quality, care, and love.
- **Practice: attention to the action, not the fruit.** Instruction: do not be concerned with the fruit of your action — give attention to the action itself; the fruit will come of its own accord. Tolle names this "a powerful spiritual practice."
- **Illustration: the arc of first glimpses.** After the first few glimpses of the timeless state, one moves back and forth between time and presence: first becoming aware of how rarely attention is truly in the Now (a knowing that is itself presence, even if it lasts only "a couple of seconds of clock time"), then choosing the present moment with increasing frequency and staying in it for longer periods, shifting between consciousness and unconsciousness — losing the Now and returning to it, again and again — until presence becomes the predominant state.

## Key quotes

- "The moment you realize you are not present, you are present." — the core mechanism of self-observation: awareness of absence is itself presence.
- "Identification with the mind gives it more energy; observation of the mind withdraws energy from it." — the energetic logic behind watching the thinker.
- "They continue to use clock time but are free of psychological time." — Tolle's description of the enlightened person's relation to time.
- "You no longer see or smell the flowers by the wayside either, nor are you aware of the beauty and the miracle of life that unfolds all around you when you are present in the Now." — the cost of turning clock time into psychological time.
- "Ultimately, there is only one problem: the time-bound mind itself." — the conclusion of the thought experiment about miraculously removed problems.
- "There is no salvation in time. You cannot be free in the future." — the pivot to presence as the sole key to freedom.
- "Your life situation exists in time. Your life is now. Your life situation is mind-stuff. Your life is real." — the chapter's central distinction, stated in parallel.
- "It is impossible to have a problem when your attention is fully in the Now." — from the Q&A exchange in which the questioner cannot name a present problem.
- "No matter what happens, I will create no more pain for myself. I will create no more problems." — the simple but radical choice Tolle proposes.
- "'How' is always more important than 'what.'" — the remedy when joy, ease, and lightness are missing from what one is doing.
- "Do not be concerned with the fruit of your action — just give attention to the action itself. The fruit will come of its own accord." — the practice Tolle calls powerful spiritual practice.
- "You lose the Now, and you return to it, again and again. Eventually, presence becomes your predominant state." — the closing description of how presence is established.

## Thread

This chapter is the practical hinge of Part One: Accessing the Power of Now. Chapters 1–2 (on not being the mind, and on consciousness and the pain-body) establish what the mind and psychological time are; here Tolle converts that diagnosis into a repertoire of entry techniques — self-observation, watching the thinker, sensory immersion, the "problem now?" inquiry, the joy/ease/lightness check, and attention to action over fruit. Its key conceptual contributions — clock time vs. psychological time, life vs. life situation, and the claim that all problems are illusions of the time-bound mind — are load-bearing for the rest of the book: the "deal with it or accept it" logic prefigures Part Three's treatment of acceptance and surrender (chapters 8–9), and the account of shifting between presence and mind identification until presence predominates sets up the later chapters' work on unconsciousness in ordinary life and in relationships (Part Two). The chapter also introduces the phrase "the narrow gate that leads to life" as a name for the Now.
