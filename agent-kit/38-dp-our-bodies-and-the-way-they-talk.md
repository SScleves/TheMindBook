# Our Bodies and the Way They Talk

## In one paragraph
This chapter is William Cooper's survey of body language (kinesics) as a channel of nonverbal communication that runs alongside — and sometimes contradicts — spoken words. He opens by distinguishing "open" from "closed" body language as a master frame for reading receptiveness, then walks through what he presents as positive gestures (relaxed limbs, open palms, measured speech pace, dilated pupils, leaning forward) and negative gestures (leaning away, crossed limbs, tight shoulders, feet turned away, cradle/catapult hand positions). He then works region by region — torso, neck, shoulders, chest, hips, hands, legs — cataloguing the specific signals each body part is said to send, mostly framed around dominance/submission, comfort/discomfort, and interest/disinterest. The chapter closes by reiterating that these cues appear consciously or unconsciously and let an observer assess what someone is really feeling even when it contradicts their words.

## Core claims
- William Cooper claims body language (kinesics) is nonverbal communication conveyed through posture, facial expressions, eye movement, gestures, and use of space, and that interpretations of specific cues are broadly "agreed-upon" even though they can vary by country.
- He claims some (unnamed) researchers concluded that unspoken cues account for a significant part of the information relayed in interpersonal interactions.
- He argues body language should stay in sync with spoken words; when voice and body conflict, the speaker appears deceptive and untrustworthy.
- He claims open body language (hand use, expressiveness, interactivity) reflects a receptive personality better able to process information, while closed body language (crossed arms/legs, hands kept close, monotone voice) reflects hidden motives and unreceptiveness to new ideas.
- He claims mastering positive posture and gestures can change how others perceive you, including in high-stakes moments like job interviews.
- He states pupils react unconsciously to stimuli and "cannot be manipulated or controlled," so pupil dilation (interest/pleasure, up to four times normal size when excited) versus constriction (anger/negative mood) is a reliable emotional tell.
- He claims a speaking pace of 140-160 words per minute is ideal; faster speech loses the audience, while overly slow speech can make the speaker seem incompetent or uneducated, though slowing down is advisable across accents or for foreign speakers.
- He asserts the torso, because it is under limited conscious control, is a particularly good indicator of true body-language signals (e.g., turning the torso away signals a wish to leave, feeling threatened, or disengagement).
- He frames the neck as a vulnerable target ("where a predator will aim, either to rip out the windpipe, or penetrate the jugular artery"), so protective neck movements (pulling down the chin, raising the shoulders) are read as reactions to a perceived threat.
- He presents shoulder, chest, hip, hand, and leg positions as each carrying a specific, catalogued set of meanings tied largely to dominance, submission, comfort, and attraction/aggression.
- He closes by claiming these cues occur consciously or unconsciously depending on context, and that learning to read and use them lets a person assess true feelings — even when they contradict spoken words — and connect with others more effectively.

## Concepts
- **body language / kinesics** — nonverbal communication using physical behaviors (posture, facial expressions, eye movement, gestures, use of space) to convey information; interpretation of specific cues is described as broadly agreed-upon, with some cross-country variance.
- **nonverbal communication** — the broader channel of nonverbal communication that runs alongside spoken words, able to reiterate, reinforce, or contradict the verbal message.
- **open body language** — a posture style (hands used while speaking, interactive, expressive, limbs uncrossed) tied to a receptive personality better able to process information.
- **closed body language** — a posture style (crossed legs/arms, hands kept close to the body, monotone voice) tied to hidden motives and unreceptiveness to new ideas.
- **positive bodily gestures** — the author's catalogue of movements generally understood to indicate favorable, engaged, or trustworthy states (relaxed limbs, open palms, measured speech, leaning forward, etc.).
- **relaxed/uncrossed limbs** — limbs that hang loosely and rarely cross except for comfort; tense arms are held close and rigid while relaxed arms move smoothly; jerky hand movements show fear/tension while stillness is natural; legs may be loosely crossed but "not wound around each other," with wrapped/tense legs revealing hidden tension even when the upper body appears relaxed.
- **open palms** — a family of three palm gestures (closed fist, palms down, palms up), each said to relay a different attitude.
- **palms down** — a gesture showing dominance and authority, signaling control of the conversation; can be used to non-verbally ask someone to wait or to "create a wall" between speaker and listener; intensity scales with the forcefulness/incline of the motion.
- **palms up** — a gesture signaling honesty, trustworthiness, and nothing to hide (explaining why hiding one's hands looks suspicious); can also signal submissiveness, useful for gaining trust and getting a request accepted as a favor rather than an order.
- **pointing gesture** — used as a teaching/counting tool since childhood, but pointing at people (especially with the thumb) is described as rude, read as ridicule or accusation, best avoided except to provoke.
- **speed of speech** — pacing of spoken words, said to shape perceived meaning, intent, credibility, impact, and emotional state; the author gives 140-160 words per minute as a good pace, notes accents/regional inflection may require slowing down, and warns that speech that is too slow can make a speaker seem incompetent or uneducated.
- **pupil size** — an unconscious, uncontrollable ocular signal; dilation (up to 4x normal size) tracks excitement/interest/pleasure and the wish to "take in more of the world," while constriction tracks anger or negative mood and the wish to limit information flow; also used to distinguish interest/attraction from hostility/anger when someone holds a gaze.
- **eyebrow lift** — described as "a long-distance equivalent of 'hello'"; a brief rise used to draw attention.
- **leaning forward** — a signal of engagement, attentiveness, and trust-building; shows willingness to cooperate and that the leaner is not a threat; described as a universal sign of listening/respecting the other party's words.
- **negative bodily gestures** — the author's catalogue of movements that give a bad impression, detectable by others' subconscious even without formal body-language training (leaning away, crossed limbs, tight shoulders, feet turned away, cradle/catapult, head-on-hand).
- **leaning away (leaning out)** — signals disinterest in the person, their words, or their ideas; relevant in dating, business, and friendship contexts; also valid standing, where it signals being "closed off"; leaning back specifically can also display dominance, while pointing the body toward the conversation signals submissiveness.
- **crossed limbs** — a category covering crossed arms and crossed legs, read (consciously or unknowingly) by others as meaningful nonverbal signals.
- **crossed arms** — read as insecurity, distance, defensiveness, anxiety, or stubbornness.
- **crossed legs** — sitting with legs crossed ankle-over-knee signals dominance and confidence (described as predominantly male but increasingly used by women).
- **ankle lock** — crossing the legs at the ankle while seated; can mean uncertainty, holding back, or fearfulness.
- **tight shoulders** — muscular constriction around the neck caused by anxiety/distaste; hunched shoulders with folded/crossed arms can signal feeling cold or extreme discomfort; raising shoulders and lowering the head is an instinctive protective reaction to a feared attack.
- **feet turned away** — a leg/foot-positioning signal; feet pointed toward someone suggests liking, interest, and agreement, while feet turned away suggests a wish to leave, "as if they are already walking away."
- **leaning on two hands / head resting on one hand** — hand-support postures that can have mundane physical causes (fatigue, stretching) but are read for unconscious meaning; hands placed behind the head while leaning back forms either the "cradle" or the "catapult" depending on palm/arm placement.
- **cradle position** — hands behind the head, one variant of the leaning-back posture, signaling insecurity and a need for comfort.
- **catapult position** — hands behind the head, the other variant of the leaning-back posture, signaling dominance and aggression.
- **head resting on one hand** — signals disinterest in the current situation and boredom.
- **torso body language** — the trunk of the body (from Latin "thyrsus," meaning stalk); flexible but under limited conscious control, which the author says makes it a particularly honest indicator; turning the torso away can signal a wish to leave, feeling threatened (protecting vital organs), disengagement, defensiveness, insecurity, being in a hurry, or hiding something.
- **neck body language** — signals sent via the neck, including "hiding" (pulling the chin down or raising the shoulders to protect the throat/jugular from a perceived predator/attacker, largely unconscious) and "turning" (rotating the neck to extend vision, to deliberately give or remove attention, or — when done as exercise — to signal tension or boredom).
- **shoulders body language** — a set of shoulder signals: raised shoulders with lowered head (protecting the neck from feared attack, discomfort/unease); curved forward shoulders (folded arms, reduced body width, defensive posture, desire to be unseen); pushed back shoulders (chest exposed, showing lack of fear and dominance, a stance assumed before a challenge); circling shoulders (exercising tension/anxiety, or signaling preparation for combat/aggression, or a power display when done while another person is talking); shrug (a one-off raise-and-lower usually meaning "I don't know," possibly with raised eyebrows; a quick subconscious shrug can indicate lack of understanding/uncertainty; shrugging instead of speaking can indicate lying; an animated, prolonged shrug can indicate aggression-readiness or frustration/irritation); turning (rotating the shoulders while still looking at the other person, signaling a wish to leave/end the conversation).
- **chest body language** — chest signals: thrust out (attracts attention, can display romantic interest — noted for women drawing on male arousal to breasts — or, in men, displaying strength for both attraction and aggression, e.g., "I am strong and can protect you" versus "I am strong and had better not cross me"); withdrawn (protecting vital organs, signaling a wish to hide, appear inoffensive, or insecurity); leaning (toward another person can show romantic interest, or can invade personal space as an aggressive, dominance-showing threat).
- **hips body language** — hip signals: thrust out (a provocative/suggestive gesture involving primary sexual organs, or used to mock/taunt); held back (hiding the genitals, a defensive, "uneasy" posture); moving (side-to-side movement in dance signaling carefree/fun/sensual attitude and attracting attention/flirtation; back-and-forth movement simulating intercourse, read as arousing).
- **hands body language** — hand signals: holding (cupped hands symbolizing a fragile idea being held, or giving an object; gripping indicating ownership/possessiveness/desire; self-touch such as holding one's own hands as self-comfort or restraint, e.g., to stop oneself from attacking when angry, with more forceful gestures indicating more intense emotion); control (handshakes — prolonged, forceful holding asserts dominance; a floppy, palm-up handshake with quick withdrawal signals submission; a vertical-palm handshake with simultaneous withdrawal signals equality); "when asking" (palms upward as a plea gesture, palms downward asking the other person to calm down, pressed-together fingers-upward "prayer position" indicating anxious pleading, touching the chin during this indicating thoughtfulness or unease); rubbing (rubbing hands together can indicate feeling cold, or — when tense — anxiety/stress and fidgetiness; touching the chin can indicate evaluating, deciding, or thinking).
- **prayer position** — pressing one's hands together with fingers pointing upward, indicating a more anxious form of pleading than a simple upturned palm.
- **leg body language** — leg/stance signals: open standing (feet about shoulder-width apart, a relaxed, normal pose; a wider stance signals dominance/power by making one appear bigger); closed standing (feet together, displaying anxiety and a desire to take up less space); crossed legs while standing (signals shyness, especially with hands behind the back and lowered head; an ankle-cross while seated with legs tucked under the seat can show concealed anxiety); moving (a crossed leg moved up and down indicates impatience; a leg swinging to music indicates enjoyment/relaxation); walking (a fast walk shows being in a hurry, a slow walk shows having time to spare, and an "unaffected"/deliberately normal walking speed indicates self-consciousness about how one is perceived).

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Job interview scenario**: the author offers a hypothetical of someone who "aced every question" in an interview yet is passed over for another candidate, suggesting the explanation may lie in the candidate's posture or gestures rather than their answers — illustrating why positive body language matters in professional settings.
- **Palms-down interruption example**: raising a hand with palms down/toward someone is given as a concrete technique to non-verbally ask someone to wait or stop interrupting, or to signal a metaphorical "wall" between speaker and listener.
- **Palms-up request example**: asking someone to do something with palms up is described as increasing the chance the person treats the request as a favor to grant rather than an order to resist.
- **Pointing with the thumb**: singled out as specifically rude, read as a gesture of ridicule or accusation, distinguished from ordinary pointing used in teaching/counting.
- **Speech-pace numbers**: the author gives a concrete range — 140-160 words per minute — as the effective speaking pace, with anything higher risking audience comprehension, and notes regional/accented speech may warrant slowing down further.
- **Pupil dilation magnitude**: the author states pupils can dilate "up to four times the normal size" when a person is excited, offered as the measurable extreme of the pupil-size cue.
- **Eyebrow-lift-as-greeting**: described as functioning like a distant "hello," used to draw attention when two people are too far apart for words.
- **Dating/business/friends leaning-away example**: the author walks through the same "leaning away" cue across three relationship contexts — a partner on an outing (propping toward them signals wish for intimacy, away signals inattention), a business meeting (sitting at the edge of your seat and leaning toward a presenter signals eagerness for information), and a lunch with friends (leaning forward while engaging portrays interest) — to show the gesture's meaning is consistent across settings, just inverted when reversed.
- **Partners' dominance-reading example**: the author suggests observing a pair of partners' relative leaning-back vs. body-pointing to determine "who is in control," treating leaning back as dominance and pointing the body toward the conversation as submissiveness.
- **Shoulder-tap flinch example**: imagining being approached and tapped on the shoulder by a stranger is used to illustrate the raised-shoulders "recoil from an unwanted advance" reaction.
- **Chest-thrust gender-specific example**: women pushing their chest forward is framed as potentially inviting intimacy or teasing (trading on male arousal to breasts); men pushing their chest forward is framed as a dual-purpose strength display, phrased in direct-quote form toward a mate ("I am strong and can protect you") versus toward a peer/rival ("I am strong and had better not cross me").
- **Hip movement in dance**: side-to-side hip movement to music is offered as an example of "carefree, fun or sensual" signaling that also attracts attention and invites flirtation, contrasted with front-to-back movement that simulates intercourse and reads as arousing.
- **Handshake variants**: three concrete handshake forms are contrasted — prolonged/forceful (dominance), floppy palm-up with quick withdrawal (submission), and vertical-palm with simultaneous withdrawal (equality) — as a compact case study in how a single social ritual encodes different power signals.
- **Feet-pointing example**: the author gives the direct behavioral read that feet pointed toward a conversational partner signal liking/interest/agreement, while feet turned away signal a wish to leave, "as if they are already walking away."

## Thread
This chapter sits within the book's broader project of equipping the reader to read (and, implicitly, to deploy) nonverbal signals of trust, dominance, and deception — a natural companion to chapters on deception detection, persuasion, and influence elsewhere in the book. It supplies a detailed, body-region-by-body-region vocabulary (torso, neck, shoulders, chest, hips, hands, legs) that other chapters on lie detection, seduction/attraction tactics, or power dynamics can draw on as building blocks. The recurring dominance/submission and comfort/discomfort axes established here (open vs. closed, palms down vs. up, leaning in vs. away, chest thrust vs. withdrawn) echo the book's larger interest in reading and managing social power, making this chapter a foundational "signals" reference rather than a self-contained persuasion technique.
