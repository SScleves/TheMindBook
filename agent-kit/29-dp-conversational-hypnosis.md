# Manipulation and Conversational Hypnosis

## In one paragraph
In this chapter William Cooper takes what he calls an "objective look" at hypnosis and its covert cousin, conversational hypnosis, presenting both how manipulators use it to gain an edge over victims and how readers can ward off unwanted advances. He first defines hypnosis (from the Greek for "to rest") as leading someone into a transformed, suggestion-receptive state via verbal instructions, distinguishes its legitimate therapeutic form (hypnotherapy) from covert use, and frames conversational hypnosis as dark psychology precisely because it happens without the target's knowledge or consent. He then connects the practice to NLP and to Milton Erickson's indirect, metaphor-based "Ericksonian Hypnosis." The core of the chapter is a four-step process — building rapport (via mirroring, language, and voice matching), securing the target's focus, inducing a trance-like state, and implanting the message — followed by a treatment of anchoring (types, designs, installation, tips). Cooper closes with a toolbox of specific conversational-hypnosis tactics: conversational postulates, hidden/embedded commands, analog marking, negative commands, and metaphor, each with sample scripts.

## Core claims
- Cooper argues that hypnosis itself "is not an evil or benign practice" — it is a neutral practice used both in psychotherapy and in dark manipulation, which is why manipulators have found practical uses for it.
- The book defines hypnosis as the ability to lead someone into a transformed (daydream-like) environment using verbal instructions so that they "respond much more to suggestions"; it is very distinct from sleep despite the Greek root meaning "to rest."
- Cooper says hypnosis accesses the subconscious mind so ideas and suggestions can be implanted, potentially producing actions "that go against the wishes of the individual" — in a manner of speaking, controlling others' willpower.
- Conversational hypnosis is "covert" hypnosis: it occurs without the target's knowledge and therefore without consent, which is why Cooper classes it as part of dark psychology.
- The manipulator resorts to it because the target "would not consciously agree" to the scheme; underhanded trickery is needed to secure compliance.
- Learning it has a two-fold intent, per Cooper: to recognize when it is used against you, and to gain tactics for your own daily life — with the warning that using them means "imposing your will on that of others."
- Ethically, Cooper holds that anything labeled "covert" isn't exactly ethical; conversational hypnosis is not officially sanctioned by any organization and is deemed unethical, though it "isn't necessarily illegal."
- Its effectiveness "largely depends on the mental strength of the target" — some people have greater resistance, making covert tactics largely ineffective on them.
- NLP, though generally seen as a means of helping people, can be used without consent — combined with subliminal messaging and "trigger" words to implant ideas and spur desired action.
- Cooper presents conversational hypnosis as a stepwise process: rapport → secure focus → trance-like state → implanting the message; the trance is a real brainwave phenomenon (flattened activity, deep focus) in which the conscious mind "doesn't put up much resistance, leaving the subconscious mind exposed."
- Erratic brainwave activity (e.g., under great stress) is not conducive to hypnosis; a deep trance is required or the implanting "won't be effective."
- The unconscious mind "does not understand denial" — there is no mental representation of the word "not" — which is what makes negative commands work.
- Citing researchers at Stanford University, the book claims metaphorical language conditions individual choices by activating brain areas involved in the emotional connotation of events, beyond awareness.

## Concepts
- **hypnosis** — Per Cooper, the ability to lead someone into a transformed (daydreaming-involving) environment using verbal instructions, which ensures they respond much more to suggestions; once achieved, the hypnotist may offer direct and indirect recommendations (e.g., to break habits like cigarettes, unhealthy diet, fears). A means of accessing the subconscious mind to implant ideas — usable, "in a manner of speaking," to control the willpower of others. The word comes from the Greek meaning "to rest," but hypnosis is very distinct from sleep.
- **hypnotherapy** — The therapeutic application hypnosis was turned into once proven effective at modifying behavior; used to help people break habits or adopt new ones by implanting "suggestions" in the subconscious mind.
- **conversational hypnosis (covert hypnosis)** — A term for "covert" hypnosis: hypnosis practiced without the knowledge — and therefore without the consent — of the target, which is why it is considered part of dark psychology. Its purpose is to access the target's subconscious to get them to comply with actions that may not accord with their beliefs or will.
- **Neuro-linguistic Programming (NLP)** — Previously introduced in the book as a means of influencing others to implant new behaviors or modify existing ones; generally seen as helping people, but usable without consent, in conjunction with subliminal messaging, to implant ideas.
- **trigger words** — Words used by NLP practitioners to spur the individual to act in the desired manner; Cooper says slogans are trigger words that "automatically signal the individual that it's time to do one thing or another."
- **Ericksonian Hypnosis** — Hypnosis named after the methods developed by Milton Erickson (whom Cooper calls "the father of hypnotherapy") in which hypnosis is used for therapeutic purposes; suggestions and indirect commands spur the individual to do, or not do, something — e.g., metaphors and stories used to transmit meaning to targets.
- **rapport** — The first step of conversational hypnosis: "a powerful manipulation tool" consisting of getting others to be comfortable around you so they trust you, built from a strong dose of non-verbal communication plus the right mix of verbal and para-verbal communication.
- **mirroring** — Copying the other person's moves (they cross their legs, you cross yours; hand under chin, same) to send a non-verbal signal that both parties are very similar, reducing resistance and creating a friendly environment; being too obvious makes you seem awkward and ruins the technique. Matching the target's volume, speed, and tone of voice is a mirroring technique that works particularly well in negotiations.
- **secure focus of targets** — The second step — the classic "you are getting sleepy" part, though practitioners often use less overt means (music, images, scents, textures) to capture attention; the objective is to get the target into a state of relaxation so hypnosis can begin.
- **trance-like state** — The state induced once relaxation is achieved; not the movie kind but a brainwave pattern in which activity "tends to flatten," indicating deep focus and concentration; the conscious mind puts up little resistance, leaving the subconscious exposed.
- **implanting the message** — The last step: "installing" the manipulator's message in the target's subconscious while the brain is in deep focus; can be overt (saying what targets should imbibe) or via suggestions and imagery, but always requires a deep state of relaxation.
- **anchoring** — Attaching a feeling or state to a stimulus in the unconscious mind so the stimulus later re-evokes the feeling (as an old song re-triggers past sensations); hypnotists have subjects relive a memory's emotions while performing an action (e.g., touching fingers together) so the action becomes the anchor, and the process can motivate self-improvement such as weight loss.
- **sensory acuity** — What you rely on when anchoring: being confident enough to know when someone is experiencing the feelings you want to evoke, so you can "fix" the anchor as the sensation rises to the top.
- **conversational postulates** — A means of directing conversation so you tell the other party what you expect without overtly telling them to do it — e.g., offering only choices that presuppose agreement, or "you don't want that, do you?", which implicitly tells them they don't want something while leaving the door open with "do you."
- **hidden command** — A linguistic model in which an instruction, instead of being given directly, is hidden inside a broader sentence, so it is received by the unconscious mind while going unnoticed by the conscious mind.
- **embedded command** — A hidden order within a broader statement; the most common type is the "call-to-action."
- **call-to-action** — A message containing elements that compel the target to act in a certain manner; "call now before it's too late" contains both an embedded command and a threat, and given the context the target may not even notice they are being commanded.
- **analog marking** — Identifying words in a sentence by verbal or non-verbal means — delays, changes in intonation or pitch, tone of voice, expressions, gestures. The conscious mind hears the whole material but only takes notice of the marked words; it "makes a sensitive mind confused and enables direct access to the unconscious mind." Hidden commands are particularly persuasive when combined with this "underlining by analogy."
- **negative commands** — A kind of reverse psychology exploiting the claim that the unconscious mind does not understand denial (no mental representation of "not"): you affirm what you want to occur, preceded by negation, so the conscious mind — rationally not receiving a command — doesn't filter or block it, while the unconscious does receive it.
- **metaphor** — Transferring the characteristics of one object (or person) onto another to emphasize the original meaning; the persuader using metaphor triggers changes emerging from the unconscious and emotionality rather than rationality — a covert way of painting a picture in interlocutors' minds without actually saying what you mean, leaving words open to interpretation so only attentive listeners pick up the clues.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Hollywood vampire hypnosis** — Classic films show vampires using mind-control power to hypnotize victims into compliance; Cooper uses this as the mythical depiction against which he contrasts hypnosis as "a valid practice."
- **Fast-food slogans** — Fast food chains use slogans on a continuous basis so that people feel compelled to consume this type of food; Cooper's illustration of trigger words / NLP in advertising.
- **Athletes' mantras and coaches** — Athletes repeat certain mantras over and over to condition mind and body to perform; coaches use phrases and specific words to train an athlete to perform a specific action. Cooper notes this isn't necessarily covert or unethical, but is often involuntary — the individual isn't fully aware of what the coach is doing.
- **Children's fairy tales** — Ericksonian example: the lesson in each children's story is made more digestible by embedding it in a fairy tale's "magical aura" — metaphors and stories transmitting meaning to targets.
- **"Sex sells" advertising** — Cooper calls the phrase a great example of Ericksonian Hypnosis: sexually appealing and suggestive material makes the mind more receptive to the message; an attractive model presents a product, reducing the likelihood the consumer rejects the advertising itself.
- **The old song in the car** — Anchoring illustration: hearing a track you hadn't heard in a long time re-evokes sensations from when you first heard it; the song became an anchor of those sentiments in the unconscious mind, so hearing it now "cause[s] the brain to enjoy it again."
- **The finger-touch anchor** — A hypnotist takes the subject into a particular memory, has them replicate the emotions felt, and simultaneously has them touch their fingers together; afterward, touching the fingertips together brings back the same happy feelings each time.
- **Weight-loss anchoring** — The hypnotist works with the subject to create a positive focus connected to a mental image — the subject picturing themselves "in a slim, sexy body"; re-imagining the picture stimulates the anchor and delivers the optimistic push. Cooper claims desire for weight loss "in hypnosis is significantly dramatically increased" compared with those who don't use it.
- **Romantic-setting anchoring** — As a relationship builds, "hot and fuzzy emotions" become attached to the person's face, so merely thinking of the face evokes the feelings; Cooper says you can deliberately use the anchoring mechanism to speed these feelings up.
- **The date double-bind script** — Sample conversational postulate: asking a girl he just met whether she'd like to go out "tonight or tomorrow night" assumes she'll agree to go out at all; likewise asking a friend whether he'd rather go to a restaurant or a fast-food restaurant assumes he has already agreed to dinner.
- **"You don't want that, do you?"** — Sample conversational postulate that implicitly tells the other party what they don't want while nominally inviting their reaction.
- **Hidden-command scripts** — Three examples with the command hidden in a broader sentence (commands italicized in the book): "I wonder if you'd like to collaborate with me."; "I don't know when you'll be buying our products."; "If you want to read my latest article, go ahead."
- **"Call now before it's too late"** — A call-to-action containing both an embedded command and a threat; the target takes it merely as sales persuasion, not noticing they are being commanded.
- **Negative-command scripts** — Three examples affirming the desired outcome behind a negation: "I'm not asking you to trust me."; "You don't have to decide right away. Take all the time you need."; "I'm not here to list all the advantages of our proposal."
- **Stanford metaphor research** — Cooper cites "researchers at Stanford University" for the claim that metaphorical language can condition individual choices because it "activates those areas of the brain involved in the emotional connotation of events," affecting choices and behavior beyond awareness.
