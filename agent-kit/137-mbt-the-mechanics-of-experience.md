# The Mechanics of Experience

## In one paragraph
Thomas Campbell opens by restating that a human is "a bounded chunk of consciousness, chipped from the old AUM block," experiencing PMR inside a digital consciousness system run by TBC, and he offers a fabricated-but-illustrative number (10^48 times more powerful than present desktop computers) to make TBC's computational burden feel plausible. The chapter's real work is defining how experience itself is manufactured: local reality is not an objective place but a byproduct of two filters — sensory perception and interpretation — applied to an inherently unknowable "what is," producing only "what appears to be." Campbell argues that this two-filter process explains why individuals, cultures, and species diverge so wildly in their sense of what is real, using a battery of thought experiments (a blind-and-deaf passenger, infrared goggles, a dog's nose, a Brazilian tribesman next to an MIT physicist) to show that shared physical reality is a much smaller, less objective thing than people assume. He extends the AI Guy analogy from earlier chapters to argue that any conscious being inside a simulation — human or artificial — is equally cut off from its own "un-experienceable" host system, and closes by re-collapsing PMR physics, NPMR, TBC, EBC, and AUM into a single hierarchy of consciousness-constructs, restating that digging deep enough into any reality always bottoms out at something mystical.

## Core claims
- Campbell claims humans are individuated, bounded pieces of the larger consciousness (AUM), experiencing PMR under a rule-set that is computed, executed, tracked, evaluated, and modified within TBC.
- He claims a "massy body" and physical objects (like a rock) exist only as simulation constructs, yet still produce real experienced consequences (pain, trauma) because the rule-set computes those consequences consistently.
- He offers the fabricated number 10^48 as TBC's performance advantage over present-day desktop computers, explicitly stating the number itself doesn't matter — only the "general idea numerically illustrated" — and ties it back to the earlier assumed ratio of AUM's clock (10^36 ticks to our one).
- He claims experience is built from exactly two interdependent components — perception (input data) and interpretation — and that stored perception can be interpreted later, after the fact, when consciousness needs it.
- He claims local reality is not "a hard, fixed thing" but "a collection of interpreted perceptions," and that its apparent objectivity is an illusion produced by the internal consistency of the space-time rule-set, not evidence that "what appears to be" equals "what is."
- He claims "what is" (the un-experienceable/un-experienced reality behind perception) must remain theoretically and practically unknowable from the PMR viewpoint — the same standard of unknowability the book earlier defined as "mystical" (Chapter 18, Book 1).
- He claims the only thing actually known about the source of experience is how it interacts via specific energy transfers: discrete packets sent and discrete packets received, even where the exchange (e.g., pressure) feels continuous.
- He claims the rule-set only needs to define players and their interactions, where a "player" is any thing, entity, or energy that interacts with anything else — and that this same minimal prescription underlies both PMR and the AI Guy virtual world.
- He claims the boundary between un-experienceable and experienced reality can be modeled as an "energy packet exchange interface" between nonphysical TBC and physical space-time players, and that this interface is the ultimate source of PMR experience.
- He claims there are "no less than five processes" between an object and a person's sense of its reality (light interacting with the object, light interacting with the eye/optic nerve, generation of nerve data, interpretation, integration with prior beliefs/experience), each with its own limitations and error sources.
- He claims cultural and individual differences in local reality (dim vs. bright, educated vs. isolated, scientific vs. mystical cultures) arise because different beings not only interpret differently but literally perceive/notice different data given the same environment.
- He claims that as world cultures coalesce into a few large overlapping blocks (he names "Western Christian industrialized first world," "Middle Eastern Muslim non-industrialized third world," "haves" vs. "have nots"), the friction between colliding local realities is a temporary turbulence of a transition to an "information age," analogous to the earlier violent transition into the industrial age.
- He claims "a local reality is local to the individual, not the environment," and that from any individual's perspective there is no reality except their own local/personal reality.
- He claims your "story" (sentient intent, purpose, and free will) determines the "set" (the consistent space-time rule-set), not the reverse — and that believing physical reality is primary and the self a secondary derivative of it is a common error.
- He claims your local reality is purposely, subtly modifiable within "the virtual reality generator" to maximize each player's individual learning opportunity, while the rule-set still appears fully consistent to all players, with the psi uncertainty principle keeping cross-dimensional energy transfers adequately obscured to preserve honest, non-cheating interaction.
- He claims consciousness quality operates in an interdependent, spiral-shaped feedback loop: quality-informed intent creates virtual-reality actions that produce learning opportunities that raise quality further, and this spiral can bootstrap upward (progressive, decreasing entropy) or downward (degenerative, increasing entropy).
- He claims consciousness is "energy, the most basic form of energy and perhaps the only form of non-virtual energy," and that experienced reality, un-experienced reality, TBC, PMR, the individual, and the space-time rule-set are all constructs of the single consciousness called AUM.
- He claims the labels "physical" and "nonphysical" are purely relative to the observer's own point of immersion — what's physical to one entity (e.g., AI Guy's mainframe-world, or NPMR to its residents) is nonphysical to another, and each is equally real and extant within TBC, EBC, or AUM.
- He claims (with evident amusement) that today's "objective" PMR physics is only a partial exposition of a larger digitally implemented rule-set governing interaction among individuated units of nonphysical consciousness.
- In a "right-brained" aside, he claims that beliefs (scientific, religious, or cultural), emotions, attitudes, and values are mental constructs created within and by each individuated consciousness, and that despite the chapter's focus on physical experience, these subjective contents are primarily responsible for the bulk of the content of most individuated consciousness and most often push "the evolutionary levers of intent and motivation."
- He claims Big Picture significance is invested in people, not things, while Little Picture significance is invested in things, not people, and that "if one focuses exclusively on the seeds, one will never experience the splendor of the fruit" — the objects in an environment matter less than the interpretations they initiate within an individuated consciousness.

## Concepts
- **AUM** — the apparently infinite, all-encompassing digital consciousness system from which individuated beings ("bounded chunks") are drawn ("chipped from the old AUM block"); everything else discussed (TBC, PMR, NPMR, EBC, experienced/un-experienced reality) is described as a subset or construct of AUM's single consciousness.
- **TBC (The Big Computer)** — the operational/computational part of AUM that manages (computes, executes, tracks, evaluates, and modifies) the PMR simulation; assumed for illustration to be about 10^48 times more powerful than present-day desktop computers, consistent with the earlier 10^36-tick ratio from Chapter 31, Book 1.
- **PMR (Physical Matter Reality)** — the interactive, rule-bound virtual physical reality within which humans experience space-time; described here as a "consciousness evolution training simulator" governed by a constrained rule-set (the "science of energy transfers").
- **NPMR** — the nonphysical reality that is "in, and of, consciousness," explicitly said not to be "a distant moon" (i.e., not a place from which physical samples can be retrieved) — only the mind can travel and gather experience there.
- **individuated consciousness** — a particular type of constrained, bounded consciousness (i.e., a human/player) experiencing a virtual physical reality within the larger digital consciousness system.
- **space-time rule-set** — the constrained set of experience-rules/rules-of-interaction defining energy exchange within the PMR multiplayer simulation; provides the internal consistency that creates the illusion of objectivity.
- **player** — as defined in the AI Guy discussion referenced here, any thing, entity, or energy that interacts with anything else; the rule-set only needs to define players and the interactions between them.
- **energy packet exchange interface** — Campbell's model for the boundary between the un-experienceable (nonphysical TBC) and the experienced (space-time players): players send discrete energy packets and receive appropriate return packets per the space-time rule-set; proposed as the ultimate source of PMR experience.
- **"what is" vs. "what appears to be"** — "what is" is the un-experienced/un-experienceable underlying reality that interacts with our sensing apparatus; "what appears to be" is the product of perception and interpretation applied to "what is"; the two are never identical, and "what is" remains necessarily unknown.
- **un-experienceable reality / un-experienced reality** — the source of experience that lies beyond perception's reach and is, by definition, theoretically and practically unknowable from PMR; identified in this chapter with TBC operating the energy packet exchange interface, and equated with what Chapter 18 (Book 1) called "mystical."
- **the two filters** — perception (limited by sensory apparatus) and interpretation (limited by understanding and perspective); together they transmute "what is" into "what appears to be," and no machine extension of the senses can circumvent them.
- **local reality** — the reality in which a being appears to exist and function; not a hard fixed thing but a collection of interpreted perceptions, mixing subjective/private components with sharable/objective ones; "local to the individual, not the environment."
- **personal reality** — the largely subjective, personalized product of one's unique interpretation of limited perception data; distinguished from local reality as more explicitly about the individual's inner content (relationships, beliefs, emotional state) rather than the shared "set."
- **objective causality** — the appearance, to a locally limited awareness, that physical reality is governed by strict cause-and-effect; Campbell frames this as the surface-level view that dissolves once one recognizes PMR as a virtual-reality trainer with optimizing feedback loops connecting choice quality to in-game consequences.
- **mystical** (recalled from Chapter 18, Book 1) — anything theoretically and practically unknowable from within the local causal system; anything that "steps beyond our local causal logic, beyond our collective local reality."
- **psi uncertainty principle** — the mechanism, referenced from earlier chapters, that keeps cross-dimensional energy transfers "adequately obscured" so that the PMR rule-set's "set" is followed without deviation except as it allows, preserving fair play/honesty of interaction.
- **consciousness quality** — the evolving quality of an individuated consciousness's understanding/awareness, which strongly influences both experience and its interpretation, and determines the size of the "decision space" a being can access.
- **consciousness quality spiral** — the cyclical (more precisely spiral) feedback loop in which quality-informed intent produces virtual-reality actions, which yield learning opportunities, which raise quality, which supports more profitable intent; can bootstrap upward (decreasing entropy, progressive) or downward (increasing entropy, degenerative).
- **AI Guy** — a recurring analogy (from earlier "war games" chapters) of an artificial intelligence running inside a computer simulation; used here to argue that any conscious entity inside a system faces the same structural limitation — inability to directly perceive its own host hardware/software/creators — regardless of whether that entity is biological or artificial.
- **OS (Our System)** — named in passing as one of the things TBC's larger rule-set implements the physical and nonphysical components of, alongside "us," "our local reality," and PMR.
- **EBC** — named alongside TBC and AUM ("as real and extant within TBC, EBC, or AUM as any of the others") in the chapter's closing relativity-of-physical/nonphysical argument; not further defined within this unit.
- **Path of Knowledge / "pudding tasters"** — a phrase used for beings of notable consciousness quality who practice direct experiential evaluation of reality ("regular pudding tasters on the Path of Knowledge"), giving them a more practiced ability to judge the quality, significance, and opportunity of their experience, and access to a larger decision space.
- **discrete energy packets** — the underlying character of all energy transfer between space-time players and TBC; even apparently continuous exchanges (like pressure) are, at a finer level of detail, discrete, consistent with the "discrete character of time" established in Chapter 29, Book 1.
- **cultural blocks** — Campbell's term for large overlapping groupings that world cultures are coalescing into as geography loses its grip on cultural identity; he names as examples the "Western Christian industrialized first world," "Middle Eastern Muslim non-industrialized third world," and the world of "haves" vs. "have nots."
- **subjective reality as mental construct** — raised in a "right-brained" aside: beliefs (scientific, religious, cultural), emotions, attitudes, and values are mental constructs created within and by each individuated consciousness; though the chapter otherwise stays focused on physical experience, Campbell notes this subjective content is primarily responsible for the bulk of the content of most individuated consciousness and most often pushes "the evolutionary levers of intent and motivation."
- **Big Picture vs. Little Picture significance** — Big Picture significance is invested in people, not things; Little Picture significance is invested in things, not people; illustrated with "if one focuses exclusively on the seeds, one will never experience the splendor of the fruit" — the objects in an environment matter less than the interpretations they initiate within an individuated consciousness.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The rock/body thought experiment**: a "virtual rock" and a "massy body" exist only within the simulation, yet because both are simulated, the rock can strike the head and the person will experience trauma per the rule-set's computed "science of energy transfers" — illustrating that virtuality does not negate experienced consequence.
- **The 10^48 number**: a deliberately fabricated performance-improvement factor for TBC relative to present-day desktop computers, explicitly offered only as a conceptual aid consistent with the earlier 10^36 AUM-clock-tick ratio from Chapter 31 (Book 1); Campbell stresses the specific value carries no logical weight, only the "general idea."
- **The blind-and-deaf passenger**: a man born without sight or hearing rides in a car with the narrator; when a truck driver forces the car onto the shoulder, the narrator reacts with anger and gestures while the passenger merely notices "a bumpy section of the highway" needing repair — used to show how differing sensory input produces radically different experienced realities from the identical physical event.
- **Infrared/ultraviolet goggles thought experiment**: imagining goggles permanently fitted over the eyes — gaining new sensory information (infrared/UV) while losing old capacities (e.g., reading print under fluorescent light, appreciating color photos) — to show that any change in sensory apparatus forces a wholesale relearning of "what appears to be," while still passing through the same two filters.
- **The dog's nose**: the dog's rich olfactory/auditory local reality (e.g., enthusiasm for sniffing other animals' excrement) is offered to show that possessing the same sensors would still not replicate the dog's interpretation/experience — perception and interpretation are both required and both differ.
- **Brazilian rainforest individual vs. MIT physics professor**: both, placed together, can see the same poisonous frog, tropical snake, trees, river, laser device, CD player, airplane, and blackboard equations, but their attention, discomforts, anxieties, fears, needs, desires, cultural/religious/scientific beliefs, and ego-stuff differ vastly; only their "love-stuff" (caring, compassion, giving) is similar enough to easily overlap into common experience — illustrating that shared sensory access does not produce shared reality, while quality-of-consciousness (love) can bridge otherwise incompatible local realities.
- **The five processes between object and perceived reality**: (1) light interacting with the object, (2) that light interacting with the eye/optic nerve, (3) generation of nerve data, (4) interpretation of that data, (5) integration with the rest of one's experience and beliefs — each carrying its own limitations, random components, and error sources, explaining why the "same" object produces consistent perception but variable interpretation across repeated viewings.
- **Reference back to AI Guy / war games chapters**: reused to argue that AI Guy's inability to perceive the computer hardware, software, manufacturers, or unrelated jobs running on other machines is structurally identical to a human's inability to perceive TBC/AUM — "not qualitatively different from ours."
- **Cross-references to earlier chapters** used as supporting continuity: Chapter 31 (Book 1) for the 10^36 clock-tick assumption; Chapter 29 (Book 1) for the discrete character of time; Chapter 18 (Book 1) for the definition of "mystical" as theoretically/practically unknowable; Chapter 20 (Book 1) for the idea that a "little picture" mystic may be a "bigger picture" scientist.
