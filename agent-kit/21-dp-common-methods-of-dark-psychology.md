# 8 Common Methods of Dark Psychology

## In one paragraph
In this chapter William Cooper catalogues eight of what he calls the most common but effective methods of dark psychology, promising a detailed discussion of why each strategy works. He notes that some of the methods are common and overt while others are so subtle "that you don't even know they are there; but they are." The eight methods are deception through lying, lying by omission, denial, avoidance, generalizations, playing the victim, generating fear, and creating insecurity. For each, Cooper explains the mechanism from the manipulator's point of view — often addressing the reader as the practitioner ("you need to mix lies…") — and illustrates it with everyday scenarios such as fake news, social-media rumors, trial attorneys, a crying child, and scare-mongering news bulletins. He also offers occasional defensive advice, such as discrediting a rumor-spreader or not watching the news every day.

## Core claims
- Cooper claims lying is almost always the manipulator's favorite go-to maneuver — "the foundation of their playbook" — used because they realize "the lies go much better than the facts."
- He claims manipulators deliberately build up as much credibility as they can, because fake news becomes far more powerful when its source is credible; victims then take the manipulator at face value, making them "quite dangerous."
- He claims targets usually cannot tell they are being lied to until it is too late to do anything about it; the only real protection is to watch out for the character types who specialize in trickery and deception.
- He claims half-truths must be mixed so the lies make sense when scrutinized against the truth; manipulators add or omit convenient details so the information is broadly true but twisted — "the devil is in the details."
- He claims rumors do not even need to be believed to work: sowing a seed of doubt, or partial acceptance of the information's validity, is enough, and the manipulator should "strike first and often" so the victim is forced into crisis mode before they can set the record straight.
- He claims the defense against a rumor attack is to hit back and discredit the manipulator "at all costs," sowing doubt about the source of the information.
- He claims denial is a form of lying — pretending something happened when it didn't, or hiding that something did happen — commonly used to avoid responsibility and punishment; a sincere-seeming denial plants a seed of doubt in onlookers.
- He claims avoidance (rambling, dodging questions, gradually and non-obviously changing topics) aims to confuse the target so they question the true version; the manipulator takes advantage of the other party's frustration, transitioning attention away from their own deeds and on to what they really want to achieve. Over time the target can no longer determine what is genuine because the manipulator makes them question everything they know or think is valid.
- He claims generalizations are dangerous direct attacks on a person or group, effective at creating a negative atmosphere; manipulators "love nothing more than to create animosity among people," and blanket statements serve their fundamental tenet of "divide and conquer."
- He claims playing the victim is manipulation we have all learned since childhood, and that it is one reason many people go into depression and stay in it — "feeling special in their problem" attracts attention and makes them feel important in others' eyes.
- He claims fear makes people vulnerable and therefore more susceptible to manipulation; through fear, rulers can manage us better and keep us under control, and news bulletins are "a champion" at this. Intimidation needn't be shouted or explicitly threatened — it is a matter of announcing dangers attached to certain behavior, e.g. telling you that you "must" act a certain way or risk an undesirable outcome, which only appears to appeal to reason.
- He claims continuously questioning everything an interlocutor says is one of the most powerful mind-control techniques: question others repeatedly and eventually they will question it themselves. Negative criticism and complicating people's mistakes are manipulative mechanisms based on insecurity.

## Concepts
- **deception through lying** — the manipulator's foundational maneuver: resorting to lies to gain any kind of advantage, because the lies "go much better than the facts."
- **flat-out lies** — straightforward false information; if the victim doesn't know any better, they are simply misled by it and fall for the ruse.
- **pathological liars** — those who compulsively lie all the time, even when there is no need to.
- **fake news** — lies cleverly disguised by wrapping them in a context where they seem accurate but are truly false: fraudulent information "not known to be true, but also not known to be untrue," so the target cannot distinguish fact from fiction and must go along unless willing to do their own research. Its power multiplies when the source is credible.
- **lying by omission** — a subtler lie in which the manipulator tells a few things about the truth but rejects (leaves out) some key issues; Cooper says this may be called "faking out" in some instances.
- **half-truths** — mixing lies with the truth so the blend is credible and makes sense when contrasted with the truth; the information is true on the whole, but details are added or omitted so the truth is twisted to the manipulator's benefit.
- **rumors** — a good means of implementing half-truths, now spread effectively via social media; success requires only sowing a seed of doubt or partial acceptance, not full belief, and striking first and often before the victim can restore their reputation.
- **denial** — a form of lying: pretending something happened when it didn't, or hiding that something did happen; in simple form, avoiding responsibility for an action to escape its punishment. A sincere-seeming denial confuses onlookers and plants a seed of doubt.
- **avoidance** — not giving straight answers or moving the discussion to a different topic using diversion tactics — rambling, talking endlessly in a meandering fashion, or changing topic gradually and non-obviously — with the ultimate game of confusing the target so they question the true version. The manipulator takes advantage of the other party's frustration, transitioning attention away from their own deeds and on to what they really want to achieve.
- **generalizations / blanket statements** — statements intended as direct attacks on a person or group of people ("all men are…", "all women are…"), unfairly misrepresenting them; effective at confusing and deceiving people and creating a negative atmosphere and animosity.
- **divide and conquer** — what Cooper calls one of the fundamental tenets of manipulators; blanket statements are "great at achieving that."
- **playing the victim** — manipulating others by acting like a victim to attract attention and make a person do something for you; learned since childhood and, per Cooper, a sneaky manipulation "equally present in many people."
- **generate fear** — sowing fear, often imperceptibly, by announcing dangers attached to certain behavior, because the more scared people are the more vulnerable and susceptible to manipulation they become; includes "must"-framed warnings that seem to appeal to reason but actually condition through fear.
- **need for "security"** — what Cooper says we satisfy by watching the news; his proposed remedy against fear-based manipulation is not to watch the news every day.
- **create insecurity** — continuously questioning everything the interlocutor says so that, done repeatedly, they eventually question it themselves; also exploiting highlighted weaknesses, convictions, or self-love.
- **negative criticism** — criticism of what we do or say, which Cooper calls a manipulative mechanism based on insecurity, alongside confusing people by making their mistakes more complex or making them believe they know themselves better than they do.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The gas/food money borrower (lying by omission)** — a manipulator argues they must borrow money for gas or food, but really needs it to buy drugs or alcohol. The truth (needing money) is told; the real reason is left out. Cooper's illustration of how omission differs from a flat-out lie.
- **Social-media rumor campaigns (half-truths)** — spreading rumors on social media is so effective you don't need people to believe them; sowing a seed of doubt or partial acceptance suffices. By the time the affected parties try to restore their reputations, "it will have been too late" — hence strike first and often, forcing the victim into crisis mode. Counter-move given: discredit the manipulator at all costs to sow doubt on the source.
- **The trial attorney (denial)** — skillful trial attorneys convince jurors their client is "not guilty" by seeming truly sincere in the defense, planting a seed of doubt. Cooper stresses this doesn't mean the accused is innocent — only that the other party couldn't prove culpability beyond a reasonable doubt.
- **The child crying behind a locked door (playing the victim)** — since childhood, wanting parents to do something meant locking yourself in your room crying to attract their attention; "even today a little victimhood will help you achieve this result."
- **Depression as attention-getting (playing the victim)** — Cooper claims this is one reason many people go into depression and stay in it: "feeling special in their problem" attracts others' attention and makes them feel important in their eyes.
- **The news bulletins (generate fear)** — the news is "a champion" at terrifying us; the more scared, the more vulnerable and manipulable, letting rulers manage and control us. Remedy offered: don't watch the news every day (which we watch to satisfy the need for "security").
- **The mother and the stove (generate fear)** — the classic example: a mother who doesn't want her child to do something tells him something deliberately frightening — "if you touch the stove you will burn your hand and then we have to take you to the hospital" — so "the child will never go near the kitchen!"
- **The "are you sure?" script (create insecurity)** — sample dialogue: you say "you know that I was able to achieve this result," and the manipulator replies "but are you sure that the result you have achieved is thanks to you?" Repeated questioning of everything a person says eventually makes them question it themselves.

## Thread
This chapter is the book's first concrete techniques catalogue: after the opening chapters' framing of what dark psychology is, Cooper here enumerates eight everyday methods — a family of deception variants (lying, omission, denial, avoidance, generalizations) plus three emotional levers (victimhood, fear, insecurity) — establishing the book's recurring pattern of named tactic + mechanism + everyday example + occasional defense. Themes seeded here recur throughout the manual: seed-of-doubt dynamics and making the target "question all that they know" anticipate the later treatment of gaslighting; fear and insecurity as levers anticipate the persuasion and mind-control chapters; and the practitioner-voiced advice ("you need to mix lies…") sets the book's dual defensive/instructional tone. It also foreshadows a later chapter's material by advising the reader to watch for "the different types of characters that are specialists at trickery and deception."
