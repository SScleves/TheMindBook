# Ch 14 — This Kid is Weird Enough

## In one paragraph
This is a short autobiographical chapter in which Campbell recounts how his childhood out-of-body training in the nonphysical was abruptly terminated. Having learned to slip out-of-body unassisted, he attempts a solo excursion, only to be forcibly shoved back into his body by one of two nonphysical "workers" who announce they have been instructed to seal off his exit passage. The stated reason: he has completed what he was doing, and further other-world experience would make him "too weird" to develop a healthy personality and fit into the world he would grow up in — juggling multiple realities simultaneously would become a problem as he aged. When Campbell begs the workers to look ahead and see whether the door will ever be unsealed, they glimpse something in his future that shocks them, triggers a security scramble involving higher authority, and sets a "plan B" in motion — something they were not supposed to know. The chapter ends with the door slammed shut on out-of-body experience, though Campbell notes he retained easy telepathic communication with his guides.

## Core claims
- Campbell claims that as a child he could shift into "adult mode" on the boundary between awake and asleep, and eventually slip out-of-body easily and unassisted, without his instructors.
- Campbell claims his out-of-body access was deliberately terminated: two nonphysical workers, acting on instructions ("a work order"), forcibly returned him to his body and sealed the passage so he could no longer exit.
- Campbell reports the worker confirming his physical age ("You are a young kid aren't you"), to which he replied that this was true only of his body ("Yes, but just my body … That has never been a problem before"), maintaining that his nonphysical persona was unaffected by his body's youth.
- Campbell reports the workers' rationale: he had "completed what he was doing," and "they" did not want him to grow up too weird — he needed to grow up like a normal kid and develop a healthy personality; too much other-world experience would make him strange and unfit for the world he would grow up in.
- Campbell reports the worker's further claim that while managing one reality at a time was easy for a child, juggling multiple realities simultaneously would become a problem as he got older.
- Campbell says he intuitively knew the worker was right, trusted him, and in any case had no choice — the worker was far more powerful and knowledgeable, and Campbell knew he could not slip thoughts into his mind.
- Campbell claims the workers, at his pleading, looked ahead into his future and were shocked by what they saw — the problem being not the rule they broke by looking (which was quickly forgiven) but that they now knew something they were not supposed to know.
- Campbell claims the incident escalated: higher authority was briefed, connected others were brought in under tight security, decisions were made quickly, and a "plan B" was set into motion — while the content of what they saw remained "securely and purposely unavailable" to him.
- Campbell claims that although out-of-body experiences ended entirely, he was not shut out completely: he could still easily communicate telepathically in his mind with his guides (he did not call them that, but that was their function).

## Concepts
- **NPMR (nonphysical-matter reality)** — used here (not re-defined) as the nonphysical realm where Campbell's childhood classes and out-of-body experiences took place; the workers were "sent to close and seal the door on my experiences in NPMR."
- **the nonphysical** — Campbell's shorthand for the realm beyond the physical where his "learning time" occurred, accessed by slipping out-of-body on the awake/asleep boundary.
- **out-of-body experience** — the mode by which the child Campbell left his physical body to attend nonphysical "class"; initially assisted by instructors, later easy and unassisted; terminated in this chapter when the passage is sealed.
- **instructors** — the nonphysical teachers who had previously come to take Campbell "to class" in a highly structured training; their unexplained absence opens the chapter.
- **workers** — two nonphysical beings sent with a "work order" to seal Campbell's exit passage; portrayed as functionaries within an organized nonphysical order with rules, forgiveness for infractions, and higher authority above them.
- **guides** — the nonphysical friends Campbell could still reach telepathically after the door was sealed; he notes he did not call them "guides," but that was their function.
- **telepathy** — mind-to-mind communication: Campbell could feel the workers' feelings and pick up surface thoughts, knew he could not "slip thoughts into" the worker's mind, and afterward communicated with his guides telepathically "in my mind."
- **multiple realities** — the worker's warning concept: being in only one reality at a time is manageable, but juggling multiple realities simultaneously would become a problem for a maturing child.
- **sealed passage** — the closing and sealing of Campbell's out-of-body exit "door," done by instruction; whether it would ever be unsealed was unknown even to the workers.
- **plan B** — the alternate plan Campbell says higher authority quickly set into motion after the workers inadvertently glimpsed his future; its content is never revealed.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
This chapter is a single extended first-person case study — no thought experiments or analogies, just the narrative event:
- **The solo out-of-body attempt.** After a week or more without his instructors, Campbell slips out-of-body unassisted ("Now it was easy"), wonders where his teachers are, and — unused to freedom after highly structured training — speculates they may be watching to see what he does on his own.
- **The forced return and sealing of the passage.** Someone stops him cold and forcefully shoves him back into his body. Two workers, one of whom speaks, state they have been instructed to seal off the passage; he will no longer be allowed to exit his body. Campbell challenges them ("Are you sure you have the right person?"); the worker checks his work order and confirms: "No mistake … you are the one." Pressed on why, the worker notes Campbell is "a young kid," to which Campbell objects that this is true only of his body — "that has never been a problem before."
- **The "too weird" rationale.** The worker explains the purpose: Campbell has completed what he was doing; "they" don't want him to grow up too weird; he needs to grow up like a normal kid with a healthy personality; and juggling multiple realities simultaneously would become a problem as he got older. Campbell protests he can handle it but intuitively knows the worker is right and has no choice.
- **The forbidden look into the future.** At Campbell's desperate pleading ("Can you find out for me? Please look and see"), both workers look "far away into the distance" — a long, intense look — then simultaneously gasp, shocked and momentarily stunned. Campbell strains to see the picture in their minds but cannot; the startling content is "securely and purposely unavailable," though he can feel their worry and sense of impending personal jeopardy.
- **The security response and plan B.** The workers brief higher authority; a few connected others are brought in; security is tight; decisions are made quickly; a plan B is set in motion; everyone but the two workers disappears. Campbell notes the rule-breaking look itself was a small, quickly forgiven issue — the real problem was that the workers now knew something they were not supposed to know. When Campbell tries to bluff his way into the information (posing as "a team member who had … just happened to miss the preceding conversation"), the workers stare at him in quizzical amazement, say nothing, and slam the door hard.
- **The residual channel.** Though out-of-body experiences end entirely, Campbell reports he could still easily communicate telepathically with his guides.

## Thread
This chapter closes the childhood-training thread of Campbell's autobiographical arc in Section 1 ("Delusion or Knowledge"), where he lays out the personal experiences that ground his later theory. It follows the chapters recounting his structured nonphysical schooling with instructors, and ends that phase decisively: the door to NPMR is sealed "for a long time," with a heavy foreshadowing device — the workers' shocked, classified glimpse of his future and the mysterious "plan B" — implying his nonphysical career will resume and matter later. It also plants the normal-development rationale (don't grow up "too weird"; one reality at a time) and leaves open the telepathic guide channel, both of which set up his later adult return to these experiences in the chapters that follow.
