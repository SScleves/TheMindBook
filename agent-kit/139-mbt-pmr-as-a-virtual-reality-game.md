# PMR as a Virtual Reality Game

## In one paragraph
Thomas Campbell asks the reader to run a gedanken (thought) experiment: imagine a future virtual-reality game so advanced that a computer, wired directly to a floating, sensory-deprived body, produces experience indistinguishable from real experience, in a multiplayer world shared with friends and strangers under a consistent rule-set. He uses this analogy to reframe physical reality (PMR) as the "seeming reality" produced by mind interacting with a rule-set, while a "larger reality" — apart from and non-causally-connected to the local game — remains where the player's actual body and life persist. He then pivots to a hardware argument: given how far computing has come in sixty years (from ENIAC to near-VR) and where Moore's Law points ninety years out, the technical gap between today's crude virtual reality and a perfect one is trivial compared to the resources available to AUM, the vastly greater digital consciousness the book proposes underlies reality. The chapter closes with a colloquial flourish suggesting AUM ("The Big Dude," "B.D.") runs many parallel versions of PMR effortlessly, using a time increment eighty orders of magnitude smaller than a human second.

## Core claims
- Thomas Campbell argues that individuals customize and interpret their own local reality but do not create all of reality — a larger, apparently nonphysical reality exists outside the local physical reality dimension, and individual minds are a subset of it.
- He claims bodies and physical experience are the product of minds interacting within the constraints of a "space-time rule-set," together with other players (sentient and non-sentient) and other minds, in a mutual exchange of discrete energy packets he calls "interactive experience."
- Campbell asserts that a sufficiently advanced VR game — wired directly to the nervous system with no gloves, helmets, or suits needed — could simulate/stimulate sensory input so precisely that the experience would be scientifically indistinguishable from real experience.
- He argues that because real bodily input is blocked during such VR immersion, a participant could physically starve while immersed in a food-themed game world and never notice their real hunger — illustrating that virtual experience can fully mask the state of the "real" body.
- He extends the single-player thought experiment to a multiplayer version: friends and thousands/millions of others share a persistent world (e.g., a "Jungle Safari" game) where the computer supplies environment, creatures, weather, and events, and free-will choices by participants drive the action to logical, rule-based, causal, objective conclusions.
- Campbell states the "outside" reality (the floating body, spouse, mortgage, parked car) is not causally connected to the virtual reality and, from inside the virtual game, is non-operational, nonphysical, and effectively does not exist.
- He frames the VR-game-as-training-machine idea as a good vehicle for evolving "the quality of your consciousness."
- He argues from the history of computing (ENIAC, 1942–1946; Einstein-era ignorance of digital computers) and Moore's Law projections (10^12 times faster in 60 years, 10^18 times faster in ~90 years) that what looks like science fiction now will look primitive later, and that our current inability to imagine future computing capacity is itself evidence of how much room there is for growth — even allowing that processing speed could "go asymptotic" before reaching those multipliers.
- He claims that if this level of virtual reality is plausible for humans within decades of primitive computing, AUM's version of the same feat is comparatively trivial: AUM's sets are merely larger/more detailed, requiring only more memory and processing power, not a different kind of solution.
- He describes AUM as operating in a "frequency-space" with a basic time increment eighty orders of magnitude smaller than a human second, and states that, as a result, there are "many, many versions of PMR" running in parallel with no strain ("The Big Dude never breaks a sweat").

## Concepts
- **PMR** — used in this chapter (title and closing lines: "many, many versions of PMR humming along in parallel") as the label for the physical/local reality under discussion; this unit does not spell out the acronym itself, but treats PMR as equivalent to the "local virtual reality" / "local physical reality dimension" the chapter otherwise describes — the "seeming reality" created by mind-plus-rule-set that is being likened to the VR game.
- **AUM** — named here as the entity whose computational resources dwarf humanity's; described as an "apparently infinite, brilliant, digital-consciousness" that runs "puddles of individuated consciousness" without needing to be wired, fed, pay mortgages, drive cars, or seek physical gratification, and that can run many parallel versions of PMR simultaneously.
- **Larger reality** — the reality that exists apart from the individual, appearing centered in the nonphysical because it lies outside the local physical reality dimension; individual minds are described as a subset of it, interacting with it.
- **Local reality / seeming reality** — the reality an individual creates or customizes through personal interpretation of virtual perceptions; contrasted with the larger reality that contains it.
- **Gedanken experiment (the VR-game thought experiment)** — Campbell's device for the chapter: imagining a future virtual-reality game so advanced it is indistinguishable from real experience, used to make the PMR-as-simulation idea rationally graspable.
- **Sensory-deprivation tank setup** — in the thought experiment, the player's body floats in a perfect sensory-deprivation tank while a computer connects wirelessly/directly to the nerves or brain-areas that receive sensory input, eliminating the need for gloves, helmets, suits, or motion platforms.
- **Space-time rule-set** — the constraint system within which minds interact to produce bodies and physical experience; in the game analogy this is the computer's rule-set governing the simulated world.
- **Interactive experience** — Campbell's term for the process of aware but limited consciousnesses exchanging discrete energy packets with each other and with the rule-set, producing physical experience.
- **Free will (initiative)** — the players'/participants' choices, which drive the VR game's action to its logical conclusions and simulated physical ramifications within the computer's rule-set.
- **Multi-player game analogy** — the extension of the single-player VR thought experiment to a shared world (friends plus thousands or millions of others) in which the computer tracks and integrates all players' interactions, illustrated by the example of choosing a "Jungle Safari" game populated with appropriate creatures, objects, and environmental events.
- **Causal consistency / rule-based objectivity** — the property Campbell attributes to the VR world: it is rule-based, consistent, causal, and objective, with outcomes determined jointly by the rule-set and the players' actions (or inaction).
- **Non-causal "outside" reality** — the claim that the reality containing the player's actual body and mundane life (spouse, mortgage, car) has no causal connection to the virtual reality, and is nonphysical/non-operational/non-existent from the virtual reality's internal point of view.
- **Moore's Law projection** — Campbell's use of Moore's Law to project computing power increasing by a factor of 10^12 (one trillion) in sixty years and 10^18 (a million trillion) by roughly ninety years from the time of writing, with the caveat that processing speed increases could "go asymptotic" before reaching these multipliers, and that breakthrough technologies could make the trillion-fold figure "an outrageous understatement."
- **AI Guy** — referenced as a stand-in figure for future generations/AI who will use future digital capability in ways presently unimaginable.
- **Frequency-space** — the domain in which AUM is said to operate, characterized by a basic time increment that is eighty orders of magnitude smaller than a human second, used to explain how AUM can run many versions of PMR in parallel without computational strain.
- **The Big Dude / B.D.** — Campbell's informal nickname for AUM in this passage, used with the aside "B.D. makes the coolest games, man. Totally cool!"

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The "Gourmet Foods Of The World" VR game** — Campbell's illustrative scenario: a participant who lingers too long in this VR game, "regularly" eating virtual filet mignon, steamed vegetables, and fresh fruit, could physically starve to death without noticing their real body's hunger, because natural bodily inputs are blocked while hooked to the machine. Used to show that virtual experience can be total enough to mask the true state of the real (larger-reality) body.
- **The "Jungle Safari" multi-player game** — the example used to illustrate the multi-player extension: the player and friends choose this game, and the computer supplies the rain forest environment plus all appropriate critters, objects, and environmental energy (weather, climate, earthquakes, wind, volcanoes). Used to show how a shared rule-set can generate a consistent, causal, populated world for many participants' free-will actions to play out in.
- **Sam's Virtual Reality Emporium** — the fictional parking lot where the player's actual car sits while they are immersed in the game, used as a concrete, homely image of the "larger reality" left behind and rendered non-operational from within the VR experience.
- **ENIAC / sixty-years-ago comparison** — Campbell notes that sixty years before writing, people (including Einstein, then pondering relativity) would not have known what a digital computer was, citing ENIAC as developed at the University of Pennsylvania for the US Army between 1942 and 1946. Used as a historical anchor for how unimaginable today's computing would have seemed, to argue future computing (and by extension AUM's capacities) is comparably unimaginable to us now.
- **Moore's Law arithmetic** — Campbell works out that sixty years of Moore's Law implies a trillion-fold (10^12) increase in computer speed/capability, and ninety years implies a million-trillion-fold (10^18) increase, while noting speed could plateau ("go asymptotic") before that. Used to build the case that the VR-game thought experiment is not fantastical but a near-term extrapolation, and that AUM's version of the same feat is a matter of scale (more memory/processing), not a different kind of problem.

## Key quotes
- "A larger reality exists apart from us that appears to be centered in the nonphysical because it exists outside our local physical reality dimension."
- "This virtual reality machine produces an artificial or virtual experience that is indistinguishable from real experience. You cannot tell the difference."
- "You could physically starve to death without noticing the real you was hungry."
- "Your mind and the computer's rule-set together create a rule based, consistent, causal, objective space-time experience where the free will initiative of you and your friends drives the action to its logical conclusions and simulated physical ramifications."
- "This outside reality is not causally connected to your virtual reality, and from the point of view of your virtual reality, it is non-operational, nonphysical, and does not exist."
- "Sixty years from now, computers will be (assuming Moore's Law) one trillion... times faster and more capable than they are today."
- "What kinds of digital magic do you think AUM might be able to pull off with his puddles of individuated consciousness that don't need to be wired, fed, pay mortgages, drive cars, or rub up against each other for personal gratification?"
- "...it is so easy there are many, many versions of PMR humming along in parallel — and The Big Dude never breaks a sweat. B.D. makes the coolest games, man. Totally cool!"

## Thread
This unit sits in Book 2's Section 4 ("Solving the Mystery"), where Campbell builds intuition-friendly analogies for his claim that PMR functions like an information/virtual-reality system generated by mind-plus-rule-set interacting within a larger, nonphysical reality. It follows from the book's earlier groundwork about consciousness, rule-sets, and AUM as the encompassing digital consciousness, and it prepares the reader for later chapters that develop the computer/simulation model of reality in more technical detail (rule-sets, TBC-style computational framing, and AUM's scale) by first securing the analogy on relatable, near-future technological grounds (VR gaming, Moore's Law) before asking the reader to extend it to a being whose capacities dwarf ours.
