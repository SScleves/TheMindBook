# How to Analyze People?

## In one paragraph
This chapter lays the groundwork for reading people: William Cooper argues that analysis must start from a baseline understanding of normal human nature, because everyone "wears a mask" yet leaks unconscious signs of their real feelings. He then defines nonverbal communication (also called "manual language," a term he traces to Jurgen Ruesch and Weldon Kees in 1956), lists its seven dimensions, its five abilities (repetition, contradiction, substitution, complementing, accenting), and five principles for interpreting it (culture/context, setting, combining signals, incongruence, intuition). A large middle section maps the body's "zones" for nonverbal cues — face, eyes, hands, posture, head, proximity, feet and legs — and contrasts nonverbal with verbal communication, including studies on incongruence between the two. The chapter closes with a tour of the mind's architecture as Cooper presents it: Freud's conscious/unconscious/preconscious frame, the engaged/automatic/analytical minds, the limbic system's role in unconscious behavior, and the "old brain" (medulla, pons, reticular formation). The practical thrust is that mastering these cues lets you both read others accurately and control the impression you give.

## Core claims
- Cooper claims the first and most important skill in analyzing people is understanding human nature and the normal spectrum of behavior — like a judge, you interpret actions against a baseline of how people typically behave and what motivates them.
- He claims people can defy the baseline: some never feel jealousy; most poor people do not steal because it is not in their character, while "frequently the greatest, most flamboyant thief is the person who already has all that they need."
- Everyone wears a mask, Cooper says, but unconscious signs (a spontaneous laugh, a twinkle in the eye, a giddy foot-tap) reveal emotional state — perhaps only FBI agents are skilled enough to give no clue at all.
- The book says the term "nonverbal communication" was brought about in 1956 by psychiatrist Jurgen Ruesch and author Weldon Kees, whose book observed that body contours disclose the brain's disposition and expressive motions reveal current humor and state of mind.
- Cooper claims 60% of human interaction is nonverbal and 30% is tone — so 90% of what a person "says" is not in the content of their words.
- He claims mirroring someone's actions subconsciously communicates that you are on the same wavelength, putting others at ease.
- Nonverbal cues can contradict speech, and the whole body finds it hard to lie — true meaning "will come out through other channels," so signals must be combined rather than read in isolation.
- Gestures are culture-dependent, so interpreting physical signs requires understanding the person's background (the same hand sign can mean "okay" or be offensive depending on region).
- Cooper reports that experts traditionally agree nonverbal communication impacts the message — a claim supported by 93% of people, with only 7% considering solely verbal information valid — but he cautions "this data is not infallible" and other research does not support the 93% claim.
- He claims audiences automatically associate your aesthetic (dress, grooming) with your work and ideas, and that an audience's ability to retain information is based on their relationship with you.
- The book says the limbic system is responsible for unconscious behavior, its "common languages" are fear or peace, and human action "can be manipulated easily by training the behavioral aspect of this part of the brain."
- Cooper claims verbal communication includes both spoken and written language (this book itself being written communication), and that both verbal and nonverbal forms can be spoken and written.

## Concepts
- **analyzing people** — Using a baseline understanding of human nature, the spectrum of human emotion, the behaviors linked to those emotions, and what motivates people, in order to interpret what individuals say and do.
- **wearing a mask** — Cooper's image for the fact that people's intentions are not always clear; despite the mask, people reveal emotional state through unconscious signs.
- **nonverbal communication (manual language)** — A process where you send and receive a message without the use of words, inscribed or spoken; it usually exists within parts of a spoken message. Term coined in 1956 by Jurgen Ruesch and Weldon Kees.
- **body movements / kinetics** — The first nonverbal dimension, including facial expressions and eye contact.
- **vocalists** — The book's term for the vocal dimension: volume, timbre, pitch, and rate. (So spelled in the text.)
- **individual appearance** — A nonverbal dimension: your personal look as part of the message.
- **sign language (as a gestural system)** — Gestures range from monosyllabic responses up to complex systems like American Sign Language for the deaf, where nonverbal signs carry a direct translation; meaning is prescribed to these gestures with the help of facial expression and intensity.
- **physical surroundings** — A nonverbal dimension: your environment and the objects that comprise it.
- **personal space** — A nonverbal dimension concerning distance between people.
- **haptics** — The nonverbal dimension of touch.
- **time** — Listed by Cooper as the seventh nonverbal dimension.
- **repetition** — Nonverbal ability: reinforcing what has been said (e.g., nodding while saying yes).
- **contradictions** — Nonverbal ability: when cues contrast the message being relayed, it may be seen as untruthful.
- **substitution** — Nonverbal ability: gestures replacing or taking the place of words, which can add intensity.
- **complementing** — Nonverbal ability: enhancing a verbal message.
- **accenting** — Nonverbal ability: highlighting a certain point in a message.
- **mirroring** — Copying someone's actions to subconsciously communicate that you are on the same wavelength, making them feel at ease.
- **being presentable** — Using dress and grooming as nonverbal message; a tidy appearance signals you are organized and in control, encouraging others to follow your lead.
- **the 60/30/90 claim** — Cooper's figures: 60% of human interaction is nonverbal, 30% is tone, so 90% of what a human says is not in the words' content.
- **showing and telling** — The two elements of a presentation that must be interwoven, each tackled with care.
- **principle 1: age, geographic location, gender, and culture are vital** — Gestures have different definitions in various regions; culture and family rules affect how you handle and react to nonverbal signals.
- **principle 2: put things into setting** — Before concluding (e.g., crossed arms = defensiveness), place the interaction in the context of topic, timing, and external influences — crossed arms may just mean the person is chilly.
- **principle 3: combine signals** — It is challenging for the whole body to lie; true meaning comes out through other channels, so read signals together.
- **principle 4: incongruence** — Situations where words and nonverbal signals are not in alignment; instincts must judge the intent, and psychological discomfort can indicate distrust (though the same uneasy feeling may mean something else in another situation).
- **principle 5: trust your intuition** — Intuition is "an insentient process during which information is demonstrated through physical feelings"; being authentic matters because people pick up on insincere communication.
- **zones for nonverbal cues** — The different body regions that manifest unspoken cues; combining them "paints an accurate picture of the intent behind the words."
- **face (zone)** — The most noticeable, overpowering cue zone; considered universal (unlike culture-bound zones), with specific expressions for happiness, sadness, anger, surprise, anxiety, and disgust.
- **eyes (zone)** — One look can display complex cognitive functions; too much eye contact is considered aggressive and causes others to glance away with an inaccurate impression; the eyes also monitor situations and feedback.
- **gestures and hands (zone)** — The indicators most affected by cultural or geographic norms; the same sign conveys vastly different impressions across regions.
- **posture (zone)** — Position defines attitude, confidence, and personality: sitting/standing straight conveys pride and sureness; slouching appears lazy or disconnected.
- **head position (zone)** — Lowering the face suggests insecurity (usually accompanying a weak or closed posture); raising the head can signal interest.
- **proximity (zone)** — Positioning and space relative to others, established by social/cultural norms and the relationship; violating these standards leaves others uneasy or reads as aggression.
- **feet and legs (zone)** — An accurate indicator of your relationship with your audience; the direction of feet is a "turning cue" showing eagerness and readiness to move.
- **verbal communication** — Language both written and spoken — the use of words — versus nonverbal's use of body language, gestures, and silence; used to share agreed meanings. Cooper stresses it is not only spoken dialogue.
- **engaged mind** — The state emerging when one is fully immersed in the present activity (crying over a sick friend, skiing, eating pizza); the absorbed are more satisfied and generally happier with relationships and lives.
- **automatic mind** — The brain impulsively performing tasks: noticing environmental shifts, making positive/negative judgments, categorizing experience; influenced by atmospheric conditions, instincts, prior learning, and perceptions; essential for survival and adaptation.
- **analytical mind** — The frame of mind, enabled by self-awareness, that intentionally steps back from current feelings and thoughts to mentally observe and manipulate information; includes all complex reasoning.
- **limbic system** — The brain structure responsible for unconscious behavior, acting through emotional responses of pain and pleasure; its "common languages" are fear or peace; it feeds on emotions and reacts intensely to strong feelings; also handles memory, emotions, and responses to reward and punishment. Its neurons sit buried deep in the cerebrum, influencing daily meditation and emotional/sexual/social behavior while also controlling internal homeostasis and basic needs like hunger and thirst. Besides the hypothalamus, amygdala, hippocampus, and septal nuclei, Cooper also names the anterior cingulate, posterior cingulate, thalamic nuclei, reticular activating system, orbital frontal lobes, and certain nuclei of the cerebellum as part of this structure.
- **amygdala** — Regulates how you perceive fear and how you react to nervousness and aggression; influences facial responses and displays unconscious perception of emotions; connected to stress- and aggression-related neurotransmitters.
- **hypothalamus** — Located under the amygdala; regulates hunger, thirst, and sex drive, and creates feelings of pleasure when those needs are satisfied.
- **hippocampus** — Critical for storing information in long-term memory; damage could result in amnesia.
- **septal nuclei** — Named with the hypothalamus, amygdala, and hippocampus as of specific importance to unconscious behavior and the brain's emotional and psychic functions (including meditation).
- **old brain** — The innermost brain region, including the stem, present in our ancestors for hundreds of thousands of years; responsible for the most basic survival instincts.
- **medulla** — Old-brain part controlling heart rate and air intake; influences eating, breathing, and movement, sustaining life.
- **pons** — Old-brain part helping control movement and balancing.
- **reticular (formation)** — Filters stimuli from the spinal cord and relays the remainder to relevant brain parts; also influences walking, eating, sexual activity, and sleep; severing it results in a coma.
- **behavior detection officers** — Airport security personnel trained at great expense to spot terrorists via nonverbal cues; Cooper notes critics claim the operation has never stopped a single criminal.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The flamboyant thief** — Cooper's opening illustration of human unpredictability: most poor people do not steal even when in need, because it is not in their character; frequently the greatest, most flamboyant thief already has everything they need. Shows the baseline of "normal behavior" admits surprises.
- **FBI agents as the exception** — Everyone leaks unconscious signs (spontaneous laugh, twinkle in the eye, giddy foot-tapping); "perhaps only FBI agents are so skilled that they never give you some sort of clue."
- **Ruesch & Kees (1956)** — Psychiatrist Jurgen Ruesch and author Weldon Kees coined the term in their book *Nonverbal Communication*, with notes on the visual perception of human relations: body contours disclose the brain's disposition, and motions of expression reveal current humor and state of mind.
- **The thumb-and-forefinger circle** — The gesture forming an "a" means "okay" in the United States but is an offensive interpretation in some Latin American countries; Cooper's proof that interpretation requires cultural background.
- **"Hook 'em Horns"** — Holding up index and small finger makes a horn symbol that in Texas means "Hook 'em Horns" but in Spain is considered offensive — his warning for work with international clients.
- **Film-yourself exercise** — Cooper's practice drill: film yourself preparing a presentation, on more than one occasion, checking whether your face communicates happiness, anger, fear, anxiety, surprise, or boredom; note posture so you do not look uninterested; rate yourself on facial expression, tonal variations, posture, gesture, and eye contact.
- **Snap-judgment exercise** — Next time you are in a public place, pay attention to the snap judgments you form about others based on the way they dress.
- **Ekman & Friesen on verbal/nonverbal interdependence** — Psychologists Paul Ekman and Wallace Friesen are cited as explaining how gestures affect oral discourse: experienced speakers use forced pauses and changes in vocal rate or volume to hold attention; saying yes while nodding is repetition for emphasis.
- **The flat-faced swimmer** — Someone tells you they had a great time out swimming, but their facial expressions look flat and unemotional — Cooper's example of nonverbal contradiction of speech.
- **The 93%/7% deceiving studies** — Experts traditionally agree nonverbal communication impacts the message; the claim was supported by 93% of people, with 7% considering only verbal information valid. A study done on two occasions compared vocal and facial cues; other research does not support the 93% claim, though it agrees children and adults mostly rely on nonverbal cues. Cooper's caveat: "This data is not infallible," and individual reliance on unspoken signs varies greatly.
- **Airport behavior detection officers** — Security administrations spent "a lot of cash" training officers to recognize nonverbal cues identifying terrorists; critics claim the operation has never successfully stopped even a single criminal among thousands of passengers a year — Cooper's example of nonverbal miscommunication and the limits of self-styled body-language experts.
- **Crossed arms in context** — Seeing crossed arms may just mean the person is chilly; conclusions must wait until the interaction is placed in the setting of topic, timing, and external inspirations.
- **Feet in conversation and in groups** — Two people in intimate conversation should have shoes pointed toward one another; in a group, feet positioned toward the inside of the circle indicate engagement, while feet facing outside the ring show the person is ready to move on.
- **The Kio University facial-expression study** — Seventeen students who led healthy lifestyles participated (nine females; the text gives "a mean age of 20-76 years"). Details of the investigation were explained beforehand, but the purpose was withheld to avoid biases; Kio University approved the protocol. Cooper presents it in the context of research examining brain activity linked to facial changes, aiming to determine changes in an individual's trustworthiness from verbal interactions and facial expressions. (The chapter reports the setup but no results.)
- **Freud's structure of the mind** — Cooper credits Sigmund Freud with stating that the mind's structure encompasses the conscious, the unconscious, and the preconscious, after which several psychologists came to understand the framework and its influence on behavior.
- **Engaged-mind examples** — Crying about a sick friend, skiing down a mountain, eating a delicious slice of pizza: attention fully connected to current happenings.
- **Friends' laughter** — When you joke with your friends and they laugh, that is a form of nonverbal communication — Cooper's illustration that verbal communication is not only spoken dialogue.

## Thread
This chapter opens the book's people-reading toolkit: where earlier chapters catalog manipulative tactics and personality types, Chapter 17 supplies the observational foundation — baseline human nature, the nonverbal channel that carries (per Cooper) 90% of the message, and the neural machinery (limbic system, old brain) he says drives unconscious behavior. It sets up subsequent chapters on body-language cues, deception tells, and personality typing, since all of them presuppose the zones, principles, and verbal/nonverbal incongruence framework laid out here. The limbic-system passage also loops back to the book's manipulation theme: the same system that leaks emotion is the one Cooper says can be "trained" to manipulate human action.

## Connections
- Within this book: complements the chapters on body-language cues and deception tells (the zones and incongruence principle here are their reading key); the mask/leakage idea underpins the gaslighting and dark-persuasion chapters, where manipulators exploit exactly the signals catalogued here; Ekman's name recurs wherever the book treats facial expression; Chapter 33's case studies apply this analysis toolkit in scenario form.
- Cross-book (speculative/thematic, not Cooper's reference): the engaged/automatic/analytical mind trichotomy loosely parallels the consciousness-layer models in *my-big-toe*; the claim that most communication happens beneath awareness resonates thematically with *the-holographic-universe*'s interest in subconscious perception. Neither link is made by the author.
