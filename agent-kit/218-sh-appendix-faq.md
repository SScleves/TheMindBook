# Appendix — FAQ About the Simulation Hypothesis

## In one paragraph
In the five or so years since the first edition, Virk interacted with thousands of readers in person, online via social media, and via email, and certain questions came up again and again. This appendix collects those frequently asked questions and gives his "most recent thoughts on the answers" as of the second edition (written in 2024). Seven questions are answered: whether he really believes we're in a simulation, the odds of it, who created it, its purpose, when we'll reach the simulation point, whether life is meaningless if it's true, and whether it's "simulations all the way down." Along the way he updates his famous 51-percent estimate to "closer to 70 percent," restates Bostrom's trilemma in his own simulation-point terminology, and closes with a twist: even failing to reach the simulation point could itself be evidence that we are in one.

## The questions and Virk's answers

**1. Do you really think we live in a simulation?** — Yes: "I wouldn't have written this book if I didn't think we were in some kind of simulation." But there are subtleties — the answer depends on the *type* of simulation, because the simulation hypothesis is "several different ideas wrapped into one": some ideas uncontroversial, some highly speculative, some "positively outlandish." **Big idea #1:** the universe is made of information rather than matter, and this information is computed and then rendered for us into what we think of as the physical universe. A Nobel Prize–winning physicist Virk recently met at the University of Cambridge (UK) told him the first part — that the universe consists of information — is no longer controversial. There is now a whole branch called digital physics (defined in Chapter 14). Where Virk was taught classical laws as a kid (conservation of energy, conservation of momentum, second law of thermodynamics), physicists today speak of "conservation of information" — how much information is in the world, whether it can be destroyed, what happens if it is lost. The universe seems to be "some type of computation machine": Stephen Wolfram (physicist turned computer scientist) is working on a method using cellular automata as a model of how the universe works based on computation rather than mathematics (detailed in *The Simulated Multiverse*, mentioned in Part IV); Vopson's proposed second law of infodynamics (Chapter 14) concerns information entropy vs. physical entropy as a system evolves, and seems on the surface to correspond with Campbell's fundamental process (Chapter 8), at least in the profitability function for biological systems being a state of least entropy. In the past physics was described using mathematics; in the future it may be described by computation. The "information rather than matter" half is slightly more controversial, but Virk argues the more you look for "physical matter" the harder it is to find: his desk is more than 99 percent (perhaps 99.99 percent) empty space; like nested Russian dolls (matryoshka or babushka dolls), each smaller level of matter reveals more empty space, and at the bottom sits "a particle" — which physics cannot easily define. John Wheeler (Chapter 15) coined "it from bit" because at the bottom level the only thing distinguishing one particle from another is answers to yes/no questions — a series of bits. How that information gets presented as the "physical world" (space and time) is still not really understood, beyond the brain processing sensory input; to Virk the video game metaphor — take information from the server and render it — is the best explanation, and if everyone is doing this, it becomes like a massively multiplayer online role-playing game. A more controversial rival view: a physical world exists but our perceptions of it are inaccurate — back to philosophy (Descartes's evil demon; Donald Hoffman's desktop metaphor from Chapter 14). Whether it is a *classical* computer game (von Neumann architecture) is an open question: quantum-computing researchers Seth Lloyd (MIT) and David Deutsch (Oxford) argue the universe is computing all the time; Deutsch modified Wheeler's phrase to "it from qubit" (more in *The Simulated Multiverse*). **Big idea #2** (which both derives from and informs #1): the world around us isn't real — not merely a user interface but "a kind of purposeful hoax," an illusion. This comes from philosophy and religion; among the world's religions (Part III) it isn't controversial at all — Virk calls it "one of the most fundamental articles of faith" among them. Taken together — information rendered for us + purposeful hoax — you get the essence of the simulation hypothesis; either flavor (NPC or RPG) and variations in between fit. Honest research into both big ideas, Virk says, leads to his conclusion: the physical universe is some kind of simulation or video game, "a counterfeit world that is built on information and is not exactly what it seems."

**2. What are the odds that we live in a simulation?** — Virk stands by his past statement that we are *at least 51 percent* likely to be in a simulation (more likely in than not). When he said this in 2019, shortly after the first edition, the story went viral as an "MIT professor" claiming we live in a simulation. He surveys other attempts (all relying on Bostrom's simulation argument to some extent), then restates Bostrom's trilemma (Chapter 6) using his own terminology of the *simulation point* instead of Bostrom's *posthuman* — his term also covers avatars of "real" characters, not just posthuman AI — and instead of probabilities summing to 100 percent, he gives the chance we're in a simulation *if* each leg is true: **Option 1** — no civilization ever reaches the simulation point: 0%. **Option 2** — all civilizations that reach it ban "ancestor simulations" and create none: low, almost zero (~<1%). **Option 3** — one or more civilizations reach it and each creates a large number of simulated universes indistinguishable from ours: ~99+%. Bostrom put no percentages in his original paper (he used the name "the simulation hypothesis" only for option three); asked later, he first said all three equally likely (33.333% each), then when pressed put option three slightly lower, around 20 percent — "based mostly on intuition." Elon Musk (2016) said the chance we're in *base reality* is one in billions — i.e., ~99.999+% simulated — but since Musk's argument relies on option three, Virk notes you'd technically multiply 99.999% by that option's likelihood (33.333% on Bostrom's first estimate), landing near one-third. In 2020 Columbia University astronomer David Kipping published a paper using Bayesian reasoning: slightly less than 50 percent, matching Neil deGrasse Tyson's estimate of 50 percent. David Chalmers (whose original Matrix Hypothesis paper came out the same year as Bostrom's, 2003) says a perfect simulation would be undetectable — which Virk reads as a coin flip, 50 percent (though Chalmers never stated a percentage). The key variable: everything hinges on whether the simulation point is reachable — the whole argument runs from 0 percent to almost 100 percent depending on that. "If it is possible to build a Matrix-like simulation... then it's likely that someone has already gotten there and we are already inside a simulation." If video games, their interfaces, and the AI in those games all continue to improve, we will reach the simulation point "for sure" — and at 100 percent likelihood of reaching it, we end up pretty much in Bostrom's third proposition (ignoring for the moment the branch that bans simulations). We have only one technological civilization to use as data — ours — and Virk estimates we are at **67 percent, two-thirds down the road** to the simulation point (an *average* progress across the ten stages of Part I, not seven complete stages plus three at zero). Thus, writing in 2024, he puts it "in the neighborhood of 70 percent" — based primarily on the technological argument, and ignoring the other clues (quantum mechanics and physics weirdness, reports of the mystics). **Revised second-edition answer: at least 50 percent, most probably closer to 70 percent.**

**3. Who created the simulation?** — One of the top questions he gets (equivalently: who are the simulators?); he tried to answer it in Chapter 15. Bostrom was mostly concerned with *future humans* creating ancestor simulations — akin to us simulating ancient Rome or Greece (the West being descendants of the Romans, or the ancient Hindus, or others). But "future humans" is confusing because it implies they don't exist yet; in an ancestor simulation *we* would be simulations of the ancestors of the builders, and theoretically the simulation ends when it catches up to the builders — unless the simulators mess with time inside the simulation to make it run faster ("a distinct possibility"). Virk therefore prefers "a more advanced species": a species of human on an Earth outside the simulation, or some nonhuman intelligence ("aliens?") that decided to simulate our civilization — true for both NPC and RPG versions. An accurate ancestor simulation also becomes a form of *simulated time travel*, the feeling you might get in a game like *Assassin's Creed*. Most avatars we create in simulations are human, but a considerable percentage could be another species entirely; the sim's purpose might be to see how humans as a computational species evolve and what they accomplish, collectively or individually. In *The Matrix* the creators are superintelligent AI — another popular theory (the machines we left behind) — though the doomsday version depicted there (humans as batteries) seems "unlikely and unnecessary" to Virk. He takes more seriously than most academics the idea that **we are the creators** — possibly not biological (human or alien) at all, but spiritual beings without physical bodies looking to have a corporeal existence, which is why Part III looks at parallels with the world's religions. This holds open the idea that we are the creators or at least architects of our fate — of what games we play. There may also be more advanced players, and — in a technological twist on traditional theology — possibly a single creator (one we would consider God) or creators. Because simulations generally show signs of intelligent design, this seems a reasonable conclusion to Virk and again links simulation theory to religion.

**4. What is the purpose of the simulation?** — Virk answers with two related questions hitting similar underlying themes (the costs and benefits of running simulations): *Why do we play video games?* and *Why do we run computer simulations?* **Games:** usually "because they are fun," and for realistic games like 3D MMORPGs, because they let us have experiences (and make choices) we can't have in the physical world — his joke that he can't fly on a dragon and shoot arrows at orcs in the "real" world; most of us can't (and wouldn't) steal cars and hurt people as in *Grand Theft Auto*; we can't yet explore other planets or fly spaceships with laser battles, but can in space-based MMORPGs. This extends to collaborative group and team experiences and one-on-one relationships: in *Second Life* (one of the first successful virtual worlds or metaverses), players logged in for social experiences and virtual relationships — including virtual jobs like nightclub bartender, paid peanuts in virtual currency but giving a sense of purpose and socializing missing from day jobs. Unlike shooters/action games that skewed male, virtual worlds were almost a fifty-fifty male/female split — "virtual social worlds." Acting as a "virtual anthropologist," Virk found married people getting married again online to someone across the world, others married IRL choosing promiscuity with many virtual partners, visitors to *Star Trek* and other themed sims, furries (anthropomorphic animal avatars — cats, dogs, etc.), and role-players of time periods or fictional worlds; a few met their virtual romantic partners IRL, "with mixed success." This leads to "at least a partial explanation" for the purpose of the RPG games: **to have experiences**. Per Chapter 12, the world is meant to be a kind of game according to religious traditions — the Koran's Arabic term *al ghouriri matau* ("enjoyable delusion"), the Hindu *maya* ("carefully crafted illusion") and *lila* (the divine play of the gods). Perhaps the experiences here — pleasure and pain, relationships and emotions — can't be had in a spiritual world. That includes unpleasant experiences (betrayal, breakups, anger, physical ailments, financial troubles, wars, suffering generally): Virk's framework for why hard experiences might be valuable, noting actors tend to win Oscars for roles that go through serious challenges and "a difficult but rewarding life." **Simulations:** we run them to figure out what might happen — the most likely, or most/least desirable, outcome — running multiple versions while changing variables. They're needed for chaotic and complex processes with many independent variables, which are *computationally irreducible*: you can't reduce them to a formula; the only way to know step 2,000 is to simulate step 1,999, which requires 1,998, and so on. Famous example: the three-body problem (three gravitationally bound bodies, like a triple-star system — stable configuration? collision? one flung into the galaxy? Hard to say without simulating). Coincidentally, when Virk interviewed Philip K. Dick's wife Tessa, she pointed him past the popularized quote from Dick's 1977 Metz, France speech ("We are living in a computer-programmed reality…") to the rest of the sentence: "and the only clue we have to it is when some variable is changed, and some alteration in our reality occurs." The speech, "If You Think This World Is Bad, You Should See Some of the Others," articulated Dick's theory of beings able to change the variables of our simulation and rerun timelines, each timeline like another world; Tessa said Dick came to believe *The Man in the High Castle* (Axis powers win WWII) was a real timeline that had been "unwound." Virk wrote *The Simulated Multiverse* as a thought experiment kicked off partly by that speech's core idea. **Key point:** in many cases the purpose of the simulation is to see the outcome of a computationally irreducible process — and the only way to do that is to run it. Both questions together give "a good first answer" for either the RPG or NPC version.

**5. When will we reach the simulation point?** — In the first edition he thought it was a few decades away at best, a century (or two) at worst — and even a thousand or ten thousand years is "the blink of an eye" in cosmological time; as long as we don't destroy ourselves, we're likely to get there. By normal science fiction assumptions (e.g., Ursula K. Le Guin's Hainish Cycle, with remnants of an advanced civilization around for a million years) or normal assumptions about our galaxy (assuming a physical universe), there would likely be civilizations a thousand or ten thousand years ahead of us — so it's reasonable to assume many civilizations in a physical galaxy have already reached the simulation point, validating Bostrom's conclusion. Virk's best estimate: **fifty to a hundred years** — though at the current pace of AGI development many experts believe it will be here much sooner, possibly in a few years. Given how fast video game and AI-based rendering is expanding, as soon as chatbots hit human-level conversational ability (which many think we already have as he writes in mid-2024), they can be paired with humanlike avatars and will feel real — passing the Metaverse Turing Test, like the virtual girlfriends in *Her* and *Blade Runner 2049*; in 2024 many people already turn to AI companions as virtual boyfriends/girlfriends (Chapter 5). AI will do many human tasks within this decade, but true humanlike AI — able to learn from its (virtual) environment and have self-awareness of its virtual body without major flaws — will take a few more years. Virk's wave model: **first wave** = rules-driven expert systems (in the 1980s they predicted something like AGI by decade's end); **second wave** (current) = data-driven, supervised and unsupervised training of models like LLMs, enabling ChatGPT or Google's Gemini to generate realistic text, plus diffusion techniques generating new images from training data — but LLMs "don't technically follow rules and can't calculate"; **third wave** (arriving soon) = machine learning combined with rules-based reasoning, an AI architecture analogous to a von Neumann architecture — in September 2024 OpenAI previewed its newest model claiming humanlike reasoning; the result will be agents that make judgment calls based on logic and implement existing and new rules. As the book goes to press, the CEOs of OpenAI and Anthropic have gone on record that AGI will be available as soon as 2025 or by 2027 ("you can be the judge of how close we have gotten"). Virk: at least five years, possibly a decade, to true AGI virtual characters passing the Metaverse Turing Test (some demos already look impressive). BCIs to read and write (stages 7 through 8) may be among the most difficult stages — at least another decade; downloadable consciousness is unclear in timing (or even feasibility, assumed possible for the discussion). Bottom line: **the simulation point by 2049 at the earliest and possibly up to 2075** (at most fifty years out); by even the most conservative estimate, by the end of the twenty-first century.

**6. Is life meaningless if we live in a simulation?** — Some see the hypothesis as nihilistic or solipsistic — a justification for self-serving or incredibly bad behavior. Virk (admitting he had to look the terms up) quotes definitions: *nihilism* — "rejecting all religious and moral principles in the belief that life is meaningless"; *solipsism* — "the quality of being very self-centered or selfish," or in philosophy "the view or theory that the self is all that can be known to exist." First response: you don't need simulation theory to justify self-centered behavior — just look at individuals, companies, and nation-states today. Both objections map onto the **NPC-vs-RPG axis** (0 to 100 percent), counted by number of players: *n = 0* — no live players (entities outside the simulation with avatars inside); 100 percent NPCs. *n = 1* — one player; everything happens for that person's benefit. *n = 2 to 99%* — multiple players and many NPCs. *n = 100%* — everyone is a source player, no NPCs. The nihilistic response assumes zero source players — everyone (including oneself) is just computer code, so nothing matters and you can do anything bad. The solipsistic view — "I am the only real person" — is the single-player game (as many console/computer games originally were), leading to the conclusion you can mistreat anyone, *Grand Theft Auto*-style, because they're probably NPCs. The height of this thinking is the **"Matrix defense"**: considered as a possible defense by Joshua Cooke, a nineteen-year-old who killed his parents and blamed watching *The Matrix* too many times — legal experts classify it as a kind of insanity defense, and it didn't work in court, for Cooke or anyone. (It relies on the NPC flavor.) Toward the RPG side — where the people you meet are (mostly) real source players — how you treat them matters. The RPG version resembles most world religions (we exist outside the physical world as souls, with avatars/characters inside the game), and in those religions killing is more or less the worst thing you can do on Earth — one of the Ten Commandments God gave to Moses, and uniquely grave as sin or bad karma elsewhere. Even in Hinduism and Buddhism — where you "wake up" into another life at death — killing is extremely bad karma affecting many lives to come. Virk retells the Buddhist story of the goat and the priest (which he first saw at the start of the movie *Little Buddha*, told by a Tibetan lama as a children's story): a non-Buddhist priest about to sacrifice a goat sees it smiling — it has been a goat for five hundred lifetimes and is finally going to "level up" (Virk's language) to a human life — then crying: "Because like you, I used to be a priest who would kill goats, and so I had to live five hundred lives as a goat." Main point: thinking an RPG simulation makes it OK to do bad things to people is equivalent to saying the world's religions make it OK — which means you've missed the main point of both (he's referring to what religions *say*, not what adherents *do* — adherents are just as likely to be self- or tribe-centered and commit atrocities as anyone). From Part III: the **life review** in near-death experiences supports the idea of a 3D world recorded and generated on demand, replayable scene by scene — and since you must relive each moment *from the point of view of other people*, "the real purpose of life is to be kind to other people (and other living things) and treat them well." NDE-ers report that the little moments — smiling at someone having a bad day, giving a compliment, watering a plant — often matter far more than "big things" like giving to charity; and mistreating others, emotionally or physically, is an experience you'll have to relive from their perspective. The NPC model is comparable to the materialist view (no afterlife; nothing you did will matter when you're gone) — yet many materialist, atheist scientists are among the most moral people Virk knows, showing you don't have to be religious to be moral. His verdict: the simulation hypothesis is not a license to treat people badly — under the RPG version, exactly the opposite. Also: virtual doesn't mean unreal or unimportant — learning a language in virtual reality would matter both inside and outside the virtual world; David Chalmers in *Reality+* made the point that "just because it's virtual doesn't mean it's not real" — you can have real conversations, and what you say and do matters. Finally, believing the physical world is a game can be *comforting* in difficult situations (physical, financial, or social): like Oscar-winning actors, you can believe you chose difficult roles or quests, learned what you needed, and are ready to move on.

**7. Is it simulations all the way down?** — On stacked or nested simulations (one simulation creating another inside it): if we are in a simulation and reach the simulation point, we'll be able to create sub-simulations / child simulations / "sims within sims." A complex subject, explored in science fiction, and again dependent on where our simulation falls on the NPC-vs-RPG axis. It is often raised to try to *disprove* Bostrom: his simplified argument has many parallel simulated worlds and one base reality (Chapter 6; his original paper favored ancestor simulations, though they needn't be); with nesting, each child creates children and grandchildren, producing a treelike structure — but the basic argument still holds, just nested rather than parallel. Complications: in classical computing, each child simulation has less computational power and theoretically less complexity than its parent (unclear for quantum computing, since the idea would be to use the *same* computing device as the parent universe). An argument *against* the simulation put forth by some, including physicist Sean Carroll: in such a tree most simulations sit at the bottom level as extremely low-fidelity simulations, so we should most likely be in an 8-bit world like old 8-bit games (*Pac-Man*, *Space Invaders*); since our world is complex, we're probably not in a simulation. A variant: infinite child simulations would exhaust base reality's computing power — a "stack overflow error," the classic bug from hand-written infinite recursive algorithms; Virk notes it happens not from running an operation infinitely but from each run consuming more memory that is never cleared (no garbage collection) — a memory leak. This could be a good reason infinite simulations aren't allowed. Unlike physicists, who often lean on infinity, even Bostrom relies on a finite (but large) number of simulations; child sims need not always be allowed to create grandchildren, and there could be limits — in fact, dwindling computing resources in lower simulations preventing us from creating child simulations "may even be a clue that we are in a simulation." Reaching the simulation point means creating simulations *indistinguishable from* physical reality — not necessarily as *complex*, thanks to optimization techniques (today's high-fidelity games and AI-generated videos approach photorealism without matching our world's complexity). And our universe's apparent complexity doesn't mean it matches base reality's: with 8 bits you get 256 color values; Virk remembers 1980s computers advertising "millions of colors" (lifelike imagery also needing pixels and simulated depth). Inside an 8-bit game you might never realize colors were missing unless you stepped outside the game. Has anyone stepped outside? NDE-ers (Chapter 12) report leaving their bodies for a world — some call it heaven; religious scholars, *ultimate reality*; in simulation-speak, base reality — with significantly more colors and sounds than our physical world; on return they say "that world" was "more real" than this one, more vivid and colorful, with shades of subtlety and frequency in music we cannot reproduce in our limited "fake" world. This implies base reality may have higher resolution and fidelity — though not necessarily multiple nested levels beyond it (there may be!); we could be in stacked simulations that only run several levels deep. Nested-simulation ability may itself be a key part of any simulated environment: circling back to purpose, the point could be to see how many nested simulations reach some key filter or singularity — get off the planet, blow themselves up, reach artificial intelligence, or reach the simulation point. Sci-fi example: *The Thirteenth Floor* (which Virk calls possibly a better representation of ancestor simulations than *The Matrix*): the simulation of 1999 was one of thousands, but the only one able to create its own nested simulations — and the simulators, living in the year 2024 (incidentally the year Virk writes this second edition), were going to shut it down for using too much computing power. The ability to affect the parent world's computational environment may be an important clue — perhaps what we do with our own nested simulations. **The twist:** normally, if we *can't* reach the simulation point, the same logic says we're not in a simulation. But stacked-simulation rules belie this: the simulators may have limited our ability to reach it, or there may be no computing power remaining at our level — "even if we can't reach the simulation point, we might still be in a simulation!"

## Concepts

- **Conservation of information** — a modern physics principle Virk contrasts with the classical conservation laws (energy, momentum) he learned as a kid; physicists now ask how much information is in the world, whether it can be destroyed, and what happens if information is lost.
- **Simulation point (Virk's trilemma terminology)** — his replacement for Bostrom's "posthuman" wording; broader because it includes not only simulations with posthuman AI but also the ability to include avatars of "real" characters. He reformulates the trilemma as conditional probabilities of being simulated (0%, ~<1%, ~99+%) rather than three options summing to 100 percent.
- **NPC-vs-RPG axis by player count (n)** — a formalization of the axis as the number of live players: n = 0 (all NPCs), n = 1 (single-player, everything for one person's benefit), n = 2 to 99% (mixed), n = 100% (all source players, no NPCs). Nihilism maps to n = 0; solipsism maps to n = 1.
- **Computational irreducibility** — property of chaotic/complex processes that cannot be reduced to a simple formula; the only way to know the state at step 2,000 is to simulate every step up to it. The three-body problem is the famous example; this is why simulations exist at all.
- **Matrix defense** — a legal defense (classified by legal experts as a kind of insanity defense) considered by Joshua Cooke, who killed his parents and blamed watching *The Matrix* too many times; it has never worked in court, and logically relies on the NPC flavor of the hypothesis.
- **Metaverse Turing Test** — the bar at which human-level conversational AI paired with humanlike avatars "will feel real," like the virtual girlfriends in *Her* and *Blade Runner 2049* (used here without re-definition; timed at five-plus years away for true AGI virtual characters).
- **Three waves of AI** — first: rules-driven expert systems (1980s); second (current): data-driven supervised/unsupervised training of LLMs plus diffusion techniques for images, where LLMs don't follow rules and can't calculate; third (arriving): machine learning combined with rules-based reasoning, analogous to a von Neumann architecture, yielding agents that make judgment calls.
- **Stacked / nested (child) simulations** — simulations created inside simulations, forming a treelike structure of child/grandchild sims instead of Bostrom's parallel ones; the basic simulation argument still holds under nesting.
- **Stack overflow error / memory leak / garbage collection** — programming concepts used as an argument for why infinite nested simulations may be disallowed: recursion exhausts memory only when each run consumes more memory that is never cleared (no garbage collection) — a memory leak causing a stack overflow.
- **Ultimate reality / base reality** — what NDE-ers call heaven and religious scholars call ultimate reality is, "in simulation speak," base reality — reported as having more colors, sounds, vividness, and musical subtlety than our world, implying higher resolution and fidelity than our level.
- **Al ghouriri matau / maya / lila** — the Koran's Arabic term for the game ("enjoyable delusion"); the Hindu terms for "carefully crafted illusion" and the divine play of the gods, respectively (restated from Chapter 12).
- **Nihilism / solipsism** — quoted definitions: "rejecting all religious and moral principles in the belief that life is meaningless"; "the quality of being very self-centered or selfish," or philosophically "the view or theory that the self is all that can be known to exist."
