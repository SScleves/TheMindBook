# Ch 3 — The Holographic Model and Psychology

## In one paragraph
Talbot argues that the holographic model — Bohm's implicate order plus the interconnectedness it implies — makes sense of a whole family of psychological phenomena that conventional psychology cannot explain. Consciousness itself, per Bohm, exemplifies "undivided and flowing movement": thoughts are like ripples and whirlpools in a stream, and deep down "the consciousness of mankind is one." On this basis the chapter reinterprets Jung's collective unconscious and archetypes, Ullman's ESP-dream results and the wisdom of dreams, psychotic and manic experience as unmanaged glimpses of the implicate order, Wolf's theory of lucid dreams as "real-image" holograms and visits to parallel universes, and — most dramatically — Grof's thirty-plus years of LSD and holotropic research, in which patients seemed to access womb memories, ancestral and racial memories, other species' consciousness, and even other universes. The chapter closes with thought vortices (Shainberg), multiple personality disorder as holographic fragmentation of the self, and Jungian synchronicity, which physicists now take seriously — Davies calls nonlocal quantum effects "a form of synchronicity," and Peat reads synchronicities as "flaws" in the fabric of reality revealing that mind and matter are not divided at the deepest level. The upshot, per Talbot: the psyche is "essentially commensurate with the whole universe" (Grof's epigraph), and objective reality is more dreamlike than we suspect.

## Core claims
- Talbot argues that consciousness is a perfect example of Bohm's undivided flowing movement: thoughts unfold from a deeper, not precisely definable reality, like ripples, eddies, and whirlpools in a stream — some stable and recurring, some evanescent.
- Talbot argues that Jung's collective unconscious, though embraced by untold thousands of psychologists and psychiatrists, has no mechanism in our current understanding of the universe — but the holographic model's infinite interconnectedness explains it: "we are beings without borders."
- Anderson's view is that we aren't all walking encyclopedias because of "personal resonance": like a tuning fork, an individual consciousness can only tap implicate-order information directly relevant to its own memories.
- Ullman's view is that ESP dream results evidence Bohm-style interconnectedness, and that the deeper wisdom of dreams — which honestly and repeatedly depict our failings — may be a "natural transformation of the implicate into the explicate," striving to preserve species-connectedness, not just individual welfare. If true, this inverts the traditional psychoanalytic view that dream content ascends from a primitive substratum of the personality.
- Both Bohm and Pribram have noted that the experiences mystics have reported through the ages — cosmic oneness with the universe, unity with all life — sound very much like descriptions of the implicate order, and suggest mystics may somehow peer beyond ordinary explicate reality and glimpse its deeper, more holographic qualities.
- Ullman's view is that psychotics genuinely glimpse the holographic level of reality but cannot order the experience rationally, so their reports are "tragic parodies" of mystics' reports; manic-depressives are even more deeply immersed in it than schizophrenics.
- Wolf's view is that all dreams are internal holograms; ordinary dreams are virtual images while lucid dreams are real images (converging waves), which explains their vividness — and lucid dreams (perhaps all dreams) are actually visits to parallel universes, "smaller holograms within the... cosmic hologram."
- Grof's view, after thousands of LSD sessions, is that the data "indicate an urgent need for a drastic revision of the existing paradigms for psychology, psychiatry, medicine, and possibly science in general," and that the holographic model — boundaries as illusion, part/whole non-distinction, enfolded space-time, enormous information capacity, composite and multiple-image holograms — is the framework that accounts for transpersonal experiences.
- Levenson's view is that all psychoanalytic approaches are "purely ceremonial"; therapeutic change actually comes from resonance between therapist and patient calling forth holograms buried in the patient's psyche, like a laser of the right frequency retrieving an image from a multiple-image hologram.
- Shainberg's view is that Bohm's thoughts-as-vortices should be taken literally: stable thought vortices explain fixed opinions, blocked creativity, feelings of disconnection, and even the nuclear arms race, whereas a child's open aliveness shows unimpeded unfolding-enfolding consciousness.
- Talbot argues that MPD is perhaps the ultimate example of Bohmian fragmentation — the psyche splits into smaller wholes, not jagged shards, which itself suggests a holographic process — and that per-personality brain-wave changes (a global brain property) hint that the "self" is a hologram the brain can shuttle between like a slide projector.
- Jung's view is that synchronicities are not chance but relate to the psychological processes of those who experience them, requiring a new "acausal connecting principle"; now that nonlocal connections are established, some physicists are looking again — Davies via nonlocal quantum effects, Peat via the implicate order.
- Peat's view is that synchronicities are momentary fissures giving a glimpse of the unitary order under nature; their scarcity in our lives measures how far we have fragmented ourselves from the general field of consciousness. If subjective and objective are ultimately undivided, objective reality is more like a dream — though not an illusion, and individuality is not lost, "any more than the image of a rose is lost once it is recorded in a piece of holographic film."

## Concepts
- **Collective unconscious** — Jung's concept of a shared source, common to all people, from which myths, dreams, hallucinations, and religious visions all spring; symbols in patients' dreams and hallucinations that resemble the world's great mythologies rather than personal history.
- **Archetypes** — Jung's name for images bubbling up from the collective unconscious; he believed them so ancient that it is as if each of us carries "the memory of a two-million-year-old man" in the depths of the unconscious mind.
- **Personal resonance** — Anderson's term for the selective process by which we can only tap implicate-order information directly relevant to our own memories, likened to a tuning fork resonating only with another fork of similar structure, shape, and size.
- **Undivided and flowing movement** — Bohm's characterization of consciousness: a deeper, more fundamental reality out of which thoughts and ideas unfold like ripples, eddies, and whirlpools in a stream.
- **Lucid dreams** — dreams in which the dreamer keeps full waking consciousness, knows he or she is dreaming, can often control the dream (turn nightmares pleasant, change settings, summon people), and experiences unusual vividness and vitality (eerily solid marble floors, dazzling flowers); researchers believe they may lead to new ways to stimulate personal growth, enhance self-confidence, promote mental and physical health, and facilitate creative problem solving.
- **Virtual image vs. real image** — a piece of holographic film generates two images: a virtual image apparently behind the film (an illusion, no extension in space, like a mirror image) and a real image that comes to focus in front of the film, formed by converging waves, possessing real extension in space but invisible in empty air unless dust or smoke passes through it.
- **Parallel universe awareness** — Wolf's proposed better name for lucid dreaming: parallel universes "arise as other images in the hologram," and dreams are visits to them.
- **Transpersonal** — Grof's term for experiences in which consciousness transcends the customary boundaries of the personality.
- **Transpersonal psychology** — the branch of psychology Grof founded in the late 1960s with like-minded professionals including psychologist and educator Abraham Maslow, to study such phenomena.
- **Holotropic state of consciousness** — a nonordinary state in which it is possible to access the holographic labyrinth connecting all aspects of existence: one's biological, psychological, racial, and spiritual history; past, present, and future of the world; other levels of reality.
- **Holotropic therapy** — the Grofs' nondrug technique for inducing such states using only rapid and controlled breathing, evocative music, and massage/body work.
- **Vortices of thought** — Shainberg's literal reading of Bohm: ideas and opinions as stable vortices that can become cemented in consciousness, dominating behavior and blocking assimilation of new information.
- **Multiple personality disorder (MPD)** — a syndrome in which two or more distinct personalities inhabit a single body; "multiples" often don't know their condition and experience it as amnesia, confusion, or blackouts; most average eight to thirteen personalities, "super-multiples" over a hundred.
- **Fragmentation** — Bohm's notion (applied here) that dividing wholeness eventually proves destructive; MPD is presented as its ultimate psychological example.
- **Synchronicity** — Jung's term for coincidences so unusual and meaningful they can hardly be chance; the noteworthy class relates to events deep in the psyche and peaks at periods of emotional intensity and transformation.
- **Acausal connecting principle** — the new principle Jung proposed to account for synchronicities, since psyche-to-world causation was inconceivable classically; nonlocal effects are likewise not cause-and-effect and therefore acausal.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
1. **Jung's solar-phallus patient (1906)** — While making rounds, Jung found a young paranoid schizophrenic staring at the sun and moving his head side to side; the man said he was looking at the sun's penis, whose side-to-side movement caused the wind to blow. Years later Jung read a translation of a two-thousand-year-old Persian religious text of rituals and invocations designed to bring on visions, one of which said a participant looking at the sun would see a tube hanging from it whose side-to-side movement caused the wind. Since it was extremely unlikely the man had contact with the text, Jung concluded the vision bubbled up from the collective unconscious, not the personal unconscious. Talbot uses this as the founding example the holographic model can finally explain.
2. **Anderson's tuning-fork analogy (Robert M. Anderson, Jr., Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute, Troy, New York)** — An illustration, not an experiment: a vibrating tuning fork sets up vibration only in a fork of similar structure, shape, and size; hence "personal resonance" limits which of the almost infinite implicate images an individual can access. It explains why enlightened persons glimpsing unitive consciousness centuries ago didn't write out relativity theory — they weren't studying physics in a context similar to Einstein's.
3. **Maimonides ESP dream experiments — Tamayo's *Animals* (Ullman, Dream Laboratory, Maimonides Medical Center, Brooklyn; 1960s–70s)** — Typical protocol: a paid volunteer claiming no psychic ability slept in a lab room while a person in another room concentrated on a randomly selected painting, trying to make the volunteer dream its image. Results were sometimes inconclusive, but with Tamayo's *Animals* (two dogs flashing their teeth, howling over a pile of bones) the subject dreamed of a banquet without enough meat where everyone warily eyed one another while greedily eating their allotted portions. Talbot presents the Maimonides studies as some of the best empirical evidence that in dreams we communicate in ways that cannot presently be explained.
4. **Maimonides ESP dream experiments — Chagall's *Paris from a Window*** — Target was a brightly colored painting of a man looking out a window at the Paris skyline, with a cat with a human face, small figures of men flying through the air, and a chair covered with flowers. Over several nights the subject dreamed repeatedly of things French — French architecture, a French policeman's hat, a man in French attire gazing at "layers" of a French village — plus apparent references to the vibrant colors and odd features: bees flying around flowers, and a brightly colored Mardi Gras-type celebration with costumes and masks.
5. **Ullman's clinical observation of dream wisdom** — In his psychoanalytic practice Ullman found that even patients who were mean, selfish, arrogant, exploitative, and manipulative while awake — with fragmented, dehumanized relationships — had dreams that invariably depicted their failings honestly, with metaphors designed to prod them gently toward self-awareness. When a patient failed to accept a truth about himself, it surfaced again and again in dreams in different metaphorical guises linked to different past experiences. Ullman reads this self-monitoring as nature striving for species survival and species-connectedness, and suggests dreams may be a bridge from the implicate (an infinite information source) to the explicate.
6. **Studies of schizophrenic logic (cited by Ullman)** — Studies have shown schizophrenics often treat the converse of any relation as identical to the relation: "event A follows event B" equals "event B follows event A"; if a man's head is above his shoulders, his shoulders are also above his head. All points in time are viewed as equal; spatial relations lose meaning — like the image in holographic film, things no longer have precise locations. Ullman reads this, plus schizophrenics' oceanic oneness feelings, loss of self-other boundaries, belief their thoughts aren't private and they can read others' thoughts, and classing things into ever larger subclasses, as expressing the holographic quality of the reality they find themselves in — glimpses of the implicate without the ability to order them rationally.
7. **Manic-depression as deep holographic involvement (Ullman)** — Whereas the schizophrenic gets only whiffs of the holographic order, the manic is deeply involved and grandiosely identifies with its infinite potential, unable to keep up with the overwhelming flow of thoughts, forced to "lie, dissemble, and manipulate" to accommodate his expansive vista — mostly chaos with occasional creative outbursts — then becomes depressed on returning from the "surreal vacation" to everyday life. Ullman adds that normal dreamers escape these effects because we leave dream logic behind on waking and possess a natural protective mechanism limiting contact with the implicate.
8. **Wolf's lucid-dream talk (physicist Fred Alan Wolf, 1987 annual meeting of the Association for the Study of Dreams, Washington, D.C.)** — Wolf, an occasional lucid dreamer, argued all dreams are internal holograms: ordinary dreams are less-vivid virtual images (diverging waves), but the brain can also generate real images (converging waves), which is what happens in lucid dreaming — a "viewer" at the focus is bathed in and contained by the scene, hence "lucid." Like Pribram, Wolf holds the mind creates the illusion of "out there" via the processes Bekesy studied; the same processes let lucid dreamers create subjective realities as tangible as objective ones, making him question whether lucid dreams should be considered subjective at all. He postulates lucid dreams (perhaps all dreams) are visits to parallel universes — smaller holograms within the cosmic hologram — and calls the skill "parallel universe awareness."
9. **Grof's Prague LSD research (Psychiatric Research Institute, Prague, Czechoslovakia; begun 1950s)** — Investigating clinical/therapeutic uses of LSD, Grof found not the expected recurring stress reaction but a definite continuity across each patient's sessions: "a successive unfolding of deeper and deeper levels of the unconscious." Serial LSD sessions expedited psychotherapy, shortened treatment time, unearthed traumatic memories that had haunted people for years, and sometimes even cured serious conditions such as schizophrenia — then patients moved beyond their illnesses into areas uncharted by Western psychology.
10. **Womb-memory experiences under LSD (Grof)** — Patients relived being in the womb. Grof first thought them imagined, but the embryological knowledge in the descriptions often far exceeded the patients' education: accurate accounts of the mother's heart sounds, acoustic phenomena in the peritoneal cavity, specific details of placental blood circulation, even cellular and biochemical processes — plus the mother's important thoughts, feelings, and physical traumas during pregnancy. Grof verified assertions where possible by questioning the mother and others involved. Psychiatrists, psychologists, and biologists in the program (all participating therapists had to undergo several LSD psychotherapy sessions themselves) were similarly astonished at the experiences' apparent authenticity.
11. **The prehistoric-reptile patient (Grof)** — A female patient became convinced she had assumed the identity of a female prehistoric reptile, gave a richly detailed account of being encapsuled in that form, and noted that the most sexually arousing part of the male's anatomy was a patch of colored scales on the side of its head. She had no prior knowledge of such things; a zoologist later confirmed to Grof that in certain reptile species colored head areas do play an important role as sexual-arousal triggers.
12. **The mother-at-age-three experience (Grof)** — A woman experienced being her own mother at age three, accurately described a frightening event that had befallen her mother then, plus a precise description of the house her mother lived in and the white pinafore she was wearing — all details the mother later confirmed and admitted she had never talked about. Other patients gave equally accurate accounts of events befalling ancestors decades and even centuries before.
13. **Racial and collective memories under LSD (Grof)** — Individuals of Slavic origin experienced participating in the conquests of Genghis Khan's Mongolian hordes, dancing in trance with the Kalahari bushmen, undergoing Australian aborigine initiation rites, and dying as Aztec sacrificial victims — with obscure historical facts at odds with the patient's education, race, and exposure. One uneducated patient gave a richly detailed account of Egyptian embalming and mummification: form and meaning of amulets and sepulchral boxes, materials for fixing the mummy cloth, size and shape of mummy bandages, and other esoteric facets of Egyptian funeral services. Others tuned into Far East cultures, describing what it is like to have a Japanese, Chinese, or Tibetan psyche and relating Taoist or Buddhist teachings.
14. **The apparently limitless range of LSD experiences (Grof)** — Subjects seemed able to know what it was like to be every animal and even plant on the tree of evolution; to be a blood cell, an atom, a thermonuclear process inside the sun, the consciousness of the entire planet, even of the entire cosmos; to transcend space and time, occasionally relating uncannily accurate precognitive information; and sometimes to encounter nonhuman intelligences — discarnate beings, spirit guides from "higher planes of consciousness," and other suprahuman entities.
15. **The Ladislav/Kromeriz case (Grof)** — A young man being treated for depression found himself, in one session, in an eerie luminescent dimension crowded with sensed but unseen discarnate beings. A presence communicated telepathically, asking him to contact a couple in the Moravian city of Kromeriz to tell them their son Ladislav was well taken care of and doing all right — giving name, street address, and telephone number. The information meant nothing to Grof or the patient, but Grof, despite fearing colleagues' jokes, phoned the number and asked for Ladislav; the woman answering began to cry and said their son had passed away three weeks earlier. Talbot presents this as one of the most unnerving pieces of evidence of travel to other levels of reality.
16. **Maryland Psychiatric Research Center studies (1960s onward)** — Grof moved to the U.S. to a position at the center, which ran controlled studies of LSD's psychotherapeutic applications — both on individuals with mental disorders and on "normal" volunteers: doctors, nurses, painters, musicians, philosophers, scientists, priests, and theologians. The same phenomena recurred again and again — as if LSD gave consciousness access to "a kind of infinite subway system... that literally connected everything in the universe with everything else." After personally guiding over three thousand LSD sessions (each lasting at least five hours) and studying records of more than two thousand colleague-conducted sessions, Grof concluded a drastic paradigm revision is urgently needed.
17. **Composite holograms as a model of archetypes (Grof)** — Holography can build up a sequence of exposures (e.g., every member of a large family) on one piece of film, yielding a genuinely composite image representing all of them at once — "an exquisite model" of archetypal images like the Cosmic Man, Woman, Mother, Father, Lover, Trickster, Fool, or Martyr. If each exposure is taken at a slightly different angle, the film instead yields a series of holographic images that appear to flow into one another — modeling the visionary tendency of countless images to unfold in rapid sequence, each dissolving into the next. Grof also notes a single LSD image can holographically embody a whole personality-constellation (attitude to life, childhood trauma, self-esteem, feelings about parents and marriage), with each small part of the scene containing further floods of data retrievable by free association on minuscule details.
18. **Holotropic therapy (Stanislav and Christina Grof)** — A nondrug technique using only rapid and controlled breathing, evocative music, and massage/body work to induce holotropic states. Thousands of workshop attendees report experiences every bit as spectacular and emotionally profound as the LSD subjects' — Grof's most remarkable discovery, per Talbot, since it shows the phenomena need no drug. Methods detailed in Grof's book *The Adventure of Self-Discovery*.
19. **Levenson on therapeutic change (psychiatrist Edgar A. Levenson, New York)** — Observing that transformative change in psychotherapy happens regardless of the therapist's technique or psychoanalytic approach, Levenson concludes the approaches are purely ceremonial and change comes from resonance: the therapist resonates with something the patient already unconsciously knows, a "huge, three-dimensional, spatially coded representation of the patient's experience" builds up until an "overload" when everything falls into place. He models these representations as holograms buried in the psyche, called forth like a same-frequency laser retrieving an image from a multiple-image hologram.
20. **The Great Red Spot and thought vortices (psychiatrist David Shainberg, associate dean, Postgraduate Psychoanalytic Program, William Alanson White Institute of Psychiatry, New York)** — Studies show vortices are remarkably stable: Jupiter's Great Red Spot, a gas vortex over 25,000 miles wide, has remained intact since first discovered 300 years ago. Shainberg argues the same stability cements thought vortices (ideas, opinions) in consciousness, dominating behavior, blocking new ideas, causing repetition and disconnection — and may even explain the nuclear arms race as a vortex of greed among people isolated in separate selves. Counter-illustrations: children, who have not had time to form vortices, show open, flexible, "sparkling aliveness" (unimpeded unfolding-enfolding); and conversational behavior as a diagnostic — people with set beliefs defend opinions and never change with new information, while the open person explores conversational interactions.
21. **MPD statistics and symptomatology** — Most multiples average eight to thirteen personalities (super-multiples over a hundred); 97 percent have histories of severe childhood trauma, often monstrous psychological, physical, and sexual abuse — leading researchers to see the disorder as the psyche's way of parceling out unendurable pain among several personalities. Side effects include depression, anxiety and panic attacks, phobias, heart and respiratory problems, unexplained nausea, migrainelike headaches, and self-mutilation tendencies. Most multiples are diagnosed between ages twenty-eight and thirty-five — a regular-as-clockwork "coincidence" suggesting an inner alarm — and multiples reaching their forties undiagnosed frequently report sensing that without help soon, recovery would be lost. Talbot: despite temporary advantages, well-being and perhaps survival still depend on wholeness — Bohm's claim that fragmentation always eventually proves destructive. Notably, the fragments are not jagged shards but smaller wholes, complete and self-sustaining, related to the original personality's dynamics — itself suggesting a holographic process.
22. **Per-personality physiological changes in MPD (Frank Putnam, National Institutes of Health; figure redrawn from Bennett G. Braun's article in the *American Journal of Clinical Hypnosis*)** — Each of a multiple's personalities has a different brain-wave pattern — surprising because, as Putnam points out, a person's brain-wave pattern normally doesn't change even in extreme emotion. Blood flow patterns, muscle tone, heart rate, posture, and even allergies also change as a multiple shifts selves. Since brain-wave patterns are a global property of the brain, not confined to any neuron group, Talbot suggests the "self" may be a hologram, with the brain shuttling slide-projector-like between whole stored personalities (fig. 10 shows brain-wave patterns of four subpersonalities). The physiological implications are deferred to the next chapter.
23. **Talbot's Buffalo Bill synchronicity (January 1983)** — While doing push-ups with a game show on TV, Talbot suddenly shouted "Buffalo Bill!" — his unconscious had answered the host's question "What other name was William Frederick Cody known by?" though he wasn't consciously attending. Hours later a friend phoned with a theater-trivia dispute: "Is it true that John Barrymore's dying words were, 'Aren't you the illegitimate son of Buffalo Bill?'" Later that day a *Smithsonian* magazine arrived with a lead article, "The Last of the Great Scouts Is Back Again" — about Buffalo Bill. (Talbot couldn't answer the trivia question and still doesn't know.) He classes this as a synchronicity meaningful only in its improbability, distinguishing it from the deeper psyche-related kind.
24. **Jung's scarab story** — Jung was treating a woman whose staunchly rational approach blocked therapy. After frustrating sessions she reported a dream of a scarab beetle; Jung knew the scarab represented rebirth in Egyptian mythology and suspected her unconscious was announcing a psychological rebirth. Just as he was about to say so, something tapped the window — a gold-green scarab (the only time one ever appeared at Jung's window). He let it fly into the room while presenting his interpretation; the stunned woman tempered her excessive rationality and her response to therapy improved. Jung noticed such coincidences almost always accompanied emotional intensity and transformation (belief changes, insights, deaths, births, profession changes) and peaked just as a new realization was about to surface.
25. **Meier's Wuchang hospital case (Zurich psychiatrist Carl Alfred Meier, longtime Jung associate)** — An American woman surgeon, seriously depressed, traveled from Wuchang, China (where she had headed a mission hospital for twenty years and become an expert in Chinese philosophy) to be treated by Meier. She dreamed of the hospital with one wing destroyed; Meier read it as her losing her sense of self / American identity, advised return to the States, and her depression vanished as predicted. Before departing she made a detailed sketch of the crumbling hospital. Years later the Japanese attacked China and bombed Wuchang Hospital; she sent Meier a *Life* magazine with a double-page photograph of the partially destroyed hospital — identical to her drawing of nine years earlier. Talbot: the dream's symbolic personal message had spilled beyond her psyche into physical reality.
26. **Pauli–Jung collaboration and physicists' reappraisal** — When Jung proposed the acausal connecting principle most physicists dismissed it, though the eminent physicist Wolfgang Pauli thought it important enough to coauthor a book with Jung, *The Interpretation and Nature of the Psyche*. Now that nonlocal connections are established, some physicists are looking again: Paul Davies calls nonlocal quantum effects "a form of synchronicity" — correlations between events for which any causal linkage is forbidden.
27. **Peat's boss-dinner dream analogy (F. David Peat)** — Thought experiment: dreaming of dinner with your boss, you're served an unpleasant dish which the waiter says is named "Your Boss" — an "inner" feeling spilling into the dream's "outer" reality. On waking, the synchronicity is no mystery: there was never any division between inner self and outer dream reality, and the dream objects' separateness was an illusion produced by the unbroken wholeness of your unconscious mind. If mind and world are undivided in the implicate, the same holds for objective reality — which doesn't make the universe an illusion (both implicate and explicate create reality) or erase individuality: we are vortices in a river, unique but inseparable from the flow of nature.

## Thread

Talbot's closing "full circle" states the two-way thesis: consciousness contains the whole of objective reality, and the material universe contains the innermost processes of consciousness. It explicitly sets up the next chapter on health (via MPD's physiological changes) and promises the nature-of-dreaming/parallel-universe ideas will be explored in greater depth later in the book.
