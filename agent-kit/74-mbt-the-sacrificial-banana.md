# Ch 3 — The Sacrificial Banana

## In one paragraph
Campbell narrates his TM (Transcendental Meditation) initiation: a week after signing up, he arrives at "this seedy little house" carrying the required clean handkerchief and a banana, feeling foolish and suspecting the whole thing is "either goofy or fraudulent." He interrogates the ritual trappings — the fruit offering, the Sanskrit ceremony, the "secret" mantra — and reduces each to a mundane explanation (the fruit is eaten; the ceremony is an obligatory formality he tolerates only after stipulating he need not profess beliefs or make promises). Then the anomaly: told to practice the mantra silently for twenty minutes, he drifts into "a pleasant nowhere," discovers forty-five minutes have passed with consciousness unbroken, and finds his body temporarily refuses to respond to his will — heavy, "thick, viscous, semi-solid." He calls this his "first trip into inner space." The chapter dramatizes the stance Campbell wants the reader to adopt: aggressive skepticism combined with open-mindedness, and commitment to follow undeniable firsthand experience through to the end.

## Core claims
- Campbell claims he approached TM as a hard-nosed skeptic who found the ceremonial trappings (fruit, handkerchief, Sanskrit chanting) irrational, "irrelevant," and at odds with the rational image TM had presented at its public lecture.
- Campbell claims the participants around him accepted the ritual uncritically — doing what they were told without thinking about it — which he found difficult to understand.
- Campbell claims he decided humoring the organization's request (showing up with the banana and handkerchief) was the better strategy, since the request seemed harmless enough and, more importantly, they already had his $20.
- Campbell claims he agreed to go along with the ceremony itself only on condition that he not have to profess any beliefs or make any promises — a condition the initiator accepted with amusement.
- Campbell claims that in his very first mantra practice he expected nothing, yet undeniably experienced "something dramatically unusual": twenty minutes of practice stretched unnoticed to forty-five, and he could not initially move his body afterward.
- Campbell claims he had not fallen asleep — he was "positive that my consciousness had been unbroken" — and rules out fatigue as an explanation (he had gotten more sleep than usual the night before).
- Campbell claims this was his "first trip into inner space" — something strange experienced within the same hour as doing something "goofy."
- Campbell claims his response to the anomaly was to remain skeptical (which "was and is" his nature) while also remaining open-minded — the epistemic posture he models throughout.
- Campbell claims his commitment to see the TM adventure through came from three things: the undeniable unusual experience, his Scottish ancestry (i.e., thrift), and the fact that his $20 was irretrievably gone.

## Concepts
- **TM (Transcendental Meditation)** — The meditation organization/technique Campbell has paid $20 to learn: a public lecture, an initiation with fruit-and-handkerchief offering and Sanskrit ceremony, a personal mantra, and four one-hour training sessions. Campbell treats it in this chapter purely as the vehicle of his first experiment, not as a doctrine he accepts.
- **mantra** — Campbell's "personal meditation sound," delivered by the initiator after a brief chanted ceremony; a "magic Sanskrit" word-sound to be repeated silently over and over in the mind. Campbell notes wryly it is his "very own personal (probably shared by millions) secret mantra."
- **the ceremony / offering** — The required ritual: kneeling on a rug before "a poor man's altar" (banana on a clean white handkerchief over a small plate) while the initiator chants Sanskrit. The fresh fruit "was to represent the traditional offering a student would bring to his teacher"; Campbell learns the initiates simply eat it.
- **inner space** — Campbell's name for the interior, subjective territory entered through meditation; his forty-five-minute drift into "a pleasant nowhere" is retrospectively labeled "my first trip into inner space." Not further defined here — the chapter is its first concrete instance.
- **skepticism + open-mindedness** — The paired attitude Campbell explicitly claims as his nature and models here: doubting every claim and ritual, yet refusing to dismiss an undeniable firsthand experience, instead following it up with growing curiosity.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The banana-and-handkerchief arrival** — Campbell shows up at the "seedy little house" with the required clean handkerchief and banana, feeling "more than a little foolish"; he had considered showing up without them "just to see what would happen" but decided humoring the organization was the better strategy since the request was harmless and "they already had my $20." Shows his experimental instinct even toward ritual, and his cost-benefit pragmatism.
- **Questioning the clueless fellow initiate** — Campbell derisively asks the first person he sees "What's with the banana?" The man — "as clueless as I" — had never thought to question the meditation-fruit-handkerchief relationship; his answer is an exaggerated shrug and a big broad smile, which reminds Campbell of Alfred E. Neuman. For Campbell this illustrates people doing what they are told "without thinking about it," uncritical acceptance he cannot understand.
- **The rotting-fruit hypothesis and its deflation** — Campbell imagines "a huge pile of rotting symbolic fruit — a sad wasteful testament to the uselessness of ceremonial gestures"; the initiator's answer ("We eat it") converts the mystery into free groceries, a one-banana surcharge on the $20 fee. Campbell would have preferred straightforwardness but lets it go — an example of him testing and discharging a suspicion rather than nursing it.
- **The well-dressed neighbor and the making-ends-meet hypothesis** — The man standing next to Campbell was about his own age, well dressed, soft-spoken, and seemed intelligent and serious — "he didn't look like a fruit hustler." Reasoning that the fault might lie with the organizers rather than the ordinary participants, Campbell hypothesizes that "perhaps the people who set this business up knew that they would have a difficult time making ends meet," and lets the matter go — a second, distinct pass at explaining the fruit racket before he moves on to the ceremony itself.
- **The initiation ceremony itself** — Kneeling side by side on a small kitchen rug before the banana-on-handkerchief "altar," the initiator chants "a little Sanskrit (at least that is what he said it was) mumbo jumbo" for about a minute, pauses thirty seconds, then gives Campbell his mantra, which he memorizes in under three minutes. Campbell consents only after securing that he need not profess beliefs or make promises.
- **The first practice session — the anomaly** — Told to repeat the mantra silently for twenty minutes, Campbell expects nothing but is determined "to follow directions and give it a fair try." Time passes slowly, then he drifts into "a pleasant nowhere"; noticing later initiates leaving, he checks his watch twice: forty-five minutes have passed. Trying to stand, he finds his body "simply refused to respond to my will and effort — that had never happened before," limbs heavy as after long sleep, though consciousness was unbroken. He forces his "thick, viscous, semi-solid body" to stand and walk. He considers and rejects the fatigue explanation (more sleep than usual the night before), concluding "This is interesting... very interesting."
- **The commitment calculus** — What keeps him in the program for the remaining four sessions: the undeniably unusual experience, his Scottish ancestry, and the unrecoverable $20 — a self-deprecating model of how skeptics actually get drawn into investigation.

## Key quotes
- "As per the directions, on the appointed day I showed up at this seedy little house with a clean handkerchief and a banana." — the absurd opening image of the initiation.
- "Anybody who needed fresh fruit and a handkerchief to teach me how to meditate was likely to be either goofy or fraudulent." — his skeptical prior going in.
- "Their inane request seemed harmless enough, and more importantly, they already had my $20." — why he humors the ritual.
- "'We eat it,' he said with a smile." — the initiator deflating the mystery of the fruit offerings.
- "I will go along as long as I do not have to profess any beliefs or make any promises." — the condition under which Campbell submits to the ceremony.
- "I paid $20 and a banana for this magic Sanskrit mantra? What could repeating some word-sound do for my mind?" — his expectation of nothing before the first practice.
- "The twenty minutes of practice went by slowly at first, but then I drifted off into a pleasant nowhere." — the time-loss experience begins.
- "My body simply refused to respond to my will and effort — that had never happened before." — the bodily anomaly on trying to stand.
- "I was positive that my consciousness had been unbroken — I had not fallen asleep." — his ruling-out of the sleep explanation.
- "I had done something strange, or at least goofy, and experienced something weird (my first trip into inner space) all in the same hour." — naming the experience.
- "I remained skeptical, which was (and is) my nature, but I also remained open minded." — the epistemic stance the book keeps invoking.

## Thread
This chapter follows the setup in which Campbell, a young hard-science physicist, has signed up (and paid $20) for TM, and it delivers the trilogy's first empirical data point: an unexpected, firsthand altered-state experience ("my first trip into inner space") that survives his attempts to explain it away. Its function in the argument is autobiographical epistemology — establishing that Campbell's later Big TOE is rooted not in belief but in skeptical, open-minded engagement with anomalous personal experience, and that ritual trappings can be discarded while the underlying phenomenon remains. It sets up the four remaining TM training sessions and the deepening exploration of inner space that the following chapters of Section 1 ("Delusion or Knowledge") pursue.
