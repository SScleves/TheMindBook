# Preface: A Tour of Silicon Valley with Jacques Vallee

## In one paragraph
Pasulka opens the book with an ethnographic scene: Jacques Vallee — whom she calls the "father of the modern study of UFOs and an early visionary of the internet" — drives her and her colleague Robbie Graham on a personal tour of Silicon Valley, telling them "There are many secrets in this valley." She sketches Vallee's "orthodox history" (NASA Mars mapping, ARPANET engineering, venture capital, consulting for Spielberg's *Close Encounters of the Third Kind*) and his "unorthodox history" (work alongside SRI scientists whose declassified programs studied remote viewing, precognition, and ESP under The Stargate Project). She links these military-funded "psychic cosmonauts" to a longer religious history exemplified by Emanuel Swedenborg's claimed planetary travels. Reading Vallee through Heidegger's "The Question Concerning Technology," she argues that the human relationship with technology is religiouslike — technology as a sacred "frame" and potential "saving power," not mere instrument. The preface announces the book's subject: UFOs, technology, and a group of people she found who believe anomalous technology functions as creative inspiration — and her intent to tell the complex version of the story, not the simple Hollywood one.

## Core claims
- Pasulka presents Vallee as having "perhaps done more for the field of ufology than anyone else in its short history," even though he calls the study of UFOs his hobby.
- The book says Vallee has both an orthodox history (astronomer, computer scientist, venture capitalist, author, Spielberg consultant) and an equally interesting unorthodox history connected to the Stanford Research Institute (SRI), whose activities, per declassified 1970s–80s documents, made it "a research site for the extraordinary."
- Pasulka reports (citing Jeffrey Kripal) that Vallee's early internet work was done under a program probably called "Augmentation of the Human Intellect."
- The book says the classified Stargate Project, funded by the US military in partnership with the SRI, studied remote viewing, precognition, and ESP, hoping to harness naturally psychic people for intelligence gathering — and that participants reportedly uncovered unintended targets like UFOs and reported traveling through space to the moon and Mars, so that the program "allegedly developed, intentionally or not, psychic cosmonauts."
- Pasulka argues that psychic travel had long been reported in the history of religions — Swedenborg is her example — but that the SRI cosmonauts' goals differed: they aimed to operationalize the knowledge for terrestrial targets, remote viewing being one of many methods of attempted data collection.
- She notes the historical simultaneity: "efforts to create human portals to other planets were taking place under the same auspices and at the same time as technologies of connectivity like the internet."
- Drawing on Heidegger, Pasulka argues that humans misread technology as pure instrumentality, that the human relationship with technology is religiouslike, and that a noninstrumental engagement with technology reveals it as "a saving power" — technology as birthing "a new era of human experience, a new epoch."
- She notes that although Vallee "most likely never read Heidegger's essay," his depiction of Silicon Valley as the home of the new resonates with Heidegger's vision of technology as bringing to birth a new epoch.
- She claims "the symbol for this new epoch is the UFO," invoking Carl Jung's naming of the UFO as "a technological angel."
- Pasulka states the book's subject and stance: it is about UFOs and technology and "a group of people who believe anomalous technology functions as creative inspiration" — people she found — and it will tell the complex story (which Vallee says might not be extraterrestrial at all), not the simple Hollywood one.

## Concepts
- **ufology** — The field of the study of UFOs; Pasulka calls it a "short history" field in which Vallee has perhaps done more than anyone else, though he calls it his hobby.
- **orthodox / unorthodox history** — Pasulka's framing of Vallee's biography: the public record (astronomer, ARPANET engineer, VC, Spielberg consultant) versus the "equally interesting" hidden record of work adjacent to SRI's classified research into the extraordinary.
- **psychic cosmonauts** — Pasulka's term for people who report traveling psychically through space to the moon and other planets; the Stargate Project "allegedly developed, intentionally or not," such cosmonauts, and figures like Swedenborg "crop up throughout the history of religions."
- **remote viewing** — One of the esoteric skills studied under the Stargate Project; in the SRI context it was "one of many methods of attempted data collection" aimed at operationalizing knowledge about terrestrial targets.
- **precognition / extrasensory perception** — The other esoteric skills studied at SRI under the Stargate Project, in hopes of harnessing naturally psychic people for intelligence gathering.
- **The Stargate Project** — Per the book, a classified program funded by the US military in partnership with the SRI to develop and harness psychic skills for intelligence; its psychic viewers reportedly uncovered unintended targets like UFOs.
- **"Augmentation of the Human Intellect"** — The program under which Vallee did his early work on the internet; as Jeffrey Kripal writes (cited by Pasulka), the program was "probably called" this.
- **instrumentality / "The Question Concerning Technology"** — Heidegger's essay, "dubbed impenetrable by many readers," per Pasulka: humans do not understand the essence of technology, are blinded by it, and wrongly interpret it as a mere instrument.
- **sacred "frame"** — Pasulka's rendering of Heidegger's examples: the Greek temple housed the gods and was thus a sacred frame; the medieval cathedral likewise embodied and housed the presence of God.
- **saving power** — What technology "really is," on Heidegger's account as Pasulka presents it, when humans engage with it noninstrumentally; the human relationship with technology is "religiouslike."
- **technological angel** — Carl Jung's name for the UFO, which Pasulka adopts as the symbol of the new technological epoch.
- **anomalous technology** — The kind of technology that the group of people Pasulka found believe "functions as creative inspiration"; the book's central subject alongside UFOs.
- **zeitgeist** — What Pasulka glimpsed on the drive: "the exciting ideology and philosophy behind the revolution" of Silicon Valley technology.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The Silicon Valley car tour (framing scene)** — Vallee drives Pasulka and Robbie Graham through Bay Area traffic past places "that loom large in the history of 'the Valley,'" recalling the early technology revolution ("They were on fire and purely democratic. Pure scientists, fueled by discovery."). Pasulka recognizes her own childhood neighborhoods but sees them anew "through Jacques's eyes," "shining with the allure of mystery"; she never works up the nerve to ask what he means by the Valley's secrets. The scene establishes Silicon Valley as a field site and Vallee as key theorist/informant.
- **Vallee's credentials (orthodox history)** — As an astronomer he helped NASA create the first detailed map of Mars; as a Northwestern PhD computer scientist he was an early engineer of ARPANET (Advanced Research Projects Agency, precursor of the internet); he is a successful venture capitalist and prolific author; he consulted for Spielberg on *Close Encounters of the Third Kind* (1977), whose scientist character, played by François Truffaut, is based on him.
- **SRI / Stargate Project (unorthodox history)** — Vallee worked with scientists affiliated with the Stanford Research Institute (now SRI International, Menlo Park). Declassified documents from the 1970s–80s indicate it researched the extraordinary: The Stargate Project, US-military funded, studied remote viewing, precognition, and ESP. Psychic viewers reportedly uncovered unintended targets like UFOs and reported travel to the moon and Mars — for Pasulka, evidence that psychic-cosmonaut phenomena and internet-building shared a time, place, and institutional auspices.
- **Emanuel Swedenborg** — The eighteenth-century philosopher/theologian claimed that, assisted by an angel, he visited Mercury, Mars, Venus, and the moon, spoke to beings there, and published the experiences in *Life on Other Planets* (1758). Pasulka uses him to show that psychic travel long predates SRI — the SRI cosmonauts' activities "may have resembled" his interstellar adventures, "but their goals could not have been more different": SRI hoped to operationalize the knowledge acquired about terrestrial targets.
- **Heidegger's temple and cathedral** — For the Greeks the temple housed the gods; for medieval Europeans the cathedral embodied and housed God's presence. Pasulka deploys these as Heidegger's models of technology as sacred frame, mapping them onto Vallee's Silicon Valley as "home of the new."
- **The Spielberg anecdote** — In the 1970s Vallee encouraged Spielberg to portray the complex version of the phenomenon — "that the phenomenon is complex and might not be extraterrestrial at all" — but Spielberg chose the simple story everybody would understand, saying "This is Hollywood." Pasulka positions her book as the anti-Hollywood move: the complex story, told so anyone can understand it.

## Key quotes
- "These are the hills of Silicon Valley. There are many secrets in this valley." — Vallee's opening line on the tour, the preface's epigraph-like hook.
- "They were on fire and purely democratic. Pure scientists, fueled by discovery." — Vallee recalling the early days of the technology revolution.
- "Jacques has perhaps done more for the field of ufology than anyone else in its short history, and yet he calls the study of UFOs his hobby." — Pasulka's assessment of her guide.
- "In other words, the program allegedly developed, intentionally or not, psychic cosmonauts." — Her summary of Stargate's unintended output.
- "These efforts to create human portals to other planets were taking place under the same auspices and at the same time as technologies of connectivity like the internet." — The simultaneity claim binding the psychic and the digital.
- "If Jacques were an essay, he would be 'The Question Concerning Technology' by the philosopher Martin Heidegger." — Her theoretical key for reading Vallee.
- "Heidegger suggested that the human relationship with technology is religiouslike, that it is possible for us to have a noninstrumental relationship with technology and engage fully with what it really is: a saving power." — The Heideggerian thesis the book builds on.
- "The symbol for this new epoch is the UFO. Carl Jung called the UFO a technological angel." — The pivot from technology to the book's object.
- "This is a book about UFOs and technology, but also about a group of people who believe anomalous technology functions as creative inspiration. I found these people." — The book's statement of subject and of Pasulka's fieldwork claim.
- "But Spielberg went with the simple story, the one everybody would understand. He said, 'This is Hollywood.' This book does not tell the simple story, but I believe it is a story anyone can understand." — The closing methodological promise.

## Thread
The preface installs the book's three load-bearing elements before the argument begins: (1) Jacques Vallee as the presiding theorist-informant, whose double biography (public technologist / covert student of the extraordinary) models the book's theme of hidden histories; (2) Silicon Valley as a field site where technology, secrecy, and the sacred converge — anticipating the later claim that technology is a site of the sacred; and (3) the Heidegger/Jung framing that makes the UFO the religious symbol of a technological epoch rather than a mere hardware question. It also declares method and stance — Pasulka "found" the believers in anomalous technology and will tell the complex, possibly non-extraterrestrial story — setting up the Introduction and the ethnography of her scientist-Experiencers (Tyler D., James) in the numbered chapters, and foreshadowing the Conclusion's concern with "The Artifact."
