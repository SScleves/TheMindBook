# Ch 10 — Spirits in an Illusory, Video Game–Like Dream World

## In one paragraph

Opening Part III ("The Mystics"), Virk pivots from quantum physics to the world's spiritual traditions, arguing that Eastern mystics — Hindus, Buddhists and related faiths — have for thousands of years described reality in terms that map more closely onto the simulation hypothesis than anything modern science has produced. The chapter centers on the Eastern metaphor that the world is *maya* (illusion) and, more specifically, a shared dream — from Vishnu dreaming the universe into existence to Tibetan dream yoga, whose explicit goal is to "wake up" from the dream of physical reality. Virk's key move is to treat dreaming as "biological technology": he walks through the naturally occurring features of dreams (immersion, direct mind projection, NPC-like characters, dream-only memories) and shows they already fulfill most of the advanced stages (7–11) of his Part I roadmap to the simulation point. He closes with the secret "seventh yoga" of forceful projection — Tibetan adepts transferring consciousness into animals and fresh corpses — as evidence that downloadable consciousness is an ancient idea, setting up the next chapter on karma and reincarnation.

## Core claims

- Virk argues that the quantum-physics conclusion that the observer is an essential part of the material universe was "neither new nor surprising" to seekers in other traditions — Eastern mystics in particular have taught for thousands of years that the world is an illusion tied to our consciousness.
- Virk argues that what the mystical traditions are actually describing "comes much closer to the simulation hypothesis, albeit with a religious or spiritual interpretation, than anything modern science has come up with to date."
- Virk claims the Eastern dream metaphor is not merely figurative: Buddhist and Hindu yogis use dreams "not only as a metaphor but as a way to actually describe our physical world — which we 'wake up' from after death," with the stated goal being "liberation" from the illusory dream world while still alive.
- Virk argues that if we think of the physical world as a shared video game, karma and reincarnation are better described than by most modern scientific theories — video game terminology (*multiple lives*, *avatars*, *quests*) maps "very well" onto these twin tenets (detail deferred to the next chapter).
- Virk claims the Western traditions (Judaism, Islam, Christianity) also fit the lens: a "temporary world" where acts are watched and recorded by angels overseen by an all-powerful creator reads, in simulation terms, as beings outside the rendered world watching and influencing us; recorded deeds, score evaluation and event replay are common in video games, which also have autonomous watching processes (*daemons*) and AI responding to player requests.
- Virk reports that more than one self-described atheist scientist, upon considering the simulation hypothesis, realized that beings "outside the simulation" might seem like supernatural beings — gods and angels — to us, even if not technically divine.
- Virk argues the simulation hypothesis both rationally explains what religions have long taught and explains phenomena unexplained by modern science — NDEs, OBEs, UFOs, synchronicity, déjà vu (covered in the last chapter of Part III).
- Virk argues that *maya* as "magic show" implies the illusion exists in our own perception — "we deceive ourselves into believing the illusion is real" — and that to modern ears the interactive, collectively-played nature of maya makes it best described as a multiplayer video game.
- Virk argues the dream is "tailor-made" for the simulation hypothesis: like a simulation, a dream runs for a set period and completes; the rendered world seems real and permanent to characters inside, but is ephemeral and lasts only while rendering is going on.
- Virk's central claim: dreaming is a natural process showing "we have already developed in biological form most of the stages that a technological civilization must achieve to reach the simulation point" — and that reaching the simulation point may simply be reproducing, with computation and video game technology, what every human already does naturally each night.
- Virk claims that shared dreaming "is one of many unexplainable phenomena that could not happen in a purely physical world, but could easily occur in a simulated world."
- Virk argues, from the Tibetan forceful-projection tradition, that if consciousness can be projected into another physical body, then consciousness "must be something more than physical. It becomes more like digital information that exists outside the physical but can be put into physical machines" — i.e., downloadable consciousness already exists in biological form, and Kurzweil-style singularity seekers "are looking at the wrong kinds of machines."
- Virk claims the most significant "downloads" in religious traditions happen at birth (soul/consciousness downloaded) and death (uploaded back to the "real" world).

## Concepts

- **maya** — Sanskrit term meaning "illusion," but not just any illusion: it represents that we take the world to be real when it is in fact a temporary, illusory world where each of us works out individual karma across a series of lives. A second translation is a magic power or magic show revealing things that appear real "but have no lasting reality"; Virk adds that maya is also the power of the gods to deceive us into thinking this is the "real" world, and — per some scholars' subtler reading — that *we deceive ourselves* into believing the illusion is real.
- **lila** — Hindu Vedic term roughly translating to "play," designating the world as the divine play of the gods. *Play* has dual meanings: the world is built from a spirit of playfulness and creativity, and the world is like a stage play (a metaphor used by mystics and by Shakespeare). Virk updates it: an interactive movie we all play together — i.e., a multiplayer video game.
- **shared dream / collective dream** — The maya of the world "would have to be a shared dream that we all live in, with characters created for fulfilling our own personal karma"; our "true self" resides outside this dream world and we only catch glimpses of the "real" world while here.
- **bardo** — Tibetan Buddhist term literally meaning "in-between": the resting place for souls between lives / the intermediate state after death. The Tibetan Book of the Dead, *The Bardo Thodol*, is meant to prepare monks for the transition at the time of death.
- **Brahma / dharma (the beyond-illusion)** — What lies beyond maya: in the Hindu traditions, Brahma, "the absolute 'real' world beyond form"; in the Buddhist traditions, the dharma, "the absolute basis of reality from which all phenomena emanate." The traditions don't say exactly what this world is like.
- **nirvana / Buddha ("one who is awake")** — In Buddhism, the search for nirvana is the effort to wake up from the surrounding illusion; the very word *Buddha* means "one who is awake." The only way to "wake up" is to recognize the true, illusory dreamlike nature of physical reality.
- **Six Yogas of Naropa** — Tibetan esoteric practices meant as shortcuts to enlightenment or liberation from the wheel of samsara ("wandering around in the dream world in life after life"). As listed in Serinity Young's *Dreaming in the Lotus*: tummo (yoga of inner heat), gyulus (yoga of the illusory body), milam (dream yoga), ösel (clear light), phowa (transference or projection of consciousness), bardo (intermediate state after death). Not fully independent nor practiced in fixed order; all concern the illusory nature of waking reality and developing a dreaming consciousness and dreaming body that can travel independent of the physical body.
- **dream yoga (milam)** — A series of practices allowing the practitioner, through mastery of the dream world, to realize the illusory/dreamlike nature of the physical world. Often involves meditating on certain chakras or symbols while falling asleep to maintain consciousness along the continuum from fully awake through the hypnagogic state into the dream state; its two key aspects are being "awake" in a dream and finding oneself in the "clear light" in the dream state. Unlike lucid dreaming, its aim is spiritual: awareness that neither dreams *nor the physical world* are real, maintained through falling asleep and beyond the physical world into death.
- **lucid dreaming** — A more modern term for recognizing that you are dreaming, studied by Stephen LaBerge in the Stanford sleep lab; some people can do it at will just by setting intention. Its aim is usually psychological — controlling dreams to help with nightmares or to have dream adventures/fantasies — in contrast to dream yoga's spiritual aim.
- **lucidity test** — A way to determine you are in a dream when you suspect it but aren't sure (experts recommend developing one). In the film *Inception* it is a carried object called a *totem*; Virk's personal test is seeing whether he can fly.
- **threat simulation theory** — A research thread on dreaming's evolutionary purpose: dreams let us "simulate" complex threats before they happen and hone our responses, so those who do this are better prepared for real-life threats — linking dreams to natural selection.
- **overdetermined (dream science)** — Dreaming may be one of the few fields in science that is *overdetermined*: most of the reasons given by those who study dreams may be true at least some of the time.
- **non-ordinary reality** — Where, per those who study shamans, the beings shamans encounter in dreams and on shamanic journeys actually exist; dreams are in one sense subjective and in another objective experiences visiting beings and worlds outside our own.
- **dreamtime** — Aboriginal Australian concept: dreaming is a way to communicate with the dreamtime, the "real world" beyond the physical.
- **dream within a dream / nested realities** — Waking up and thinking you're in physical reality while really in another dream; shows "we can have multiple nested realities that our minds are not capable of distinguishing, at least while we are in them" (linked to simulations within simulations, Part IV and FAQ).
- **shared dreams** — Instances where different people report a similar dream; thousands of anecdotal reports and coverage in modern psychology trades (including *Psychology Today*), though no validating studies.
- **secret seventh yoga / forceful projection** — A variation of phowa, systematically taken out of the Six Yogas or kept secret because considered dangerous: adepts at the time of death intentionally transfer consciousness out of the body from a point in the forehead (the "third eye") and can "forcefully project" it into another biological host nearby — animals at first, later fresh corpses. Two of the Six Yogas are directly related to transferring the consciousness of a dying person out of the physical body to "somewhere else."
- **downloadable consciousness** — The idea (stages 9–10 of Virk's roadmap) that consciousness can be transferred into other vehicles; the Tibetan material suggests it already exists biologically, and religious traditions place the most significant download at birth and upload at death.
- **simulation-point stages mapped to dreaming** — Stage 7 (mind interfaces), stage 8 (implanted memories), stage 9 (artificial intelligence), stage 10 (downloading consciousness into a simulated body), stage 11 (the simulation point / full immersion) — all naturally occurring features of dreams (see Examples).
- **server epochs** — In simulations and multiplayer worlds, "epochs" or "stories" run on particular servers for a subset of players; when that world ends, the server shuts down and a new one starts — Virk's analog for cycles of creation in the Vishnu myth.
- **daemons / watchers outside the rendered world** — Video games' autonomous watching processes and request-answering AI on unseen servers, offered as the simulation-lens reading of angels and demons in the Western traditions.
- **IRL / metaverse persistence** — Inside metaverses like *Second Life*, players can remember and reference the world outside the simulation ("in real life"), and virtual lives can be "picked up" the next time the game starts — the analog of living continual lives inside dreams.

## Experiments, examples & case studies

1. **Yogananda epigraph** — "Birth and death are doors through which you pass from one dream to another" (Paramahansa Yogananda). Frames the whole chapter: birth/death as transitions between dream-worlds.
2. **Maya as magic show (Vedic texts)** — In many Vedic texts māyā connotes "a magic show, an illusion where things appear to be present but are not what they seem." Virk layers meanings: gods' power to deceive us; and — like a magic show where we know the magician isn't really sawing the woman in half — we deceive ourselves, and "it's the illusion that makes the show fun." Shows the illusion exists in our own perception, yet the world is "a real construct as far as our limited consciousness in the physical world is concerned" (some Yogic texts).
3. **Lila and the stage play → interactive movie → multiplayer game** — The Vedic term *lila* ("play") casts the world as divine play; Shakespeare and mystics used the stage-play metaphor; Virk argues that to modern ears the interactive movie we all play together is a multiplayer video game.
4. **Vishnu's dream (Hindu mythology, Figure 31)** — The whole world is the dream of the sleeping god Vishnu, who lies on the serpent of time (Shesha) and watches as Brahma creates his dream in reality. When Vishnu wakes, one cycle of creation ends and only a vast divine ocean (Kshira Sagara) remains; when he sleeps and dreams again, a new universe is created. Virk: one consciousness watching the dream is "eerily reminiscent of a complex simulation" — server epochs shutting down and restarting, transformed into mythological form as a being who creates worlds by dreaming.
5. **Dream interpretation across traditions** — Interpretation traditions go back to the Bible and the Talmud. Freud: dreams hold latent desires, a form of wish fulfillment. Jung: dreams give clues to what we're preparing for, and serve a "tempering"/"moderating" purpose "like a feedback loop or thermostat." Virk: the sheer number of approaches suggests the complexity beneath an everyday phenomenon.
6. **Native American literalism** — Many Native American traditions believe we tap into other worlds and intelligences guiding us from the dream world; some tribes take beings encountered in dreams "not just as symbols, but literally" — real beings, not imagination (contrasted with Freudian/Jungian interpretation).
7. **Sufi dream-telling** — In Sufi traditions one tells dreams to a *shaikh* (spiritual leader), who interprets them for you rather than you relying on your own associations.
8. **Kleitman & Aserinsky, University of Chicago, 1953** — Stumbled onto the discovery that dreams occur during REM sleep. Aserinsky also found that sleep-deprived people started to experience hallucinations; Virk notes it "could very well be that the mind processes dreams to prevent people from having hallucinations while awake." Science has validated that dreams are necessary and probably purposeful without a definitive biological reason (competing views: memory integration vs. random neurons firing off recent memories).
9. **Threat simulation theory** — Dreams as evolutionary rehearsal: simulating complex threats before they happen to hone responses, conferring a natural-selection advantage. Cited as one thread of research into why dreaming evolved.
10. **Dreams tuning physical needs / overdetermination** — "Any teenager can tell you" dreams provide an outlet for sexual fantasies; but dreaming may be *overdetermined* — most proposed purposes true at least some of the time.
11. **Elias Howe's sewing machine dream** — The inventor couldn't get his machine to sew. He dreamed he was kidnapped by cannibals and, stewing in the fire while natives moved spears rhythmically, noticed the spears had holes near the tip — giving him the idea of putting the thread at the tip of the needle, the final piece he needed. Example of dreams as creative inspiration.
12. **Kekulé's benzene snake** — Chemist August Kekulé supposedly derived the (circular) structure of the benzene molecule after dreaming of a snake eating its tail.
13. **Mendeleev's periodic table** — Chemist Dmitri Mendeleev saw the elements organize into the columns and rows of the periodic table while dozing in front of a fireplace at a resort on the Caspian Sea.
14. **Shamans and non-ordinary reality** — Mystics and shamans of all traditions believe dreams tap into other worlds; those who study shamans say the beings encountered in dreams and on shamanic journeys "actually exist in 'non-ordinary reality.'"
15. **Muhammad and Gabriel** — The prophet of Islam first encountered the angel Gabriel in a dream while dozing in a cave in the mountains outside Mecca during the holy month of Ramadan — Virk's example that many world religions have come from what might be termed dreams.
16. **Aboriginal dreamtime and the Maricopa** — Aboriginal Australian tribes believe dreaming communicates with the dreamtime, the "real world" beyond the physical; the Maricopa Indians of the American Southwest believe anything that happens in the real world happens in the dream first and then makes its way into the physical world. Many dreamers also report visits from the deceased in the "other world."
17. **Fred Alan Wolf's parallel dream lives** — In *The Dreaming Universe*, physicist Wolf writes he has interviewed people who "are also apparently capable of waking up night after night in a parallel world where they have a continuous life in a different body." Some report living entire lives in dreams — disjointed because interrupted by waking life, but continual, with clear recall of the whole life. Virk: this sounds "eerily similar" to a virtual life in a video game you can "pick up" next session — the whole purpose of metaverses like *Second Life*, where players inside reference the outside world as "IRL."
18. **Buddhist framing: Buddha = "one who is awake"** — Nirvana as waking up from the illusion; the bardo as the in-between resting place for souls; *The Bardo Thodol* (Tibetan Book of the Dead) prepares monks for the death transition. Contrast drawn with Western traditions' eternal heaven/hell: Eastern traditions focus on the transitions (dying, and birth/incarnation).
19. **The Six Yogas of Naropa (via Serinity Young's *Dreaming in the Lotus*)** — tummo (inner heat), gyulus (illusory body), milam (dream yoga), ösel (clear light), phowa (consciousness transference), bardo (intermediate state). Shortcuts to enlightenment/liberation from samsara; all concerned with waking reality's illusory nature and developing a dreaming consciousness/body that travels independent of the physical body.
20. **Dream yoga practice mechanics** — Meditating on chakras or symbols while falling asleep to keep consciousness continuous from waking through the hypnagogic state into dreaming; key aspects are being "awake" in the dream and finding the "clear light." In Virk's parlance: if we view dreams as simulations and learn to recognize them, we can see the physical world as a dreamlike simulation and "awaken" to what's outside it.
21. **Lucid dreaming research and Virk's own experiences** — Studied by Stephen LaBerge in the Stanford sleep lab; research suggests a larger-than-expected percentage of people have had a lucid dream (e.g., recognizing a nightmare as a dream). Some can go lucid at will just by intention. In *Zen Entrepreneurship* Virk describes his own precursor to lucidity: things feel "off" — a relative he knows has passed suddenly appears, or a restaurant he knows is in Boston appears in California — "some kind of 'glitch in the Matrix.'" Lucidity is hard to maintain; those with control can start to manipulate the dream state.
22. **The lucidity test / Inception's totem** — Experts recommend a test for whether you're dreaming; in *Inception* it's a carried object (the *totem*) verifying you're not in physical reality. Virk's personal test: seeing if he can fly ("I can almost always do that in my dreams"), with the bonus that once lucid he can fly to different places in the dream world.
23. **Dream yoga vs. lucid dreaming (aims)** — Similar techniques, different goals: lucid dreaming's aim is psychological (nightmare control, adventures/fantasies); dream yoga's is spiritual — realizing that neither dreams nor the physical world are real, and maintaining awareness while falling asleep and beyond the physical world into death. The physical world "appears real while we are in it, but like dreams it is a construction of the mind — perhaps a shared construction, but a delusion nevertheless."
24. **Nature-inspired technology precedent** — When scientists needed inspiration they looked to natural processes: AI/machine learning rooted in neural networks inspired by brain neurons; heavier-than-air flying machines borrowed wings from birds and bats. Sets up the argument that dreaming is the natural process to copy for simulation-point stages 7+ (stages 1–6, through physical broadcast and 3D printing, are already understandable from existing technology).
25. **The dreaming-as-simulation-point checklist (core of the chapter)** — Naturally occurring characteristics of dreaming mapped to Virk's Part I stages: the "player" lies down and the body goes to "sleep" (stage 10: downloading consciousness into a simulated body); pictures and sounds are projected directly into the mind's eye (stage 7: mind interfaces); the player is so immersed they forget the world isn't real (stage 11: the simulation point); the player is conscious at some level while unaware of their normal outside consciousness (full immersion — the definition of the simulation point); the dream reads intentions directly from the mind (stage 7); non-player characters appear as full-fledged real personalities (stage 9: artificial intelligence); dream memories exist that aren't part of the person's physical life (stage 8: implanted memories). Conclusion: reaching the simulation point may be reproducing technologically what every human does naturally.
26. **Enlightenment as the red pill** — In Buddhist terms, enlightenment is waking up from the dream while it's still going on; in a simulation, part of an individual's goal may be becoming aware of the world beyond the game — we're only "playing" our current "characters" while other players play on, "blissfully unaware that they are in a transitory, temporary video game." Science-fiction analog: Neo taking the red pill in *The Matrix* and waking up outside the simulation.
27. **Dreams from the simulation perspective (four features)** — (a) Dreams are little simulations created for each of our minds to enter each evening, with us as players; (b) dreams mix familiar and unfamiliar elements (TV/book elements, loved ones, generic characters, or elements familiar to the dream self but not the waking self) — similar to how simulations may contain things from previous non-simulated reality; (c) waking produces confusion, then the realization it was "only a dream" — i.e., "only a game" or "only a simulation"; (d) the dream within a dream shows nested realities our minds can't distinguish from inside.
28. **Shared dreams and the 2024 Silicon Valley experiment** — Dreams look like single-player simulations where everyone else is an NPC — "Or are they?" Many reported instances of shared dreams (dreaming of someone you hadn't interacted with, then learning they were thinking of you that night); thousands of anecdotal reports, written up in psychology trades including *Psychology Today*, though no validating studies. In 2024, a Silicon Valley startup claimed to verify that two lucid dreamers communicated with each other in their dreams, each communicating with the startup's servers — a technologically based communication illustrating the overlap between biological and technological dreaming. Virk: shared dreaming couldn't happen in a purely physical world but could easily occur in a simulated one.
29. **The secret seventh yoga (forceful projection)** — A variation of phowa systematically removed from the Six Yogas or kept secret as dangerous. Adepts, at exactly the time of death, intentionally transfer consciousness out through a point in the forehead (the third eye) and can forcefully project it into another biological host nearby — practiced first with creatures (birds, chickens, foxes), then, "in scenes reminiscent of modern horror films," with fresh corpses deposited at the edges of towns. Yogis would reanimate recently dead bodies and, if not too deteriorated, could keep living in them or project back; the original body sat in suspended animation nearby until the consciousness returned. Per Tsongkhapa's *Six Yogas of Naropa* (translated by Glenn Mullin), Indian and Tibetan texts were filled with such anecdotes, and gurus repeatedly demonstrated it for pupils in Tibet under the strictest secrecy.
30. **Marpa's son becomes Tipupa, the pigeon saint** — Marpa "the translator" (he crossed the Himalayas from Tibet to study with Naropa in India and translated the teachings from Sanskrit into Tibetan; without him the Six Yogas might have been lost as Buddhism waned in India but waxed across Asia) taught the seventh yoga to his son. The son broke his neck in a riding accident, used consciousness transference/forceful projection to enter a pigeon flying nearby, flew (as the pigeon) to the outskirts of an Indian town — where dead bodies were plentiful since only the rich cremated — and projected into a recently deceased young man's body. In this youthful body he took the name Tipupa ("the pigeon saint") and taught the Six Yogas in India. Twist: years later Milarepa, "perhaps Tibet's most famous yogi" and also a direct student of Marpa, was asked about this yoga by a student and sent him to study with Tipupa, the best living expert on the secretive practice.
31. **Kurzweil and the wrong machines** — Ray Kurzweil and others await the singularity as the point where consciousness can be transferred to artificial machines (stages 9–10); Virk argues Naropa's Six Yogas suggest downloadable consciousness already exists — "we just need to develop (or grow) biological machines (or entities) into which the consciousness can be transferred." The religious traditions' most significant downloads: birth (download) and death (upload back to the "real" world).
32. **Western traditions preview (angels, daemons, replays)** — Judaism, Islam, and Christianity describe a "temporary world" where acts are watched and recorded by angels overseen by an all-powerful creator; through the simulation lens: beings not in the rendered world watching and influencing us (angels/demons), recorded deeds, score evaluation and event replays as in video games, autonomous watching processes (*daemons*), and server-side AI answering player requests. Also: atheist scientists realizing beings outside the simulation would seem like gods/angels. (Full treatment promised in a later chapter of Part III.)

## Thread

This chapter opens Part III and executes the book's pivot from science to mysticism: Part II ended with quantum physics forcing the observer into the material universe, and Virk begins here by noting that Eastern mystics found that conclusion unsurprising. The chapter's strategy is twofold. First, establish the Eastern vocabulary (maya, lila, the shared dream, Vishnu's dream, dream yoga) and show it reads naturally as a description of a multiplayer simulation. Second — the chapter's real engine — invert the metaphor: dreams are not just *like* simulations; dreaming is a biological process that already implements stages 7–11 of the Part I roadmap to the simulation point, so a civilization reaching the simulation point would merely be re-implementing dreaming in silicon. The forceful-projection material then extends this from rendering to consciousness transfer, arguing downloadable consciousness (stage 10) has an ancient biological precedent. The chapter explicitly hands off in three directions: karma and reincarnation (next chapter, previewed via multiple lives/avatars/quests and birth-download/death-upload), the Western traditions through the simulation lens (a later chapter in this part), and the unexplained phenomena — NDEs, OBEs, UFOs, synchronicity, déjà vu (the last chapter of this part). Nested dreams point forward to simulations-within-simulations in Part IV and the FAQ.

  - **American Cosmic (Pasulka)** — The UFO thread Virk promises for the end of Part III overlaps Pasulka's territory: technologically framed reinterpretation of religious experience, and beings "outside" ordinary reality read through a modern-tech lens.
  - **Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance (Pirsig)** — Weakest link: Pirsig's mysticism is about Quality preceding subject/object division, which loosely echoes the mystics' claim that consciousness precedes the material world, but Pirsig has no dream/simulation machinery.
