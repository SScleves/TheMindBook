# The Holographic Universe — Q&A

## Core ideas

**Q: What is the central thesis of The Holographic Universe?**
A: Talbot argues that some scientists believe the universe itself is a kind of giant hologram — "a splendidly detailed illusion" — in which everything from snowflakes to spinning electrons is a ghostly projection from a level of reality literally beyond both space and time. The idea's two main architects, physicist David Bohm and neurophysiologist Karl Pribram, arrived at it independently from opposite directions, and Talbot's most sweeping claim is that the model helps explain "virtually all paranormal and mystical experiences" — telepathy, precognition, psychokinesis, mystical oneness, NDEs, lucid dreams, and synchronicities. (Intro)

**Q: What is a hologram, and why does "whole in every part" matter so much?**
A: A hologram is a three-dimensional image made by splitting a laser into two beams, bouncing one off an object, and recording the interference pattern where the beams recollide on film; re-illuminating the film reconstructs the 3-D image. The crucial property is that every fragment of the film contains all the information of the whole image — cut a hologram of an apple in half and each piece still reconstructs the entire apple, only hazier as pieces shrink. This "whole in every part" trait is the mechanism Pribram seized on for distributed memory, and it recurs throughout the book as the model of nonlocality, body microsystems, and the ancient part-contains-whole intuition. (Ch 1)

**Q: What is the implicate order?**
A: The implicate (enfolded) order is Bohm's proposed deeper level of reality — a vast, more primary level that gives birth to all the objects and appearances of our physical world the way holographic film gives birth to a hologram. Our everyday world is the "explicate" (unfolded) order, continuously projected out of and enfolded back into the implicate in countless exchanges. Bohm prefers to call the universe the "holomovement," since "hologram" suggests a static image and misses the dynamic enfoldings and unfoldings creating our universe moment by moment. His first model of it was an ink drop in glycerine that spreads into invisibility and then reconstitutes when the cylinder is reversed — a "hidden" order revealed. (Ch 2)

**Q: What does nonlocality mean in this book?**
A: Nonlocality is the property, implied by Bohm's quantum potential, that at the subquantum level location ceases to exist — all points in space become equal to all other points, so it is meaningless to speak of anything as separate from anything else. Talbot notes that saying every part of holographic film contains the whole image is just another way of saying the information is distributed nonlocally, so a holographic universe would be expected to have nonlocal properties. Bohm's fish-in-the-aquarium analogy makes the point: two camera views of one fish look like two communicating fish, when at a deeper level they are one thing. (Ch 2)

**Q: What is the frequency domain?**
A: It is Pribram's speculative picture of what is really "out there": not solid objects but a vast ocean of waves and frequencies — "a vast, resonating symphony of wave forms" — which the brain, acting as a lens using Fourier mathematics, converts into the familiar concrete world. In the later chapters Talbot equates it with the interference-pattern reality behind appearances that mystics and aura-seers allegedly glimpse. Pribram's answer to which is real, the china cup or its interference pattern: "Both are real to me, or, if you want to say, neither of them are real." (Ch 1, Ch 2, Ch 6)

**Q: What is a "reality-field"?**
A: It is Talbot's own term for a reality collectively constructed and sustained through extrasensory interconnectedness — consensus reality as a mutually shared hologram. He proposes that stable particles like electrons are old, deeply ingrained reality-fields while plastic ones like the anomalon are recent and still inchoate, and that this logic could explain the US-vs-British aspirin anomaly and Rogo's observation that blood miracles only begin with fourteenth-century San Gennaro. Taken to the limit, everything stable and eternal — laws of physics, the substance of galaxies — would be "will-o'-the-wisps no more or less real than the props in a giant, mutually shared dream." (Ch 5)

**Q: What does Talbot mean by "omnijective"?**
A: "Omnijective" is Talbot's 1976 coinage for phenomena that are neither purely subjective nor purely objective but both — products of the psyche that are also quite real, such as UFOs and Marian visions. He later learned the Sufi scholar Henry Corbin had already coined "imaginal" for the same blurred status: a world created by imagination yet ontologically no less real than physical reality. In the final chapter the whole universe becomes omnijective — a seamless continuum where mind and reality interpenetrate and strict objectivity ceases to be possible. (Ch 8, Ch 9)

## The science claims

**Q: What experimental support does Talbot claim for the model in the Introduction?**
A: Two main items: numerous neurophysiology studies said to corroborate Pribram's predictions about the holographic nature of memory and perception, and Alain Aspect's 1982 Paris experiment showing the web of subatomic particles possesses "what appears to be an undeniable 'holographic' property." He adds the Princeton PEAR lab's 1987 announcement of "unequivocal evidence" that minds can affect machines, and argues the character and courage of Bohm and Pribram themselves add weight. (Intro)

**Q: What was the Aspect experiment and what did it show, per the book?**
A: In 1982 Alain Aspect, Jean Dalibard, and Gerard Roger at the Institute of Optics, University of Paris, produced twin photons by heating calcium atoms with lasers, sent them through 6.5 meters of pipe to filters that switched between polarization analyzers in 10 billionths of a second — about 30 billionths of a second less than light needs to cross the 13 meters separating the photon sets. The photons still correlated their polarization angles, ruling out communication by any known physical process. Talbot reports the result is generally viewed as virtual proof of nonlocal connection; he is careful to say it does not prove Bohm's model but gives it "tremendous support." (Ch 2)

**Q: What was the engram debate, and what evidence undermined localized memory?**
A: Penfield's 1920s stimulation of conscious epileptics' temporal lobes evoked vivid replayed memories from fixed spots, which was taken as proof each memory has a specific brain location (the "engram"). But Karl Lashley spent some thirty years surgically removing portions of trained rats' brains and could never eradicate the maze memories; patients with brain sections removed never lost specific memories; and neither Pribram nor Penfield himself could replicate the stimulation results in non-epileptics. Talbot reports this convinced Pribram memory is distributed throughout the brain as a whole — and a mid-1960s Scientific American article on holograms gave him the mechanism. (Ch 1)

**Q: What experiment convinced Pribram his Fourier theory of vision was right?**
A: The 1979 work of Berkeley neurophysiologists Russell and Karen DeValois, who converted plaid and checkerboard patterns into simple wave forms with Fourier's equations and found visual-cortex cells responded not to the original patterns but to their Fourier translations — implying the brain uses the same mathematics holography uses. Talbot reports the finding was confirmed by many other labs, and sits atop earlier frequency-analysis findings: Helmholtz on the ear, "osmic frequencies" for smell, and Bekesy on skin and taste. (Ch 1)

**Q: What were Pietsch's salamander experiments?**
A: Indiana University biologist Paul Pietsch, initially trying to disprove Pribram, found he could remove a salamander's brain, flip its hemispheres, turn it upside down, and even mince it — and in over 700 operations the animal's feeding behavior always returned to normal once the tissue was replaced. Talbot reports the results turned Pietsch into a believer, drew a 60 Minutes segment, and are recounted in his book Shufflebrain. (Ch 1)

**Q: What did Valerie Hunt claim to measure?**
A: UCLA kinesiologist Valerie Hunt, using electromyograph electrodes, reported an additional field radiating from the body at frequencies averaging 100–1600 cps (sometimes far higher, up to 200,000 cps in some individuals), strongest at the chakra points rather than at brain, heart, or muscle. She reported instrumental correlation with the colors aura readers see, talent bands (psychics 400–800 cps, trance channelers 800–900, mystics above 900), and a never-repeating "chaos holograph pattern" from just 3–4 seconds of data. Her conclusion, as Talbot quotes it: "The mind's not in the brain. It's in that damn field." (Ch 6)

**Q: What do the Libet and Feinstein timing experiments show, per Talbot?**
A: The neurophysiologists found a touch stimulus registered in the brain 0.0001 second after it occurred and patients pressed a response button 0.1 second after — but conscious awareness came only after almost 0.5 second, meaning the decision was made by the unconscious while the brain created "the comforting delusion" of conscious control. Later studies showed the brain generates movement signals one and a half seconds before we "decide" to move. Talbot pairs this with Hunt's finding that the energy field's EMG responds to stimuli before the brain's EEG, to argue the mind may not be in the brain at all. (Ch 6)

**Q: What new discoveries does Talbot say are already flowing from the model?**
A: Three: Hugo Zuccarelli's holophonic sound, which uses the fact that human ears emit sound (an audio "reference laser") to make recordings so three-dimensional listeners duck at matches lit near their faces and which stay 3-D through one headphone side; Prigogine's Nobel-winning dissipative structures, read by Prigogine and others as the implicate becoming explicate; and holographic computing — Cohen's multiplexed holographic gratings and Anderson's optical memory with associative recall. Talbot stresses the computing advances remain refinements of the mechanistic approach. (Ch 9)

## Mind & body

**Q: What is the book's core mind-body claim, and what imagery evidence backs it?**
A: Talbot argues that because real and imagined experience reduce to "the same common language of holographically organized wave forms," the body cannot always distinguish them, so mental images produce dramatic and specific physiological effects. Evidence he reports: Simonton's throat-cancer patient Frank (under 5 percent survival odds, cancer gone in two months with bullet-imagery visualization), Simonton's 159 "medically incurable" patients averaging 24.4 months survival — over twice the national norm; Achterberg's students who raised neutrophils or T-cells depending on which cell type they imaged; the Soviet athlete study where the 75-percent-visualization group improved most at Lake Placid 1980; and Richardson's basketball study where visualization-only practice gained 23 percent versus 24 percent for physical practice. (Ch 4)

**Q: What is the Wright/Krebiozen case?**
A: Wright, a cancer patient with orange-sized tumors reported by psychologist Bruno Klopfer, received one injection of the touted drug Krebiozen and his tumors "melted like snowballs on a hot stove"; he relapsed when articles said the drug didn't work, recovered again after his physician injected plain water billed as a "new highly concentrated" version, and died two days after the AMA pronounced Krebiozen worthless. Talbot uses it to argue we possess the power to melt tumors but must be fooled into using it — with tragic results when belief collapses. (Ch 4)

**Q: How strong is the placebo effect, per the book?**
A: Talbot reports that on average 35 percent of people receiving a placebo experience a significant effect; placebos were 54 percent as effective as aspirin and 56 percent as effective as morphine in double-blind studies; 1950s sham angina surgery (cutting patients open and sewing them up) relieved angina as well as the real operation; subjects given placebos in a supposed LSD study had hours-long "trips"; and cis-platinum's effectiveness fell from about 75 percent to 25–30 percent once its wonder-drug aura faded. He concedes the counterexamples: vitamin C cures scurvy and insulin works for skeptical diabetics. (Ch 4)

**Q: What does multiple personality disorder show about the body, per Talbot?**
A: That the body is a "multiple image hologram" that can switch biological states "as rapidly as the flutter of a deck of cards": Braun's patient was allergic to orange juice in every personality but one; Howland's patient's wasp-swollen eye healed within an hour when his "anesthetic personality" took over; diabetes, epilepsy, visual acuity, voice patterns, menstrual cycles, and even eye color change with personality, and each personality has a different brain-wave pattern (Putnam). Since integrated multiples retain the ability, Talbot argues it must lie latent in all our psyches — and since brain waves are a global property, the "self" may be a hologram the brain shuttles between like a slide projector. (Ch 3, Ch 4)

**Q: What is the Brocq's disease case and why does Talbot make so much of it?**
A: In 1951 hypnotherapist A. A. Mason at Queen Victoria Hospital, London, put a sixteen-year-old boy with Brocq's disease — a hereditary condition covering the skin in thick, cracking reptile-like scales — into deep trance and told him the disease was healing; the scales on his left arm fell off in five days, the arm was normal in ten, and he was symptom-free for at least five years. Because Brocq's is genetic, Talbot argues this means the mind can override "the masterplan, our DNA programming itself." (Ch 4)

**Q: What is the Vittorio Michelli case?**
A: Michelli was admitted to the Military Hospital of Verona in 1962 with a malignant sarcoma that completely disintegrated his left hip, leaving his upper leg bone floating in soft tissue; after bathing in the spring at Lourdes he felt heat move through his body, the tumor shrank, and over several years his hip bone completely regenerated — which Talbot reports medicine generally views as impossible. The Vatican's Medical Commission ruled it a miracle, confirming "categorically and without doubt" a bone reconstruction "of a type unknown in the annals of world medicine." Talbot suggests no physical law was violated — rather an unknown natural process, likely triggered by faith accessing very deep levels of the psyche. (Ch 4)

**Q: How does Talbot explain stigmata?**
A: As self-induced, mind-generated images rather than divine wounds: all stigmatists' wounds are in the hands and feet because that is how art has depicted the crucifixion since the eighth century, whereas Roman practice was to nail through the wrists; Gemma Galgani's wounds precisely mirrored her own favorite crucifix; and Thurston argued wounds vary between stigmatists and many sufferers showed hysteria. The nail-like protuberances of St. Francis and Therese Neumann, and the Passion symbols found on St. Veronica Giuliani's heart at autopsy, fulfill for Talbot the Soviet researchers Dubrov and Pushkin's criterion — "ejection of psychophysical structures outside the brain" as direct evidence of brain holograms. (Ch 4)

**Q: What is the human energy field, and what structure is claimed for it?**
A: A subtle field around the body — prana in India, ch'i in China, nefish in the Kabbalah, listed under 97 names in 97 cultures by White and Krippner — which Talbot reports is visible to people with a developed capacity, himself included (he sees the crown chakra). Many psychics describe seven layers (etheric, astral/emotional, mental, causal, plus three higher) and chakras connected to endocrine glands; healers like Barbara Brennan claim strikingly accurate diagnoses from it, and Brennan reads auras at distances as far as New York to Italy, which Talbot takes as evidence the field is nonlocal. He warns, though, that the structures may be participatory — partly created by observers' beliefs, as with the Sanskrit letter yam seen in the heart chakra. (Ch 6)

**Q: What is therapeutic touch and what study supports it?**
A: NYU nursing professor Dolores Krieger, after finding the healer Oscar Estebany could raise ill patients' hemoglobin levels by manipulating their fields, learned to feel and heal field blockages under clairvoyant Dora Kunz and named the method "therapeutic touch"; she has taught it to thousands of nurses. In Janet Quinn's double-blind study, heart patients treated by trained nurses showed a 17 percent drop in anxiety after five minutes, while patients receiving mimicked motions from untrained nurses showed no change — the lead story in the New York Times Science Times of March 26, 1985. (Ch 6)

**Q: What evidence suggests the body itself is holographic?**
A: Talbot cites acupuncture microsystems: Nogier's 1957 "little man in the ear" map (which the Chinese had drawn nearly 4,000 years earlier), Oleson's blind ear-diagnosis study agreeing with medical diagnoses 75.2 percent of the time, ear acupuncture eliminating drug requirements in 12 of 14 addicts in an average of 5 days, and Dale's evidence for eighteen micro-acupuncture holograms across the body. He adds hand-pattern findings — 72 percent of Alzheimer's patients versus 26 percent of controls with the ulnar-loop fingerprint on 8+ fingers, and 10 genetic disabilities associated with hand patterns — as signs every part contains an image of the whole. (Ch 4)

## Psi & miracles

**Q: What is the San Gennaro blood miracle and what does Talbot conclude about it?**
A: Twice yearly in Naples a vial of brown crusty substance — allegedly the blood of St. Januarius, beheaded in A.D. 305 — turns to bubbling bright-red liquid when the crowd shouts at it; University of Naples scientists verified spectroscopically in 1902 that it is real blood, and failures to liquefy have preceded disasters like the 1976 earthquake. Talbot concludes the liquefaction is more likely caused by the intense devotion and belief of the witnessing crowd — that is, collective psychokinesis — than by the saint. He adds Rogo's observation that no blood miracles predate San Gennaro, reading it as a reality-field "flickering into existence" in the fourteenth century. (Ch 5)

**Q: Who were the convulsionaires and why do they matter to the argument?**
A: Jansenist worshipers at the Paris tomb of deacon François de Paris (from 1727) who fell into contagious convulsions and displayed invulnerability: Montgeron documented twenty-year-old Jeanne Maulet taking one hundred blows to the stomach from a thirty-pound hammer that then knocked a hole in a stone wall at the twenty-fifth blow, and a woman arched over a sharp peg unharmed by a repeatedly dropped fifty-pound stone. Witnesses included hostile Church investigators who went away confirming the events, and Hume wrote that never were so many miracles ascribed to one person. For Talbot they pose the inertia problem — where does the deflected energy go? — which pushes him beyond simple PK to Grof's idea of reprogramming reality's "generative matrix" in the implicate order. (Ch 5)

**Q: What fire-immunity cases does Talbot cite?**
A: Botanist William Tufts Brigham crossing a Kilauea lava flow with three kahunas, socks burning off his feet while the feet stayed unharmed; convulsionaire Marie Sonnet lying over a blazing fire for half an hour; the Camisard leader Claris preaching unharmed atop his own burning pyre before six hundred witnesses; blacksmith Nathan Coker holding an incandescent shovel to his feet before doctors (New York Herald, 1871); Mohotty walking a twenty-foot pit measured at 1,328 degrees Fahrenheit by a National Geographic team; and Pentecostals holding hands in acetylene flames. Talbot uses fire immunity as the proof case that the laws of physics are suspendable "habits." (Ch 4, Ch 5)

**Q: What does Talbot report about Sathya Sai Baba?**
A: The Indian holy man reportedly materializes jewelry, sizzling-hot delicacies, out-of-season fruits, and prodigious amounts of sacred ash out of thin air; Icelandic psychologist Erlendur Haraldsson studied him for over ten years, witnessed an on-request materialization of an extremely rare double rudraksha capped in twenty-two-carat gold, and with Karlis Osis actually watched ash appear in the air below Sai Baba's palm. Haraldsson admits he cannot conclusively rule out sleight of hand but notes no credible evidence of fakery in half a century of demonstrations, freely filmed. Talbot also reports witnesses seeing Sai Baba vanish and reappear a hundred yards away — suggesting to him that bodies are holographic projections. (Ch 5, Ch 8)

**Q: What is Therese Neumann's inedia and how was it verified, per the book?**
A: The Bavarian stigmatist gave up food and water entirely in 1927 and reportedly consumed nothing for the next thirty-five years. That year the bishop of Regensburg sent a commission: from July 14 to 29, four Franciscan nursing sisters under Dr. Seidl watched her day and night, measuring even her mouth-rinsing water — she never dehydrated, kept constant weight (regaining even the nearly nine pounds of blood lost in her weekly stigmata bleedings), and the observers were completely convinced. Talbot argues this implies she regularly materialized the water and nutrients keeping her alive, since the body can rarely survive even a week without water. (Ch 5)

**Q: What was Tart's mutual hypnosis experiment?**
A: Charles Tart had two graduate students, Anne and Bill — both deep-trance subjects and skilled hypnotists — hypnotize each other; opening their eyes, they jointly experienced a beach of unearthly beauty with champagne-like seas and crystalline glowing rocks, talked to each other inside the shared world while silent to Tart, and later found they had shared details for which no verbal stimuli existed on the tapes. The realness eventually frightened them into stopping, and Bill gave up hypnosis entirely. Talbot presents it as a perfect example of a holographic reality: a fully sensory 3-D construct created out of interconnectedness and sustained by consciousness. (Ch 5)

**Q: What is remote viewing and who established it, per Talbot?**
A: Physicists Harold Puthoff and Russell Targ at Stanford Research Institute found just about everyone they tested could accurately describe what a distant person was seeing simply by relaxing and reporting mental images — duplicated by dozens of labs, and working even with no human observer at the target. Jahn himself, receiving for a colleague in Paris, saw a knight in armor; the sender stood before a building ornamented with exactly such statuary. Bohm's reading is that remote viewing is "inverse psychokinesis" — a resonance of meanings from object to mind rather than mind to object. (Ch 5, Ch 7)

**Q: What is the Tom and the invisible daughter story meant to show?**
A: A hypnotist told Tom his teenage daughter Laura would be invisible on waking; with Laura standing directly before him, Tom looked through her and then correctly identified — and read the inscription on — a watch the hypnotist pressed against her back, information unknown to everyone present. Talbot, who witnessed it, argues perception is not based solely on the five senses: Tom may have received the information telepathically from the hypnotist's mind. He extends this to propose that normal waking consciousness is itself a kind of hypnosis (noting hypnosis produces no unusual EEG patterns) and consensus reality a collectively constructed reality-field. (Ch 5)

**Q: How does Talbot explain poltergeists?**
A: As unconscious psychokinesis centered on a focal person rather than a place — the view he says many parapsychologists hold, and one he accepts from experience: his own childhood home hosted a poltergeist that followed him to college, tracked his moods, showered gravel and polished stones, and once dropped cold spaghetti on his chest in a sealed New York apartment; in 1976 he watched a piece of brown drift glass pop into existence in midair. He pairs this with Watson's Indonesian case of Alin, the resented half-sister around whom salt poured from midair and a table bucked and flipped, and Owen's survey of materializing objects in poltergeist cases dating from A.D. 530. (Ch 5)

**Q: What is the "laws of physics as habits" claim?**
A: Following Peat and the holographic idea, Talbot argues matter and physical law are constantly born anew out of the implicate order, so repetition of pattern is a "material neurosis" — chairs and tables are habits in the flux, and even physical laws are deeply ingrained programs (vast whirlpools of inertial power, like Shainberg's thought vortices) that can be suspended under special circumstances. Bohm's own proposed mechanism for PK is a noncausal "resonance of meanings" between mind and material system, requiring a "super" nonlocality deeper than ordinary quantum nonlocality. The through-line Talbot names: the deeper and more emotionally charged our beliefs, the greater the changes we can make in body and reality. (Ch 5)

## Time

**Q: What is retrocognition and what is the star case?**
A: Retrocognition is "the ability of certain individuals to shift the focus of their attention and literally gaze back into the past." The star case is Polish clairvoyant Stefan Ossowiecki, who from a fragment of petrified foot saw an Egyptian prince's daughter and could "fast-forward" her life like a movie; tested by ethnologist Stanislaw Poniatowski with provenance-concealed stone tools, he repeatedly identified age, culture, and origin, and gave over one hundred initially implausible details later proved correct — complex Magdalenian hairstyles and Stone Age oil lamps among them. Talbot's frame: the past re-enfolds into Bohm's implicate order, where mind and the record of the past "are, in a manner of speaking, already neighbors." (Ch 7)

**Q: What is psychic archaeology, per the book?**
A: Talbot claims that "for three-quarters of a century psychic archaeology has been a reality" (quoting Schwartz): Toronto professor Norman Emerson's psychic informant George McMullen paced out an Iroquois longhouse on bare ground that excavation confirmed six months later, and Emerson told Canada's leading archaeologists in 1973 the work deserved "first priority"; archaeologist Clarence Weiant credited a psychic with his famous Tres Zapotes find; Croiset and Garrett produced similar psychometry results for Tenhaeff, Valkhoff, and LeShan. (Ch 7)

**Q: What laboratory evidence for precognition does Talbot cite?**
A: The Rhines' card-guessing at odds of three million to one against chance; Helmut Schmidt's random-event machine results at one billion to one over sixty thousand trials; Maimonides Dream Laboratory subjects dreaming of paintings chosen the next day (one dreamed a patient escaping through an archway the night before Van Gogh's Hospital Corridor at St. Remy was drawn); Hella Hammid describing the "black iron triangle" (a squeaking swing) Puthoff would visit before the target was even selected; Jahn and Dunne's 62 percent accuracy over 334 formal trials; and Croiset's chair tests — in the 1969 Eisenbud study he described, from Utrecht, the Denver man who would sit in a randomly chosen seat seventeen days later, correct in every detail but three-quarters of an inch of height. (Ch 7)

**Q: Is the future fixed, according to the book?**
A: No — Talbot argues it is "substantive enough for us to perceive it, but malleable enough to be susceptible to change." Loye pictures multiple parallel holographic futures between which we make "hololeaps" when acting on premonitions; Bohm says coming events cast "shadows... deep in the implicate order"; Swann and the kahunas speak of "crystallizing possibilities," with great world events crystallized furthest in advance. The nineteen documented Titanic premonitions (some heeded, some not) and Cox's finding that significantly fewer people rode trains on accident days are his evidence that we constantly precognize and can choose. (Ch 7)

**Q: What did Whitton's between-life regression research find?**
A: Toronto psychiatry professor Joel Whitton's roughly thirty hypnotic subjects reported numerous past lives (some twenty to twenty-five), a light-filled between-life realm without time or space, and a hyperhonest "metaconscious" state in which they had planned their next life's important events — one woman had planned her own rape at thirty-seven to transform her "entire soul complexion," and a man had chosen kidney disease as self-punishment with a healing encounter prearranged. Corroborating details include a Canadian with a childhood British accent who recalled dying as a British WWII pilot, and a subject who produced authentic Old Norse and extinct Sassanid Pahlavi script. Talbot explicitly notes the evidence does not prove reincarnation and the book does not argue that it does. (Ch 7)

**Q: What is Stevenson's birthmark evidence?**
A: University of Virginia psychiatrist Ian Stevenson, in thirty-plus years of studying thousands of children who spontaneously remember apparent past lives (typically talking about them between ages two and four, with verifiable names and places), found injuries carrying over as birthmarks: a boy murdered by throat-slitting bore a long reddish mark across his neck, and another who shot himself had two marks aligned along the bullet's entry and exit trajectory, sometimes confirmed against hospital or autopsy records. Stevenson infers a nonphysical "template" body carrying wound imprints between lives — which Talbot links to Tiller's holographic energy-field template. Stevenson found no evidence of retributive karma or an external judge: "we are the makers of our own souls." (Ch 7)

**Q: What is retroactive psychokinesis?**
A: In the Schmidt–Schlitz work announced at the 1988 Parapsychological Association convention, volunteers listening to prerecorded cassettes of random tone sequences tried to lengthen the pleasing sounds — and the original recordings, examined afterward, contained significantly more pleasing sound than probability allowed, as if subjects had "reached back through time" to influence the randomization that had already occurred. Regular meditators exerted a greater effect than non-meditators, suggesting to Talbot that contact with the unconscious is the key to the reality-structuring psyche. (Ch 7)

**Q: What is the Moberly–Jourdain Versailles case?**
A: On August 10, 1901, Anne Moberly and Eleanor Jourdain — principal and vice principal of St. Hugh's College, Oxford — walking in the garden of the Petit Trianon saw a shimmering pass over the landscape, after which everyone wore eighteenth-century dress; they saw an aristocratic lady painting whom they later concluded was Marie Antoinette on the day of the sacking of the Tuileries, and when the vision vanished their path was blocked by an old stone wall. Talbot stresses they did not merely view the past but walked into it, and that publishing risked their academic reputations for no benefit. He groups it with a 1955 sighting in the same garden and Buterbaugh's view of a Nebraska campus as it was fifty years earlier. (Ch 7)

**Q: What did Wambach's regressions and Snow's future progressions find?**
A: Helen Wambach's group hypnotic regressions of thousands of subjects were strikingly anti-fantasy: over 90 percent recalled lives as peasants, laborers, and food gatherers, under 10 percent aristocrats, none famous — and details tracked history, such as three-pronged forks before 1790 and four-pronged after. Her future-progression project, completed by Chet Snow with 2,500 participants, found nearly all agreed Earth's future population had decreased dramatically, and reports split into four future types: sterile space-station dwellers, harmonious "New Agers," bleak hi-tech urbanites, and post-disaster survivors. Snow's holographic reading, like Loye's, is that several potential "holoverses" are forming and humanity is choosing among them en masse. (Ch 7)

## NDEs, OBEs & other realities

**Q: What is the Maria tennis-shoe case and why does it matter?**
A: Maria, a Seattle coronary patient, went into cardiac arrest and later told skeptical social worker Kimberly Clark she had floated out of her body, "thought herself" outside the hospital, and ended up "eyeball to shoelace" with an old tennis shoe on a third-floor ledge — noting the little toe worn through the fabric and the lace stuck under the heel. Clark could only see the shoe by pressing against a narrow window and could only confirm the details after retrieving it; the perspective required floating right outside at close range. It matters as veridical evidence — Clark: "It was very concrete evidence for me" — that OBE perception can be accurate about things the physical senses could not access. (Ch 8)

**Q: What was Sabom's controlled comparison of cardiac patients?**
A: Emory cardiologist Michael Sabom compared 32 cardiac patients who reported OBEs during heart attacks with 25 who had not: of the non-experiencers asked to imagine their resuscitations, 20 made major mistakes; of the experiencers, 26 gave correct general descriptions, 6 gave highly detailed accurate ones, and 1 gave a blow-by-blow account so accurate Sabom was stunned. He became an ardent believer: "The out-of-body hypothesis simply seems to fit best with the data at hand." (Ch 8)

**Q: Why does Talbot argue NDEs are not hallucinations?**
A: Five converging facts he cites: NDEs occur worldwide; studies show no relationship to age, sex, race, religion, education, income, or church attendance; the core experience is cross-culturally consistent (Osis and Haraldsson's comparison of nearly nine hundred deathbed visions in India and the US found the same core, though Americans saw a Christian figure and Indians a Hindu one); perception during NDEs can be veridical, even in the blind (Kubler-Ross's blind patients described clothing colors and jewelry); and NDEs occur in patients with flat EEGs, whereas hallucinations register on an EEG. A 1981 Gallup poll put American NDEers at eight million — about one in twenty adults. (Ch 8)

**Q: What is the life review and why does Ring call it holographic?**
A: The panoramic, instantaneous replay of one's entire life in complete sensory detail — "like climbing right inside a movie of your life... Total, total recall. And it all happens in an instant" — including every thought and the felt emotions of everyone one affected (one woman relived snatching a toy and felt her sister's loss). Judgment is self-judgment only; the beings of light never condemn. Ring calls it "a holographic phenomenon par excellence" — three-dimensional, wraparound, an aleph-like moment containing all moments — and per Moody and others, many NDEers themselves use the word "holographic." (Ch 8)

**Q: What do the beings of light teach, per the NDE reports Talbot collects?**
A: Two values above all: love — compassion as "the barometer of grace," with the recurring question "Did you do it out of love?" — and knowledge, one of the few things "you will be able to take with you after you have died." Their appearance (Christian personage, Hindu deity) is sculpted by the experiencer's beliefs; Whitton's subjects said the between-life counselors were entities who had completed their incarnations, while Monroe found their ultimate identity unknown. Moody's conclusion, as Talbot reports it: we live in a universe far more benevolent than we realize. (Ch 8)

**Q: How common are OBEs, and what does the second body turn out to be?**
A: Surveys Talbot cites: Bourguignon found OBE traditions in 437 of 488 world societies (89 percent); student polls by Green and Irwin ran 19–34 percent, averaging out to roughly one in five people (other studies say one in ten); and Gabbard, Twemlow, and Jones found 85 percent of 339 OBEers called the experience pleasant. The "second body" is not a fixed phantom double but a hologram sculpted by beliefs — naked or clothed, ball of light, cloud, or no form at all; Monroe holds our core form is a "vibrational pattern [comprised] of many interacting and resonating frequencies." (Ch 8)

**Q: What does Talbot actually conclude about UFOs?**
A: That probably no single explanation covers the phenomenon, but UFOs are best understood as omnijective — real yet mind-shaped holographic phenomena — not extraterrestrial spacecraft: there are too many sightings, the occupants are implausibly humanoid, they don't behave like visitors or like physical objects (radar-tracked right-angle turns, shape-shifting, vanishing; Vallee: "It is the behavior of an image, or a holographic projection"). He entertains Grosso's and Jung's collective-unconscious projections and Vallee's and Raschke's extradimensional intelligence, and suggests both at once — collective belief may open a "doorway" through which such intelligences appear, with our minds sculpting their appearance and the "medical examinations" possibly symbolic probings of our subtle anatomy. (Ch 8)

**Q: What are the Zeitoun apparitions and how does the book read them?**
A: From 1968 to 1971, glowing three-dimensional figures of Mary, Joseph, and the Christ Child appeared weekly on the dome of a Coptic church in a Cairo suburb — heralded by flashes of light, coalescing from amorphous luminosity, accompanied by doves "formed of pure light," seen by hundreds of thousands and extensively photographed, waning to luminous fog as interest waned. Grosso says witnesses sound like they are "describing some sort of holographic projection" and reads such visions as psychic projections of a collective unconscious disturbed by modern threats; Rogo adds that a 1925 prophecy-dream may have built a forty-year "psychic blueprint" of the Virgin in the church. (Ch 8)

**Q: What does the book say lucid dreams are?**
A: Physicist Fred Alan Wolf's view (1987 Association for the Study of Dreams talk) is that all dreams are internal holograms: ordinary dreams are less-vivid virtual images made of diverging waves, while lucid dreams are "real images" formed by converging waves whose viewer is bathed in and contained by the scene — hence their eerie vividness. Wolf postulates lucid dreams (perhaps all dreams) are actually visits to parallel universes — "smaller holograms within the... cosmic hologram" — and calls the skill "parallel universe awareness." (Intro, Ch 3)

## Traditions & the ancient world

**Q: How do ancient traditions prefigure the implicate/explicate order, per Talbot?**
A: He argues virtually all of them do: the Tibetan void is the birthplace of all things pouring out in "boundless flux," with only the void real; the Hindu Brahman is the formless illusion-maker behind maya, with consciousness more fundamental than matter; the Zohar notes the Hebrew verb baro, "to create," implies creating an illusion; a Dogon elder says "To draw up and then return what one had drawn — that is the life of the world"; and Douglas Sharon calls a vital essence underlying visible forms probably the central concept of shamanism everywhere. (Ch 9)

**Q: What are Indra's net and Fa-Tsang's demonstration?**
A: The Avatamsaka Sutra likens the universe to a network of pearls over the god Indra's palace, arranged so that looking at one shows all others reflected in it — each object "involves every other object and, in fact, is everything else." When Empress Wu couldn't grasp it, the seventh-century Hua-yen founder Fa-Tsang hung a candle in a room full of mirrors (the One to the many) and placed a polished crystal at the center reflecting everything (the many to the One) — and like Bohm with the holomovement, he stressed his static model missed the cosmos's constant movement. (Ch 9)

**Q: What is the macrocosm/microcosm lineage — and the Leibniz "full circle"?**
A: The twelfth-century Sufis said "the macrocosm is the microcosm," Hermes Trismegistus taught "the without is like the within of things," the alchemists distilled it into "As above, so below," and the idea runs through Anaximenes, Pythagoras, Heraclitus, Plato, Philo, and Maimonides — with Black Elk's "Anywhere is the center of the world" and Empedocles' circle "whose center is everywhere" as further echoes. Talbot's full-circle point: Leibniz, arguably influenced by Hua-yen Buddhism, proposed whole-reflecting monads and also gave the world integral calculus — the very mathematics that enabled Gabor to invent the hologram. (Ch 9)

**Q: What do the kahunas, Tibetan tantrics, and Sufis say about thought creating destiny?**
A: The kahunas hold that the aumakua (high self) sees the crystallized future and weaves destiny from thought forms made of kino mea, "shadowy body stuff" that also templates the physical body; the Tibetan tantrics say every mental act produces waves of tsal, the collective tsal of all beings animating the universe, with Kargyupa monks spending up to seven years perfecting visualization; the Persian Sufis' alam almithal forms idea-images on the adjacent template plane, governed by the heart chakra (himma). Talbot lines these up with Cayce's "thought is the builder" and the Buddha's "We are what we think," and judges the idea "not so farfetched. It even seems probable" in a holographic universe. (Ch 7)

**Q: Who was Swedenborg and why does the book care?**
A: The Swedish polymath (born 1688) — "the Leonardo da Vinci of his era" — whose trances took him out-of-body to "heaven"; his veridical feats included relaying to the queen of Sweden information only she and her dead brother knew, and locating a widow's secret desk compartment (the incident that prompted Kant's Dreams of a Spirit-Seer). His nearly twenty volumes describe the tunnel, beings of light, the life review ("the opening of the Book of Lives"), knowledge as nourishment, and dense picture-language "portrayals" in a "wave-substance." Theologian George Dole notes Swedenborg's universe is sustained by two wavelike inflows — "we can view ourselves as interference patterns... we are where the waves meet" — a striking anticipation of the hologram, per Talbot. (Ch 8)

**Q: What is the dreamtime, and how does the book end with it?**
A: The Australian aborigines' transcendent reality, visited by shamans in trance and nearly identical to Western afterlife descriptions (per Thero) — a dimension without time, space, or boundaries requiring "survival in infinity"; aborigines hold that the true source of mind is there, that the whole world once was dreamtime, and that the earth will return to it. Talbot speculates that as we learn to manipulate Jahn and Dunne's "interface between consciousness and its environment," reality may become plastic again, and the rising tide of NDEs, visions, and encounters exists to teach us to handle it. The book's final line: "We are, as the aborigines say, just learning how to survive in infinity." (Ch 8, Ch 9)

## People

**Q: How do Pribram's and Bohm's routes to the hologram differ?**
A: Pribram, a Stanford neurophysiologist and author of Languages of the Brain, got there from the failures of standard brain theory: Lashley's inextinguishable rat memories and his own monkey studies drove him to distributed memory, and a mid-1960s article on holograms supplied the mechanism (first holographic-brain article, 1966). Bohm, a University of London physicist and Einstein protégé, got there from the failures of standard quantum theory: plasmas behaving like organisms, dissatisfaction with Bohr's "complete" theory, his 1952 quantum potential, and the ink-in-glycerine device led him to the implicate order (Wholeness and the Implicate Order, 1980). Talbot stresses they arrived independently, from opposite directions, and welds them together at the end of chapter 2: the brain is a hologram enfolded in a holographic universe. (Intro, Ch 1, Ch 2)

**Q: Why does Talbot dwell on Bohm's and Pribram's courage?**
A: He argues their character adds weight to the hypothesis: Bohm refused to testify against Oppenheimer before McCarthy's committee in 1951, lost his Princeton job, and never taught in the US again; Pribram, as a young neurosurgeon in Jacksonville and then at Yale, refused to allow lobotomies during the era when Moniz's procedure won a Nobel Prize and Walter Freeman boasted lobotomies "made good American citizens" of misfits — a stance that nearly cost Pribram his job. Backing so controversial an idea with their reputations, Talbot argues, was not the easy path. (Intro)

**Q: Who is Stanislav Grof and what did his research find?**
A: A psychiatric researcher (Prague, then the Maryland Psychiatric Research Center and Johns Hopkins) who personally guided over three thousand LSD sessions and studied two thousand more: patients reported womb memories with embryological detail exceeding their education, ancestral and racial memories (an uneducated patient detailing Egyptian embalming), identification with animals, and in the Ladislav case a telepathic message from a discarnate son verified by a phone call to Kromeriz. Grof concluded existing paradigms need "drastic revision" and only a holographic model can explain such transpersonal experiences; his and Christina Grof's drug-free "holotropic therapy" (breathing, music, bodywork) produces equally spectacular reports. (Intro, Ch 3)

**Q: Who are Jahn and Dunne, and how does their theory differ from Bohm's?**
A: Robert Jahn, dean emeritus of Princeton's engineering school, founded the PEAR lab in 1979 after a student's PK experiment provoked him; with clinical psychologist Brenda Dunne he accumulated REG and pinball data showing small but statistically significant PK in the majority of volunteers, with stable individual "signatures." Their theory holds that consciousness itself has a wave/particle duality and that reality results from the interface of consciousness's wavelike aspect with matter's wave patterns — and, unlike Bohm, they believe subatomic particles lack distinct reality until consciousness enters the picture ("we are indeed creating reality by any reasonable definition of the term," Jahn says). Talbot notes they explicitly do not identify with the holographic model, though Dunne offers the metaphor of an individual consciousness as a laser intersecting a pattern in the cosmic hologram. (Intro, Ch 5)

**Q: Who is Kenneth Ring and what is his position?**
A: A University of Connecticut psychologist, president of the International Association for Near-Death Studies, and one of the first to apply statistical analysis to NDEs (Life at Death, 1980). He proposed in 1980 that NDEs — and death itself — are "really nothing more than" the shifting of consciousness from one level of the hologram of reality to another; he reads the afterlife realm as built of interacting "thought structures" forming patterns like interference waves, argues UFO encounters are imaginal experiences akin to NDEs, and concludes from rising NDE numbers that we may be witnessing "the shamanizing of modern humanity" — an evolutionary thrust toward higher consciousness. (Intro, Ch 8, Ch 9)

**Q: What does Talbot claim about himself?**
A: That he grew up in a psychic family and experienced the phenomena firsthand: a poltergeist that followed him from childhood through college and materialized stones, coins, and once mid-air drift glass; vivid precognitive dreams; spontaneous aura sight from age seventeen (he sees the crown chakra); a teenage OBE in which he located a neighbor's lost library book; and the 1975 "umbrella incident," in which he and a professor — minutes after discussing Castaneda's two-equally-real-realities claim — watched a stranger's green umbrella crackle, flicker, and transform into a gnarled stick, their written accounts matching exactly. He concedes such experiences can only be viewed as anecdotal evidence, but says for him they are the most compelling proof of all. (Intro, Ch 5, Ch 6, Ch 8)

## Critical & meta

**Q: What does the book admit is controversial or unproven?**
A: Talbot concedes in the Introduction that the holographic model is highly controversial, not accepted by a majority of scientists, and still "an idea in the making" — a mosaic of viewpoints not yet integrated — so calling it a "model" or "theory" is loose usage, and Bohm and Pribram themselves do not embrace all the views and conclusions in the book. Later he flags further limits: his own experiences are anecdotal; the reincarnation evidence "does not prove that reincarnation exists"; iridology and reflexology are "not experimentally vindicated"; and structures psychics find in the energy field may be partly created by their own beliefs. (Intro, Ch 4, Ch 6, Ch 7)

**Q: What do the named skeptics in the book say?**
A: Neuropsychologist Frank Wood: "there are precious few experimental findings for which holography is the necessary, or even preferable, explanation." Yale physicist Lee Smolin does not find Bohm's theory "very compelling, physically," and Abner Shimony admits he doesn't understand it and wonders how literally to take the metaphor — while still crediting Bohm's courage. Talbot also reports Pribram's counter (a book in press with over 500 supporting references) and Bohm's defense that theories like superstrings won't be testable for decades either. (Ch 1, Ch 2)

**Q: How does Talbot explain science's resistance to the paranormal?**
A: As prejudice and belief-addiction rather than objectivity: scientists share everyone's cultural prejudices, and Yale surgeon Bernie Siegel's observation — people are addicted to their beliefs and act like addicts when challenged — is generalized by Talbot ("We are addicted to our beliefs... the powerful opium of our dogmas"). His exhibits: a famous skeptical physicist who assured him an experiment showed "no evidence of any psychic functioning whatsoever" when its results were striking, and Irvin Child's American Psychologist finding that the Maimonides ESP dream work was ignored and so "severely distorted" in print that its importance was obscured. (Intro)

**Q: What restructuring of science does the final chapter call for?**
A: Three components: accept the existence of psychic and spiritual phenomena (Harman: grant face validity to experiences reported "through the ages and across cultures"); broaden what counts as evidence so mass anecdotal testimony about important matters isn't dismissed (Stevenson: "better to learn what is probable about important matters than to be certain about trivial ones"); and replace detached objectivity with participation, on the model of Harner drinking ayahuasca rather than taking notes. Talbot adds the fashionability double standard — the unproven Big Bang is accepted while equally compelling NDE claims would be attacked — and, citing Weiss's deluge of secretly believing psychiatrists and a survey in which 58 percent of 228 psychiatrists called psychic phenomena important, says what's needed is "the parapsychological equivalent of a Rosa Parks." (Ch 9)

**Q: What is the book's final answer to who is dreaming the hologram?**
A: That the question is meaningless: the Tibetan Book of the Dead, the Vedas, and Christianity all cast reality as a dream or divine thought, but in a holographic universe the part is the whole, so asking whether God or the collective consciousness of all things is the dreamer collapses — Wheeler's self-reference cosmology, in which we create the particles and they create us. Talbot gives the last word on this to the Kalahari Bushmen: "The dream is dreaming itself." (Ch 8)
