# Being and Enlightenment

## In one paragraph
This opening chapter lays down the metaphysical and practical foundation of the whole book: Eckhart Tolle introduces Being — the eternal, ever-present One Life beyond and within all forms — and defines enlightenment not as a superhuman accomplishment but as the natural state of felt oneness with Being. He identifies the chief obstacle as identification with the mind, which makes thinking compulsive, creates a false mind-made self (the ego), and produces the illusion of separation, fear, and conflict. The remedy he offers is immediately practical: "watching the thinker" — listening impartially to the voice in the head as a witnessing presence — or, alternatively, directing full attention into the Now, both of which create gaps of "no-mind" in the mental stream where stillness, peace, and the joy of Being emerge. Tolle then extends the meaning of "mind" beyond thought to include emotion, which he defines as the body's reaction to the mind, and teaches watching emotions in the body's inner energy field as the same witnessing practice. The chapter closes by naming the inner energy field of the body as "the doorway into Being," setting up the practices of the rest of Part One.

## Core claims
- Tolle claims there is an eternal, ever-present One Life beyond the myriad forms of life subject to birth and death; many call it God, he calls it Being — an open concept that does not reduce the infinite invisible to a finite entity and of which no mental image can be formed; because Being is your very presence, accessible as the feeling of your own presence, "it is only a small step from the word Being to the experience of Being."
- He asserts that Being is not only beyond but deep within every form as its innermost invisible and indestructible essence, and is therefore accessible now as your own deepest self and true nature.
- Being, he says, cannot be grasped or understood mentally; it can only be known/felt when the mind is still and attention is fully in the Now.
- Enlightenment, per Tolle, is simply regaining awareness of Being and abiding in that state of "feeling-realization" — your natural state of felt oneness with Being, not a superhuman accomplishment (the ego likes to keep it looking superhuman); it is finding your true nature beyond name and form.
- The inability to feel this connectedness, he argues, gives rise to the illusion of separation — perceiving yourself as an isolated fragment — from which fear arises and conflicts within and without become the norm.
- The greatest obstacle to experiencing connectedness is identification with the mind, which makes thought compulsive; not being able to stop thinking is "a dreadful affliction" that goes unrecognized because almost everybody suffers from it and it is considered normal; this incessant mental noise also creates a false mind-made self that casts a shadow of fear and suffering.
- Identification with the mind creates an opaque screen of concepts, labels, images, words, judgments, and definitions that blocks all true relationship — between you and yourself, others, nature, and God — and makes you forget that beneath appearances you are one with all that is.
- The mind is a superb instrument used rightly, he says, but the disease is that you usually don't use it at all — it uses you; the delusion is believing you are your mind, as if possessed by an entity you take to be yourself.
- The beginning of freedom is the realization that you are not the thinker; the moment you start watching the thinker, a higher level of consciousness becomes activated.
- Tolle claims there is a vast realm of intelligence beyond thought — thought is only a tiny aspect of it — and all things that truly matter (beauty, love, creativity, joy, inner peace) arise from beyond the mind.
- Freeing yourself from your mind is "the only true liberation," and you can take the first step right now.
- The "I am" realization — the sense of your own presence as witness of a thought — is not a thought; it arises from beyond the mind.
- When you feel the conscious presence behind a thought, the thought loses its power and subsides, because you are no longer energizing the mind through identification with it — the beginning of the end of involuntary, compulsive thinking.
- Gaps of "no-mind" in the mental stream bring stillness and peace whose depth has no end, plus a subtle emanation of joy from within — the joy of Being; this inner connectedness makes you more alert than in the mind-identified state and (he says) raises the vibrational frequency of the energy field that gives life to the physical body.
- The state of pure consciousness is selfless, not selfish: the presence felt there is essentially you and at the same time inconceivably greater than you.
- The success criterion for presence practice is one thing only: the degree of peace you feel within.
- The single most vital step toward enlightenment, per Tolle, is learning to disidentify from the mind; every gap created in the mind stream strengthens the light of consciousness.
- The ego — the phantom, false self formed from personal and cultural conditioning and kept going by constant thinking — considers only past and future important; to it the present moment hardly exists, which is a "total reversal of the truth" and why the mind in ego mode is so dysfunctional.
- Even when the ego seems concerned with the present, it misperceives it through the eyes of the past or reduces it to a means to a mind-projected future end ("One day, when this, that, or the other happens, I am going to be okay, happy, at peace").
- The present moment holds the key to liberation, but you cannot find it as long as you are your mind.
- In the enlightened state you still use the thinking mind when needed, but more focused and effectively, free of involuntary internal dialogue; creative thinking requires oscillating every few minutes or so between thought and stillness, mind and no-mind, because thought disconnected from the vaster realm of consciousness quickly becomes barren, insane, destructive.
- Mind, as Tolle uses the word, includes emotions and all unconscious mental-emotional reactive patterns; emotion arises where mind and body meet — the body's reaction to your mind, a reflection of your mind in the body.
- The more identified you are with thinking, likes/dislikes, judgments — the less present you are as watching consciousness — the stronger the emotional energy charge; if you are cut off from your emotions, you will eventually experience them purely physically, as a physical problem or symptom.
- The body gives a truthful reflection of the mind: where thought and emotion apparently conflict, the thought will be the lie and the emotion the truth — not the ultimate truth of who you are, but the relative truth of your state of mind at that time.
- Unconscious mind activity you cannot yet catch as thoughts will always be reflected in the body as emotion, of which you can become aware; watching emotion this way brings all that is unconscious in you into the light of consciousness.

## Concepts
- **being** — The eternal, ever-present One Life beyond the myriad forms of life subject to birth and death; also deep within every form as its innermost invisible and indestructible essence. Tolle prefers the word over "God" because it is an open concept — impossible to form a mental image of, claimable by nobody as exclusive possession — and it is your very presence, immediately accessible as the feeling of your own presence.
- **enlightenment** — Regaining awareness of Being and abiding in that state of "feeling-realization"; your natural state of felt oneness with Being — connectedness with something immeasurable and indestructible that is essentially you yet much greater than you. Later restated as "rising above thought": using the mind when needed but being free of involuntary internal dialogue.
- **feeling-realization** — Tolle's term for the mode in which Being is known: felt when the mind is still and attention is fully in the Now, never understood mentally.
- **the-now / presence** — The present moment; being fully and intensely attentive in it. The present moment "holds the key to liberation," but cannot be found as long as you are your mind.
- **identification-with-the-mind** — The greatest obstacle to experiencing connectedness: believing you are your mind, so that the instrument uses you ("It’s almost as if you were possessed without knowing it"). It causes compulsive thought, creates the opaque screen of concepts/labels/judgments blocking all true relationship, and generates the false mind-made self.
- **compulsive-thinking** — The inability to stop thinking; "a dreadful affliction" unrecognized because nearly universal and considered normal. Its incessant mental noise prevents finding the inner stillness inseparable from Being.
- **illusion-of-separation** — The perception of yourself as an isolated fragment, arising from the inability to feel connectedness with Being; from it come fear and inner/outer conflict, and the forgetting that beneath appearances you are one with all that is.
- **watching-the-thinker** — The foundational practice: listening to the voice in your head as often as you can, impartially and without judging, being there as the witnessing presence. The moment you start watching the thinker, a higher level of consciousness activates.
- **witnessing-presence / i-am-realization** — The sense "There is the voice, and here I am listening to it, watching it." This sense of your own presence is not a thought; it arises from beyond the mind and constitutes a new dimension of consciousness.
- **gap-in-the-mind-stream / no-mind** — A discontinuity in the mental stream experienced when a thought subsides (or when attention is directed fully into the Now): consciousness without thought, in which you are highly alert and aware but not thinking. Gaps start at a few seconds and lengthen with practice, bringing stillness, peace, and the joy of Being. Tolle notes the state is "sometimes called" no-mind "in the East."
- **pure-consciousness** — The state reached by going deeply into no-mind: your own presence felt with such intensity and joy that thinking, emotions, body, and the external world become relatively insignificant; a selfless (not selfish) state beyond what you previously thought of as "your self."
- **meditation** — Per Tolle, its essence is drawing consciousness away from mind activity to create a gap of no-mind by becoming intensely conscious of the present moment — highly alert and aware but not thinking.
- **disidentification-from-the-mind** — "The single most vital step on your journey toward enlightenment"; no longer taking the content of the mind seriously because your sense of self does not depend on it. Every gap created in the mind stream strengthens the light of consciousness.
- **ego** — The phantom self: a mental image of who you are formed as you grow up from personal and cultural conditioning. As Tolle uses the term, it means a false self created by unconscious identification with the mind, consisting of mind activity and kept alive only by constant thinking; for it, only past and future matter and the present moment hardly exists.
- **mind (Tolle's usage)** — Not just thought: it includes emotions and all unconscious mental-emotional reactive patterns.
- **emotion** — The body's reaction to your mind; a reflection of your mind in the body, arising at the place where mind and body meet. Its energy charge grows stronger the more identified with thinking (and less present) you are; unfelt emotions eventually manifest as physical problems or symptoms.
- **watching-an-emotion** — The same practice as watching a thought, except that an emotion has a strong physical component and is primarily felt in the body; you allow it to be there without being controlled by it — you are no longer the emotion but the watcher, the observing presence. Practiced, it brings all that is unconscious into the light of consciousness.
- **inner-energy-field-of-the-body** — The felt field accessed by feeling the body from within; the starting point for those who have difficulty feeling emotions, and — when no emotion is present — "the doorway into Being."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Practice: watching the thinker (listening to the voice in your head).** Instruction: listen to the voice in your head as often as you can, paying particular attention to repetitive thought patterns — "those old audiotapes that have been playing in your head perhaps for many years." Listen impartially, without judging or condemning (judging means the same voice has come back in "through the back door"). Intended effect: you realize "There is the voice, and here I am listening to it" — a new dimension of consciousness (the witnessing presence) comes in; the thought loses its power and subsides because you stop energizing the mind through identification; gaps of no-mind appear (a few seconds at first, lengthening with practice) bringing stillness, peace, and eventually the joy of Being.
- **Practice: creating a gap by attention to the Now.** Instruction: instead of watching the thinker, simply direct the focus of attention into the Now — become intensely conscious of the present moment. Intended effect: consciousness is drawn away from mind activity, creating a gap of no-mind in which you are highly alert and aware but not thinking; Tolle calls this "the essence of meditation" and "a deeply satisfying thing to do."
- **Everyday exercise: the stairs.** Every time you walk up and down the stairs at home or work, pay close attention to every step, every movement, even your breathing — be totally present. Shows how a routine activity that is normally only a means to an end can be given fullest attention so it becomes an end in itself.
- **Everyday exercise: washing your hands.** Attend to all the sense perceptions of the activity: the sound and feel of the water, the movement of your hands, the scent of the soap, and so on. Same intended effect: full presence in a routine act.
- **Everyday exercise: getting into your car.** After you close the door, pause for a few seconds and observe the flow of your breath; become aware of "a silent but powerful sense of presence."
- **Success criterion for the practice.** Tolle gives one certain measure: the degree of peace that you feel within.
- **Milestone sign: smiling at the voice.** One day you may catch yourself smiling at the voice in your head "as you would smile at the antics of a child" — a sign you no longer take the mind's content seriously and your sense of self no longer depends on it.
- **Observation exercise: catch the ego at work.** "Observe your mind and you’ll see that this is how it works" — verifying for yourself that the mind in ego mode keeps the past alive ("because without it — who are you?"), projects into the future to ensure its continued survival and to seek some kind of release or fulfillment there, misperceives the present through the eyes of the past, or reduces it to a means to an end. Illustrative ego self-talk given: "One day, when this, that, or the other happens, I am going to be okay, happy, at peace."
- **Practice: feeling emotions via the inner body.** If you have difficulty feeling your emotions, start by focusing attention on the inner energy field of your body — feel the body from within; this puts you in touch with your emotions. Diagnostic rule: if thought and emotion apparently conflict, the thought is the lie, the emotion the truth (the relative truth of your state of mind at that time).
- **Practice: watching an emotion.** Feel the emotion in your body and allow it to be there without being controlled by it — be the watcher, the observing presence rather than the emotion. Intended effect: all that is unconscious in you is brought into the light of consciousness.
- **Habitual self-inquiry.** Make it a habit to ask: "What's going on inside me at this moment?" — the question points you in the right direction, but don't analyze, just watch; focus attention within and feel the energy of the emotion. If no emotion is present, take attention more deeply into the inner energy field of the body — the doorway into Being.

## Thread
As chapter 1 of Part One (Accessing the Power of Now), this unit is the book's foundation stone: it names the goal (felt oneness with Being = enlightenment), diagnoses the obstacle (identification with the mind, compulsive thinking, the ego), and delivers the first practices (watching the thinker, attention to the Now, everyday presence exercises, watching emotion). Its extension of "mind" to include emotion, and its closing pointer to the inner energy field of the body as "the doorway into Being," directly set up the later Part One material on the inner body and on freeing yourself from mind-made suffering; the ego's past/future fixation introduced here underpins the book's whole treatment of the Now, and the emotion-as-body's-reaction teaching prepares the ground for the pain-body concept. Everything that follows in the book — relationship as practice (Part Two) and acceptance/surrender (Part Three) — presupposes the disidentification from mind taught here.
