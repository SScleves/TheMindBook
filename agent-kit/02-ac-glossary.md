# Glossary

All definitions below are D.W. Pasulka's own (or those she adopts and attributes), drawn from the chapter notes; none are "corrected" toward outside knowledge, and none are endorsed as fact. **Chapter legend:** pointers use the note chapter numbers — **0** = Preface / front matter ("A Tour of Silicon Valley with Jacques Vallee"); **1** = Introduction; **2–8** = the numbered chapters; **9** = Conclusion ("The Artifact"); **10** = the author's own Glossary.

- **The absurd** — Vallee's constant factor in UFO and devotional testimony (e.g., Bigfoot seen from the astral plane near Mars) that triggers ridicule, keeps the phenomenon marginal, and thereby protects it while its symbols are absorbed unconsciously. Chapters: 6. Related: logic of camouflage, koan, credo quia absurdum.
- **Agents of information** — Pasulka's term for today's screenwriters, TV producers, and authors who interpret, spin, and market the UFO story, playing the role first-century scribes and redactors played for early religions. Chapters: 1. Related: mechanisms of belief.
- **Alien abduction** — The abduction narrative established by the Betty and Barney Hill case and central to Mack and Hopkins; Pasulka does not discount a real experience but insists it is "remembered with and through" media. Chapters: 5. Related: hypnotic regression, wraparound eyes.
- **Amor fati / "personal providence"** — From Nietzsche's *The Gay Science*: the love of fate, paired with his warning that reading every chance event as significant "precisely for us" threatens spiritual freedom; credit "beloved Chance," not an anxious Divinity. Chapters: 4. Related: synchronicity.
- **Amplificatory interpretation** — Jung's concept that, on meeting an unknown aerial object, an individual or group must "complete" its meaning, the unconscious amplifying associations into a framework attached to the event; non-reductive, admitting a real event while withholding certainty about it. Chapters: 4. Related: living myth / universal mass rumor, book encounter.
- **Anomalous** — The researchers' term of art for a phenomenon ruled out as known aircraft, drones, blimps, or military exercise, often leaving physical traces (burns on objects, grass, or people). Chapters: 3. Related: anomaly, flap.
- **Anomalous cognition** — Knowledge of future events, or other knowledge, for which there is no conventional explanation; a mode by which contact by nonhuman intelligence manifests, alongside telepathic communication. Chapters: 3, 7. Related: quantum field of information, remote viewing.
- **Anomalous technology** — Technology that the people Pasulka found believe "functions as creative inspiration"; the book's central subject alongside UFOs. Chapters: 0. Related: religion of technology.
- **Anomaly** — Data that does not fit the hypothesis; James redirects students toward anomalies (as Kuhn advised Mack) because scientific revolutions come through attending to them. Chapters: 9. Related: anomalous.
- **The artifact / donation** — The foil-like metallic material found at the New Mexico site whose analysis suggested it "could not have been made on Earth in 1947" — or "in this universe"; James calls such material a "donation" left by nonhuman beings, and the book notes religions always produce such physical objects. Chapters: 2, 3, 4, 9. Related: sacred artifact / relic, artifact–observer symbiosis.
- **Artifact–observer symbiosis** — Tyler's idea (crediting Strieber's "the artifacts we studied also studied us") that a symbiotic relationship exists between the artifact and those near it: "It generates information. Some people are able to pick up on that information." Chapters: 9. Related: the artifact / donation.
- **Associative universe** — Vallee's model that space-time is "a cultural artifact made possible by the invention of graph paper"; if the universe is informational and associative like software, miracles cease to be irrational and coincidences are simply how it retrieves information. Chapters: 6. Related: synchronicity, materiality of information.
- **Atmospherics** — The Marines' training term (per Pasulka's student Jose) for a point man's knack for sensing IEDs or danger beyond physical observation; validated pragmatically, not by belief. Chapters: 3, 6. Related: anomalous cognition.
- **"Augmentation of the Human Intellect"** — The program (per Kripal, "probably called" this) under which Vallee did his early internet work. Chapters: 0.
- **Autonomous agents** — Orsi's challenge to understand gods and saints as autonomous agents; Pasulka argues media puts fictional "beings" into the unconscious as "potentially real and autonomous agents" — "The beings really are in our heads." Chapters: 5, 6. Related: the new unconscious, media ecology.
- **"Based on true events" strategy** — A marketing technique (popularized by *The Blair Witch Project* writers) of framing fiction as a record of real events; religion films rely on viewers' prior assumption the events are real. Chapters: 5. Related: mechanisms of belief, realist montage.
- **Bedeviled** — James's most-used word for people tormented by unwelcome contact; his mission is an "antidote" to malicious contact. Chapters: 3. Related: contagion.
- **Belief communities / communities of interpretation** — The faith communities that form from interpretations of a contact event; in the history of religions, contact event → interpretations → institutions, each with a stake in the reading. Chapters: 1. Related: contact event.
- **Biblical–UFO framework / hermeneutic / interpretation** — An interpretive frame reading biblical and historical religious events as UFO events (Ezekiel's wheel the prime example); used by Eddy, Barry Downing, Michael J. S. Carter, and the Nation of Islam. Vallee's version refuses to define the events. Chapters: 1, 4, 6. Related: UFO hermeneutic / mythos, Merkabah.
- **Big data approach (to UFO reports)** — Pasulka's criticism that non-local, untrained researchers lump all database reports (misidentified blimps, drones, lightning) under "UFO," inflating apparent unknown sightings. Chapters: 7. Related: field investigators.
- **Bilocation** — A rare Catholic "charism": appearing to be in two places at once; Sister Maria of Agreda allegedly bilocated from Spain to colonial New Mexico. Pasulka's atheist colleague linked it to quantum superposition. Chapters: 7, 8. Related: charism, superposition, levitation.
- **Biotechnical imaginaries / programmable matter** — Gaymon Bennett's account of Silicon Valley synthetic biologists' worldview that life "can and should be treated as 'programmable matter'"; their success made it normative, though they wrongly took information to be immaterial. Chapters: 6. Related: materiality of information.
- **Book encounter / media encounter** — Pasulka's coinage for the seemingly serendipitous book (or media) that supplies an explanatory framework for one's anomalous experiences, attaching them to a cultural narrative (Tyler's Sagan, James's Mack, Scott's Blum, Betty Hill's Keyhoe, Teresa's angelology, Rey's NDE binge). Chapters: 4, 5, 6, 7. Related: library angel, spiritual conversion.
- **Bracketing (religious-studies method)** — Setting aside metaphysical / objective truth to study incontestably real social effects; the strategy Vallee advocated to the UN in 1979. Chapters: 1. Related: real and imaginary.
- **Brain receiver / human interface** — James's theorized biological hardware for receiving anomalous information: since genes define tissue architecture, they would underlie a "brain receiver," and identifying the molecules begins "the long road towards identifying the human interface." Chapters: 3. Related: human receiver, heritable sixth sense.
- **Building-block approach** — Ann Taves's framework for how events become religious events, using cognitive science, sociology, and history to explain how people identify experiences as religious (or UFO-related). Chapters: 3. Related: mechanisms of belief.
- **Calling down (UFOs)** — The practice, learned from UFO literature, of mentally summoning craft; Rey tried it and, on giving up, saw an enormous object. Chapters: 7.
- **Canonization** — The Church process (advanced by a postulator) declaring a person a saint; the differing outcomes for Joseph of Cupertino (canonized) and Maria of Agreda (not) frame Pasulka's Rome research. Chapters: 8. Related: postulator.
- **Charism** — The Church's term for a rare sacred skill, of which bilocation is an example. Chapters: 8. Related: bilocation.
- **Code of silence / academic code of transparency** — The two opposed research ethics: scientist-ufologists in official capacities are vetted, take oaths, and may not discuss the very thing they study (partly for national security), while academics are bound to reveal their sources. Chapters: 2. Related: the invisibles, message management.
- **Collapse of the real/virtual dualism** — Pasulka's conclusion that in a digital landscape the modernist real/unreal distinction ceases to be meaningful; "unreal" media have real physiological effects, so she "gave up the dualism of real and virtual." Chapters: 4. Related: virtual UFO, media ecology, real and imaginary.
- **Computer conferencing** — Vallee's 1970s ARPANET-era medium; its timestamping confirmed simultaneous impressions among far-separated individuals, and he predicted network communication could create states conducive to psychic functioning. Chapters: 6. Related: remote viewing.
- **Confabulation** — "The inability to distinguish fact from fantasy"; the Total Recall Effect goes beyond it, though it could be a technologically nurtured form of it. Chapters: 5. Related: Total Recall Effect.
- **Consciousness Paradigm** — Rey Hernandez's proposed replacement for the ufology paradigm (which "revealed very little... over the last sixty years"): FREE's program to understand consciousness and contact via the contact modalities. Chapters: 7. Related: contact modalities, quantum hologram theory.
- **Consumerism at sacred sites** — Pasulka's observation that "where hierophanies appear, consumerism often follows": Roswell's museum, themed food, and festival parallel Lourdes's memorabilia economy. Chapters: 2, 3. Related: hierophany.
- **Contact event** — The perceived interface between the human and the intelligent nonhuman being from the sky; present within most religions, it becomes a religious or UFO event only through an interpretive process. Chapters: 1, 10. Related: hierophany, belief communities.
- **Contact modalities** — Rey Hernandez's (and Mitchell's) term, received in Rey's "spinning wheel" download, for the varied anomalous experiences — UFO contact, NDEs, out-of-body experiences — each a way nonhuman intelligence interacts with humans. Chapters: 7. Related: quantum hologram theory, Consciousness Paradigm.
- **Contagion** — James's observation that the phenomenon can act like a contagious agent, spreading from an affected individual to others in contact with them. Chapters: 3. Related: bedeviled.
- **Control mechanism / schedule of reinforcement** — Vallee's speculative conclusion that apparitions and UFOs are manifestations of a single control mechanism working like a behavioral schedule of reinforcement (Pavlov's dogs); graphs of UFO flaps appear to suggest it. Chapters: 6. Related: flap, morphology of miracles.
- **Credo quia absurdum** — "I believe because it is absurd"; Vallee's epigraph to the Conclusion ("Credo quia absurdum, eh, Diana?"), which Pasulka finds "written into the fabric of the artifact." Chapters: 9. Related: the absurd.
- **Cultural authorities** — Authority figures (scholars, theologians, NASA spokespersons, CEOs) whose media commentary lends a production credibility; Pasulka realized she was one for *The Conjuring*. Chapters: 5. Related: mechanisms of belief.
- **Cultural revitalization movements** — Anthropologists' finding that the supernatural figures in thousands of such movements, with paranormal interest surging when cultures undergo profound change. Chapters: 6. Related: trickster.
- **Cyborg epistemologies** — Theories of knowledge, associated with Donna Haraway, of how human–technological engagement effaces binaries between humans, animals, and technologies. Chapters: 6. Related: extended cognition, technogenesis.
- **Debunker** — In ufology, one who disbelieves and actively discredits witnesses; nearly the worst label in the field. Pasulka inverts it for Scott Browne. Chapters: 4. Related: true believer.
- **Devotional vs. doctrinal Catholicism / private vs. public revelation** — The Catholic split between devotional practice (Mary, the Rosary) and doctrine, formalized as private revelation (apparitions; belief optional) vs. public revelation (scripture; belief obligatory); verification collapses onto the witness's trustworthiness, as in UFO reports. Chapters: 6. Related: two datasets.
- **Disclosure / "Disclosure from the Bottom Up" / unofficial disclosure** — Ufologists' term that UFOs have revealed themselves, or that there is public awareness of them; FREE's motto centers study on the experiencer, and the December 2017 *NYT*/Elizondo story set off a "disclosure" firestorm. Chapters: 7, 9. Related: mechanisms of belief, realism.
- **Disinformation / hoaxes** — Bogus information, some deliberate disinformation and some created "for the sole reason that it sells," inspired by rumors of the scientists' findings and forming the basis of UFO media and mythologies. Chapters: 1. Related: message management, virtual UFO.
- **DNA as receptor/transmitter; human as satellite / biological internet** — Tyler's belief that DNA receives and transmits at the frequency used for deep-space satellites (~150 MHz), stores biophotonic data, follows language-like syntax, and with bone-calcium antennae and entangled photons makes the body "a biological internet"; "Humans are a type of satellite." Chapters: 2, 6. Related: human receiver, Tyler's protocol.
- **The download / download experience / extraterrestrial telegram** — The UFO-community name for connection with off-planet entities delivering ideas "like a hit"; Grant Cameron ties creativity to tapping "nonlocal intelligence," and Rey received a rush-hour telepathic mandate "not via a voice, but via information." Chapters: 2, 7. Related: "memories," anomalous cognition.
- **Dyadic model of consciousness** — Mitchell's model for remote viewing, telepathy, and mysticism: mystical insights are "just information that requires interpretation"; in samadhi the body/brain resonates with the zero point field, ecstasy signaling the experience should be repeated. Chapters: 7. Related: zero point field, quantum hologram theory, savikalpa / nirvikalpa samadhi.
- **Eight plus one rule** — Tyler's sleep rule: eight hours, wake, then force one "top-off" hour that "makes or breaks" the day. Chapters: 2. Related: Tyler's protocol.
- **Electrogravitic machine** — Tyler's term for the propulsion of some off-planet craft, using "a highly charged capacitor of sorts." Chapters: 2.
- **Elves** — James's laughing name for the subconscious processes he assigns a problem to before sleep, waking with the answer — while wondering whether the information "comes from somewhere else." Chapters: 3. Related: the download.
- **Epistemological shock** — John Mack's term, adopted by Pasulka: a shock to one's fundamental understanding of the world; hers came from learning elite scientists believe, and that rumors of their findings fuel hoaxes. Chapters: 1.
- **Ethereal beings** — Tsiolkovsky's term (quoted by Pasulka) for higher beings beyond our dimensions who read our thoughts and send messages through celestial symbols, which a genius channels into technology. Chapters: 2. Related: psychic cosmonauts.
- **Experiencers / contactees / UFO-abductees** — People who have sighted UFOs or believe they are in contact with the phenomenon, interpreting it as spiritual or religious; the group Pasulka set out to study. Chapters: 1, 2, 3, 10. Related: meta-experiencer.
- **Extended cognition** — Andy Clark's finding that cognition is not brain-bound but occurs in a network extending into the body and environment; what is new, Pasulka adds, is technologies' self-programming autonomy. Chapters: 5, 6. Related: technogenesis, the new unconscious.
- **External world skepticism** — The idea, at least since Plato, that we cannot assume what we perceive with our senses is real. Chapters: 5.
- **Extra-factual memory** — Implanted virtual memories, the product of the evil company in Philip K. Dick's 1966 story behind *Total Recall*. Chapters: 5. Related: Total Recall Effect, prosthetic memory.
- **Fabricated UFO** — The object of the new era Pasulka announces (with Scott Browne): UFO belief built on faked videos and belief-generating mechanisms, yet "the object of near-universal belief." Chapters: 5. Related: virtual UFO, future-real.
- **Fakelore / fake religion** — David Chidester's category: religion that, "although invented, mobilized, and deployed by frauds, can produce real effects in the real world." Chapters: 5. Related: invented religions, Jediism.
- **Fiction-based religions** — Markus Davidsen's proposed category for religions drawing inspiration from fictional narratives that create a world of their own; Pasulka's reading of Jediism differs, placing it in a milieu positing real ETs. Chapters: 5. Related: Jediism, invented religions.
- **Field investigators** — MUFON-trained volunteers who study reports on-site; the exemplary North Carolina director rules out all explanations before granting "UFO"/"unidentified" status. Chapters: 7. Related: big data approach.
- **First contact (imagined)** — Maria's and Tyler's shared role as "inadvertent colonists" whose imagined contact precedes and enables real colonization — Maria's for Spanish missionaries, Tyler's for the media-fueled colonization of space. Chapters: 8. Related: meta-experiencer.
- **Flap / UFO flap** — A series of UFO sightings by several people over a few days; graphing flaps suggested the reinforcement schedule to Vallee. Chapters: 3, 6. Related: control mechanism.
- **Future-real** — Pasulka's gloss on "I want to believe" / "The truth is out there," and on UFO belief anchored not in the past but in what is potentially real in the future: "a postponed truth is not false but future-real." Chapters: 4, 5. Related: fabricated UFO, realism.
- **Ground zero of the new religion** — Pasulka's phrase at the X-Files mesa: because media had made the site "true for millions," her belief in its objective truth "didn't matter." Chapters: 2. Related: hierophany, mechanisms of belief.
- **Heritable sixth sense** — James's claim that the ability to interface with the phenomenon is heritable like other brain-wiring traits — "a 6th sense... associated with a material component we already possess" — so experiencers run in families. Chapters: 3. Related: brain receiver.
- **Hierophany / sacred place / sacred site** — In Pasulka's field, a manifestation of the sacred, occurring when a nonhuman intelligent being descends or reveals itself (Moses's burning bush; Roswell; Fatima); such sites draw pilgrimage and a consumer economy. Chapters: 2, 3, 6, 10. Related: contact event, consumerism at sacred sites.
- **High strangeness** — A UFO-studies term (via Susan Demeter-St. Clair) for encounters marked by profound, anomalous effects, such as a whole family hearing an external voice. Chapters: 7.
- **Human receiver / humans as receivers** — The book's title thesis that the phenomenon functions like a technology and the human is a receiver and transmitter of information; per Mitchell and Tyler, humans with DNA and cellular receptors work "like mini satellites" and are the best contact sensors. Chapters: 6, 7. Related: brain receiver, DNA as receptor.
- **Hypnotic regression / false memories** — The practice of recovering purportedly suppressed abduction memories under hypnosis (established by the Hill case, central to Mack and Hopkins); Scott refused it because it can create false memories, and Pasulka says the tradition needs reassessing given media's influence on memory. Chapters: 4, 5. Related: reconstructive memory, pseudo-memories.
- **Imagination exteriorized** — Pasulka's image for the present condition: "our imaginations have become exterior to ourselves, existing out there in our media, and our media then determines what is in our heads." Chapters: 5. Related: media ecology.
- **Immersive virtual reality** — Per Pasulka the real game-changer: the Stanford VHIL finding that "the brain often fails to differentiate between virtual experiences and real ones." Chapters: 5. Related: mechanisms of belief.
- **Incorruptible saints** — The tradition that certain bodies do not decay after death and are pronounced "incorruptible," often displayed in glass (e.g., Pope John XXIII). Chapters: 8. Related: relic.
- **Information operations** — The military employment of media and electronic media for national-security purposes, which Pasulka argues has helped create global UFO belief. Chapters: 3. Related: perception management, message management.
- **Instrumentality / "The Question Concerning Technology"** — Heidegger's essay (Pasulka's theoretical key for Vallee): humans misread technology's essence as mere instrument, blinded to it. Chapters: 0. Related: saving power, technology as the new God-era.
- **Internet refugees** — Pasulka's term for people (like Alison Kruse) who flee to In the Field from the harassment of the online ufology world. Chapters: 4.
- **Invented religions** — Carole Cusack's category of religions that openly advertise their invention; studying them shows how science fiction and new technologies inform belief. Chapters: 5. Related: fakelore, fiction-based religions.
- **Invisible College / parallel research tradition** — Hynek's term (written of by Vallee) for a hidden group of scientists and academics studying the phenomenon anonymously though their results impact society; harks back to the Robert Boyle circle, precursor to the Royal Society. Chapters: 1, 6. Related: the invisibles, scientist-believers.
- **The invisibles / invisibility** — People who study the phenomenon from inside, whose names are "washed from the internet on a regular basis," "removed from history as if they never existed"; at the Vatican, Tyler's inverse — visibility — grants access. Chapters: 2, 7, 8. Related: code of silence, Invisible College.
- **Jediism / the Force** — A religious movement treating *Star Wars* as sacred scripture and the Force as a transcendent element; because Lucas drew on Daoism and Zen, adherents hold it points "like a finger pointing to the moon" to eternal truths. Chapters: 5. Related: fiction-based religions, UFO religions.
- **Koan** — A short nonsensible Zen anecdote posed to fatigue the rational mind toward enlightenment; Vallee wonders whether the UFO's absurdity works as a "mass koan" on millions. Chapters: 6. Related: the absurd.
- **Last rites / anointing of the sick** — The last rites absolve sins to prepare the dying; the anointing of the sick is a sacrament of blessing with holy oil — central to the hospital rounds that broke Tyler open. Chapters: 8. Related: spiritual conversion.
- **Levitation** — A phenomenon copiously documented in Joseph of Cupertino's canonization records and a common UFO-contact theme (people levitated from beds into craft); members of aeronautic agencies contacted Pasulka about levitating saints. Chapters: 8. Related: bilocation.
- **Library angel** — Arthur Koestler's term for the serendipitous arrival of exactly the book one needs; differs from the book encounter, which explains the reader's own experiences. Chapters: 4. Related: book encounter.
- **Living myth / universal mass rumor** — Jung's characterization of the flying saucer as a living myth and mass rumor "reserved for our enlightened, rationalist age"; Pasulka adds its potential physical reality keeps the myth alive ("Its realism... makes it religious"). Chapters: 4. Related: amplificatory interpretation.
- **Logic of camouflage** — Vallee's and Pasulka's idea that the phenomenon's subterranean, absurd character is part of its logic: rejected by elites while its symbols are absorbed unconsciously, as early Christianity spread before erupting into a state religion — "It is sneaky, and time is on its side." Chapters: 6. Related: the absurd, koan.
- **Materiality of information** — The chapter's title claim (against the disembodied soul and immaterial-information assumption) that information is physical — Nicole Yunger Halpern's "I like my quantum information physical" and Tyler's insistence the body/matter are indispensable. Chapters: 6. Related: quantum field of information, biotechnical imaginaries.
- **Mechanisms of belief** — Pasulka's umbrella term for the media techniques that manufacture belief: "based on true events" framing, realist montage, cultural authorities, splicing digital characters into historical photos, and depictions of ordinary life. Chapters: 1, 5, 7, 9. Related: realism, Total Recall Effect.
- **Media ecology / digital infrastructure** — The framework in which Pasulka situates the phenomenon: digital infrastructure provides the environment ripe for pervasive UFO belief and erases the real/virtual boundary. Chapters: 4. Related: collapse of the real/virtual dualism, imagination exteriorized.
- **"Memories" (Tyler's)** — Tyler's term for his technology ideas, which arrive fully formed as memories of things he never learned and which he decided to "use for good." Chapters: 2. Related: the download.
- **Men in black** — The two grim visitors to James's office after the artifact publicity, who turned out fully human — a university researcher with intelligence ties and an aerospace-firm scientist — serious non-public researchers of the phenomenon. Chapters: 3.
- **Merkabah** — The Jewish mystical tradition arising from the visionary aspects of Ezekiel's wheel; scholar David Halperin uses it in analyzing Barney Hill's account. Chapters: 5. Related: biblical–UFO framework, wraparound eyes.
- **Message management** — Pasulka's observation that the UFO belief system, like state-era Christianity, has had its message managed by powerful interests; disinformation remains a live possibility. Chapters: 2. Related: perception management, disinformation.
- **Meta-experiencer / scientist-believer** — Pasulka's coinage for scientists who don't claim their own sightings but scoop data from primary experiencers for their work (Tyler); extended in Rome to Sister Maria of Agreda as a historical meta-experiencer. Chapters: 1, 2, 8, 10. Related: scientist-believers, experiencers.
- **Miracle of the Sun** — The culminating Fatima event of October 13, 1917: a starable, sharp-edged solar disc spinning "in a mad whirl," the sky turning amethyst, the sun seeming to plunge earthward, witnessed by tens of thousands. Chapters: 6. Related: morphology of miracles.
- **"Mind control" (during encounters)** — The irrational passivity experiencers recognize only afterward: full cognizance of everything except the event's significance (no photos, no alerting others). Chapters: 7. Related: unphotogenic UFOs.
- **Missing time** — The idea Rey adopted to reconcile his and Dulce's conflicting timelines: that the beings had taken, healed, and returned his wife and dog. Chapters: 7.
- **Modern Prometheus myth** — The notion (fueled by Corso's *The Day After Roswell*) that nonhuman intelligences gave humans advanced technology — feeding both wonder and a government-cover-up conspiracy. Chapters: 2. Related: weapon of information.
- **Monolith as donation / monolith as screen / human–monolith continuum** — James reads Kubrick's *2001* monolith as a "donation" like the desert artifact; Rob Ager reads it as the cinema screen; Pasulka argues it is both, and that "there is a human–monolith continuum" (the monolith resembling her iPhone). Chapters: 4, 5. Related: the artifact / donation, mechanisms of belief.
- **Morphology of Miracles** — Vallee's table placing Marian-apparition elements beside UFO events: humming, a shining disc/globe, post-sighting enervation, thunder/booms, unusual clouds. Chapters: 6. Related: Miracle of the Sun, control mechanism.
- **The new unconscious** — Hayles's finding that the unconscious plays a far larger role than thought in setting goals and priorities, responding to the environment while inaccessible to consciousness. Chapters: 6. Related: extended cognition.
- **"Nuts and bolts" school / methodology / materialists vs. subjectivists** — The materialist school of ufology focused on physical evidence (radiation, radar, sightings), opposed to the subjective/psychic school focused on experiencer testimony; one of Pasulka's scientists applies "nuts and bolts" analysis to artifacts. Chapters: 1, 6, 7. Related: two datasets, Consciousness Paradigm.
- **Orthodox / unorthodox history** — Pasulka's framing of Vallee's double biography: the public record (astronomer, ARPANET engineer, VC, Spielberg consultant) versus the hidden record of work adjacent to SRI's classified research. Chapters: 0.
- **Percipients** — Vallee's term for experiencers; his method attends to their first-order reports while ignoring second-order interpretations (e.g., "the Virgin Mary," "Zeta Reticuli"). Chapters: 6. Related: bracketing.
- **Perception management** — Government programs to cover up and manage UFO information, exhaustively documented in declassified records; motivated by security, fear of alarm, or ignorance. Chapters: 3. Related: training and debunking, message management.
- **The phenomenon** — The book's preferred term for the UFO/contact reality, more accurate than "UFO" because it does not presuppose extraterrestrial origin; scientists study it but never conclude what it is. Chapters: 1, 2, 3, 10. Related: experiencers.
- **The phenomenon tricks and deceives** — Stinnett's warning (with Vallee and George Hansen) that real encounters and meaningful synchronicities dupe researchers into thinking they know what the phenomenon is — "Well, it's not." Chapters: 4. Related: synchronicity, trickster.
- **Plasma-being / light being** — Rey's coinage for what he saw: a being of pure, multicolored, translucent energy plasma, cylindrical without outer edges, "because it did control my mind." Chapters: 7. Related: "mind control."
- **Postbiological intelligence** — Susan Schneider's claim that any advanced nonhuman intelligence humans encounter would be in postbiological (artificial-intelligence) form. Chapters: 5. Related: technogenesis.
- **Postulator** — A Vatican functionary who presents the case for canonization or beatification; Pasulka reached key postulators only via Tyler. Chapters: 8. Related: canonization.
- **Precognition / extrasensory perception (ESP)** — Esoteric skills studied at SRI under the Stargate Project in hopes of harnessing naturally psychic people for intelligence. Chapters: 0. Related: The Stargate Project, remote viewing.
- **Psychic component** — The direct knowing of what the beings communicate "as if there are no barriers," reported by the Fatima children and Teresa of Avila alike; via media it "can now be experienced by millions." Chapters: 6. Related: morphology of miracles.
- **Psychic cosmonauts / esoteric cosmonauts** — Pasulka's terms for people who report traveling psychically through space, or who believe nonhuman beings help humans reach space (Swedenborg; the Stargate viewers; Tsiolkovsky, Parsons, Tyler, Mitchell) — "on the fringe... [but] may be on the fringe of our future." Chapters: 0, 7, 8. Related: The Stargate Project, ethereal beings.
- **"Punks"** — Alison Kruse's pet name for the starlike objects she films, which she speculates open "like a zipper" to release smaller probes. Chapters: 4.
- **Purgatory / mind–body dualism** — The thirteenth-century Catholic doctrine of a purifying state, whose scholastic debate over whether immaterial souls could leave material traces is the medieval version of Vallee's physical/spiritual conundrum. Chapters: 6. Related: materiality of information.
- **Qi (Chi)** — The information-imparting life force Qian Xuesen believed in, with a long history in Asian religious traditions as a sacred force tappable with training. Chapters: 3. Related: ethereal beings.
- **Quantum field of information** — James's speculative source for anomalous cognition, drawing on entanglement ("spooky action at a distance"): a field his subjects tap, transferring into neuronal hardware as a "recognition." Chapters: 3. Related: quantum hologram theory, materiality of information.
- **Quantum hologram theory of physics and consciousness (QHTC)** — Edgar Mitchell's theory that information consists of patterns of energy given off by matter; per Rudy Schild it explains all the contact modalities and retires the subjective-vs-materialist divide. Chapters: 7. Related: dyadic model, contact modalities.
- **Quantum non-locality (as basis for psi)** — Mitchell's claim that non-locality provides a physical basis for psi: synchronized brain waves transfer information across Faraday barriers, not obeying the inverse-square rule — "a macro-scale version of quantum non-locality." Chapters: 7. Related: superposition.
- **Real and imaginary (simultaneously imagined and real)** — The book's central methodological claim: Pasulka makes no ontological claim that extraterrestrials are "real in the sense that couches are real, although they could be," but argues perceived contact "has very real effects with powerful social implications." Chapters: 1, 8. Related: bracketing, collapse of the real/virtual dualism.
