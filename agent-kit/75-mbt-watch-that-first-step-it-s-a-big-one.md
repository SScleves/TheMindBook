# Ch 4 — Watch That First Step, It's a Big One

## In one paragraph
Campbell narrates his first Transcendental Meditation (TM) training class and the two months that followed, during which meditation stopped being a curiosity and became a demonstrably useful tool. In his very first in-class practice session he drops immediately into a "point of conscious awareness" in a void, loses twenty minutes of subjective time, and nearly falls out of his chair — an experience no one else in the class shares. Practicing twice daily, he learns to combine the blank state with controlled visuals and discovers he can solve physics problems and debug code roughly ten times faster in that state than in normal consciousness. This forces him to revise his strictly operational philosophy of reality: alongside the measurable, he must now admit non-measurable subjective states that consistently produce objective, measurable results. The chapter's "big step" is this first expansion of Campbell's reality picture, and he closes by noting that further expansion would eventually require personal growth — an increase in the quality of his consciousness.

## Core claims
- Campbell reports that TM as taught to him was purely process-oriented — "just technique," no theory, ceremony, or metaphysics — and that the guru and his organization were irrelevant to what he could do with the technique; the mantra's secrecy he interpreted as protection of intellectual property, not hidden knowledge.
- Campbell claims that in his first practice session he became "nothing but a single point of conscious awareness existing in a void of nothingness" — no thoughts, no body, no room — and that twenty minutes elapsed which he experienced as only a few.
- Campbell claims his experience was unusual: no one else in the class had anything like it ("Two for two — this meditation could be strange stuff").
- Campbell claims the blank state and controlled visuals can be combined, though they may seem incompatible; he learned this by trial and error.
- Campbell claims meditation had direct, objective, practical value: in the point-awareness state he could solve physics problems, design experiments, analyze research data, and write and debug computer code "ten times more quickly, and with better results" than in a normal state — and that this became a dependable routine, not a one-off, which he tested repeatedly and eventually used first rather than as a last resort.
- Campbell claims the change was externally noticed: other graduate students commented on his improved abilities and sought his help — yet none would try TM themselves, which he attributes to meditation lying too far outside their personal and cultural belief systems.
- Campbell claims that two months in, meditating twice daily and always careful to be positioned so he would not fall, the point-awareness experience remained pleasant and became invigorating — it seemed to boost his energy — though he usually cut it short because he was extremely busy.
- Campbell states his prior philosophy: meaningful existence was confined to an operational reality — if something can be measured (interacts with our senses or devices) it is real; if not measurable, its existence is irrelevant; the inferrable-but-unmeasurable falls into a gray area of conjecture not to be confused with real things.
- Campbell argues he was forced to expand that philosophy: there exist things that are non-measurable yet functionally operational — subjective experiences with objective results — and an altered consciousness directed by intent can consistently and directly affect real things (his computer code), acting as a kind of "consciousness operator" (using "operator" in the mathematical/scientific sense).
- Campbell argues that, unlike conceptual constructs such as justice and love, altered states and their objective results are measurable, consistent, and reasonably well defined — amenable to research and experiment, with similar activity producing similar results for all experimenters. He insists he was not special: anyone in the state can do these things.
- Campbell's expanded reality criterion: "If a thing is well defined and consistently functional... then it must also be real," on the reasoning that only real things can be functional operators within and on an operational reality — something not real could not directly affect real things.
- Campbell requires objectivity for real things: they must exist universally and consistently for others, independent of him, or they would be merely private hallucinations rather than part of the shared larger reality.
- Campbell claims his relevancy criteria for altered states are: consistent functionality, actual help to performance in objective reality, and measurable effects; altered states with no universal, consistent functionality are by definition useless and irrelevant.
- Campbell defines "altered" as different from normal, and "normal" as wide-awake and focused in the physical world.
- Campbell claims this philosophical approach has not significantly changed in the thirty-three years since: any credible conception of reality must include subjective experience that can consistently and universally lead to useful objective (measurable-by-anyone) functionality.
- Campbell claims that expanding reality beyond a certain beginning level required personal growth — increasing the quality of his consciousness — and that understanding the bigger picture and growing up worked together reciprocally.

## Concepts
- **tm / transcendental-meditation** — The "Indian meditation thing" Campbell paid twenty dollars to learn: a purely process-oriented technique of maintaining a mantra in the mind, taught by an instructor with the sole condition that the mantra not be given away or sold. Campbell treats the technique as separable from the guru and organization that sold it.
- **mantra** — The sound Campbell was taught to think/maintain in his mind as the TM technique; his first session went "blank" when he stopped saying it, and realizing this snapped him back to ordinary awareness.
- **point-consciousness / point-awareness** — Campbell's term for the state he entered: existing as "nothing but a single point of conscious awareness existing in a void of nothingness — floating free, doing nothing — existing as conscious mind without extent or form," with no thoughts, body, or surroundings. Always pleasant, invigorating, and — with visuals added — a productive place to work.
- **blank-state** — The thoughtless void/float state of point consciousness; Campbell learned through trial and error to combine it with controlled visuals, noting the two "may seem incompatible, but they are not."
- **controlled-visuals** — Deliberate visual imagery added to the blank state, turning meditation into a workspace for solving physics and programming problems.
- **altered-state-of-consciousness** — Campbell's proper definition of his meditation state; "altered" is defined as different from normal. Each altered state has its own functionality; only those with universal, consistent functionality matter.
- **normal-state** — "Normal" means "wide-awake and focused in the physical world — as you are now while you are reading this book."
- **operational-reality** — Campbell's pre-meditation philosophy of reality: if something can be measured (interacts with our senses or with a device that does), it is real; if not measurable, its reality is irrelevant. In an operational reality, non-real things by definition have no measurable effect on and no relevance to real things.
- **gray-area-of-conjecture** — Where the non-measurable-but-inferrable lives: theoretical and hypothetical things (at that time, quantum mechanical wave packets, black holes, quarks, justice, and love). Acceptable as conceptual constructs but not to be confused with real things; the academic scientist's primary mission is to gather enough valid, repeatable measurement data to transfer a gray construct into a real object or effect.
- **subjective-experience-with-objective-results** — The new category that forced Campbell's expansion: non-measurable yet functionally operational states of mind (like his meditation state) that can be used to operate on real things — the crux of his revised reality definition.
- **consciousness-operator** — Campbell's coinage (using "operate"/"operator" in the mathematical or scientific sense) for the mechanism by which a non-measurable subjective experience becomes "a reliable and effective scientific tool" — altered consciousness directed by intent consistently affecting real things.
- **daydreaming / directed-imagination** — Self-directed imagination or purposeful visual imagery; one specific type of altered state. Campbell rates it consistently functional (e.g., mentally rehearsing a speech), measurable in its effects, and helpful to objective performance — "a definite 'yes' on all counts."
- **objective-measurability / functionality** — Campbell's expanded criterion for the real: real, significant things must be either objectively measurable or consistently and predictably interactive with real things; "objective" means existing universally and consistently for others, independent of the observer — otherwise it is only private hallucination.
- **reality-expansion** — The chapter's process: Campbell's realization that "the scope of the reality picture was much bigger than I had previously thought," that he had been "blindly unaware of a significant part of my reality for twenty-some years," and his resolve to push against the boundaries.
- **quality-of-consciousness / personal-growth** — Foreshadowed at the end: expanding reality beyond a beginning level required increasing the quality of his consciousness; personal growth and understanding the bigger picture "worked together."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The first TM training class** — About fifteen casually dressed students on gray metal folding chairs; a clean-cut instructor teaches the mantra technique with "no theory... no metaphysical mumbo jumbo — just technique." The only condition: don't give away or sell the mantra, which Campbell reframes as copyright/patent protection ("protecting their source of income and fresh fruit"), not secrecy hiding something from critical review. Shows Campbell's insistence on separating usable technique from ideology and his instinctive distrust of the secret.
- **The first in-class meditation (near-fall and time loss)** — Within minutes of starting the mantra, Campbell becomes a single point of awareness in a void; the critical thought "I am supposed to be thinking the sound of my mantra!" ends the state, external awareness rushes back, and he finds his body about to fall out of the chair — his internal alarms go off ("Ooga! Ooga! Emergency! Emergency!") and his right leg shoots forward to save him. Regaining his balance, he reflects he nearly hit the floor "like a sack of potatoes," then more practically concludes meditation could be dangerous and resolves to be more careful in the future. When the instructor calls time, Campbell discovers twenty minutes have passed that he experienced as a few. His serious question — "How do you keep from falling out of your chair?" — draws laughter twice and a surprised instructor; Campbell realizes no one else had an experience like his. This is his second strange result ("Two for two"), and it tickles his intellect: he wants to experiment, to know "if anything real was involved."
- **The seat-belt-and-helmet mental cartoon** — Amused at needing to sit angled against the wall to avoid falling, Campbell instantly "sees" a vivid mental picture of himself in a folding chair in an empty room wearing seatbelt, motorcycle helmet, goggles, and a scarf flapping in front of a big fan — which reminds him of Ace-Snoopy atop his doghouse battling the Red Baron in his Sopwith Camel. He notes he "often saw pictures like that" — some funny, some helpful, lasting only seconds. An early datum on his spontaneous visual imagery.
- **The software-debugging result (the chapter's key empirical case)** — After a full week of unsuccessfully hunting a bug in his analysis software, Campbell goes home and solves it — "not just imagining that you have solved it mind you, but actually solved it — in just ten minutes of meditation time." He generalizes: physics problems, experiment design, data analysis, and coding all went ~10x faster with better results in the point-awareness state. Crucially, he stresses it became a dependable routine, tested repeatedly, eventually used first instead of after normal methods failed. For Campbell this shows a non-measurable subjective state producing consistent, objective, real-world effects — the datum that breaks his old operational-reality philosophy.
- **The graduate-student peer check** — Fellow physics graduate students notice the change in his abilities and come to him for research discussion and debugging help ("a dozen others had clearly noticed it"), yet none will try TM when he tells them about it — meditation required "too big a step out of the comfort zones of their personal and cultural belief systems." Campbell uses this both as third-party corroboration of the effect and as evidence of belief-system closure even among physicists.
- **The gray-basket inventory** — Campbell's illustrative list of things then in the gray area of conjecture: quantum mechanical wave packets, black holes, quarks, justice, and love. Black holes are his worked example of how sufficient good-quality data could move a thing from hypothetical to real — a methodology he says he still employs.
- **The speech-rehearsal / athlete examples for daydreaming** — Repetitively practicing a speech in imagination (making points, fielding imagined questions) as a consistently functional, measurable use of directed imagination; and the appeal "ask any top-notch athlete if focused intentional mental preparation is important to his or her success." Both show, per Campbell, that mental effort within a particular altered state is a real thing because it produces real, universal, state-specific effects.
- **The revised reality model (the chapter's constructed model)** — Moving parts: (1) the old operational criterion (measurable ⇒ real); (2) the gray area (inferrable/hypothetical); (3) the new added criterion ("if a thing is well defined and consistently functional... it must also be real"); (4) the supporting argument that only real things can be functional operators on an operational reality; (5) the objectivity requirement (universal, observer-independent, same results for all experimenters — else private hallucination); (6) the relevancy filter for altered states (consistent functionality, actual performance help, measurable effects).

## Thread
Within Section 1 ("Delusion or Knowledge"), this chapter is the autobiographical hinge where Campbell's story moves from curiosity to evidence. It follows directly from his TM initiation events (the "sacrificial bananas" ceremony referenced as already past) recounted in the preceding chapters, and delivers the "first step" of the title: his first altered-state experience and, more importantly, his first forced revision of what counts as real. The chapter establishes the epistemological standard he will invoke throughout the trilogy — subjective experience validated by consistent, universal, objective results — and stakes his credibility on repeatable, practically useful effects rather than belief. Its closing lines (bigger picture requires higher quality of consciousness; growth and understanding work together) seed two of the trilogy's central themes: exploration of further altered states and the growth/entropy-reduction purpose of existence developed in later books. It also plants the theme of belief-system blindness (the physicists who won't try TM) that Campbell develops when contrasting open-minded skepticism with cultural belief.
