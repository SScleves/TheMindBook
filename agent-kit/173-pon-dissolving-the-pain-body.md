# Dissolving the Pain-Body

## In one paragraph
In this unit Eckhart Tolle introduces and teaches the dissolution of the "pain-body" — an accumulated, negative energy field of old emotional pain that lives in one's body and mind, almost like an invisible entity in its own right. He argues that most human pain is unnecessary and self-created through the unobserved mind's resistance to the present moment, and that pain exists on two levels: the pain created now and the residue of past pain lodged in the body. Tolle describes how the pain-body has dormant and active modes, "feeds" on further pain by getting its host to unconsciously identify with it, and can range from a whining nuisance to a destructive "demon" that drives illness or even suicide. The remedy he offers is presence: directly observing the pain-body as the silent watcher, which breaks identification and, through sustained conscious attention, transmutes suffering into consciousness — what he calls the esoteric meaning of alchemy. The chapter closes with warnings about ego-identification with pain (the "unhappy self" and victim identity) and the reminder that the pain-body, like darkness, cannot be fought — only watched and accepted.

## Core claims
- Tolle claims the greater part of human pain is unnecessary and self-created "as long as the unobserved mind runs your life"; pain created now is always some form of nonacceptance, some unconscious resistance to what is — judgment on the level of thought, negativity on the emotional level.
- He argues the intensity of pain depends on the degree of resistance to the present moment, which in turn depends on how strongly you are identified with your mind; the mind always seeks to deny and escape the Now. The more mind-identified you are, the more you suffer; the more you honor and accept the Now, the freer you are of pain and of the egoic mind.
- Tolle acknowledges some spiritual teachings say all pain is ultimately an illusion — and says this is true — but insists a mere belief doesn't make it true for you; the concern of the book is realizing this truth in your own experience.
- He claims pain is inevitable as long as you are mind-identified (i.e., spiritually unconscious), and that emotional pain is also the main cause of physical pain and physical disease. Resentment, hatred, self-pity, guilt, anger, depression, jealousy, even slight irritation are all forms of pain.
- Every pleasure or emotional high, he says, contains within itself the seed of pain — its inseparable opposite, which will manifest in time; negative and positive polarities are faces of the same coin, part of the underlying pain inseparable from the mind-identified egoic state.
- There are two levels to your pain: the pain you create now, and the pain from the past that still lives on in your mind and body. Every emotional pain experienced without access to the power of the Now leaves a residue that merges with past pain (including childhood pain caused by the unconsciousness of the world) and becomes the pain-body.
- The pain-body has two modes, dormant and active — dormant perhaps 90 percent of the time, but active up to 100 percent of the time in a deeply unhappy person. Anything can trigger it, particularly what resonates with a past pain pattern; even a thought or an innocent remark can activate it.
- Tolle claims the pain-body doesn't want to be observed directly; the moment you observe it, feel its energy field within you, and take your attention into it, the identification is broken — a higher dimension of consciousness, presence, has come in, and the pain-body can no longer pretend to be you or replenish itself through you.
- The pain-body wants to survive like every other entity and can only survive by getting you to unconsciously identify with it; it needs "food" — any experience resonating with its energy (anger, destructiveness, hatred, grief, emotional drama, violence, even illness). "Pain can only feed on pain. Pain cannot feed on joy."
- Once the pain-body takes you over, he argues, you want more pain — you become a victim or a perpetrator (with little real difference between the two) — and although you claim not to want pain, your thinking and behavior are designed to keep it going; made fully conscious, the pattern would dissolve, "for to want more pain is insanity, and nobody is consciously insane."
- The pain-body is "the dark shadow cast by the ego," afraid of the light of your consciousness and of being found out; its survival depends on your unconscious identification with it and on your unconscious fear of facing the pain that lives in you — and if you don't bring consciousness into the pain, you will be forced to relive it again and again. Yet it is "an insubstantial phantom" that cannot prevail against the power of your presence.
- After disidentification the pain-body keeps a momentum for a while, like a spinning wheel no longer being propelled, and may try to trick you back into identification or create passing physical aches; being unconscious means being identified with some mental or emotional pattern, a complete absence of the watcher.
- The moment your thinking is aligned with the energy field of the pain-body, you are identified with it and again feeding it with your thoughts — if anger is its predominant energy vibration and you think angry thoughts, dwelling on what someone did to you or what you are going to do to him or her, the pain-body has become "you." "Where there is anger, there is always pain underneath."
- Sustained conscious attention severs the link between the pain-body and your thought processes and brings about transmutation — pain becomes fuel for the flame of consciousness. This, Tolle says, is the esoteric meaning of alchemy: base metal into gold, suffering into consciousness; the split within is healed and your responsibility is then not to create further pain.
- The watching practice is profoundly powerful yet simple — it could be taught to a child, and Tolle hopes it will one day be among the first things children learn in school; understood by experiencing it, it is "the most potent transformational tool."
- If your sense of self is invested in the pain-body — a mind-made "unhappy self" — unconscious fear of losing your identity creates strong resistance to disidentification; as long as you make an identity out of pain, you cannot become free of it and will unconsciously resist or sabotage every attempt to heal. The only way to overcome this unconscious process is to make it conscious.
- Only you can do the work, but being with someone intensely conscious — joining them in the state of presence — helps and accelerates things; a spiritual teacher (and some therapists who have gone beyond the level of mind) can function like a fiercely burning fire that intensifies your own.
- The pain-body is trapped life energy, no longer flowing — "the living past in you"; identifying with it is identifying with the past. A victim identity is the belief that the past is more powerful than the present, which Tolle calls the opposite of the truth: the only power there is is contained within this moment, the power of your presence, and you alone are responsible for your inner space now.
- Unconsciousness creates the pain-body; consciousness transmutes it into itself. And just as you cannot fight the darkness, you cannot fight the pain-body — fighting creates inner conflict and further pain; watching it is enough, and watching implies accepting it as part of what is at that moment.

## Concepts
- **pain-body** — Tolle's term for the accumulated residue of past emotional pain that lives on in your mind and body: "a negative energy field that occupies your body and mind," best understood as "an invisible entity in its own right." It is trapped life energy, "the living past in you," the dark shadow cast by the ego.
- **dormant and active modes** — the pain-body's two modes of being. It may be dormant 90 percent of the time; in a deeply unhappy person it may be active up to 100 percent of the time. Anything resonating with a past pain pattern — even a thought or an innocent remark — can awaken it.
- **pain feeding on pain** — the pain-body's survival mechanism: it feeds on any experience that resonates with its own energy (anger, destructiveness, hatred, grief, emotional drama, violence, illness) and, having taken you over, creates situations in your life that reflect back its own energy frequency. "Pain can only feed on pain. Pain cannot feed on joy. It finds it quite indigestible."
- **presence** — "a higher dimension of consciousness" that comes in the moment you observe the pain-body; the power of your own conscious presence, the power of the Now. The pain-body cannot prevail against it.
- **the watcher / witness** — the observing consciousness that watches the pain-body directly without identifying: "the one who observes, the silent watcher." Becoming the watcher breaks the pain-body's pretense of being you.
- **unconsciousness** — as Tolle uses the word here: "to be identified with some mental or emotional pattern. It implies a complete absence of the watcher." Spiritually speaking, mind-identification is unconsciousness, and pain is inevitable in that state.
- **resistance to what is / nonacceptance** — the source of self-created pain: judgment on the level of thought, negativity on the emotional level; the mind's denial of and escape from the Now.
- **egoic mind / mind-identification** — the mind-identified state of consciousness from which the underlying pain is inseparable; the more identified with the mind, the more you suffer.
- **transmutation** — what sustained conscious attention accomplishes: it severs the link between the pain-body and your thought processes so that pain "becomes fuel for the flame of your consciousness, which then burns more brightly as a result." Unconsciousness creates the pain-body; consciousness transmutes it into itself.
- **alchemy (esoteric meaning)** — Tolle reads the ancient art of alchemy — transmuting base metal into gold — as an emblem of transmuting suffering into consciousness, whereby the split within is healed and you become whole again.
- **unhappy self** — the mind-made fiction produced when you make an identity for yourself out of your pain-body; fear of losing this familiar identity produces strong unconscious resistance to disidentification and sabotages attempts to heal.
- **victim identity** — "the belief that the past is more powerful than the present, which is the opposite of the truth" — the belief that other people and what they did to you are responsible for who you are now, for your pain or your inability to be your true self.
- **victim or perpetrator** — the two roles a pain-body-possessed person takes: wanting to suffer pain or to inflict it (or both); Tolle says there isn't really much difference between the two.
- **inner space** — the interior domain you alone are responsible for now; the pain-body is an energy field "temporarily lodged in your inner space," and the practitioner is to be "the ever-alert guardian of your inner space."
- **spiritual teacher (as fire)** — one function of a spiritual teacher (and of therapists who have gone beyond the level of mind): to be an intensely burning fire of presence whose proximity makes your own light quickly grow stronger.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Practice: observe the pain-body directly.** Instruction: the moment you observe the pain-body, feel its energy field within you, and take your attention into it. Intended effect: identification is broken; presence comes in; you become the witness, so the pain-body "cannot use you anymore by pretending to be you" and can no longer replenish itself through you — "You have found your own innermost strength."
- **Practice: watch for signs of the awakening pain-body.** Instruction: watch out for any sign of unhappiness in yourself, in whatever form — irritation, impatience, a somber mood, a desire to hurt, anger, rage, depression, a need for drama in your relationship — and "catch it the moment it awakens from its dormant state." Intended effect: catching activation early prevents unconscious identification.
- **Practice: stay present after disidentifying (guarding against momentum).** Instruction: stay present, stay conscious, "be the ever-alert guardian of your inner space," present enough to watch the pain-body directly and feel its energy. Intended effect: it then cannot control your thinking; you avoid being tricked back into identification while its residual momentum runs down (it may even create passing physical aches and pains, "but they won't last").
- **Practice/meditation: focus attention on the feeling inside you.** Instruction: know that it is the pain-body; accept that it is there; don't think about it — don't let the feeling turn into thinking; don't judge or analyze; don't make an identity for yourself out of it; stay present as the observer; become aware not only of the emotional pain but of "the one who observes," the silent watcher. "Then see what happens." Intended effect: this is the power of the Now applied directly; sustained conscious attention initiates transmutation of the pain.
- **Practice: observe the resistance to disidentification.** Instruction: observe the resistance within yourself, the attachment to your pain, "the peculiar pleasure you derive from being unhappy," and the compulsion to talk or think about it; be very alert. Intended effect: "The resistance will cease if you make it conscious," after which you can take attention into the pain-body and initiate its transmutation. Tolle adds that suddenly seeing that you are or have been attached to your pain can be a shocking realization — and the moment you realize it, you have broken the attachment.
- **Example: the drug high.** Anybody who has taken drugs to get "high" knows the high eventually turns into a low, pleasure into some form of pain — Tolle's illustration that every pleasure contains the seed of its inseparable opposite.
- **Example: intimate relationships.** Many people know from experience how easily and quickly an intimate relationship can turn from a source of pleasure into a source of pain — same point, and relationships are also named as a typical situation in which otherwise-dormant pain-bodies activate (along with situations linked to past loss, abandonment, or physical/emotional hurt).
- **Illustration: the spectrum of pain-bodies.** Some are "obnoxious but relatively harmless, for example, like a child who won't stop whining"; others are "vicious and destructive monsters, true demons" — physically or (more often) emotionally violent, attacking people around the host or the host themselves; Tolle says illnesses and accidents are often created this way and some pain-bodies drive their hosts to suicide. Meeting this "alien, nasty creature" in someone you thought you knew is a shock — but it is more important to observe it in yourself.
- **Example: angry thoughts and the dark mood.** Two illustrations of thinking becoming aligned with the pain-body: dwelling on what someone did to you or what you are going to do to him or her (when anger is the pain-body's predominant energy vibration), and a dark mood in which you get into a negative mind-pattern and think how dreadful your life is — in both, you have become unconscious and vulnerable to the pain-body's attack.
- **Analogy: the spinning wheel.** After disidentification the pain-body keeps operating for a while, "just like a spinning wheel that will keep turning for a while even when it is no longer being propelled" — its momentum without your energizing identification.
- **Analogy: alchemy.** The transmutation of base metal into gold as the esoteric image of transmuting suffering into consciousness.
- **Analogy: the two logs.** A log that has just started to burn, placed next to one burning fiercely and then separated, afterward burns with much greater intensity — "After all, it is the same fire." This is how being with an intensely conscious person (a spiritual teacher, or a therapist who has gone beyond mind and can sustain intense conscious presence) accelerates your own growth in presence.
- **Quoted source: St. Paul.** Tolle cites St. Paul as beautifully expressing the universal principle that consciousness transmutes what unconsciousness creates: "Everything is shown up by being exposed to the light, and whatever is exposed to the light itself becomes light."
- **Analogy: darkness.** Just as you cannot fight the darkness, you cannot fight the pain-body — trying creates inner conflict and further pain; "Watching it is enough. Watching it implies accepting it as part of what is at that moment."

## Thread
This chapter turns the book's foundational teachings — presence, watching the thinker, honoring the Now, disidentification from mind (Part One's territory) — onto the specific problem of accumulated emotional pain. It names the pain-body as the concrete carrier of the past inside a person and shows that the same tool taught throughout the book, being present as the watcher, is the instrument of its dissolution. Opening Part Two on relationship as spiritual practice, it explains why intimate relationships flip so quickly from pleasure to pain (they are prime pain-body triggers and feeding grounds) and why people seem to become "alien, nasty creatures" — groundwork for the relationship teachings that follow in chapter 7. Its closing themes — you cannot fight the pain-body, watching implies accepting it as part of what is — also point forward to Part Three's teachings on acceptance and surrender. The two-level analysis (pain created now vs. pain from the past) links present-moment nonacceptance back to the mind's denial of the Now and forward to the responsibility Tolle assigns the reader: to stop creating further pain.
