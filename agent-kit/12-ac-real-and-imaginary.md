# Real and Imaginary: Tyler D.'s Spiritual Conversion in Rome

## In one paragraph
The book's final numbered chapter closes the frame Pasulka opened in New Mexico: the book "began with a journey, and it ends with a journey," this time to Rome, where she brings Tyler D. to the Vatican Secret Archives and the Vatican Observatory at Castel Gandolfo — "ground zero of the Catholic faith," mirroring Tyler having taken her, blindfolded, to "ground zero of the UFO myth." Sent to analyze the canonization records of two seventeenth-century levitators — St. Joseph of Cupertino (canonized) and Sister Maria of Agreda (never canonized) — Pasulka instead witnesses what she calls "perhaps the most miraculous and strange event" of her six years of research: Tyler's profound religious conversion, culminating months later in his becoming a Catholic. Theoretically, the chapter advances her most careful formulation of the book's central claim: perceived contact with nonhuman intelligence is "simultaneously imagined and real" — she makes no ontological claim about extraterrestrials, but argues that perceived contact "has very real effects with powerful social implications." Sister Maria of Agreda, the cloistered nun who claimed to bilocate to colonial New Mexico, becomes Pasulka's model of the "meta-experiencer" and Tyler's historical double: both are "inadvertent colonists" whose imagined first contact, amplified by the media infrastructures of their eras, paved the way for real colonization — Spain's of the New World, and today's elite-driven colonization of space.

## Core claims
- Pasulka restates the book's thesis: belief in extraterrestrials and UFOs constitutes a new form of religion, delivered by media and popular culture (TV, music, video games, hoaxes, websites, mixed-reality environments); new digital–human interface research shows that regardless of conscious belief, "data delivered through screens shoots straight into memory, which then constructs models of events."
- She explicitly limits her claim: she is "not making an ontological claim, that extraterrestrials are real in the sense that couches are real, although they could be" — she argues that perceived contact is simultaneously imagined and real, with very real effects and powerful social implications.
- Many individuals, she says, now interpret their own traditional religions through the lens of the UFO hermeneutic — and witnessing that transformation happen (Bledsoe, then Tyler) is entirely different from describing it.
- Sister Maria of Agreda and Tyler are structurally parallel: both "inadvertent colonists" who made imagined "first contact"; Maria's astral voyages seeded the cultural imagination with supernatural support for Spanish missionaries, while Tyler's alien-inflected technological visions are supported by a massive media infrastructure that fertilizes efforts to colonize space.
- Following Jeff Kripal, Pasulka argues for a "both/and" framework over an "either/or" one: Maria may genuinely have had experiences that cannot be easily explained away or reduced to political machinations, AND those experiences helped legitimize Spanish colonization.
- Maria's journeys were "strategically politicized" by Benavides and others as proof God wanted New Mexico under Spanish rule — imaginings, empirical or not, that helped Spain colonize part of America.
- The history of imaginal space travel is erased selectively: Maria's early cosmographies (she wrote she saw the earth from space as "a spinning sphere") were burned and are ignored in favor of her Marian biography; likewise, historians of ufology largely ignore African American and indigenous UFO traditions that predate the standard 1947 origin date — the Nation of Islam articulated a UFO narrative by the 1930s.
- Ufology and the space industry are elite endeavors: private space heads are billionaires, and (citing Brenda Denzler) the field of ufology is "overwhelmingly white and male" and over 90 percent Anglo-American; Tyler's and James's work is largely invisible.
- Levitation links the two domains: within UFO literature levitation is a common contact theme (people levitated from beds into crafts), and members of aeronautic agencies had contacted Pasulka and a colleague about levitating saints — "at least some members of the space industry believed in the possibility of levitation."
- The Jesuits at the Vatican Observatory are not searching for UFOs and are not engaged in ufology; what they demonstrate is that science is compatible with religion — so effectively that Tyler, a Baptist scientist, chose to become Catholic.
- Rome transformed Tyler's understanding of "the beings": his connection felt "supercharged," yet he also felt he knew less about what and who they were and their intentions; he came to believe they were, or were like, the angels Sister Maria said carried her — a complete shift of interpretive structure, which Pasulka compares to Rey Hernandez's shift from atheist to agnostic.

## Concepts
- **real and imaginary (simultaneously imagined and real)** — Pasulka's framing of the chapter: perceived contact with a nonhuman intelligent, divine being is at once imagined and real. She makes no ontological claim about extraterrestrials existing "in the sense that couches are real, although they could be"; the argument is that perceived contact has very real effects with powerful social implications.
- **meta-experiencer** — Pasulka's term for the historical figure Sister Maria of Agreda, whom she "came to view as a meta-experiencer" while in Rome: a figure whose anomalous experience (bilocation to New Mexico) both mirrors and illuminates the contemporary experiencer (Tyler), across centuries.
- **bilocation** — recognized by the Catholic Church as a rare "charism," or sacred skill; a person who bilocates is said to appear to be in two places simultaneously. Maria allegedly bilocated from her Spanish convent to colonial New Mexico while her sister nuns saw her rise off the floor surrounded by brilliant light.
- **charism** — the Church's term for a rare sacred skill, of which bilocation is an example.
- **levitation** — a phenomenon Pasulka had never had occasion to consider as reality until this research: a common theme in UFO contact literature (people levitated out of beds, through windows, into crafts) and copiously documented in the canonization records of St. Joseph of Cupertino.
- **the both/and framework** — following Jeff Kripal: instead of an either/or scenario that negates the anomalous and reduces Maria's claims to pure imaginings, a framework allowing both that Maria really had inexplicable experiences and that those experiences paved the way for Spanish colonization.
- **psychic cosmonauts** — Pasulka's term for a lineage within religious traditions of "people who claim to fly through space with the help of angels or beings of light"; she notes that angelic beings show up "again and again" in their discourse, and that Tyler believes in beings that help him develop technologies.
- **the UFO hermeneutic / UFO mythos** — the interpretive lens through which many people now read their own traditional religions; the mythos is delivered by media and popular culture and, per digital-interface research, bypasses conscious belief via screen-delivered data that "shoots straight into memory."
- **incorruptible saints** — the Catholic tradition according to which certain people's bodies do not decay after death and are pronounced "incorruptible" by the Church; such bodies are often displayed in glass cases, as with Pope John XXIII in St. Peter's Basilica.
- **postulator** — a Vatican functionary who presents the case for an individual's canonization or beatification; Pasulka gained access to key postulators only through her association with Tyler.
- **last rites / anointing of the sick** — the last rites provide absolution of sins, preparing the dying soul for death; the anointing of the sick is one of the seven sacraments, in which a priest blesses a person with blessed oil. Central to the hospital rounds with Father McDonnell that broke Tyler's heart open.
- **relic** — a sacred fragment (in the hospital chapel, a fragment of a bone of St. Teresa of Avila).
- **vocation** — a life of calling exemplified by Brother Guy and the observatory Jesuits, who "wedded their spiritual lives to their work lives" rather than compartmentalizing religion into weekly attendance; Pasulka calls Tyler's life "a testament to a type of vocation not recognized by secular institutions."
- **invisibility** — the condition of Tyler's (and most of James's) classified work, which makes real knowledge of the topic difficult to attain; Pasulka asks whether Tyler is "a contemporary Maria, existing in a sort of cloister of invisibility." Its inverse appears at the Vatican: when Tyler reveals who he is, he becomes "visible," and visibility provides access.
- **imagined "first contact"** — Maria's and Tyler's shared role as "inadvertent colonists": imaginary/real voyages of contact that precede and enable real colonization — Maria's for Spanish missionaries, Tyler's for the media-fueled colonization of space.
- **"secret" (of the Vatican Secret Archives)** — from a Latin word Pasulka says is more accurately translated "private"; the archives hold roughly fifty-three miles of underground shelving and require special credentials to enter.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The Rome trip itself (the culminating field episode)** — Almost two years after Tyler took Pasulka and James, blindfolded, to the New Mexico "crash" site, Pasulka takes Tyler to Rome — from ground zero of the UFO myth to ground zero of the Catholic faith. Funded by an organization that asked her to analyze the canonization trial records of St. Joseph of Cupertino and Sister Maria of Agreda (why was Joseph canonized and Maria not?), she also planned to assess historical records of the search for extraterrestrial life in the observatory archive. Tyler proposed joining: she would translate documents, he would analyze them from an aeronautics standpoint. She notes precedent: individuals from aeronautic agencies had contacted her, and a colleague studying Joseph of Cupertino, about levitating saints.
- **St. Joseph of Cupertino** — seventeenth-century Italian priest said to have levitated so frequently that the priests handling his canonization stopped counting witnesses; copious records of flights, levitations, soaring to a cathedral ceiling — on at least one occasion taking another person with him. Pasulka: the large number suggests witnesses were probably not making it up; "seemingly something had happened."
- **Sister Maria of Agreda (the chapter's central historical case)** — cloistered seventeenth-century Spanish mystic; her earliest works were cosmographies recording astral journeys through space and over the earth (she wrote she saw the earth from space as a spinning sphere), later burned by nuns of her convent with only a few copies remaining. As a young nun she claimed that, with the help of angels, she flew to New Mexico, met indigenous Americans, taught them the Catholic faith, and prepared them for baptism by Franciscans. Her biographers said her body levitated surrounded by a blinding white light in her cell while she experienced herself soaring on the wings of angels; separately, her sister nuns reported witnessing her rise a few feet off the floor surrounded by brilliant light during the bilocations. Best known for the *Mystical City of God*, a biography of the Virgin Mary; her science and cosmography are largely ignored. Her cause has been proposed to the Congregation for the Causes of Saints many times without canonization. She answered to the Inquisition, claimed she was pressured to answer Benavides as he desired, saw writings burned, recanted, then "recanted her recantations" and rewrote former works from memory. She criticized Benavides for being too "literal" about her bilocations while insisting they really happened.
- **The Jumanos and the "lady in blue"** — Franciscan missionaries in New Mexico reportedly encountered members of the Jumanos tribe who presented themselves as eager for baptism, saying they had been visited by a "lady in blue" who spoke of the Catholic faith. Alonso de Benavides, first commissioner of the Inquisition in New Mexico, carried the story to Spain, questioned Maria closely, was impressed by details he thought she could not have known, and reported to King Philip IV. Pasulka: the journeys were "strategically politicized" to justify funding and imperial expansion — used as proof God wanted the area under Spanish rule.
- **Christopher Bledsoe** — a Baptist from North Carolina, former pilot and owner of a successful construction business, who had a profound UFO sighting he interpreted as an extension of his own religious tradition; his congregation rejected the interpretation and called it demonic, an agonizing process that alienated him from his community and changed his life, though he now seems at peace. Pasulka offers him as a prior witnessed case of religious transformation; Tyler's was "an accelerated version."
- **Tyler's admission to the Vatican Secret Archives** — Pasulka spent a year arranging access (PhD, tenured professor of religious studies specializing in Catholic culture required); Tyler, with over forty patents but no PhD, was held at the first of three security stations for nearly two hours despite adjunct credentials, deans' letters, and Pasulka's letter. From the plane she first told him not to reveal who he is, then relented: "Okay, go ahead." Next text: "I'm in. My archive badge is good for six months" — the maximum. Pasulka's reading: "Tyler was known to members of the Vatican," and his visibility provided access — including hers to documents and key postulators, which "would not have happened had I been there alone." The young black-robed archivists spoke to Tyler instead of her, calling her "the lady doctor."
- **Father McDonnell** — an American priest Tyler approached when lost in the archives; known by seemingly everyone at the Vatican with access to "its every nook and cranny." He befriended Tyler, gave him rosary rings and prayer cards, blessed the project in the Vatican courtyard, and parted the camouflaged, heavily armed real guards "like Moses parting the Red Sea" (the colorful Swiss Guards, Pasulka notes, seem mostly for tourists). He figured out the UFO angle of their research but it didn't change his warmth; he invited them to Mass in St. Peter's Basilica.
- **Mass in St. Peter's Basilica** — celebrated in Latin near the incorruptible body of Pope John XXIII, with Father McDonnell and six nuns, after passing through the off-limits sacristy. Tyler, a Baptist, had never heard of incorruptible saints and was astonished to learn he had been near a dead body; two weeks later, the day after his mother's funeral, he told Pasulka the proximity to John XXIII's preserved body was comforting.
- **Hospital rounds with Father McDonnell** — Mass at a small hospital chapel containing a bone relic of St. Teresa of Avila, then last rites and anointing of the sick. Pasulka accompanied McDonnell to dying women, Tyler to dying men. At the bedside of a woman near death, her daughter holding her hand, McDonnell anointed her forehead; the woman's eyes sparkled and she smiled; McDonnell said, "Now you see. I get more from them than I give to them. In there, was beauty. It was God." Tyler spent fifteen minutes with a dying man in his late twenties or early thirties who had struggled to Mass with a walker; he emerged head bowed, unable to look at Pasulka — "His heart was broken." Pasulka says this experience prepared Tyler for his conversion.
- **The church of Santa Sabina** — during a jolting golf-cart tour of Rome (including a twenty-minute police stop), they reach Santa Sabina, the oldest remaining Roman basilica, atop the Aventine Hill, named for a Roman noblewoman converted by her servant Seraphia (both executed, both later declared saints), built on the site of Sabina's home near a temple of Juno. Tyler slipped into a side chapel and was found on his knees, praying and crying, as a wedding party assembled — "not in a normal frame of mind."
- **The door of Santa Sabina** — a historian showed Pasulka the church's carved wooden door: one of the earliest depictions of the crucifixion (Christ standing calmly, arms outstretched between the two thieves, who look "expectant, not crushed or tortured") — and, Pasulka noticed, images of levitation. The historian, confused, conceded: "I suppose, yes, these are of levitations." Two panels depict Christ's ascension; a third shows Christ ascended above a mysterious globe- or disc-like object scholars cannot identify (Pasulka later found websites associating it with a UFO); other panels show Elijah ascending on a cloud and Habakkuk ascending or lifted by an angel — "numerous examples of bodies ascending to the heavens."
- **The restaurant confession** — after the tour Tyler tells Pasulka: "No. Nothing's okay… I have to go back and help people directly. I feel like I am a complete failure," and asks her to introduce him to priests or nuns so he can serve — "I don't care if I ever work at my day job again." She recognizes a spiritual conversion in progress.
- **The Vatican Observatory at Castel Gandolfo** — one of the oldest established Western observatories, beside a volcano and a crystal-blue volcanic lake, where monks, nuns, and priests peered at the skies for centuries. Director Brother Guy Consolmagno — American Jesuit astronomer specializing in meteorites and asteroids, degrees from MIT and the University of Arizona — hands Pasulka the keys to the archive, which she calls unprecedented in half a life of archival work. The archive holds works of Kepler and Copernicus (once banned by the church, now prominently displayed — an irony Pasulka flags) and displays the first telescopes; Brother Guy lined the walls with photographs of the nuns who charted star patterns, "correcting the historical record by including those who had been left out of it."
- **The "aerial phenomena" coffee exchange** — at the daily ~10 a.m. cappuccino gathering with about ten space-credentialed Jesuits (astrophysics, astronomy), asked what they seek in the archive, Pasulka avoids the word "UFO" and says, "We are looking for instances of aerial phenomena." Stares; she laughs; they laugh; conversation over. She sets the record straight: the Jesuits are not searching for UFOs or doing ufology; Brother Guy's out-of-context jokes have fed conspiracy theories.
- **Brother Guy's talk to the European Space Agency's young scientists** — at the mountaintop observatory (a fleet of sleek cars gleaming outside), Brother Guy showed pop-culture depictions of Castel Gandolfo as a hub of mystery, then the mundane reality (priests discussing meteorite composition), collapsing the room in laughter. He recounted joining the Peace Corps after his PhD: in a very poor country, villagers he helped feed and supply with clean water would beg him nightly to bring out his telescope and asked what the sky meant — the moment "something within him clicked" and he found his vocation: helping people realize "there is more to life than just what to eat for lunch"; the wonder of the cosmos is "literally spiritual food." When Tyler was introduced with his affiliations, the young Europeans burst into cheers and applause — Pasulka's "American hero," for once not invisible.
- **The Kepler-archive recognition scene** — reading Kepler's analysis of Copernicus at night, Pasulka suddenly connects Maria's bilocation destination with the blindfolded New Mexico trip and asks Tyler, "Is this the same place where she imagined she went?" Tyler's face freezes; he looks down; he will not answer. The archive fuses in her mind with her memories of New Mexico.
- **Tyler's mother** — in the archive Tyler learns by text his mother has entered hospice; Pasulka asks the observatory priest to offer the nightly chapel Mass for her, and Tyler has rosaries bought at the Vatican blessed. Home again, he gives his mother a blessed rosary in their last conversation; she puts it around her neck and holds his hand. His Baptist siblings agree she may be buried with it. Tyler later learns from his sister — who knew nothing of the praying — that while the observatory Mass was celebrated, his mother, uncommunicative for months, roused to consciousness for several hours with perfect memory and conversed with her family.
- **Rey Hernandez** — cited as a parallel: a confirmed atheist whose experiences transformed him into an agnostic, as Tyler's understanding of his relationship to the beings "shifted completely."
- **The Nation of Islam's early UFO narrative** — offered against the "born in 1947" assumption: the founders were articulating a UFO narrative by the 1930s, and according to Elijah Muhammad, early leader Wallace Fard Muhammad had spoken of UFOs in the 1920s; historians of ufology, with few exceptions, ignore African American and indigenous UFO traditions.
- **Brenda Denzler's demographics of ufology** — Pasulka's colleague's observation that ufology and the study of anomalous aerial phenomena are "overwhelmingly white and male" and over 90 percent Anglo-American.
- **Tyler's conversion completed** — months after returning, Tyler is invited back to Rome to make his first communion as a Catholic at a small Mass with Pope Francis, at Santa Sabina — where he first felt, in his words, "the presence of the Holy Spirit." The Mass fell on St. Valentine's Day, which that year was also, rarely, Ash Wednesday. Pasulka: "For Tyler, it was not an end, but the very beginning."

## Thread
This is the final numbered chapter and the narrative resolution of the book's arc: the journey structure that opened at the blindfolded New Mexico "crash" site (with Tyler and James) closes at the Vatican and Castel Gandolfo, and the thesis argued throughout — that UFO belief is a religion-in-the-making, forged by media, experience, and the mechanisms of belief — is dramatized rather than merely described, as Pasulka watches a conversion happen "right before my eyes." It also delivers her most explicit epistemological positioning ("simultaneously imagined and real," no ontological claim) and introduces the meta-experiencer (Sister Maria of Agreda) as the historical key that reframes Tyler: the psychic cosmonaut whose imagined contact serves real colonization, then and now. The chapter's ironies compound the book's themes of invisibility and institutional power — Tyler's classified fame opens Vatican doors his professor-guide's credentials cannot; the church that banned Copernicus now displays him — and it hands off directly to the Conclusion ("The Artifact"): Tyler's revised, humbler understanding of the beings (angel-like, purposes unknown) is where the book leaves its chief informant, at "not an end, but the very beginning."

## Connections
- Mirrors the book's opening New Mexico expedition (unit for the chapter on the desert "crash" site with Tyler and James) — the blindfolded trip is explicitly recalled twice here, and Maria's claimed bilocation destination is juxtaposed with that site.
- Extends the media-and-belief mechanism chapters: the claim that screen-delivered data bypasses conscious belief and that the UFO mythos is delivered through TV, games, and mixed reality restates the book's earlier argument about how media create believers.
- Deepens the portraits of Tyler D. and James from earlier chapters (Tyler's protocol, his belief in beings that help him develop technologies, his and James's invisibility).
- Sets up the Conclusion ("The Artifact") and terms likely gathered in the author Glossary (unit 10): meta-experiencer, bilocation, psychic cosmonauts.
- Cross-book (speculative/thematic, not Pasulka's own reference): Maria's "both/and" real-yet-imaginal travel and the beings-as-information themes resonate with the-holographic-universe (nonlocal consciousness, miracles of mystics) and with my-big-toe's treatment of altered-state "travel" as data access; the media-constructed-reality argument loosely echoes the-simulation-hypothesis on rendered experience.
