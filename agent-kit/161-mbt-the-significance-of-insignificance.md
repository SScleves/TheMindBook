# The Significance of Insignificance

## In one paragraph
Thomas Campbell closes out the trilogy's final section by addressing a worry he expects readers to have after absorbing his Big TOE: that everyday worldly (PMR) pursuits — careers, hobbies, relationships — become pointless once you know consciousness quality is "the point." He argues the opposite: any activity is valuable because of what you learn from doing it and from your interactions with others, not because of the activity's content or its external value. He reframes apparent "insignificance" as a shift in what kind of value matters (ego value fading, "real value" of interactions growing), uses a juice-glass analogy to describe people who mistake their limited self-knowledge for complete knowledge, and ends with a deliberately informal, chant-like exhortation ("Just go do it!") plus a concrete list of daily practices and a self-honesty test (a serious six-month effort with objectively visible results) for readers who want to actually change themselves rather than just read about it.

## Core claims
- Campbell argues that improving the quality of one's consciousness does not make worldly (PMR) pursuits — mathematics, physics, philosophy, basket weaving, digging ditches, becoming an artist — a waste of time; everyone should have something externally productive to do that is at least of some value to others.
- He claims it is not what you do, nor how valuable it is to others, that matters — it is what you learn in the process of doing it; almost any job or activity suffices because interaction with yourself and others creates the best learning opportunities.
- He states that if, after reading My Big TOE, everything in PMR seems less interesting and less significant, the reader "has missed an important point": PMR experience is precisely the opportunity to learn by making choices precipitated by interaction with others.
- He asserts that no profession or life's work becomes less important with a bigger picture — rather, the bigger picture adds new important things that did not exist in the smaller picture; physical possessions, family, friends, and career are not more or less part of your life than before, but how you value them changes as ego, knowledge, wisdom, and perspective change.
- He claims that from the larger perspective, the ego value of what you do subsides while the real value — interactions with yourself, your tasks, and other people — grows; a career may seem less important in the Big Picture, but the potential value of its interactions should seem more important.
- He argues that what you are interested in is a function of what turns you on and where you invest your energy, and that a bigger view adds more interesting possible investments into the mix as you evolve your being (grow the quality and lower the entropy of your consciousness).
- He claims the reader can verify this by looking at their own life from age five to the present and observing that the things important to them constantly change as awareness broadens.
- He asserts that, for those able, interested, and willing, the rate of personal growth accelerates after age thirty-five and can remain exponential for some time rather than petering out asymptotically as most people assume; growth only becomes asymptotic when a person believes they already know almost everything important.
- He describes that sense of "knowing it all" as blossoming at age two, again in the teenage years, and then typically gelling permanently around age forty — labeled "terrible," "self-absorbed"/"wild," and "mature" respectively (names given by the older group in each case) — and frames this culturally-endorsed endpoint of "growing up" as actually a limiting delusion.
- He uses the juice-glass analogy to claim that people who believe they know everything significant are like a small glass of prune juice that thinks it contains all the fluid on the planet, ignorant of the world's lakes, oceans, rivers, and seas, yet feeling self-important and complete in its "mastery of fluids."
- He claims that if My Big TOE leaves a reader feeling deflated, it is because they are seeing the juice glass as half empty rather than half full — where you are now is almost certainly a great place from which to learn and grow, and you do not need to change your environment, only yourself.
- He argues that not starting self-change because you "don't know how" is a defeatist copout, comparable to a one-year-old believing it will never walk or talk because it doesn't yet know how.
- He states that actual sanity flows from freedom while only pretend sanity flows from conformity.
- He lists concrete practices for readers who want to change: understand your beliefs and their limitations; regularly inspect your ego for growth or shrinkage and expose its blatant fantasies to yourself, loved ones, and friends; dedicate daily energy to finding and verifying truth; become aware of your motivations and intents; turn off the TV and get acquainted with your mind; learn to meditate (referring readers to Chapter 23 of Book 1); be kind and loving in all interactions; stop dwelling on your own wants, needs, and desires; identify your fears and outgrow them; and continually "taste the pudding" — check objective, measurable results.
- He sets a concrete self-honesty test: if you do not get results that both you and others can easily see after a serious six-month effort, do something else; real progress requires patience and serious dedication over a long time, and one should "have fun always."

## Concepts
- **Big TOE** — Campbell's own "Big Theory Of Everything," the overall framework the book has been building; this chapter treats it as something that could leave a reader either energized or "deflated" depending on how they interpret its implications for ordinary life.
- **PMR (Physical Matter Reality)** — used here as shorthand for ordinary worldly existence and its pursuits (careers, hobbies, relationships, professions); the chapter's central question is whether PMR activity loses meaning once consciousness-quality is understood as the real point.
- **Quality of consciousness** — the trait Campbell frames as "the point of existence" throughout the book; in this chapter, growing it is what any PMR activity ultimately serves, regardless of the activity's specific content.
- **Entropy of consciousness (lowering entropy)** — Campbell's term, paired with "growing the quality," for the process of personal/spiritual evolution; as one's being evolves, the entropy of consciousness is lowered and the quality is grown, which in turn shifts the focus of one's energy investment.
- **Ego value vs. real value** — a distinction Campbell draws between the value a task or possession has for one's ego (status, importance-to-self) and its "real value," defined as the interactions it generates with yourself, your tasks, and other people; he claims ego value subsides and real value grows as perspective broadens.
- **Energy investment** — Campbell's framing of interest and motivation as a function of "what turns you on" and where you invest your energy; a broader awareness continually changes the mix of things worth investing energy in.
- **Growth rate curve** — Campbell's model of personal growth plotted as growth rate versus time; he claims it can be exponential and, for those willing, accelerates after age thirty-five rather than flattening out, contrary to common assumption.
- **Asymptotic growth (as a trap)** — the state where growth rate slows to a crawl; Campbell attributes this specifically to the belief that one already knows almost everything important, not to any necessary feature of aging.
- **The juice-glass analogy** — a four-ounce glass half full of prune juice that believes it contains all the fluid on the planet, ignorant of lakes, oceans, rivers, and seas; Campbell's image for a person who has "gelled" into believing they know everything significant while remaining unaware of how much more there is.
- **Half full vs. half empty** — Campbell's framing for two possible emotional reactions to the book's implications: feeling deflated (half empty) versus recognizing one's current position as a great place to learn and grow (half full).
- **The mind-rap / "Just go do it!"** — a short, deliberately informal chant Campbell offers ("Just go do it! Do it now! ... You don't need to know how!") as a practical antidote to the copout of not starting self-change because you don't know how; he suggests singing it to a funky beat with imaginary bongos, and, if embarrassed, passing it off to friends as a "secret Tibetan chant" from an "ancient 'Monks Do It Better' bumper sticker."
- **Sanity from freedom vs. pretend sanity from conformity** — Campbell's claim that genuine sanity comes from freedom, while sanity that comes from conforming to others' expectations is only pretend sanity.
- **The six-month effort test / "tasting the pudding"** — Campbell's proposed empirical check on one's own progress: pursue real, measurable, objective results, and if you and others cannot see results after a serious six-month effort, change your approach; still requires patience since real progress takes dedication over a long time.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The life-review exercise**: Campbell instructs the reader to "simply look at your life — say from age five to your current age" to see for themselves that the things important to them have constantly changed as their awareness broadened, offered as a self-verifiable demonstration of his claim about shifting energy investment.
- **The three "knowing it all" life stages**: Campbell cites the toddler stage (around age two, called "terrible"), the teenage years (called "self-absorbed" and "wild"), and the gelling point around age forty (called "mature") as recurring instances where a sense of complete/significant knowledge blossoms or solidifies — noting wryly that "obviously it is the older group that makes up the names."
- **The PMR-roles list**: Campbell names a spread of ordinary occupations and roles — "butcher, baker, candlestick maker, doctor, lawyer, Indian chief, housewife, teacher, physicist, bum, executive, or guru" — as examples of PMR activity that all provide the opportunity needed to learn and grow the quality of one's consciousness, regardless of how mundane or exalted the role appears.
- **The juice-glass case**: a four-ounce glass of prune juice that has never left its shelf, "secure in its ignorance," believing it holds all the fluid on the planet — Campbell's illustrative case for the person who has gelled into false completeness of understanding, contrasted with the actual scale of "the world's great lakes, oceans, rivers and seas."
- **The one-year-old baby case**: a baby who believes it will never walk or talk because it doesn't yet know how — offered as a concrete parallel to adults who refuse to start changing themselves for the same stated reason, which Campbell dismisses as "Bullpucky" and "a copout attitude laying down a defeatist rap."
- **The "secret Tibetan chant" cover story**: a specific social tactic Campbell offers — telling embarrassed friends that the "Just go do it!" mind-rap is a secret Tibetan chant translated from a primitive Sanskrit text found "etched at the bottom of an ancient 'Monks Do It Better' bumper sticker" — a comic example of how to actually perform the practice despite self-consciousness.
- **The daily-practice checklist**: a concrete, itemized set of actions Campbell recommends readers actually do (not just read about): examine beliefs and their limits; inspect the ego regularly for growth/shrinkage; expose ego fantasies to self, then loved ones, then friends; dedicate daily energy to finding/verifying truth; become aware of motivations and intents; turn off the TV; learn to meditate (cross-referenced to Chapter 23 of Book 1); be kind and loving in interactions; stop dwelling on one's own wants/needs/desires; identify and outgrow fears; and continuously check objective results.
- **The six-month benchmark**: a specific, named time frame — "a serious six-month effort" — against which Campbell says a reader should be able to see real, measurable results visible to both themselves and others, functioning as a concrete falsifiability test for whether one's chosen self-improvement approach is working.

## Thread
As the third of six chapters in Section 6, "The End is Always the Beginning," this unit functions as a practical landing point after the trilogy's theoretical Big TOE has been fully laid out: it anticipates and defuses a plausible reader reaction (existential deflation about ordinary life) and pivots the book from explanation toward instruction, converting the abstract claim that "growing consciousness quality is the point of existence" into everyday terms (career, relationships, aging, ego) and then into an actionable checklist and self-test. It explicitly reaches back to Chapter 23 of Book 1 for meditation practice, tying the trilogy's opening practical material to its closing exhortation, and sets up the book's final close by ending on the repeated imperative "Just go do it. You don't need to know how!"
