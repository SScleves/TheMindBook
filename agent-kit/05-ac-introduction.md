# Introduction

## In one paragraph
The Introduction lays out the book's twin subjects — contemporary religion, with the UFO phenomenon as case study, and technology — and argues they are intimately connected: technological infrastructure shapes religious practice, and for believers the UFO itself is advanced technology, making technology both the sacred medium and the sacred object of a new religiosity. Pasulka tells the origin story of her research: after finishing a book on the Catholic doctrine of purgatory, in which she encountered archival reports (1300–1880) of orbs, luminous beings, and disclike aerial objects, she shifted in January 2012 to studying UFO cultures ethnographically. She quickly discovered a "parallel research tradition" — an anonymous "Invisible College" of successful scientists and academics who study the phenomenon in secret — and chose, with some trepidation, to write about this group. She reports two surprising revelations: that respected, elite scientists (not "cranks and weirdos") believe in and study the phenomenon, and that what is marketed to the public barely resembles their findings, being built instead on rumor, hoax, and disinformation. She frames her method as religious studies: bracketing truth claims to analyze the very real social effects, and observing in real time the formation of a new global belief system through contact events, interpretive communities, and institutional intervention. She closes by asking "Is it real, or is it imaginary?" and answering that the book will suggest it is both.

## Core claims
- Pasulka claims that religion and technology are intimately connected because social and economic infrastructures shape how people practice religion (her uncontroversial example: the printing press giving rise to Sola Scriptura and the Protestant Reformation).
- She argues that technology is not only the infrastructural basis for widespread UFO belief (via media technologies) but is itself both the sacred medium and the sacred object of this new religiosity — while in certain theological circles technology (especially the internet) is instead theologized as "the Beast," the anti-Christ.
- She claims the association of UFO belief with the "fringe" is "the lie"; the truth is the opposite — millionaires, billionaires, and successful, innovative scientists believe in and study the phenomenon. She says Stephen Hawking was wrong to discount UFO reports as coming only from "cranks and weirdos."
- Pasulka asserts there is, and always has been, a parallel research tradition within ufology: public ufologists and a few academics on one side, and an anonymous "Invisible College" (Hynek's term, written of by Vallee) whose work is never made public but whose investigations impact society and whose rumors spawn the folklore constituting the UFO narrative.
- She claims that what is ultimately marketed to the public about the phenomenon barely resembles the findings of the scientists who study it; media narratives are often second- to fourth-hand, hoaxed, or fabricated because "it sells" — yet, ironically, it is precisely media representations that create and sustain UFO belief.
- She claims belief in the phenomenon is at an all-time high and pervasive across polls, with credible witnesses including a former US president, Air Force trained observers, pilots, police, and millions of civilians.
- She argues scholars of religion are well suited to study UFOs because religious studies brackets metaphysical truth claims to focus on social effects, "which are incontestably very real" — a strategy Vallee advocated to the UN in 1979.
- She claims she witnessed the formation of a new, unique form of religion in process — "the dynamic genesis of a global belief system" — with today's "agents of information" (screenwriters, producers, authors) playing the role scribes and redactors played in the first century CE; she, too, was part of (perhaps a pawn in) the process of belief formation.
- She argues that UFO events are linked to religion because the history of religion is, among other things, a record of perceived contact with supernatural beings descending from the skies amid light; the modern alien from the sky is comparable to the ancient god descending from the heavens (citing Kripal with Strieber).
- She claims that in the history of religions a contact event is followed by interpretations, then by institutions (religions/denominations) with a stake in how the event is read; institutions — including governments — monitor and actively shape interpretations of contact events, which are "powerful events with unpredictable social effects."
- She stresses that a contact event is not automatically a religious event, nor a sighting automatically a UFO event: these become such through an interpretive process, developing spontaneously and intentionally within popular culture and through purposive institutional involvement.
- She claims the flying-saucer-as-new-global-belief idea predates religious-studies scholarship (Jung in the 1950s; Vallee in the late 1960s), and endorses Vallee's argument that the phenomenon itself is thousands of years old ("He is right") even though the ubiquitous modern UFO framework dates from around 1947.
- She reports that the scientists she worked with, though passionately obsessed, would never offer conclusions about what the phenomenon is or where it comes from, and regarded it as too sacred both for religious dogma (the suggestion that it grounds a new form of religion elicited sneers and disgust) and for the media.
- She notes that the media's representation of the phenomenon often adds some violence to the original event that motivated the belief, and that the events themselves pale in comparison to the reality of the social effects — which she calls a shame.
- She discloses that she is not "read in" to any government program, was never knowingly privy to classified information, and is not part of any official or unofficial disclosure effort.

## Concepts
- **the phenomenon** — Pasulka's (and her informants') term for the UFO/nonhuman-intelligence phenomenon; scientists study it but never conclude what it is or where it comes from, and Davis calls UFOs "real phenomena… craft of a supremely advanced technology."
- **scientist-believers** — successful, innovative scientists (also millionaires and billionaires) who believe in and study the phenomenon; Bigelow's public statements are "typical" of them. Their charisma and conviction were difficult for Pasulka to discount.
- **experiencers** — people who have sighted UFOs or believe they have seen extraterrestrials; they interpret these as spiritual or religious events, which either fracture their traditional belief systems or lead them to reinterpret their traditions through a biblical–UFO framework.
- **meta-experiencers** — the scientists who study the experiencers and the phenomenon; meeting them confounded Pasulka's academic categories and compelled novel thinking.
- **Invisible College / parallel research tradition** — Allen Hynek's term (written of by Jacques Vallee) for a group of scientists, academics, and others who will never make their UFO work public, or not for a long time, though its results impact society; a parallel tradition within ufology, unknown to the uninitiated but well known within ufologist culture, confirmed by scientists such as Teodorani and Davis. Pasulka's participants are anonymous both because of the stigma associated with UFOs and belief in them and because classified government programs studying the phenomenon necessitated secrecy; they agreed to be included on condition of anonymity, and writing about them put her in the odd position of "almost confirming a myth" — usually the place of authors of theology, not of academic authors of books about religion.
- **ufology** — a branch of research devoted to the UFO topic, with public ufologists known for their work alongside the hidden parallel tradition.
- **contact event** — the perceived interface between the human and the intelligent nonhuman being from the sky; contact events spawn beliefs and interpretations that develop into communities of belief, and institutions appropriate, cultivate, and sometimes intervene in their interpretation. Not automatically religious — it becomes a religious or UFO event through an interpretive process.
- **belief communities / communities of interpretation** — the faith communities that form from interpretations of a contact event; in the history of religions, contact event → interpretations → institutions (religions or denominations), each with a stake in the interpretation (e.g., the thousands of interpretive communities around Christianity).
- **epistemological shock** — Harvard UFO researcher John Mack's term, adopted by Pasulka: a shock to one's fundamental understanding of the world and universe. Hers came on two levels: elite scientists believe in space-originating nonhuman intelligence, and rumors of their findings fuel hoaxes and disinformation marketed as fact.
- **mechanisms of belief** — the processes by which people come to believe and practice, which Pasulka began to record; scholars of religion since the 1960s have made progress identifying these, including how social infrastructures inspire new religious movements.
- **agents of information** — today's equivalent of first-century scribes and redactors: screenwriters, television producers, and authors who interpret, spin, produce, and market the UFO story to the public.
- **"nuts and bolts" methodology** — the approach of one of Pasulka's scientists: applying scientific analysis to what he believes are artifacts or physical parts of potential "crafts."
- **bracketing (religious-studies method)** — setting aside the metaphysical and objective truth of phenomena to focus on their incontestably real social effects; scholars of religion, with few exceptions, do not assess practitioners' truth claims. Religious studies is "not a religion, but a set of methods for studying religious phenomena."
- **Sola Scriptura** — "Scripture Alone": the doctrine that scripture is the only reliable and necessary guide for Christian faith and practice, a foundational Protestant Reformation principle; Pasulka's example of a technology (the printing press, mass-producing vernacular Bibles) reshaping religion.
- **biblical–UFO framework** — an interpretive frame in which biblical and historical religious events are viewed as UFO events; Ezekiel's wheel is the prime example, and Ancient Aliens offers a similar slant (medieval angels become aliens).
- **UFO religions** — the umbrella term for what was once belief localized in small pockets of believers; now supercharged by digital infrastructure into a widespread worldview. New religious movements such as the Nation of Islam, Scientology, and Jediism incorporate the UFO narrative into older traditions and scriptures.
- **technology as sacred medium and sacred object** — Pasulka's claim that, like nineteenth-century Spiritualists, UFO believers see technology as a portal or frequency shift connecting humans to other minds and to places outside current understandings of space-time; the UFO itself is believed to be advanced technology.
- **technology as "the Beast"** — the converse theological framing in certain circles: technology, especially the internet, characterized as the anti-Christ; either way, technology in these contexts is not secular but infused with theological meaning.
- **disinformation / hoaxes** — bogus information (some created deliberately as disinformation, some created "for the sole reason that it sells") inspired by rumors of the scientists' findings, forming the basis of media, documentaries, and UFO mythologies Pasulka watched unfold in real time.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Robert Bigelow on 60 Minutes** — as Pasulka finished the introduction, the billionaire founder of Bigelow Aerospace (founded 1998, mostly self-funded; its space habitats used by NASA) told interviewer Lara Logan that aliens/nonhuman intelligences are interacting with humans and have been for a long time; asked about the reputational risk, he answered "I don't give a damn… It's not going to change the reality of what I know." Pasulka uses this as typical of the scientist-believers she has met since 2012.
- **Stephen Hawking's 2008 TED Talk** — Hawking: "I am discounting reports of UFOs. Why would they appear to only cranks and weirdos?" Pasulka cites this as the "lie" about UFO belief; the truth is the opposite.
- **The printing press and Sola Scriptura** — her historical, "uncontroversial" example of technology reshaping religion: mass-produced vernacular Bibles gave rise to Scripture Alone and the Protestant Reformation.
- **Inheriting Brenda Denzler's library** — Pasulka began studying UFO cultures in January 2012 via conventional ethnography, inheriting an extensive resource library from Dr. Brenda Denzler (author of The Lure of the Edge), including research from MUFON and CUFOS; she read Hynek, Vallee, Keel, Hopkins, and Mack, plus academic theorists (Kripal, Strieber, Battaglia, Eghigian, Cusack, Lepsetter, Halperin).
- **Massimo Teodorani's testimony** — the astronomer writes that he has been involved in "ufo" research for at least 25 years, parallel to canonic physics/astronomy, knows "some anomalies do exist," stresses studying the problem scientifically with measurement instruments, and has studied it "behind totally closed doors" — confirming the parallel tradition.
- **Eric Davis's testimony** — the physicist states UFOs are real, artificial objects under intelligent control, "craft of a supremely advanced technology"; that scientists possess corroborated, forensically verified evidence but fear collegial backlash, not the subject; and that one tradition of study is military — "a subject for intelligence gathering collection and analysis," not for science.
- **Belief polls and credible witnesses** — sightings reported by former US president Jimmy Carter, US Air Force trained observers, pilots, commercial pilots, police officers, US Army personnel, and millions of civilians. Polls: a 2008 Scripps poll found more than 50 percent of Americans believe in extraterrestrial life, with 74 percent of 18–24-year-olds believers; National Geographic's informal 2012 poll (1,114 randomly sampled adults, run alongside its UFO-themed programming) found 36 percent believed UFOs exist and 77 percent believed there are signs aliens visited Earth in the past; the 2009 Harris Poll found 32 percent of Americans believe in UFOs.
- **The purgatory-book archives (1300–1880)** — Pasulka's prior multiyear study of the Catholic doctrine of purgatory, drawing on obscure European archives, surfaced unexpected reports of orbs of light, flames penetrating walls, luminous beings, forms of conscious light, spinning suns, and disclike aerial objects; unsure how to theorize them, she left them out of that book.
- **The coffee conversation and the UFO conference** — a friend compared her archival reports to "a Steven Spielberg film"; she dismissed it, but he found an ad for a local UFO/extraterrestrial conference the next weekend and suggested she attend. Its experiencer speakers described the same phenomena she had found in Catholic history — shining aerial discs, flames, orbs — and life transformation, interpreted as spiritual/religious events; this launched her question of whether the orbs once read as purgatory souls are still around and now read as UFOs. That question was "not so mind-bending": she could still fit the data into her academic training — orbs as social constructions based on an externally generated unknown event, or some type of perennial mystical experience interpreted through each era's reigning cultural framework. The challenge began only when she met the meta-experiencers.
- **Ezekiel's wheel and Ancient Aliens** — the prime example of scripture read through the biblical–UFO framework: many practitioners view the prophet's spinning aerial contraption as a UFO; the TV show Ancient Aliens spreads the same interpretive slant, common among youth including her students.
- **Vallee's 1979 UN address** — Jacques Vallee told the special political committee of the United Nations that "the belief in space visitors is independent of the physical reality of the UFO phenomenon"; Pasulka notes Vallee himself believes in the phenomenon's reality while holding that mass belief does not depend on it — the model for her own bracketing method.
- **The unexpected cast of characters** — after her 2012 shift, television producers, experiencers with entourages of government-affiliated agents, and household-name actors showed up unannounced; she reads them, via the history of religions, as the same cast present at the birth of every major religious tradition (first-century scribes and redactors → today's agents of information).
- **The Nation of Islam and the FBI file** — her recent example of a contact event spawning a belief community and of institutional monitoring: early leader Elijah Robert Poole (Elijah Muhammad) believed UFOs would come to Earth to bring salvation to believers and punish nonbelievers; the US government took interest and the FBI established a file on him and his community.
- **Jung and Vallee as forerunners** — Carl Jung's 1950s book Flying Saucers: A Modern Myth of Things Seen in the Skies announced the saucer as symbol of a new global belief system; separately, referring to the modern phenomenon of flying saucers, Jung wrote, "We have here a golden opportunity of seeing how a legend is formed." Vallee's late-1960s Passport to Magonia found the same patterns in folklore, religious traditions, and modern UFO events.
- **Kenneth Arnold, 1947** — historians usually date the flying saucer's emergence to the start of the Cold War and pilot Kenneth Arnold's sighting of nine flat, saucerlike discs over Mount Rainier in 1947; Pasulka sides with Vallee that the phenomenon is thousands of years old, while granting the modern cultural framework began around 1947.
- **The videographer and the two-sentence quote** — a videographer for a well-known production company asked one of the scientists for a two-sentence quote; the scientist traced the contact information back to Pasulka (the "reluctant bridge") and phoned her in disgust: he refused to condense twenty years of research on "the most profound topic in human history" into a sound bite consumed "with their TV dinner." Pasulka says such interchanges, witnessed often, reveal the chasm between those studying the phenomenon and its media representations — which nonetheless create and sustain the belief.

- "I don't give a damn. I don't care. It's not going to make a difference. It's not going to change the reality of what I know." — Robert Bigelow to Lara Logan on 60 Minutes, on the risk of publicly professing belief in UFOs and aliens.

We call these 'religions.'" — Kripal, on contact events becoming religions.
- "There is a lot of arrogance in the assumption that I am supposed to condense twenty years of research into the most profound topic in human history into a two-sentence sound bite to be broadcast out to the public so they can consume it with their TV dinner. No thanks." — one of Pasulka's anonymous scientists, refusing a media request routed through her.

## Thread
This unit is the book's thesis statement and methodological charter. It establishes the two revelations that structure everything that follows — elite scientist-believers exist (the Invisible College / parallel tradition), and public UFO media barely resembles their findings yet manufactures the belief — and installs the religious-studies frame: bracket truth claims, track contact events, interpretive communities, institutional shaping, and the mechanisms of belief, with technology as both infrastructure and sacred object of a religion-in-formation. It narrates Pasulka's own entry point (purgatory archives → orbs → conference → meta-experiencers → epistemological shock), positioning her as embedded participant rather than aloof observer, and flags her role as "reluctant bridge" between scientists and media. It sets up the anonymous scientist informants (the "nuts and bolts" artifact analyst prefigures the field cases to come), the Vallee/Hynek lineage she works within, and the closing paradox — "it is both" real and imaginary — that the numbered chapters and the Conclusion will elaborate.
