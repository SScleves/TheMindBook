# A Model of Reality and Time Probable Reality Surfaces The Simulated Probable Future Real-time, Our System, State Vectors, and History

## In one paragraph
Thomas Campbell lays out the computational machinery he says underlies "real-time" in PMR (Physical Matter Reality): The Big Computer (TBC) captures the complete "state vector" of Our System (OS) at every quantum of PMR time (DELTA-t), then runs a much faster internal simulation clock (delta-t) to project M successive probable future states of OS before the next actual DELTA-t tick occurs. These probable futures form a "probable reality surface" — a landscape of probability peaks spread across simulated future time — that is recomputed and re-saved after every real DELTA-t increment. Campbell nests this same architecture recursively (OS within NPMRN within NPMR within AUM), each outer loop running on a smaller, more fundamental time quantum than the loop it contains, with AUM's own clock — "the clock on the computer room wall" — as the ultimate reference. He then argues this model reconciles reliable prediction of the future with free will (probable is not actual until free will actualizes it), explains why group/national/planetary futures are more predictable than individual ones (larger systems have more "inertia"), and explains how NPMRN beings can lawfully "nudge" some of those probability peaks without violating the system's rule-sets.

## Core claims
- Thomas Campbell claims TBC captures, at every DELTA-t, a complete "state vector" of OS: all objects and energy in the universe plus every significant choice available to every relevant sentient being at that moment.
- He claims TBC then computes, iteration by iteration (m = 1 to M), a sequential set of probable future OS states, each iteration's output becoming the next iteration's input, using history-based expectation values (probabilities) derived from an accumulated "history file" of past choices.
- He claims this predictive sub-simulation must run on its own, much smaller time quantum (delta-t, an NPMRN time base) than the PMR quantum (DELTA-t) it is trying to predict, because it has to model dynamics in finer detail than the real-time increment it forecasts.
- He claims the OS/DELTA-t loop (Text Box 5.1) has five steps: increment DELTA-t; make free-will/material/energy choices, generating a new state vector; compare the new state vector to the previous and to the predicted one; record actualized changes and improve predictive algorithms; and calculate M sequential probable future states via the delta-t sub-loop — then repeat.
- He claims this nesting is recursive and generalizes upward: delta-t is itself "a quantum of time in NPMRN" (NPMRN's own fundamental tick), and since NPMR is an outer loop to NPMRN, NPMR must run on a quantum smaller still than delta-t — mirroring how the OS DELTA-t loop needs the smaller delta-t to forecast OS's future, the "NPMRN delta-t loop" likewise needs a quantum smaller than delta-t to forecast NPMRN's own future — with AUM's own fundamental clock ("the clock on the computer room wall," as far as Campbell can tell) ultimately controlling NPMR's outer loop.
- He claims predictions of Our System's probable state grow less accurate the further out in time they project, but that because TBC and its software are so capable, it can nonetheless progress the model of PMR time out for many years (M can be arbitrarily large) in less than a nano-nanosecond.
- He claims the result of all this iterative computation is visualizable as a "PMR space-time event surface" — a probable reality surface — with simulated time on the horizontal plane and probability (expectation value) on the vertical axis; peaks near the present are typically sharp and tall, peaks far out are usually flatter and more diffuse, though occasional tall, well-formed peaks (>.8 expectation value) can occur far out in time.
- He claims TBC also computes the rate of change of these expectation values across the surface as a function of DELTA-t, and that because this has been tracked over many DELTA-t increments, the history of how the probable reality surface has actually changed over real-time is now known — this "sensitivity of our probability functions to perturbations" is then used to compute better, more accurate probable realities.
- He claims that as real-time advances, the probable reality surface is "consumed" at the origin (t = 0, "the pinhole") while a new outer ring is continuously added at the far edge, keeping the disk's radius constant at M•(DELTA-t); every actualized present state is saved into a permanent history file.
- He claims narrow, tall, stable, growing peaks make good, reliable predictions of future events, but that tapping into this "database of probable futures" only appears to support predestination — free will is still required to convert a probable event into an actual one.
- He claims group/organizational/national/planetary probable futures can be computed as a weighted vector summation of the probable futures of the individuals who affect that group, and that this information is accessible by "query-intent" through what he calls the RWW (reality-wide-web) net.
- He claims larger systems have more "inertia" (are less perturbable by any one individual's free will or by small random fluctuations), so their probable-reality surfaces are more slowly varying, more stable, and therefore more reliably predictable than an individual's — which he says is why a nation's or the planet's future is easier to predict than one person's, and why events with a higher "signal to noise ratio" on the RWW net are accessible to more people (explaining why so many people claim knowledge of coming "earth changes").
- He claims the computational burden on TBC, while large, is finite and manageable because most possible choices produce degenerate (identical) outcomes, only a relative few individuals significantly influence outcomes at any time, large subsets of beings/energy/objects (e.g., Earth-relevant calculations versus extraterrestrial ones) can be treated as functionally independent until they interact, and PMR's own rule-set (physical "laws," e.g., "things never fall up") further constrains the possibility space.
- He claims OS must be defined to include not just PMR beings but also NPMRN residents whose free-will choices manipulate, lead, predispose, or "nudge" PMR awareness — meaning some of the large stable probability peaks on the surface exist because they are encouraged/manipulated by NPMRN residents with bigger-picture perspective and better information, rather than arising purely on their own; most peaks, he says, are a mixture of both self-arising and guided.
- He claims all OS changes must abide simultaneously by the PMR space-time rule-set, the NPMRN rule-set, the rules governing PMR/NPMRN interaction, and what he calls the "psi uncertainty principle."
- He frames PMR's own history as, from a larger vantage point, merely a subset of the overall history of OS — analogous to how European history is a subset of world history.

## Concepts
- **OS (Our System)** — Campbell's term for our collective "Big Picture" local reality: PMR plus the portion of NPMRN that interacts with and influences it. Defined explicitly in this unit to include NPMRN residents whose choices impact PMR beings, "by definition" making that interaction part of OS.
- **PMR (Physical Matter Reality)** — used throughout as our physical, embodied reality; described here as a subset of OS, and OS's real-time is said to progress incrementally via DELTA-t rather than continuously.
- **NPMRN** — the non-physical reality "near" our system (the immediate outer-loop reality of which OS/PMR is a part); it runs on a smaller/faster fundamental time quantum (delta-t) than PMR's DELTA-t and functions as an outer controlling loop to PMR.
- **NPMR** — a still-larger non-physical reality that is an outer loop to NPMRN; per Campbell it must run on an even smaller fundamental time quantum than NPMRN's.
- **AUM** — referenced here as the ultimate consciousness/system whose "fundamental clock is the clock on the computer room wall," and which (per Campbell's inference, "as far as I can tell") directly controls NPMR's outer loop.
- **TBC (The Big Computer)** — Campbell's model for the computational substrate of NPMRN that captures OS's state vector at each DELTA-t, computes probable-future simulations, and stores results; described as clever software that updates rather than recomputes from scratch, and as having no practical constraint on computational resources.
- **EBC (Even Bigger Computer)** — mentioned only by name here as a concept discussed "at the end of Chapter 11," representing a bigger-picture computational frame beyond TBC.
- **State vector / reality state vector ("state")** — Campbell's term for the total description/specification of all existing choices, things, and energy that defines OS (or NPMRN, etc.) at the end of a given DELTA-t; the fundamental data object TBC computes, compares, and saves.
- **DELTA-t** — the PMR/OS quantum of real time; the increment by which our sense of continuous time actually progresses discretely, and the unit around which the OS DELTA-t loop is built.
- **delta-t** — a much smaller NPMRN time quantum used internally by TBC's predictive sub-simulation to model OS's dynamics in enough detail to forecast each of the M probable future states between actual DELTA-t increments; "the fundamental quantum of OS simulation time."
- **OS DELTA-t loop** — the five-step iterative process (Text Box 5.1): increment DELTA-t → make free-will/material/energy choices (new state vector) → compare new state to previous/predicted → record actualized changes and refine predictive algorithms → calculate M sequential probable future states via the delta-t sub-loop → repeat.
- **NPMRN delta-t loop** — the analogous but faster nested loop one level up, in which NPMRN's own free-will choices and states are generated and used to simulate NPMRN's probable futures, running on a fundamental quantum smaller than delta-t itself (delta-t being NPMRN's own tick) — mirroring how OS's DELTA-t loop runs on the smaller delta-t.
- **Surface rate-of-change / sensitivity to perturbations** — Campbell's claim that TBC computes not only each expectation value but its rate of change over successive DELTA-t, building a running history of how the probable reality surface itself changes over real-time; this "sensitivity of our probability functions to perturbations" is fed back in to make future probable-reality calculations more accurate.
- **Probable reality surface / future probable reality surface (Figure 5-2)** — Campbell's visualization of TBC's output: a planar event surface with simulated time (t = 0 to M•DELTA-t) on the horizontal axis and probability/expectation value on the vertical axis; imagined as a circular disk with t = 0 at the center, made of M concentric DELTA-t-wide rings, continuously consumed at the center ("the pinhole") and regrown at the outer edge as real-time advances.
- **Event surface** — used synonymously with the probable reality surface; the PMR space-time surface generated in TBC's calculation-space representing all probable outcomes.
- **Expectation value** — Campbell's term for a computed probability/likelihood value attached to a given possible event or state on the probable reality surface; peaks above roughly 0.8 are cited as an example of a "sizable" far-out prediction.
- **History file** — the accumulated record of past actualized OS states (and past behaviors relative to similar choices) that TBC uses to compute likelihoods of what will happen next, and to which every new actualized present state is appended.
- **RWW (reality-wide-web) net** — Campbell's term for the network/access layer through which probable-future information (for individuals or for a specifically queried group) is available to sufficiently attuned perceivers, described via the mock address "RWW.NPMRN.OS.PMR/probable-reality-database/specific-event/specific-intent," accessible "if your intrinsic noise level is low enough."
- **Psi uncertainty principle** — named as one of several constraints (alongside the PMR rule-set, the NPMRN rule-set, and PMR/NPMRN interaction rules) that all changes within OS must abide by; not further defined within this unit.
- **Degenerate choices** — Campbell's term for the large fraction of possible choices/interactions that produce the same (functionally identical) result, letting TBC "quickly dispense with" them and reduce computational burden.
- **Manipulated choice** — Campbell's term for the mechanism by which NPMRN residents aware of PMR nudge, lead, predispose, or manipulate PMR awareness/choices so that certain probable outcomes become more likely than others; explicitly counted as part of the OS calculation-space input data.
- **Group futures / query-intent** — the process of computing a probable reality surface restricted to a particular group (family, tribe, organization, corporation, nation, culture, planet, fault line slip, endangered species, rain forest, football team, human race) by summing/filtering all relevant choices and impacts, analogous to a database query whose parameters are set by the querier's intent.
- **Inertia (of large systems)** — Campbell's analogy for why bigger systems (nations, the planet) resist perturbation by any single individual's free will or by small random fluctuations, making their probable-reality surfaces more stable and more reliably predictable than an individual's.
- **Signal-to-noise ratio (on the RWW net)** — Campbell's explanation for why information about large, stable, slowly changing future events (e.g., planetary-scale events) is more clearly/widely accessible to more people than fast-fluctuating individual futures.
- **Text Box 5.1 (The OS/DELTA-t Loop)** — the five-numbered-step diagram described in-text summarizing the loop mechanics (see OS DELTA-t loop above).
- **Figure 5-2 (Future Probable Reality Surface)** — the referenced illustration of the probable reality surface as a peaked landscape over simulated time, referenced three times in the running text (plus its own caption) as a visual aid.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
This unit is primarily a technical/model-building chapter rather than a field-report chapter, so its "cases" are Campbell's own worked thought-experiments and illustrative analogies:
- **The OS/DELTA-t Loop walkthrough (Text Box 5.1)** — Campbell walks step-by-step through one full cycle: DELTA-t increments; free-will/material/energy choices create a new OS state vector; TBC compares the new vector to the previous and to the predicted vector; actualized changes are recorded and predictive algorithms/databases updated; TBC then runs the delta-t sub-loop to generate M sequential probable future states — illustrating concretely how "real-time" and "probable-future computation" interleave.
- **The probable reality surface as a circular disk (Figure 5-2)** — Campbell offers the specific geometric image of a disk with t = 0 at the center and t increasing radially outward to M•(DELTA-t) at the rim, made of M concentric rings each one DELTA-t wide; as real time passes, the center ring is "sucked" into the pinhole (becomes the actualized present, saved to history) while a new ring is added at the outer edge — used to show how the same finite "window" of probable future is continuously regenerated and shifted forward.
- **The 0.8-expectation-value far-out peak example** — Campbell notes that even far out in simulated time, occasional "well-formed and sizable (> .8 expectation value)" peaks can occur, used as a concrete illustrative case for how a confident long-range prediction is possible without breaking his framework's logic.
- **Earth vs. extraterrestrial calculation independence** — offered as a concrete case of "functionally independent" subsets: Campbell states Earth-relevant calculations can proceed as an independent computational set "until interaction with specific extraterrestrials (ETs) from elsewhere in our universe occurs," and the same holds symmetrically for the ETs — used to argue TBC's total computational burden is reduced by such independence.
- **"Things never fall up"** — used as a plain example of a PMR rule-set constraint (law of physics) that further limits the space of possible outcomes TBC must evaluate.
- **List of possible "group" queries** — family, tribe, organization, corporation, nation, culture, planet, fault line slip, endangered species, rain forest, football team, human race — given as concrete instances of the kinds of group-level probable-reality queries the RWW/query-intent mechanism can support.
- **"Know anybody who claims knowledge of future earth changes? By the hundreds!"** — Campbell's rhetorical/anecdotal aside citing the (unnamed, uncounted) prevalence of self-proclaimed predictors of planetary future events as evidence for his claim that large-group/planetary probability signals have high signal-to-noise and are broadly accessible.
- **European history as a subset of world history** — an explicit analogy Campbell uses to characterize how PMR's history relates to OS's overall history from "a larger perspective."
- **The mock RWW address** — "RWW.NPMRN.OS.PMR/probable-reality-database/specific-event/specific-intent" is given as a literal (if metaphorical) example URL/access-path format for retrieving probable-future information via the reality-wide-web.
