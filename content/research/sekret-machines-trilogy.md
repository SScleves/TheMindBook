Three nonfiction books by Tom DeLonge and Peter Levenda — *Sekret Machines: Gods* (2017), *Sekret Machines: Man* (2018), and *Sekret Machines: War* (2024) — make a single, cumulative argument about what the authors call **the Phenomenon**: the recurring, cross-historical complex of sightings, contact, anomalous communication, apparent violations of physical law, trauma, and institutional confusion usually discussed under the narrower word "UFO."

This dossier is a **map of the argument**, not an endorsement of it. Everything below reports what the authors claim. None of it is a verified account of alien contact, and the trilogy's framework is not established ontology. The point of reading the three books together is not to be persuaded but to see clearly **how the argument is built** — which claims rest on documents, which on interpretation, and which the authors themselves flag as speculation.

A note on the individual books before the synthesis: each has its own detailed map, glossary, and Q&A in the Library. This dossier assumes you may not have read them and gives the shape of the whole first.

## The one-sentence argument, and why it takes three books

The trilogy's spine can be stated in a sentence: **a non-human intelligence has been in contact with humanity since before recorded history; that contact is the hidden source of religion, is written into human biology and consciousness, and is now the central — and most secret — problem of national security.**

That single claim spans three disciplines that modern knowledge keeps apart: **history and religion**, **science**, and **statecraft**. The authors argue that the compartmentalization itself is the problem — that "since Newton" we have been trained to study these domains in isolation, which is precisely why a Phenomenon that touches all three at once has been so hard to see. So they split the argument the way the culture splits knowledge, and ask the reader to reassemble it.

Each volume is a different **lens** on the same object. *Gods* is the historical-religious lens; *Man* is the scientific lens; *War* is the national-security lens. The authors are explicit that this division is **methodologically artificial** — the spiritual, scientific, and security dimensions "cannot really be separated" — and that the lenses are meant to be held together, not ranked against one another.

## What each volume contributes that the others do not

### Gods — the ancient record and the interpretive method

*Gods* supplies the trilogy's **foundation and its vocabulary**. Its governing claim is that **religion is humanity's oldest record of contact**: the gods, scriptures, rituals, and monuments of the ancient world preserve, in the only vocabulary those cultures possessed, a real and recurring encounter. Its boldest historical proposal is that ten thousand or more years of civilization may itself be a **Cargo Cult** — the ritual afterlife of an original contact the authors name the *ur-punkt*, or "Alien Genesis."

Two things are unique to this volume. First, the **premise**: *Gods* states openly that it will not try to prove UFOs are real ("not the belief, the understanding"); it *assumes* contact and reads history through that lens. Everything the later books build depends on that starting move. Second, the **interpretive discipline** the whole trilogy inherits — the practice, established in the chapter "Alien Genesis," of tagging each claim as documentary source, textual interpretation, author inference, or speculation, and keeping those layers visibly apart. Its most-repeated device is the **reversed-conjuration thought experiment**: if a magician's summoning circle is inverted, the flying saucer becomes the *other* world's ritual device and humans become the summoned spirits — a suggestion the authors label "outrageous" and explicitly disclaim as true, using it only to open consciousness-centered hypotheses.

### Man — the biology and the discipline of separating evidence

*Man* takes the **scientific** half of former senator Harry Reid's formula ("this is about science and national security") and asks what the Phenomenon's reported *biological, neurological, and technological* effects imply — about the Visitors and about what humanity is becoming. Its organizing reversal is to stop assuming that biological aliens pilot advanced machines and ask whether **the pilots are themselves machines**, and whether humans — gene-driven "survival machines," or divinely "manufactured" creatures — are already a kind of machine too.

What is unique to *Man* is not any single conclusion but a **method under maximum stress**. The volume runs three scientific lenses — genetics, consciousness, and human-machine technology — and its central intellectual demand is to **keep evidence classes distinct**, refusing to let an accumulation of examples make different kinds of claim look equivalent. It is the book that most rigorously separates a reported *experience* from its *interpretation* (via Hynek's "cardinal mistake"), *cited theory* from the authors' *extrapolation*, and *documented human programs* (MKULTRA, the cyborg-for-space research, remote viewing) from *alien speculation*. It even opens its most dangerous idea — an informant's suggestion that some genocides eliminated "alien-resistant" genes — first, precisely to mark it as speculation before proceeding.

### War — the strategy and the correction of the conspiracy story

*War* closes the trilogy with the **national-security** lens. Its governing claim is that the Phenomenon has been captured entirely by a single frame — war and the calculations of the battlefield — because the Alien "reacts to our weapons systems, not to our culture." Its most important and most distinctive move is a **correction of the standard conspiracy narrative**: seventy years of official strangeness, the authors argue, is best explained "not as a conspiracy against their own citizens" but as **helplessness in the face of the inexplicable** — technological states each guarding "slender pieces of the overall puzzle," which makes a coordinated century-long global cover-up wildly improbable.

*War* also does the sorting the other two books defer: it is where the trilogy promises to separate **secret human technology** (Paperclip science, stealth craft) from **genuine historical anomaly**. And it supplies the trilogy's darkest register — the Foucauldian image of humanity as prisoners in a **panopticon**, watched and "corrected" by an unseen master of the tower — and its recommended posture: threat analysis without presumed hostility, "I Want to Know" in place of "I Want to Believe."

## Shared premises versus later extrapolations

The three books rest on a small set of **shared premises**, then extend outward into progressively bolder territory. Distinguishing the two is the single most useful thing a reader can do with the trilogy.

**The shared premises** — the load-bearing floor beneath all three volumes:

- **The Phenomenon is real and its reality is assumed, not proven.** All three books take this as a starting point rather than a conclusion. *Gods* says so outright; *Man* and *War* inherit it.
- **The Phenomenon is dual — physical *and* consciousness-related — and must be studied as both.** This is the trilogy's methodological signature: neither the "nuts and bolts" nor the paranormal aspect can be filtered out.
- **Cultural vocabulary shapes the report, not the event.** The same encounter appears as a god, an angel, a demon, a jinn, a witch's Sabbat, or a Gray depending on who perceives it — a claim first developed in *Gods*, restated in *Man*, and carried into *War*.
- **Government "disclosure" is the wrong thing to wait for.** Across all three, official acknowledgment is treated as strategically unlikely and conceptually confused, because no state controls the contact it would be disclosing.

**The later extrapolations** — the claims that reach well beyond that floor, and that the authors variously flag as inference or speculation:

- That myths preserve *literal* memories of non-human beings creating, teaching, and interbreeding with humans (*Gods*' paleocontact inference, layered on top of the documentary reading of the texts).
- That trained **consciousness** is the control interface of the craft (*Gods*), that **DNA is the source of consciousness** (*Man*'s explicit inference, not a cited result), and that **quantum entanglement** could support alien telepathy and persistent "tagging" (*Man*, guarded by an explicit false-equivalence warning).
- That the genetic code carries a designed **"signature"** (directed panspermia and "Biological SETI" in *Man*), and that blood sacrifice encodes a **genetic debt** to absent creators (*Gods*).
- That the craft are **"training" rather than probing** us, and that humanity exists in a **symbiosis** with an alien that "needs" us (*War* and *Man* respectively) — claims the notes flag as asserted more strongly than the evidence supplies.

The reader who keeps the premises and the extrapolations on separate shelves will find the trilogy far more legible — and far easier to argue with — than one who lets them blur.

## The claim/evidence ladder

The trilogy's real difficulty is not that it makes extraordinary claims; it is that it braids **seven different kinds of claim** together in the same paragraphs. A declassified document, a settled scientific mechanism, a contested scholarly reading, and an explicitly "outrageous" thought experiment can appear within a page of one another, all in the same confident prose. The books mostly mark the transitions; a careful reader re-marks them.

The lesson of the ladder is not that the higher rungs are worthless — a well-flagged "what if" can be a legitimate research prompt. It is that the rungs **carry different weight and require different standards**, and that the trilogy's rhetorical momentum tends to lift a claim from a lower rung to a higher one without the reader noticing. When *Man* moves from "DNA builds neurons" (established mechanism) to "DNA must be where consciousness comes from" (author claim), or when *Gods* moves from the *Enuma Elish* tablets (documented record) to "the texts record real non-human contact" (author claim), the sentence gets more confident while the evidence does not. Catching that shift is the whole skill of reading the trilogy well.

## Internal tensions and unresolved questions

The trilogy is not internally seamless, and its own notes flag the seams. Naming them is not a refutation — it is what honest reading of the argument requires.

**Conspiracy versus helplessness.** *War*'s headline correction — secrecy as institutional helplessness, a coordinated global cover-up as "improbable" — sits uneasily beside chapters (in both *War* and *Gods*) that trace dense clandestine networks: Puharich's "Nine" threading into elite intelligence and assassination history, the Maison Rouge Nazi diaspora, the deep-politics milieu. The authors try to hold this by rejecting an international cabal while insisting the cross-national parallelism "does not make sense" without a shared threat, but the two registers pull against each other.

**The real alien versus the constructed alien.** *Man*'s Section Three asserts that the alien is *real* while simultaneously arguing that nearly every available image of it is socially constructed, anthropocentric projection. The closing "symbiosis" thesis and "the alien needs us" claim resolve this by fiat rather than by evidence; the notes mark them as stronger than the material supports.

**Assumed premise versus demonstrated conclusion.** Because *Gods* assumes contact rather than proving it, the entire edifice is a **reinterpretation** built on a granted premise. That is a legitimate move — the authors are candid about it — but it means the trilogy's persuasiveness is largely a function of whether the reader grants the premise in the first place. A skeptic who declines it will find every subsequent inference optional.

**Inference stacked on inference.** The boldest claims are frequently *layered*: paleocontact (an inference) supports the reading of ascent ritual as contact technology (a further inference), which supports consciousness-as-interface (a speculation). Each layer is individually flagged, but the cumulative structure can feel like established scaffolding when it is a stack of "if"s.

**Genuinely open questions the trilogy leaves standing:** Is the Phenomenon extraterrestrial, "ultraterrestrial" (hidden on Earth), interdimensional, a control system, or a projection of collective consciousness? Are the Visitors biological, machine, or cyborg? Is the observed behavior benign, hostile, indifferent, or "training"? The authors decline to settle any of these, and treat the refusal to settle them as intellectually honest rather than evasive.

## How the three lenses reinforce — and check — each other

Read together, the volumes are meant to be **mutually reinforcing**, and in places they genuinely check one another:

- *Gods* provides the deep-time claim that the same Phenomenon recurs across millennia; *Man* asks whether that recurrence could be written into biology and consciousness; *War* asks how a state should respond to something that has watched it for that long.
- The **Cargo Cult** analogy runs through all three: incomprehensible technology becomes religion (*Gods*), becomes a question about whether we are ourselves manufactured machines (*Man*), becomes a strategic problem of a "training" presence we cannot fathom (*War*).
- The figure of **Jack Parsons** and the **Nine** contact circle appear in all three, binding rocketry, the occult, the security state, and the consciousness question into one braided history.

But the lenses can also be used to **check** the argument. *Man*'s hard discipline of separating evidence classes is exactly the tool that exposes where *Gods* lets paleocontact inference ride on documentary reading. *War*'s "helplessness, not conspiracy" correction is exactly what undercuts the more paranoid readings the trilogy sometimes flirts with. The books are strongest when read as three disciplined perspectives that constrain one another, and weakest when read as three confirmations of a single foregone conclusion.

## What the trilogy establishes / what it does not

### What it establishes (within its own frame)

- That the world's origin myths, scriptures, and rituals contain a **recurring structure** — non-human creators and teachers, hybrids, celestial ascent, blood debt, monumental technologies of immortality — that can be laid side by side and compared.
- That a **real experience and measurable trauma** underlie a class of modern contact reports (Mack's PTSD finding), and that some close-contact witnesses suffered **documented physical injury**.
- That **governments and intelligence agencies** did in fact fund consciousness, mind-control, and remote-viewing research, and did handle UFOs across hostile regimes in strikingly parallel ways — documented in declassified files.
- That popular ancient-astronaut *particulars* (Sitchin's translations, alien pyramid-builders, the *Vymanika Shastra*) are **weakly supported** and can be set aside without abandoning the broader question.

### What it does not establish

- It does **not** demonstrate that any myth is a literal record of an extraterrestrial event, that the gods were physically present astronauts, or that the Phenomenon is verified alien contact of any kind.
- It does **not** prove that consciousness controls the craft, that DNA is the source of consciousness, that the genetic code carries a designed message, that quantum entanglement supports telepathy, or that humanity exists in a symbiosis with the alien — all are flagged as inference or speculation.
- It does **not** identify the Phenomenon's nature, origin, or intentions; the authors insist these can only be inferred, and reject waiting for "disclosure."
- It does **not** endorse its own most provocative analogies — reversed conjuration, the panopticon, planned obsolescence, the genocide hypothesis — as fact; they are interpretive instruments.

The trilogy is, on its own terms, a **framework and an invitation** rather than a proof: it assembles the ancient record (*Gods*), the scientific questions (*Man*), and the strategic stakes (*War*), models a discipline for keeping levels of claim apart, and asks the reader to hold the historical, the physical, and the political dimensions of an unexplained Phenomenon together — while judging each claim by the rung of the ladder it actually stands on.
