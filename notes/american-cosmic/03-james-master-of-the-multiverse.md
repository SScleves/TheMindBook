# James: Master of the Multiverse

## In one paragraph
This chapter introduces Pasulka's second key informant, "James" — a pseudonymous, Silicon Valley-flavored scientist who holds an endowed chair in molecular biology at one of the world's top universities and who is, in her words, the first scientist she met who was also "out" as an experiencer. Pasulka traces his biography of anomalous encounters (childhood night visitors, a soundless light formation over a paper route, a translucent presence in his thirties), his recognition of himself in John Mack's *Abduction*, and his risky public "outing" via a televised artifact analysis, which brought two grim, fully human "men in black" — and then serious researchers, including Jacques Vallee — to his door. She presents James's scientific program: the phenomenon is real, it interacts with humans through a genetically determined "brain receiver" possibly tapping a quantum field of information, and its contact leaves a traceable physiological signature — which James hopes to reverse into an "antidote" giving humans the ability to say no. The chapter culminates in the trip to the New Mexico crash site, where James digs up an anomalous foil-like artifact whose analysis, months later, suggests it could not have been made on Earth in 1947 — or perhaps anywhere. Pasulka closes by reading the episode through religious studies: credible witnesses attesting to an unexplainable object mirror the origins of Christianity and Buddhism, the site functions as a sacred site of a new religion of the UFO and of technology, and she reflects on how interpretation, media, and information operations create and sustain the myth she has now inadvertently entered.

## Core claims
- Pasulka presents James's own core assertion, delivered at the conference: the phenomenon commonly called UFOs exists, interacts with humans, cannot yet be explained, but its effects on humans and its physical channels of communication can be identified and studied scientifically.
- The book relates James's theory that the ability to contact or be contacted by nonhuman intelligence is likely genetically determined: since genes define bodily architectures, genes would underlie the components of a "brain receiver" for such information — and, because genetics is familial, experiencers run in families (like his own).
- James, per Pasulka, holds that once the phenomenon contacts a human it leaves a physical, physiological, traceable "signature" that the tools of science can identify and trace back to its molecular roots.
- Unable to explain how some subjects have knowledge of events they should not have, James (the book says) relies on quantum theory — "spooky action at a distance" — to suggest there may be a quantum field of information his subjects somehow tap into.
- Pasulka reports that contact is not always welcome: James repeatedly used the word "bedeviled" for people tormented by contact events, and his personal mission is to develop a "medicine," an antidote, shifting the relationship 180 degrees so humans gain the right and ability to say "no" — contact currently happens on "their" terms, not ours.
- James told Pasulka that behaviors of some of the things he studies would have been called demonic as recently as one hundred years ago; the phenomenon can also act "like a contagious agent," spreading from an affected individual to others in contact with them.
- Pasulka argues there is an exhaustively documented history of government "perception management" of UFO information — exemplified by the Durant Report on the 1953 Robertson Panel, which recommended a "training and debunking" mass-media campaign — so the phenomenon is usually portrayed inaccurately, and only serious researchers know this.
- Pasulka argues, from her religious-studies standpoint, that two eminently credible scientists attesting to truly unexplainable artifacts amounts to the testimony of credible witnesses — structurally the same as the founding documents of Christianity and Buddhism — and that in religious studies the unexplainable object or event would be a miracle.
- She argues the New Mexico sites function as sacred sites for a new religion — "the religion of the UFO event and, as I will argue, the religion of technology" — places of a hierophany where nonhuman beings descended and left a "donation."
- Pasulka claims UFO events and religious experiences share a key feature: neither begins as such; they *become* UFO events or religious experiences through interpretation (illustrated by Samuel and Eli, and by experiencers who initially reach for mundane explanations).
- The book claims that what we see on screens, if it conforms to certain criteria, is interpreted as real even when we know it is not; screen images embed in brain and memory and can shape past-view and future behavior — making the creation of a belief system far easier now than two thousand years ago. It is in the world of media that the UFO myth is created, sustained, and proliferates.
- Pasulka reports Vallee's position, as James summarizes it: the phenomenon seems infinitely variable, resists any single conclusion, and its overarching message appears to be "you are not alone" — styled to the level of the recipient's cultural understanding.
- She holds that James and Tyler are distinctive precisely because they refuse to conclude: they leave open even a human/military origin for the materials, and — "like Socrates" — show wisdom by admitting they do not know, while the "pure impossibility" of their findings motivates their unorthodox science.
- Pasulka concludes that regarding Roswell, both Jacobsen's account (secret military programs masked by the UFO carnival) and Corso's account (real crash debris seeded to industry) can be true on some level — she "tended to believe" Jacobsen's narrative while feeling she was "living within" Corso's.

## Concepts
- **the phenomenon** — The book's term for the UFO/contact reality James and the researchers study. James asserts it exists, interacts with humans, cannot yet be explained, but its effects and physical channels of communication can be identified.
- **experiencer** — A person who has anomalous contact experiences with the phenomenon. James and certain members of his extended family are lifelong experiencers; James was the first scientist Pasulka met who was "out" as one.
- **anomalous cognition** — Knowledge of future events or other knowledge for which there is no conventional explanation; one of the ways contact by nonhuman intelligence manifests, alongside telepathic communication with aerial objects and beings.
- **brain receiver / human interface** — James's theorized biological hardware for receiving anomalous information: since genes define tissue architecture, genes would underlie the components of a brain receiver; identifying where the information transfers and which molecules are involved begins "the long road towards identifying the human interface that is our connection to the phenomenon."
- **quantum field of information** — James's speculative source for anomalous cognition: drawing on quantum entanglement ("spooky action at a distance," where distant particles seem to know and affect each other), he suggests a quantum field of information that his subjects somehow tap into; anomalous cognition "starts on a level that is beyond the physical world of which we are aware," then transfers into human neuronal hardware as a "recognition."
- **signature** — The physical, physiological trace the phenomenon leaves once it contacts a human. Per James: "That signature is traceable... it is processed then in a world that tools of science can study. We can identify it."
- **heritable sixth sense** — James's claim that the ability to interface with the phenomenon is heritable like other brain-wiring-based intellectual traits: "It is assuredly a 6th sense that is associated with a material component we already possess"; hence the strategy of locating families where the trait is dominant.
- **bedeviled** — James's most-used word for people tormented by unwelcome contact events (alongside "harassed" and "bothered"); Pasulka notes "bedevil" means to torment or harass maliciously or diabolically, and James's mission is an antidote to the malicious contact event.
- **contagion** — James's observation that the phenomenon sometimes acts like a contagious agent: once attached to an individual, it can spread to others who come into contact with that person.
- **perception management** — Government programs to cover up and manage information about reported UFO events, exhaustively documented in declassified UK and US records; motivated by national security, fear of alarm, or simple ignorance of what UFOs are.
- **training and debunking** — The perception-management project recommended by the 1953 Robertson Panel (recorded in the Durant Report): a mass-media education campaign, enacted with academics and media moguls (the Jam Handy Co. and Walt Disney, Inc. were suggested), to control public knowledge about UFOs.
- **flap** — A series of sightings of UFOs by several individuals, or groups of individuals, over a span of a few days — a standard case-study unit for researchers of the phenomenon.
- **anomalous** — The researchers' term of art indicating that a phenomenon has been ruled out as known aircraft, drones, blimps, or military exercises, and that it often left physical traces such as burn marks on objects, grass — or people.
- **the artifact / donation** — Material associated with the phenomenon that can be studied: the foil-like fabric James dug up at the site, which his preliminary analyses suggested could not have been made on Earth in 1947 — or possibly anywhere. James, chuckling, called such material a "donation" left by nonhuman beings — "a gift or donation for us to figure out."
- **UFO as window** — Jacques Vallee's suggestion that the UFO might not be an object but a kind of "window" into another dimension; Pasulka extends the metaphor to ask whether the hardware of James's subjects is likewise physical yet conduit-like, "windowlike."
- **hierophany / sacred site** — Pasulka's religious-studies reading of the New Mexico sites: places where nonhuman beings descended to Earth and left a donation; they function as sacred sites for the new religion of the UFO event and of technology, with Roswell itself functioning as a pilgrimage site.
- **testimony of credible witnesses** — Pasulka's structural parallel: two credible scientists attesting to truly unexplainable artifacts matches the first written documents of Christianity and Buddhism; the Gospels are the testimonies of the apostles (Greek for "those who are sent," or "messengers"), and the unexplainable object/event corresponds to a miracle.
- **the Roswell myth** — The already-existing, "convoluted, mind-bending" myth of a 1947 alien crash at Roswell, which Pasulka realized she had inadvertently walked into a version of; she concluded she could not prevent her own story from folding into it.
- **weapon of information** — Pasulka's second idea of how the artifacts function: if the legend and artifacts covered up the truth of secret US military weapons development, then the legend itself is a weapon — "a weapon of information, like Kubrick's monolith."
- **information operations** — The military employment of media, social media, and all types of electronic media for national-security purposes; Pasulka was beginning to research virtual/digital media used politically under this rubric, and argues these media have played major roles in creating global belief in UFOs and extraterrestrials.
- **building-block approach** — Ann Taves's proposed framework for understanding how events become religious events, using disciplines such as cognitive science, sociology, and history to explain the processes by which people identify experiences as religious — or as UFO-related.
- **elves** — James's laughing name for the subconscious processes he assigns problems to before sleep; he lays out a problem's parts, asks the "elves" to work on it, and wakes with the answer — while wondering whether the information sometimes "comes from somewhere else."
- **Qi (Chi)** — The energetic force imparting information that Qian Xuesen believed in; Pasulka notes Qi's long history in Chinese and other Asian religious and philosophical traditions as a sacred life force that can be tapped with the right training.
- **atmospherics** — The Marines' training term (per Pasulka's student Jose) for a point man's knack for sensing IEDs or danger beyond observing physical surroundings; pragmatism, not belief, settled whether "the sense" existed.
- **unorthodox science** — Pasulka's label for the untold history of science done outside orthodox channels (Jacobsen's *Phenomena* on military remote-viewing/parapsychology being a recent overview); James reminds her the science motivated by the artifacts "is as real as what he does in his day job."
- **men in black** — The two grim visitors who knocked on James's office door after the artifact publicity; they turned out to be fully human — one a top researcher at a renowned university with long intelligence-agency ties, the other from a large aerospace firm — serious, non-public researchers of the phenomenon's radiation effects and biological interactions with humans.
- **religion of technology** — Named here in passing as Pasulka's forthcoming argument: the New Mexico sites are sacred sites "for a new religion, the religion of the UFO event and, as I will argue, the religion of technology."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **James's childhood night visitors (age five or six)** — Little people appeared in his room, standing by his bed or looking through his windows; he insists he was awake and paralyzed ("I was not asleep. Oh, and I was paralyzed."). His parents dismissed it as bad dreams; he knew the visitors were real.
- **The paper-route light formation (early teens)** — Racing to deliver Mr. Jameson's paper by 5:30 a.m., James cut through woods he'd always feared. A shapeless, completely soundless formation of lights, about twenty feet across, passed just above the treetops; he felt frozen in a "time warp of light, bright as the sun, with no apparent source except itself." It lasted under ten seconds. He kept the route but avoided those woods forever after.
- **The presence in his thirties** — Waking from sound sleep, James saw a tall, thin, smoky, translucent presence at the end of his bed; paralyzed but unafraid, he heard "Go to sleep" in his head — and did.
- **The window fear and the prank** — James developed a childhood fear of open windows that became a compulsion (he demanded his hotel room's un-closable, shade-less window be fixed). A close relative shares the fear; when teenage James pranked her by posting a drawn scary face on her window, she turned white and asked, "How did you know?" — a reaction he wondered about for years.
- **Reading John Mack's *Abduction: Human Encounters with Aliens*** — James, a science-fiction reader, first took the Harvard researcher's book for fiction, then found Mack's subjects described exactly his experiences: paralyzing night visitors who watched them sleep and spoke telepathically. By the end he realized he was "reading what amounted to the story of his life." Mack had claimed experiencers were psychologically healthy and the experiences common — a significant cultural development demanding serious scholarly attention.
- **The Durant Report / Robertson Panel (1953)** — US government committee on UFOs; concluded UFOs were not themselves a security risk but recommended "training and debunking": mass-media education via television, motion pictures, and popular articles built on case histories "puzzling at first but later explained," with the Jam Handy Co. and Walt Disney, Inc. suggested as producers and business clubs, schools, colleges, and TV stations expected to cooperate. Pasulka's exhibit A for perception management.
- **The televised artifact analysis** — To "out" himself and attract serious researchers, James offered to analyze a supposedly not-of-this-earth artifact held by public ufologists, filmed for a documentary watched by millions. His data pointed strongly to human origin; yet media hyped an alien finding, the public believed he had confirmed it, and even his corrective statements via credible science publications missed his actual point: he had ruled out "elsewhere" for this artifact without ruling out the reality of the phenomenon. Pasulka: the plan "proved to be a good idea, and a bad idea."
- **The visit of the "men in black"** — Two grim-faced men in black suits came to James's office asking what he "really found out" and why he got involved. James suspected he was being "punk'd" (he knew the TV show) and looked for cameras. Instead, one turned out to be a top university researcher with intelligence-agency ties, the other from a large aerospace firm; their research covered radiation effects and biological interactions of the phenomenon with humans. Over months they exposed him to a nontraditional path "as much a science" as his day job. "James had found his peers."
- **The conference presentation** — At Pasulka's small summer conference in the Northern California foothills (where James arrived late in a high-end roadster with red leather interior), his first slide showed a massive molecular microscope his own lab had built — "like a big, shiny, hospital CAT scanner." He then transcended the attendees' conservative baseline (psychological-projection vs. nonhuman-intelligence camps) by flatly asserting the phenomenon exists and laying out his receiver/signature research program.
- **Pasulka's cousin and the Marine's "atmospherics"** — Her law-enforcement cousin has abilities matching James's definition of anomalous cognition, which saved lives in dire situations. Her student Jose, a Marine author, wrote that seniors kept a point man with "a knack for finding IEDs or sensing things" on point — training called it atmospherics; pragmatism, not metaphysics, validated "the sense."
- **Srinivasa Ramanujan** — A poor boy from Tamil Nadu with only elementary mathematical training who became one of the most brilliant mathematicians of the early twentieth century (a journal is devoted to his still-being-understood ideas). He attributed his brilliance to the Hindu goddess Lakshmi whispering equations in his ear — an explanation that embarrassed his Oxford colleagues, but he never backed down.
- **Space-program founders and anomalous cognition** — Jack Parsons and Konstantin Tsiolkovsky both believed nonhuman intelligences sent humans symbolic messages that only some could perceive and translate into products; Tsiolkovsky suggested creative geniuses, scientists, and poets were the receivers. Qian Xuesen — the Chinese engineer who helped establish Parsons's Jet Propulsion Laboratory and China's ballistic missile program — believed in Qi as an information-imparting force. Tyler, unaware of this history, adopted his own physical "training" practices to tap a nonhuman intelligence.
- **James's creativity protocol** — Only graduate students ever ask him where his ideas come from. His process: lay out the problem's parts before bed, ask the subconscious "elves" to work on it while he sleeps, and wake with the answer (or have it pop into his head within days, usually just after waking). He believes creativity can be trained and the process should be studied — and wonders whether the information sometimes comes "from somewhere else," possibly related to brain structure and the phenomena. Scientist friends similarly report learning things in sleep "as if they travel to some place." Pasulka links this to recent creativity research showing identity-related brain regions shut down during creative acts, causing attribution to an external agent — but notes the physical artifacts James and Tyler study seem to solidify the external agent as real, not imaginary.
- **The New Mexico site trip and the artifact find** — The site of a largely forgotten 1947 New Mexico crash — explicitly *not* the Roswell event — with some eyewitnesses, one of whom (a child at the time) Tyler knows. Tyler warned of the site's "energy": people inevitably get into fights there (last time, the eyewitness and a visiting scientist nearly came to blows over nobody-knew-what); "The place will work on you over time." After arriving blindfolded, James's metal detector flagged something between rocks on the multi-acre site; after long digging he retrieved material looking like crumpled tin foil that was also a type of fabric, clumped with dirt. Months later his preliminary analyses made it "hard to believe they were made on Earth" — perhaps not makeable anywhere, certainly not on Earth in 1947. James couldn't understand how he found one structured object on a multi-acre site; his "side interest and hobby now took on a very different flavor." Pasulka's setup theory weakened but never disappeared; James noted it didn't matter to him whether the materials were planted — they potentially substantiated that something material associated with the phenomenon could be studied.
- **Pasulka's night prayer** — Hearing from James (not just Tyler) that the phenomenon can spread contagiously, Pasulka prayed that night for protection — "from what, I wasn't sure. But I prayed."
- **The Roswell UFO Festival** — Written during the annual four-day festival expected to attract over fifty thousand people, with social-media pages full of alien-costumed pet event photos: "It is a carnival." Evidence that Roswell functions as a pilgrimage site for believers and would-be believers.
- **Jacobsen vs. Corso on Roswell** — Annie Jacobsen's *Area 51* (journalist; affirms only top-secret military programs, no UFO reality) versus Philip Corso's *The Day After Roswell* (insists a real New Mexico crash occurred and that his job was disseminating alien debris to private industry under a Chinese/Russian-technology cover story for reverse-engineering). Pasulka tended to believe Jacobsen but felt she was living within Corso's narrative — and decided, prompted by Tyler, that on some level both were true. Tyler's maxim: the best place to conceal the truth is in a mess of confusion; the UFO carnival is a good cover for covert activity.
- **Samuel and Eli** — In the Book of Samuel, young Samuel hears his name called at night three times, each time assuming it is his teacher Eli; only when Eli interprets it as a calling from God does the experience become religious, conferring prophet status. Pasulka's illustration that experiences become religious (or UFO) events through interpretation.
- **The invisible gorilla experiment** — Christopher Chabris (Harvard) and Daniel Simons (University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign), in *The Invisible Gorilla*, showed subjects videos of people passing a basketball and asked them to count passes; a person in a gorilla suit walked slowly through, and half the subjects did not notice. Pasulka's example that we are trained from childhood on how to see and what not to see.
- **2001: A Space Odyssey monolith** — The film's opening: losing hominids wake to find the monolith, which inspires the first tool of war and catalyzes human evolution and dominance. Pasulka: "Arthur C. Clarke's insight is compelling: not all ideas are benign gifts" — and the Roswell legend may likewise be a weapon of information "like Kubrick's monolith."
- **Vallee's and Haraway's warnings** — Vallee told Pasulka emphatically, "Trust no one. Do not even trust what you see." Years earlier Donna Haraway had asked her what happens in the brain/mind when watching film images or witnessing apparitions of the Virgin Mary; Vallee's warning made Haraway's question click, feeding Pasulka's research into media manipulation and information operations.
- **The Katy Perry gala** — James emailed Pasulka pictures of himself dancing with pop star Katy Perry at a Los Angeles hills charity gala — yet, she writes, he "would have traded a thousand dances with Katy Perry" for the invitation to the New Mexico site.

## Thread
This chapter completes the book's pair of pseudonymous scientist-experiencers: where the earlier Tyler D. material presents an intuitive practitioner who taps creative ideas via bodily "protocols" but cannot explain the process, James is "the consummate professor" who can articulate a formula — anomalous cognition deriving from a possibly nonmaterial information field and translating into human hardware. It grounds Pasulka's larger claims: the human as biological "receiver" of the phenomenon (via James's brain-receiver/quantum-field theory), the "artifact" as studiable material trace (via the site find, prefiguring the Conclusion, "The Artifact"), and the announced thesis of the religion of technology (the New Mexico sites as sacred sites and hierophany). It also engages her chief theorist, Jacques Vallee (UFO-as-window, infinite variability, "trust no one"), stages the introduction and eventual working partnership of Tyler and James, and pivots — through the Roswell myth, Jacobsen vs. Corso, Taves's building blocks, the invisible gorilla, and screen-media research — to the mechanisms-of-belief and information-operations themes the book develops next: the myth is created, sustained, and proliferated in media.
