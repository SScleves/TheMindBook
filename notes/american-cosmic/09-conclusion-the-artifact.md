# Conclusion: The Artifact

## In one paragraph
The Conclusion returns to the physical "artifact" Pasulka found with Tyler and James — or that, she allows, "was perhaps planted" for her to find — and reports that research scientists analyzed it and judged it so anomalous that, in one scientist's words, "It could not have been made in this universe." Pasulka opens with Jacques Vallee's epigraph "Credo quia absurdum, eh, Diana?" and observes that absurdity "seems to have been written into the fabric of the artifact." She reads the object through religious studies: as a mystery it functions religiously, like Catholic relics such as the Shroud of Turin, embodying the "sacred element" that distinguishes religion from other organized practices. She then registers her two chief disquiets at the end of her research — the artifact's grip on the scientist-believers, and, even more, the power of media representations to manufacture mass belief in UFOs through the mechanisms of belief she has traced throughout the book (crystallized by the December 2017 New York Times article on Luis Elizondo and the Pentagon UFO program). She closes in the Vatican Observatory archive with Shklovsky's line "The prey runs to the predator," which she reinterprets as humanity's unreflective embrace of media and technology, and with Heidegger's warning that a technology-dominated era would be misunderstood and dangerous — "Only a God can save us now."

## Core claims
- Pasulka reports that the artifact she found with James and Tyler was analyzed by research scientists who concluded it was "so anomalous as to be incomprehensible" and, she was told, could not have been generated or created on Earth — one scientist saying "It could not have been made in this universe." She stresses this did not mean the scientists believed extraterrestrials made it; they simply did not know how or by whom it was made, and were "comfortable, if amazed" with that ambiguity.
- Pasulka argues (via James's research method and Kuhn's advice to John Mack) that anomalies are where scientific knowledge advances: the anomaly "was there for a reason," and the researcher's job is to understand it and possibly change the hypothesis — which is precisely how the scientists were proceeding.
- The book says the December 2017 New York Times article featuring Luis Elizondo's testimony about a secret Pentagon UFO program set off a firestorm of "disclosure" or "unofficial disclosure," prompted FOIA demands for government-held debris or "alloys," and promoted the "realism" that is one of the mechanisms of belief Pasulka elaborated in the book — even converting previously scoffing colleagues into interested parties.
- Pasulka argues that as an object of mystery the artifact functioned in religious ways, like the relics of Catholic devotionalism; sacred artifacts are "objects of power," and part of their power lies in their mystery.
- Pasulka's working definition of religion (as she teaches undergraduates): traditional religions have functional aspects that can be studied easily (churches, synagogues, sacred texts, oral traditions) and a "sacred" element — the object of belief, mysterious and not itself studiable objectively ("One cannot put an angel under a microscope"). It is the mysterious sacred that distinguishes religion from sports or fandoms.
- For Tyler and the scientist-believers, Pasulka argues, the artifact's mystery is not only impenetrable but compels reverence and belief; it inspires them. She notes, citing Jung, that she has seen how its reality inspired belief "and, as Jung notes, rumors that spin mythologies."
- Pasulka reports that her literal-minded suggestion — that the artifact might be technology from another country — was met with incredulity by the scientists, who saw that speculation as among the least helpful.
- Pasulka argues that even more disconcerting than the artifact was the level of belief produced by media representations of UFOs: media professionals used the mechanisms of belief to push stories at times very far removed from the inspiring event, believed by millions — revealing the extent of media influence and power over belief in UFOs and extraterrestrials.
- Reinterpreting Shklovsky's "The prey runs to the predator" (originally about the risk that contacted extraterrestrial life might be unfriendly), Pasulka applies it to our relationship to media and technology and "the unreflective embrace of both."
- The book invokes Heidegger's prediction that technology would bring a new era dominated by technology as thoroughly as the medieval era was dominated by God; technology's effects would be misunderstood, and in this misunderstanding humans would face a great and potentially very destructive crisis.

## Concepts
- **the artifact** — the anomalous physical object Pasulka found with James and Tyler (or that "was perhaps planted" for her to find), analyzed by research scientists as incomprehensible and not producible on Earth — "It could not have been made in this universe." Pasulka treats it as functioning religiously, like a relic: an object of power whose power lies partly in its mystery.
- **anomaly** — data that does not fit the hypothesis. James redirects graduate students toward anomalies rather than letting them ignore such data: the anomaly is there for a reason, and understanding it may force a change of hypothesis. Kuhn (per the book) argued scientific revolutions come about through attention to anomalies.
- **mechanisms of belief** — the processes, elaborated throughout the book, by which belief in UFOs is produced; in this chapter Pasulka shows media professionals wielding them to push stories "very far removed from the event that inspired" them, believed by millions.
- **realism** — one of the mechanisms of belief Pasulka elaborated in the book; the New York Times Elizondo article "promoted the 'realism'" that lends UFO belief credibility.
- **disclosure / "unofficial disclosure"** — the firestorm set off by the December 2017 New York Times article, including public FOIA demands that the US government produce any debris or "alloys" it might be keeping.
- **sacred artifact** — an object of mystery functioning within religion, like the Shroud of Turin: an artifact "of faith, devotion, and belief" with its own history of discovery and doubt, leaving "a trail of miracles." Sacred artifacts are objects of power, and part of their power lies in their mystery.
- **religion (functional aspects + sacred element)** — Pasulka's teaching definition: traditional religions contain functional aspects (places of worship, sacred texts, oral traditions) that can be studied easily, plus a "sacred" element — the object of belief, usually mysterious, which cannot itself be studied objectively.
- **the mysterious sacred** — the aspect of religion — the inexplicable sacred event or mysterious artifact — that distinguishes religion from other organized practices like sports or fandoms.
- **scientist-believers** — Pasulka's term in this chapter for the community of scientists who are also believers, for whom the artifact compels reverence and belief; she ends her research "an outsider" to this community.
- **artifact–observer symbiosis** — Tyler's interpretation (crediting Whitley Strieber's remark that "the artifacts we studied also studied us"): "some sort of symbiotic relationship between the artifact and those in its proximity. It generates information. Some people are able to pick up on that information."
- **credo quia absurdum** — the Latin epigraph from Jacques Vallee ("Credo quia absurdum, eh, Diana?"); Pasulka finds that absurdity "seems to have been written into the fabric of the artifact" itself.
- **"the prey runs to the predator"** — Shklovsky's phrase (in Sagan and Shklovsky's Intelligent Life in the Universe) about the search for extraterrestrial life — contacted life might not be friendly — which Pasulka reinterprets as describing humanity's unreflective embrace of media and technology.
- **technology as the new God-era (Heidegger)** — Heidegger's prediction that technology would bring an era as dominated by technology as the medieval era was by God; misunderstanding technology's effects would produce a great, potentially very destructive crisis — hence "Only a God can save us now."
- **rumors that spin mythologies** — Pasulka's Jung-derived phrase for how the artifact's reality has inspired belief and, in turn, myth-generating rumor.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The scientific analysis of the artifact** — The object Pasulka found with James and Tyler was analyzed by research scientists, who concluded it was so anomalous as to be incomprehensible and could not have been generated or created on Earth; one scientist said "It could not have been made in this universe." The scientists did not conclude extraterrestrial origin — they did not know how or by whom it was made, and were comfortable with the ambiguity. Pasulka notes the possibility that the artifact "was perhaps planted" for her to find.
- **James's graduate-student method** — James told Pasulka that when his graduate students found data that did not fit the hypothesis they often ignored it; he would redirect them toward the anomaly, explaining it was there for a reason and their job was to understand why, possibly changing the hypothesis. Pasulka presents this as how the scientists handled the artifact.
- **John Mack and Thomas Kuhn** — During his research with experiencers, Mack approached Kuhn, who had argued scientific revolutions come about through attention to anomalies. Kuhn advised Mack to focus on the raw data and persist in collecting it even when it fit no preconceived or conventional frameworks — "precisely how the scientists were proceeding," per Pasulka.
- **The December 2017 New York Times article / Luis Elizondo** — Just before the holidays in December 2017, the New York Times published the testimony of Luis Elizondo, former director of the Pentagon's Aerospace Advanced Threat Identification Program, alleging a secret US program to study UFOs. It triggered "disclosure"/"unofficial disclosure," FOIA demands for debris or "alloys," and a shift among Pasulka's colleagues — some who had scoffed at her interest were suddenly interested. Pasulka says the article promoted the mechanism of "realism."
- **The Shroud of Turin** — Pasulka's paradigm case of a sacred artifact: it bears what appears to be the image of a tortured, crucified man; millions of Christians consider it Jesus's burial cloth; the Catholic Church, its owner, has not pronounced it authentic; scientists and artists have tried to recreate medieval techniques to show a medieval origin; no theory of the image's origin is conclusive. It remains a mystery, an artifact of faith with its own history of discovery and doubt and a continuing "trail of miracles."
- **Tyler's backpack and his friend's dream** — Tyler carried the part in a backpack, stopped to visit and dine with a friend, then left. The next day the friend messaged: "I had a dream about the contents of your backpack. I dreamt that there was a separate universe that you carried in it. A universe that was created within this universe that who knows where this universe was created. Had very much the essence of turtles all the way down . . . ha!" Tyler's reading, recalling Whitley Strieber's remark that "the artifacts we studied also studied us": a symbiotic relationship exists between the artifact and those near it — it generates information some people pick up. "Don't ask me to explain it, because I can't." Pasulka offers this as demonstrating the artifact's sacred significance to Tyler and the scientist-believers.
- **Pasulka's rejected "another country" hypothesis** — When Pasulka suggested the artifact might be technology belonging to another country, the scientists looked at her incredulously, seeing that line of speculation as among the least helpful.
- **The Vatican Observatory archive / Sagan and Shklovsky** — Toward the end of her research, sitting in the Vatican Observatory archive, Pasulka came across Carl Sagan's Intelligent Life in the Universe, coauthored with Soviet astronomer Iosif Samuilovich Shklovsky, and was struck by Shklovsky's words "The prey runs to the predator" — referring to the search for extraterrestrial life and the risk that such life might not be friendly, which she came to relate instead to our unreflective embrace of media and technology.
- **Heidegger's Der Spiegel interview** — In his last interview, the German magazine Der Spiegel asked Heidegger whether philosophy could prevent the destructive crisis of the technological era; he answered, "Only a God can save us now." At his request, the interview was published only posthumously.

## Thread
The Conclusion resolves — or rather deliberately refuses to resolve — the book's opening ethnographic thread: the artifact recovered with Tyler and James at the New Mexico "crash" site in Chapter 2. Its scientific verdict ("It could not have been made in this universe") vindicates neither belief nor debunking, leaving the ambiguity that Pasulka has argued is constitutive of the phenomenon; Vallee's "credo quia absurdum" epigraph ties the ending back to the absurdity/deception themes he supplied earlier in the book. The chapter also completes the book's two theoretical arcs: the artifact-as-relic analysis lands the claim that UFO materiality now does the religious work once done by sacred objects (the mysterious sacred), and the Elizondo/New York Times episode plus the media-belief discussion cap the "mechanisms of belief" argument — showing realism operating at mass scale in real time. Pasulka's final turn to Shklovsky and Heidegger reframes the whole study: the deepest issue is not extraterrestrials but humanity's unreflective, quasi-religious relationship to technology and media. It hands off to unit 10, the author's Glossary of the book's terms.
