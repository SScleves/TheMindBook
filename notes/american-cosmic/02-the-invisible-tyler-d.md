# The Invisible Tyler D.

## In one paragraph
This chapter (opening under an epigraph from Chuck Palahniuk's Fight Club, its famous line left unfinished) introduces Pasulka's central informant, the pseudonymous "Tyler D.," and narrates her blindfolded journey with the scientist James Master to a no-fly-zone site in the New Mexico desert where, believers hold, an extraterrestrial craft crashed in 1947. Pasulka frames the site as a hierophany — a manifestation of the sacred — and insists her aim is not to verify the artifacts but to document how belief in them forms in two elite scientists. Interwoven is the story of how she met Tyler: first virtually (long texts, hidden-camera videos, his Challenger grief, his career of patent-generating "memories" he attributes to off-planet intelligence), then in person at the American Academy of Religion meeting with Jeff Kripal. The chapter details Tyler's bodily "protocol" for receiving downloads, sets him in a lineage running from Tsiolkovsky and Jack Parsons to Colonel Philip Corso, and contrasts the academic code of transparency with the "invisibles'" code of silence. It ends back in the desert with the recovery of metallic artifacts, a TSA scare, and Pasulka's realization — triggered by Tyler's X-Files claim — that she is "standing on ground zero of the new religion."

## Core claims
- Pasulka argues the New Mexico site functions as a sacred place — the site of a hierophany, where nonhuman intelligence is believed to have revealed itself — and explicitly flags this as *her* interpretation as a religious-studies scholar, not a claim about the artifacts' actual origin.
- She claims that where hierophanies appear, consumerism follows: Roswell's UFO museum, themed food, and four-day festival parallel the memorabilia economy at Lourdes.
- Pasulka argues that whether the site is "objectively" real doesn't matter to its religious function: through media (The X-Files, screens reaching millions or billions), "it had already become true for millions of people." The site is "ground zero of the new religion."
- She claims the UFO/extraterrestrial belief system's "message had been managed," like early Christianity's after Rome made one interpretation state religion — and that powerful interest groups' involvement in creating UFO belief "has already been well established"; she stays open to the possibility that she and James were pawns in a disinformation plot.
- Pasulka argues there are always artifacts in the history of religions (Ark of the Covenant, Noah's Ark, Shroud of Turin): important to believers, controversial to nonbelievers, spawning both religious communities and fictional portrayals.
- She claims two opposed codes of conduct govern research on the phenomenon: academics are bound to transparency about sources, while scientist-ufologists working in official capacities are vetted, take oaths of secrecy, and may not discuss the very thing they study — partly for national security. Respecting their silences defined the scope and limits of her research.
- She describes a class of people she calls "the invisibles" — insiders whose names are regularly washed from the internet, whose merits are never known, who are "removed from history as if they never existed."
- Pasulka coins "meta-experiencers" for scientists who don't claim their own sightings but scoop data from primary experiencers and apply it to their work; Tyler is one.
- She argues Tyler belongs to a lineage of space-program figures — Tsiolkovsky, Jack Parsons — who believed nonhuman intelligences directed their paths and seeded them with technology-generating information; the founders of both the Russian and American space programs held such beliefs, even if most current scientists and astronauts do not.
- She frames Tyler as a contemporary Colonel Corso — but where Corso went public and was punished by ridicule, Tyler "worked silently, invisibly," and his invisibility ensured, and was somehow a key to, his success.
- Pasulka argues technology is routinely described in supernatural language (programmer "wizards," "devils" in computers, mind-reading social media), and that in these lives something stranger than Clarke's magic/technology distinction is at work: "a fusion of magic, or the supernatural, and the technological," with silence as the key to the connection.
- Tyler's claim (reported, not endorsed): human DNA is a receptor and transmitter operating at the same frequency used to communicate with deep-space satellites — "Humans are a type of satellite" — so the body must be tuned (sleep, sun, hydration, no coffee) to receive and transmit signals from off-planet intelligence.
- Tyler's claim: connection is confirmed when unfamiliar thoughts arrive "like a hit" and the environment "wakes up" within hours to validate them; one must watch for confirmations and act.
- Pasulka notes Heather Berlin's neuroscience "might suggest that creativity does not originate from an external source, although some creative subjects like Tyler experience it that way" — she juxtaposes the download account with the flow-state account without adjudicating.
- She reports that Vallee's approach maps onto the trio: Tyler the physical aspect, experiencers the testimonial aspect, and Pasulka the social aspect — belief that persists regardless of verifiable evidence.

## Concepts
- **hierophany** — In Pasulka's field, a manifestation of the sacred: it occurs when a nonhuman intelligent being descends from the sky to the ground or otherwise reveals itself. Moses's burning bush is her classic example; Roswell functions the same way for millions who believe in extraterrestrials.
- **sacred place** — Pasulka's (self-described tongue-in-cheek, then increasingly serious) name for the New Mexico destination: the location marking where nonhuman intelligence is believed to have revealed itself to humans. She notes such sites attract pilgrimage and a carnival-like consumer economy (Roswell, Lourdes).
- **the phenomenon** — The book's working term for the UFO/UAP-and-contact complex that experiencers, scientists, and government-adjacent researchers study; the thing insiders are forbidden to discuss.
- **artifact** — Alleged physical pieces of the crashed craft; more broadly, the physical objects religions always produce (Ark of the Covenant, Shroud of Turin) that are important to believers, controversial for nonbelievers, and spawn communities and fictions. James holds the artifacts are either fabrications costing millions/billions or "something" made off Earth with technologies we don't understand.
- **experiencer** — People who believe they saw unidentified aerial phenomena or had contact, in some way, with their inhabitants — the group Pasulka originally set out to study.
- **meta-experiencer** — Pasulka's coinage for the scientists attracted to experiencers: mostly reticent to admit belief in UFOs, but readily scooping data from primary experiencers for their own work. Tyler, a space-industry employee, is one.
- **the invisibles** — People who study the phenomenon from the inside, whose names are washed from the internet on a regular basis; their merits and accomplishments are never to be known — "literally, removed from history as if they never existed."
- **code of silence** — The confidentiality regime governing scientist-ufologists and government-associated space researchers: extensive vetting, oaths to keep sources secret, prohibition on discussing the very thing they study, maintained partly for national security. Opposed to the academic code of transparency, an ethical imperative to reveal sources.
- **message management** — Pasulka's observation that the UFO belief system, like state-era Christianity, has had its message managed by powerful interests; disinformation remains a live possibility she does not need to resolve.
- **Tyler's protocol** — Tyler's physical-and-mental regimen for connecting with off-planet intelligence, which reminded Pasulka and Kripal of religious practices like yoga and meditation: eight-plus-one sleep, near-zero alcohol, no coffee ("Coffee really messes up the signal"), going to bed slightly dehydrated, then morning sun plus a tall glass of water to flush and rehydrate the cells — at which point connection is felt.
- **eight plus one rule** — Tyler's sleep rule: eight hours, wake, then force one more "top-off" hour that "really makes or breaks my day."
- **DNA as receptor/transmitter; human as satellite** — Tyler's belief that DNA receives and transmits at the same frequency used to communicate with deep-space satellites; humans are a type of satellite that must be tuned.
- **the download** — The UFO-community name for the process of connection with off-planet entities; researcher Grant Cameron argues creativity comes not from IQ or talent but from tapping "nonlocal intelligence," and found church-hymn composers reported the same downloads as rock musicians.
- **synchronicities/confirmations** — In Tyler's method, environmental validations arriving within hours of an anomalous thought, signaling the thought came from "them" and should be acted on (Pasulka's word for it; Tyler answers "Maybe").
- **"memories"** — Tyler's term for his technology ideas: they arrive fully formed as memories of things he has never learned (the Columbia experiment, biomedical designs), which he decided to "use for good."
- **electrogravitic machine** — Tyler's term for the propulsion of some OP (Off Planet) craft, which he says use "a highly charged capacitor of sorts"; the pacemaker-capacitor episode helped him "connect some dots" about it.
- **smart room** — A special room outfitted with the latest technologies where the world's best scientists think in complete freedom — nothing said there is laughed at ("a purple unicorn flying through space on a pickle") — and from which, Tyler claims, some of the most innovative technologies in use (he gestures at Pasulka's iPhone) emerged.
- **ethereal beings** — Tsiolkovsky's term (quoted by Pasulka) for higher beings beyond our dimensions who read our thoughts and send messages through celestial symbols; a genius is one who channels these messages into technologies, products, and art.
- **modern Prometheus myth** — The notion, fueled by Corso's The Day After Roswell, that nonhuman advanced intelligences (gods, even) provided humans with advanced technology — which simultaneously fed a conspiracy theory about government cover-up of extraterrestrial life.
- **technology as magic** — Pasulka's point that technology is described in supernatural language; Clarke's "any sufficiently advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic" both divides and connects the two. She argues what she observed is stranger: a genuine fusion of the supernatural and the technological, with silence as its key.
- **Vallee's three aspects** — Jacques Vallee's approach to the phenomenon: the physical aspect (Tyler), the testimonial aspect (experiencers/James), and the social aspect (Pasulka) — how belief persists regardless of verifiable evidence.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The blindfolded desert trip (New Mexico)** — Pasulka and James Master agree to wear blindfolds for the final forty minutes of a gravel-road drive to a no-fly-zone site (explicitly "not Area 51") where an extraterrestrial craft allegedly crashed in 1947. Tyler secretly photographs them blindfolded. A giant rainbow appears at the outset; Tyler eyes Pasulka "as if I had somehow conjured it." Dress code: sturdy leather boots against rattlesnakes, winter jackets against wind chill; Tyler, naturally warm, wears a jean jacket. Shows the ritual, secrecy, and pilgrimage character of the visit.
- **The tin-can cover-up story** — Tyler explains that after the 1947 crash the government took the craft, hid it, and disguised the area with tons of tin cans and debris to mask remaining artifacts; the rusted cans have decomposed into powdery, compost-like rubble. The metal detectors are "special," configured to identify the artifacts.
- **The X-Files mesa** — Pasulka is drawn to a familiar-looking small mesa; Tyler says the scene "was probably recreated in the first episode of the last season of The X-Files," speculating the production team had an insider. Her initial scoff gives way to a "click of realization": mythologization through media has made the site true for millions regardless of objective fact — her "ground zero of the new religion" moment.
- **Roswell and Lourdes as parallel pilgrimage economies** — Roswell's UFO museum, themed restaurants, and annual four-day festival mirrored by Lourdes, where after the Virgin Mary's 1858 apparition to Bernadette and the miraculous spring, millions now buy water, statues, and Virgin-themed food and drink. Shows consumerism follows hierophany.
- **The closed conference and the breach of silence** — Months earlier Pasulka convened a small closed meeting of ufologists, scientists, and humanities scholars (Tyler not invited). An attendee interrupted a professor mid-talk, loudly declaring he had "no authority to report his findings"; both men, it emerged, had taken oaths over a secret study unknown to the audience. The academics were so removed from the code of silence they barely registered the spat. The episode taught Pasulka the two codes of conduct are opposites and set the scope and limits of her research; it began her education about "the invisibles."
- **The invitation** — The day after the conference Tyler phones, offering to show her the phenomenon's "physical nature"; the timing makes her wonder if he knew what happened there. She refuses to go without James; Tyler first says no (special permission needed), then relents days later. James's emailed reply when asked if he'd go was instant: "Hell, yes." She later realizes Tyler researched James and wanted him — if anyone could analyze alleged alien debris, it was James — and that she was part of Tyler's own quest to understand the artifacts.
- **Tyler's surveillance habit** — Tyler's first solo contact is the longest text Pasulka ever received; he sends videos of workplaces (New Mexico, Florida), of friends unaware they're filmed, and of casual lunches with astronauts, via cameras hidden in his clothes. She knows he will film her too — a deterrent to meeting him.
- **Tyler's résumé** — Since age eighteen in the US space program, intern then space shuttle engineer; worked on almost every shuttle ever launched and speaks of each as a living thing with its own personality and sounds. Colleagues: generals, scientists, astronauts — plus surgeons and venture capitalists. More than forty patents; works from home on his deck: "I get paid to think. And to match up experts who can implement my vision." A promotional video from the CEO of one of Tyler's companies in Tampa praises him, featuring a photo of Tyler in a blue flight uniform and aviator sunglasses posed before a giant rocket. Rumors: private jet, sports car, publicized MMA fights, work for several government agencies (the rumor that made Pasulka avoid him).
- **The mentor's advice** — A retired space-program mentor told Tyler "the next discovery in my field is going to come from your field": having hit the limit of the materialist perspective, he must go to mysticism, religion, and consciousness; "the mind–machine interface is the next frontier." This is why Tyler sought out a scholar of religion.
- **The Challenger disaster (1986) and Judith Resnik** — Tyler's mentor and friend, astronaut Judith ("Judy") Resnik, died when Challenger exploded. In a video filmed at Cape Canaveral standing on the concrete slabs where the shuttle is buried ("The shuttle is buried here, in chunks of concrete"), Tyler recounts her last hug ("on some level she must have known"), watching the launch with the president viewing too, the shock of pain, going alone to the ocean to tell Judy "I'm sorry," and knowing he'd never be the same. His voice breaks; he keeps filming but can't speak. The video ends Pasulka's amusement with Tyler and reframes him as part of an American history that "would never be known" — perhaps why he compulsively records.
- **Aftermath: divorce, hospitalization, Sagan** — Post-Challenger, Tyler's wife wanted a divorce; heart palpitations hospitalized him for days; he was "a pure skeptic." A title-less black-covered book left in his office turned out to be Carl Sagan's book on the cosmos and space travel; reading it nightly became "my saving grace" and convinced him technology existed or soon would for interstellar and superluminal travel.
- **The Columbia experiment and the basement interrogation** — When a general solicited proposals for shuttle Columbia experiments, Tyler "had a memory" of an experiment testing whether a noncharged material could speak with a charged material, testable only in nongravity. Lacking a PhD, he recruited a professor who thought it wouldn't work but wanted the publication. It worked. Days later both were summoned to a Washington, DC basement, where a two-star general barked "Who the hell came up with this idea?" and interrogated Tyler, who could only say it was a memory. The next week: a plaque, a patent, and five hundred dollars. Tyler quit that week to go into business with a surgeon buddy, deciding to take his "memory" and "use it for good."
- **Biomedical career and the airport recruitment** — Tyler wrote patent after patent, sold his first biomedical company to a public corporation for an undisclosed sum, retired briefly, got bored — then two men approached him in an airport with a card and a phone number, inviting him back to the program. "I know that sounds like it is straight out of a movie, but that's what happened."
- **The shielded room** — Back in the program, Tyler worked at a desk next to a square room covered in concrete and metal ("There was something in there that either emitted frequencies or signals and they didn't want those to escape or they didn't want signals to get in. I never knew which"). He believes proximity to it "zapped" him, changing the frequencies of his body and thoughts; afterward his biomedical "memories" increased. He says he's since found himself on jobs interfacing directly with the phenomenon — "It does speak to us, in space" — placed there by "them"; even his direct boss doesn't know what he does.
- **The etched bone material and Jane** — One of Tyler's technologies: a material molecularly etched with information that human bone "reads" as itself, incorporated into diseased tissue and bone to aid recovery from cancer. Jane, a young mother of twins with bone cancer told she'd never walk again, was healed; Tyler sends Pasulka her photo and thank-you card — she walks and cares for her boys.
- **Tsiolkovsky** — The founding father of rocketry and aeronautics believed ethereal beings communicate through celestial symbols and that a genius channels their messages into technology; he discovered the equations later used to develop rockets. Pasulka quotes his "ethereal beings" passage in full.
- **Jack Parsons** — The American counterpart: collaborated with Aleister Crowley and L. Ron Hubbard, launched rockets and performed provocative rituals in the Los Angeles desert, timed launches to ritually significant days (Halloween), believed himself in contact with extraterrestrial intelligences. Died at thirty-seven when a rocket exploded in his garage; months later, after a very public UFO sighting over the White House, his widow and friends attributed the sighting to his death. Most astronauts/NASA employees Pasulka interviewed didn't know or were embarrassed by this history; Tyler admired both men's genius but was genuinely shocked by their beliefs and rituals.
- **Colonel Philip Corso** — Military man who claimed his task was to seed private industry with crashed extraterrestrial craft disguised as Russian or Chinese technology so industry would reverse-engineer it for American market advantage; his The Day After Roswell (1997) made the New York Times bestseller list and fueled both a modern Prometheus myth and a cover-up conspiracy theory. Pasulka casts Tyler as the contemporary, invisible Corso.
- **Meeting in Atlanta (AAR conference)** — Pasulka brings colleague Jeff Kripal as a check. An experiencer acquaintance had told her Tyler was "probably an angel," part human and part extraterrestrial: "He is older than both of us, but he looks twenty years younger. I don't know what he is." Tyler appears at the mirrored restaurant window combing his hair, unable to see them watching; enters in a classic suit and dress shirt with cuff links — "Gucci," Pasulka notes — amid disheveled religion scholars; books a table at his hotel, the Ritz; charms Jeff into a dinner invitation to Houston despite Pasulka's warnings; picks up the entire check. Every group she later introduces him to is similarly taken — "some kind of rock star," which deepens her suspicion.
- **The errant email / pacemaker capacitor** — Tyler's worked example of confirmation: an email misfired to the wrong "John" (a former neighbor), who read it as an invitation to fly a capacitor experiment on the NASA KC-135 "vomit comet" and sent back a one-page concept matching thoughts Tyler had had that same day but never sent. They flew the project; John's company used the data to develop a longer-lasting pacemaker capacitor; and what Tyler learned helped him connect dots on how some OP craft operate via a highly charged capacitor in their "electrogravitic machine."
- **Kary Mullis** — Biochemist who discovered polymerase chain reaction (Nobel 1993) and had an anomalous experience he referred to as a UFO encounter while carefully not "concluding" that's what it was; Pasulka quotes his description of creativity as something intruding until you give in — "The focused beam of your consciousness is very narrow, but you have a creepy sense of what is right behind you" — noting its similarity to Tyler's account.
- **Grant Cameron's download research** — Cameron set out to write a book on "the disclosure by an alien force that humans are not alone" but pivoted to downloads and inspirations. After a Boulder, Colorado lecture on the alien–music connection, audience members insisted the devil influenced modern music; Cameron (son of a four-decade church organist) checked whether church-hymn composers reported the same downloads as modern musicians — they had, meaning the devil would have composed all the church music too.
- **Heather Berlin's neuroscience** — Neurologist at the Icahn School of Medicine at Mount Sinai: in creative flow the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex — the seat of self-awareness — is turned down (as in dreams, daydreaming, some meditation and hypnosis), so ideas feel as if they come from outside; the filter comes off and novel associations are allowed. Pasulka notes this might locate creativity internally even though subjects like Tyler experience it as external.
- **The solo celebration** — Weeks after Atlanta, Tyler answers "how are you doing" with a photo of a brandy snifter and half-smoked cigar: he has sold a company for a major gain and is celebrating — alone, in his laboratory, with the artifacts.
- **The finds** — James finds the first metallic part, flagged by the metal detector, lodged in a rock crevice in a gully (a plausible rattlesnake nest), apparently washed out of a stream bed after years under detritus — which weighs against the parts having been planted for them. Tyler photographs, labels (with date and location), and bags the specimen. Teaming up (Tyler sweeping the detector, Pasulka with the shovel), they find a very large metallic piece near the mesa that Tyler believes was part of the craft's exterior. By day's end: several metallic artifacts.
- **The TSA episode** — Tyler warns the metallic parts could trigger airport security; James takes them all in his carry-on. Tyler "sailed right past security . . . He seemed to be literally beyond the law." James's bag stops the X-ray scanner, which shuts down with a loud beep; the line is redirected, the bag re-run, James fully searched, emerging sweat-covered and terrified they'd question him about a piece that shut down a TSA machine. The re-scan passes. The shared stress seals the trio's camaraderie.
- **The Vallee debrief** — On the drive to the airport all three exchange theories via Jacques Vallee's three aspects: physical (Tyler), testimonial (experiencers), social (Pasulka — belief persisting regardless of verifiable evidence).

## Thread
As the book's first numbered chapter, this unit delivers the ethnographic anchor for everything that follows: it introduces both pseudonymous scientist-informants (Tyler D. and James Master), the New Mexico desert "crash" field site, and Pasulka's method — bracketing the truth of the artifacts to study the formation of belief. It establishes the chapter-generating tensions the rest of the book works through: transparency vs. the code of silence, media-made truth vs. objective truth, the human-as-receiver model of creativity vs. neuroscience's internal account, and the historical lineage (Tsiolkovsky, Parsons, Corso) that lets Pasulka read a space-industry entrepreneur as a religious figure in the making. The artifacts James carries off for analysis, Vallee's three-aspect framework, and the phrase "ground zero of the new religion" are all set up here as running threads toward the book's Conclusion ("The Artifact").
