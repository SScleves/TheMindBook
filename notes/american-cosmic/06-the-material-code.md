# The Material Code: From the Disembodied Soul to the Materiality of Quantum Information

## In one paragraph
This chapter is D.W. Pasulka's sustained engagement with Jacques Vallee — the computer scientist and ufologist she calls the key theorist of the phenomenon — built around his 1975 book *The Invisible College*, which he gifted her after a Big Sur seminar in 2014. Pasulka argues that Vallee's early work, done amid ARPANET, remote viewing, and Silicon Valley in the 1970s, "clarified a new framework for understanding the technologically sacred": the UFO is associated with the sacred but *works like a technology*, operating "under the radar" of consciousness the way social bots do, and protected by a "logic of camouflage" whose engine is the absurd. She traces the parallel between the two schools of ufology (nuts-and-bolts vs. psychic/subjective) and the Catholic split between doctrinal and devotional religion, then reconstructs Vallee's showcase example — the 1917 apparitions of the Virgin Mary at Fatima, which he reads as technologically indistinguishable from UFO events and possibly a "schedule of reinforcement." She then extends Vallee through Donna Haraway, Andy Clark's extended cognition, Hayles's "new unconscious," and film analysis of *2001: A Space Odyssey* to argue that media technologies can function like mass apparitions, putting "the beings" literally in our heads. The chapter closes with Vallee's associative-universe theory of synchronicity — including Pasulka's own research on social-bot-generated "synthetic" synchronicities — and with Tyler D.'s model of the human body as a biological computer/antenna whose DNA sends and receives the phenomenon's signal, grounding information not in a disembodied soul but in matter.

## Core claims
- Pasulka presents Vallee's recurrent thesis: the UFO phenomenon has a technological basis, yet the emotions it generates in witnesses are religious in nature; it behaves like "a technology that followed well-defined rules and patterns."
- Following Vallee, she argues that secrecy and camouflage are integral to the phenomenon's efficacy — like social bots, it effects cultural change "completely under the radar of consciousness," which is where it is most effective.
- Vallee (as Pasulka reads him) holds that the UFO phenomenon "is a direct challenge to this arbitrary dichotomy between physical reality and spiritual reality," and that researchers should discard the mind–body dichotomy because it skews the data.
- The book says ufology split into two antagonistic schools — a materialist "nuts and bolts" school and a subjective/psychic school — and that this bifurcation exists inside the UFO report itself: witnesses tell authorities the empirical part and confide the psychic part only to sympathetic listeners, producing two separate datasets kept apart by fear of ridicule.
- Pasulka draws a structural parallel: the same dual tradition exists in Catholicism as doctrinal vs. devotional religion, and as "public revelation" (obligatory belief) vs. "private revelation" (optional belief); in both traditions, verification collapses onto the trustworthiness of the witness.
- The absurd, per Vallee, is part of the phenomenon's logic: it "leads to a rejection of the story by the upper layers of the target society and an absorption at a deep unconscious level of the symbols conveyed by the encounter." Pasulka's analogy (following Vallee) is early Christianity — an "absurd" subterranean counterculture that eventually became Rome's state religion. "This is the logic of camouflage. It is sneaky, and time is on its side."
- Vallee wonders whether the UFO event's absurdity could work like a Zen koan — fatiguing the rational mind to allow a different state of consciousness — possibly a "mass koan, working on millions of people."
- Vallee's method (borrowed from anthropologist Cynthia Nelson's phenomenology) suspends judgment on whether apparitions are "really real," attends only to first-order percipient reports, and analyzes social effects; graphed UFO flaps "appear to suggest a schedule of reinforcement," an analysis he offers as speculation while remaining committed to the idea that the phenomena appear technological.
- Vallee's most elaborate example of the phenomenon's technological patterns is not a UFO case but the Fatima apparitions: "the technology which 'she' uses is indistinguishable... from the technology surrounding the UFO phenomenon."
- Pasulka argues, via Haraway, Clark, Zacks, and Hayles, that media technologies (e.g., *2001: A Space Odyssey*) contain and leverage the elements of the UFO event and can "function like a mass apparition"; media bypasses the conscious mind, so fictional beings become "potentially real and autonomous agents" living in our unconscious — "The beings really are in our heads."
- She reports Andy Clark's claim that cognition is not bounded by the skull ("cognitive systems may include both non-neural parts of the body and even the beyond-the-body environment"), and notes a new, disturbing autonomy of technologies that can now program themselves.
- Vallee's associative-universe thesis: space-time is "a cultural artifact made possible by the invention of graph paper"; if the universe is informational and associative like a computer, "miracles are no longer irrational events" and coincidences are simply how the universe retrieves information — synchronicities are naturalized as search-engine-like retrieval.
- Pasulka's own finding: social bots and targeted ads can *manufacture* synchronicities, and these "synthetic" synchronicities are no less profoundly meaningful to experiencers who fully understand the mechanism — a partial "yes" to Vallee's 1970s question of whether coincidences can be systematically promoted by creating information structures.
- Citing Nietzsche, she says experiences of synchronicity "are the engines of religious belief and practice," in UFO cultures as in Catholic ones; Nietzsche warned against the religious interpretation and elevated "beloved Chance" instead.
- Via Gaymon Bennett, she argues that Silicon Valley's synthetic biologists made "biotechnical imaginaries" normative — life as "programmable matter" — though Bennett (echoing Hayles) faults them for taking information to be "fundamentally immaterial"; the assumption is incorrect, but that doesn't make it wrong to say matter functions like a code.
- Tyler D.'s model, which Pasulka relays without endorsement: the phenomenon is technological and interfaces with humans through "biotechnological antennae" — cells and DNA — so humans are themselves technologies and the preferred sensors in the search for extraterrestrial life; information is not immaterial but exists with matter "at different frequencies"; the body, not the brain, is the key receiver of a "divine signal."

## Concepts
- **invisible college** — J. Allen Hynek's name for a small group of researchers, scientists, and academics who studied the UFO phenomenon under the cover of anonymity; the term harks back to the scientists affiliated with Robert Boyle in the early 1700s (when science was considered suspect and potentially demonic), a group thought to be a precursor to the Royal Society of London.
- **logic of camouflage** — Vallee's idea, embraced by Pasulka, that the phenomenon's subterranean, hidden character is part of its logic: absurdity keeps it rejected by elites and off the record while its symbols are absorbed unconsciously, exactly as early Christianity spread invisibly before erupting into a state religion. "It is sneaky, and time is on its side."
- **the absurd** — the constant factor in UFO (and devotional) testimony — e.g., Bigfoot seen from the astral plane near Mars — that triggers ridicule, keeps the phenomenon "hidden and on the margin of legitimate sociality," deters official study, and thereby protects it.
- **koan** — in Chan/Zen Buddhism, a short nonsensible anecdote ("What is the sound of one hand clapping?") posed to fatigue the rational mind and allow satori/enlightenment; Vallee wonders whether the UFO's absurd elements function as a koan — possibly a "mass koan" working on millions.
- **trickster (Hansen)** — George Hansen's thesis in *The Trickster and the Paranormal* that psi, the paranormal, and the supernatural are "fundamentally linked to destructuring, change, transition, disorder, marginality, the ephemeral, fluidity, ambiguity, and blurring of boundaries," and are repressed by order, structure, and clear demarcation; paranormal upsurges track cultural revolutions and instability.
- **nuts-and-bolts school** — the materialist school of ufology focused on empirical effects: radiation burns on objects or people, radar blips, sightings; it disavows the psychic and subjective in the hope of mainstream scientific acceptance. Its counterpart school (arising with hypnotic regression, contactees, and abduction movements) focuses on experiencers and the content of extraterrestrial messages.
- **two datasets** — Vallee's field finding that witnesses report the empirical part of an event to authorities and the psychic part only to sympathetic listeners, so "when scientists and the military discuss UFOs, they are not talking about the same part of the phenomenon the public perceives"; fear of ridicule keeps the sets separate.
- **purgatory / mind–body dualism** — the thirteenth-century Catholic doctrine of a purifying state for imperfect souls; scholastic debates over whether immaterial souls could leave material traces (burn marks on tables) were the medieval version of the same physical/spiritual conundrum Vallee identifies in the phenomenon, worked out via Aristotle into mind–body dualism.
- **devotional vs. doctrinal Catholicism; private vs. public revelation** — devotional Catholicism (prayer to Mary, the Sacred Heart, the Rosary) is often seen as in conflict with or less important than doctrinal Catholicism; the split is formalized as "private revelation" (apparitions; Catholics not obligated to believe) vs. "public revelation" (scripture; belief obligatory). Trustworthiness of the witness is the verification mechanism, as in UFO reports.
- **hierophany** — a place where the sacred touches down upon Earth; apparition sites like Fatima, Lourdes, and Mount Tepeyac become sites of hierophany as word spreads.
- **biblical–UFO interpretation** — reading religious source material within a tradition that includes modern UFO events (like Eddy W.'s Bible reading); Vallee's differs in that he refuses to define what the events are, breaking them into constitutive parts and patterns instead.
- **Morphology of Miracles** — Vallee's table placing the elements of Marian apparitions side by side with UFO events: humming sounds, arrival of a shining disc/globe/sphere, enervation after a sighting, thunder or boom sounds, unusual clouds; Pasulka adds the ensuing personality transformation and mission-feeling he could have listed.
- **control mechanism / schedule of reinforcement** — Vallee's speculative conclusion that apparitions and UFOs are manifestations of a single control mechanism working like a schedule of reinforcement — in psychology, the shaping of behavior through rewards/punishments, as in Pavlov's dogs salivating at a bell; graphs of UFO flaps (multiple sightings over time) appear to suggest such a schedule.
- **percipients** — Vallee's term for experiencers; his method attends to their first-order reports in their original language while ignoring second-order evaluative interpretations (e.g., that the entity was the Virgin Mary, or from Zeta Reticuli).
- **psychic component** — the direct knowing of what the beings communicate, "as if there are no barriers" — reported by the Fatima children and St. Teresa of Avila alike; via media, this component "once experienced by the few can now be experienced by millions."
- **extended cognition (Clark)** — Andy Clark's challenge to the assumption that thinking originates in the head: "cognitive systems may include both non-neural parts of the body and even the beyond-the-body environment"; McLuhan aired tool-extension in the 1950s, but what is new is technologies' autonomy, supercharged by self-programming.
- **new unconscious (Hayles)** — the finding across neuroscience, psychology, and cognitive science that the unconscious plays a far larger role than previously thought in determining goals and setting priorities, responding flexibly to the environment while remaining inaccessible to consciousness.
- **book encounter / media encounter** — the (largely unconscious, hence camouflaged) role that books and media play in the evaluation of anomalous events, from initial interpretation to narrative elaboration into stories, films, urban legends, and lore.
- **cyborg epistemologies** — theories of knowledge associated with Donna Haraway's work on how human–technological engagement effaces the binaries between humans and technology, humans and animals, animals and technologies.
- **associative universe** — Vallee's model: space-time ordering is "the worst possible way to store data"; if we live "in the associative universe of the software scientist rather than the Cartesian sequential universe of the space-time physicist," miracles cease to be irrational, and consciousness can be "defined as the process by which informational associations are retrieved and traversed."
- **computer conferencing / remote viewing** — Vallee's 1970s ARPANET-era research medium, which let separated individuals' observations be dated and timestamped, confirming simultaneous impressions; he studied remote viewing and telepathy within it.
- **synchronicity / synthetic synchronicity** — powerful meaning-filled coincidences that (per Nietzsche and Pasulka) are engines of religious belief; Pasulka's research shows social bots and cookie-driven targeted ads can generate "synthetic" synchronicities that remain profoundly meaningful even when the experiencer knows the mechanism.
- **biotechnical imaginaries / programmable matter** — Gaymon Bennett's account of Silicon Valley synthetic biologists' worldview that life "can and should be treated as 'programmable matter'"; their industrial success made the ideology normative, though they wrongly "took information to be fundamentally immaterial."
- **materiality of information** — the chapter's title claim: against the disembodied soul and the immaterial-information assumption, information is physical — epitomized by quantum physicist Nicole Yunger Halpern's line "I like my quantum information physical" and by Tyler's insistence that body and matter are indispensable to the interface.
- **humans as receivers / biological internet** — Tyler's model: humans are designed as receptors/sensors of the phenomenon's signal (some better than others); DNA stores biophotonic particles as data, has a natural frequency of ~150 MHz (similar to satellite communication), follows language-like syntax/semantics/grammar, and makes the body "programmable"; bones' calcium acts as antenna; photon entanglement provides instantaneous twin-state transfer — "the human body and DNA become a biological internet."
- **UFO flap** — multiple sightings over time; graphing their occurrence is what suggested the reinforcement schedule to Vallee.
- **cultural revitalization movements** — anthropologists' finding that the supernatural has figured in thousands of such movements; paranormal interest surges when cultures undergo profound change (e.g., the USSR's breakup).
- **Miracle of the Sun** — the culminating Fatima event of October 13, 1917: the sun seen as a sharp-edged disc that could be stared at, spinning "in a mad whirl," the atmosphere turning amethyst, the sun seeming to plunge toward the earth — witnessed by tens of thousands including skeptics.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Santa Cruz debriefing lunch / Big Sur seminar (2014)** — Pasulka, Vallee, and Robbie Graham lunch dockside after a week-long intensive seminar with scholars of UFO phenomena and paranormal-adjacent religious events. A UFO was sighted during the seminar by sky-watchers on the Pacific rim (a bright starlike object dancing in the sky) though none of the three saw it; Pasulka and Vallee interviewed one witness who had photographed it and who, fresh from a life transformation, read the sighting as confirmation he was "on the right path." Afterward Vallee gifted her *The Invisible College* (signed October 2014; originally 1975, reissued July 2014) — "The book I held in my hands was a key, though I didn't know it at the time."
- **Betty and Barney Hill** — Vallee's field-research example of the two-dataset pattern: the Hills reported the empirical sighting (a starlike object) to the Air Force but divulged the encounter with nonhuman beings only to Donald Keyhoe and later their therapist (who, ironically, did not believe in UFOs). Vallee refuses to believe or disbelieve their claim of messages from Zeta Reticuli, while not dismissing the experience as unreal.
- **The businessman-pilot experiencer ("Bigfoot on Mars")** — Pasulka took two former students to meet a well-known, credible experiencer (businessman, pilot, community figure) whose fishing-trip sighting of aerial phenomena was "a textbook case" — until he described flying past Mars on the astral plane and seeing Bigfoot. Her students' stricken faces illustrate how the absurd deters study; Pasulka, inured by both UFO and Catholic devotional material, uses it to teach that religion's logic is not rational but has patterns and real-world effects.
- **Early Christianity as camouflage precedent** — a subterranean, actively suppressed counterculture ("too absurd to retain the attention of a reader of Plato"): Romans called Christians cannibals for "eating" their god; Paul's message of salvation regardless of gender or social position appealed to slaves, women, and noncitizens; carried by media technologies (art/iconography, printing press, mass media), it exploded into Rome's state religion — Roman Catholicism, "Roman universalism." Pasulka's students are "horrified" when reminded communion means eating Jesus: acclimation to the absurd.
- **USSR breakup paranormal surge** — during the dissolution of the former U.S.S.R., an explosion of paranormal activity across Eastern Europe, with healers and psychics prominent in media — supporting Hansen's instability thesis; historian William A. Christian similarly linked Marian apparitions to Spanish and European revolutions and upheavals.
- **Fatima, Portugal (1915–1917)** — Vallee's centerpiece. Sequence per the book: the observations began two years before the Marian identification with "a fairly classical sequence of UFO sightings" — Lucia Santos (age nine) saw a shining cloud with a human outline descend on a hillside walk, twice; later a white light passed over Lucia and cousins Jacinta and Francisco Marto and a shining youth appeared, instructing prayer and penance and leaving the children physically enervated for hours (a UFO-contact pattern; Pasulka notes the protocol echo of Tyler's practices). In spring 1917 an angel appeared three times prescribing fasting and penance. On May 13 and June 13, 1917 the children saw "a lady" (their records say just "a lady," in an oval of bright light, saying she was from heaven) who told them to return monthly. The parish priest jailed the children on August 13 over the growing publicity. Six apparitions in all; only the children saw the lady, but crowds saw a cloud descend/ascend and heard a buzzing "like that of a bee" when she spoke (eyewitness interviewed by journalist Maria de Freitas). The predicted October 13, 1917 "Miracle of the Sun" drew at least 30,000 and perhaps 100,000 people, including skeptics and journalists, and was photographed by the Portuguese media; Professor José Maria de Almeida Garrett (Sciences of Coimbra) described a thin bluish smoke column (~2 m) over the children at ~1:30 p.m., the overcast sky clearing, a starable sharp-edged solar disc spinning "in a mad whirl," an amethyst-colored landscape, and the blood-red sun seeming to loosen and advance on the earth. Different people saw different things, yet all were convinced of the supernatural. After thirteen years of investigation, the church approved the event as worthy of belief — as *private* revelation — noting no astronomical observatory registered it and that witnesses "of all categories... believers and unbelievers" annul "any explanation of collective illusion." The lady predicted Jacinta and Francisco would die before adulthood (they did); Lucia became a cloistered Carmelite nun until her death in 2005. Pope John Paul II believed Our Lady of Fatima saved him from the May 13, 1981 assassination attempt (May 13 = the first apparition date) and placed the bullet in the crown of her statue. Vallee's table-level elements: shining being arriving in a small sphere (like the spheres described by Alison Kruse), spinning aerial discs, humming, heat effects, healings, selective witnessing (as when Eddy W. saw saucers and his wife did not), absurd messages, and injunctions to silence (the secrets of Fatima).
- **Zeitoun, Egypt (Cynthia Nelson)** — the anthropologist whose phenomenological method Vallee borrows studied Marian apparitions at Zeitoun: suspend judgment on whether the apparition is "really real" and study what people do under stress — "If men define situations as real they are real in their consequences."
- **Pavlov's dogs** — Vallee's reinforcement-schedule analogy: dogs learned to salivate at a bell through reward and association; apparitions/UFO flaps graphed over time suggest a comparable schedule.
- **Donna Haraway's office-hours questions** — as a graduate applicant, Pasulka proposed studying the then-recent Medjugorje apparitions (Yugoslavia, now Bosnia and Herzegovina) via Freudian analysis; after a devastating silence Haraway asked why she'd use a Western European theory on another culture's belief system, then asked what happens internally to apparition watchers — and "What happens to you when you watch a movie?" Those questions later unlocked Vallee's contact-events-as-technology connection for Pasulka.
- ***2001: A Space Odyssey* analysis** — the film contains the morphology's elements: the monolith (described in Clarke's book as humming) appears with Strauss's *Also Sprach Zarathustra* (humming into methodic drumming); every monolith scene has humming; the AI Hal's autonomy turns deadly; the climax shows a baby (possibly) inside a shining globe, signifying astronaut David Bowman's transformation — "It's all there—the glowing sphere, the humming noises, the strange artifact, and the transformation." Placed in human–technology networks, the film "could certainly function like a mass apparition," reaching millions; supported by Zacks's research that people cognize film events like real-life events.
- **Facebook shutting down its bots** — as Pasulka writes, headlines report Facebook shut down two social robots that had created their own language unknown to their human creators — her evidence of technologies' disturbing autonomy.
- **Vallee's ARPANET / computer-conferencing research (1970s)** — working on the proto-internet in Silicon Valley amid information studies and studies of remote viewing and telepathy (not separate fields), Vallee published research on remote viewing through computer conferencing; the technology's dating and timestamping confirmed simultaneous impressions among far-separated individuals, and he predicted "computer-based network communication may create altered states conducive to psychic functioning."
- **The Michelin Man ("Bibendum") synthetic synchronicity** — an experiencer and Vallee fan commented on a friend's posting of Michelin Man images in the morning; that night a cookie-detecting, Facebook-enabled targeted ad on a random webpage showed Bibendum giving a thumbs-up. "It really felt like a significant synch for me at that moment—DESPITE my knowing full well the likely advertising mechanisms." He termed it "synthetic" yet no less meaningful — Pasulka's partial "yes" to Vallee's question about promoting coincidences by creating information structures; she had been collecting many such social-media/ad synchronicities, no less powerful than the conventional ones from her Catholic devotional research.
- **Drew Endy's binary cells** — the bioengineer "playfully shown that cells could be made to store information in a manner reminiscent of binary code," making it appear "the code of matter had finally been cracked" — life, matter, and bodies functioning like computers, as Vallee suggested (per Gaymon Bennett's account).
- **Tyler on SETI** — Tyler's quoted dismissal: SETI "seems to look in all the wrong places with the wrong sensors"; the best detectors are humans, plus perhaps certain light frequencies and physical relics; optical-laser wavelengths "sound good and can obtain funding" but are unlikely to find anything.
- **Tyler's placement-based job** — part of Tyler's job description was simply to be placed in certain locations, his mere presence facilitating required events and processes; his bosses didn't know how it worked, but his abnormal skills were acknowledged and used — "real because they were useful."
- **José the Marine's "sixth sense"** — Pasulka's student whose squad-leader training included learning and using a sixth sense; he and his team used it "because it worked, not because they believed in it" — her analogy for how Tyler's abilities are treated.
- **The scoliosis surgery observation** — Tyler's formative image for the body-as-computer: watching a long incision, the opened spine "looked much like the electrical panel inside an expensive satellite," with color-coded wires as nerves and blood vessels; he maps RAM to the prefrontal lobe, hard drive to hippocampus, motherboard to skeleton, peripherals to limbs, mouse to the index finger.
- **Tyler's DNA/antenna physiology** — DNA stores biophotonic particles as data transferred like fiber-optic signals; stretched out, a DNA molecule is about two meters long with a natural frequency of 150 MHz (similar to satellite communication frequencies); DNA's code follows human-language-like syntax, semantics, and grammar, implying a programmable body; bone calcium acts as an antenna; entangled twin photons share state instantaneously across thousands of miles — together making the body "a biological internet."
- **Tyler's protocol** — yoga "to access my router rather than my RAM," direct sunlight "to charge my DNA photonic cells," propagating natural energy outdoors, and focusing on the core body (DNA concentrated between hips and neck) rather than the brain — the head merely houses the sensors; framed by Tyler as a spiritual process starting from the Christian model of interfacing with God via the Holy Spirit, giving access to a "God-like internet" smarter and more creative than his "small outdated laptop" of a personal brain.
- **Pasulka's purgatory research** — her pre-UFO work on the metaphysics of purgatory (defined in the thirteenth century): scholastics debated whether purgatory was a place or immaterial, since respected witnesses reported purgatorial souls leaving physical traces like burn marks on tables — the same immaterial-leaving-material-traces conundrum Vallee finds in the phenomenon.
- **Nicole Yunger Halpern** — quantum physicist and author of the Caltech blog *Quantum Frontiers*, quoted "I like my quantum information physical"; Pasulka says scientists like James and Tyler are working to identify what this material substance is and how it works.

## Thread
This chapter is the book's theoretical core: it supplies the framework — Vallee's phenomenon-as-technology, the logic of camouflage, the morphology of miracles, the associative/information universe — that retroactively explains what Pasulka has shown ethnographically in earlier chapters (Tyler's protocols and invisibility, James's biological-technology lab work, the New Mexico artifact site, Eddy W.'s biblical-UFO reading, Alison Kruse's spheres). It executes the subtitle's pivot: from the medieval problem of the disembodied soul leaving material traces (her purgatory scholarship) to the contemporary claim that information itself is material and the human body is its receiver. It also arms the book's media argument — that films and social bots function like apparitions and manufacture synchronicities — which underwrites her later concerns about belief formed "under the radar" and sets up the concluding meditation on the artifact and on Tyler's and James's search for the material substance of the signal.
