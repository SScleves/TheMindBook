# American Cosmic: UFOs, Religion, Technology — D.W. Pasulka: Book Map

## The thesis in one page

D.W. Pasulka's central claim is that the contemporary belief in UFOs and extraterrestrials is **a new religious form being born in real time**, and that its medium and its sacred object are one and the same: **technology**. As a historian and scholar of religion, she brackets the metaphysical question of whether the objects are "real" (a religious-studies method she borrows from Jacques Vallee's 1979 UN formulation, "the belief in space visitors is independent of the physical reality of the UFO phenomenon") and studies instead the incontestably real *social effects* of perceived contact. Her two structuring revelations, both sprung on her after she pivoted from studying the Catholic doctrine of purgatory to studying UFO cultures in January 2012, are: (1) that elite, successful, innovative scientists — not "cranks and weirdos," as Stephen Hawking dismissed them — secretly believe in and study the phenomenon, forming an anonymous "Invisible College" or parallel research tradition; and (2) that what is marketed to the public through media barely resembles what those scientists actually find, being built instead on rumor, hoax, and disinformation — yet it is precisely this media apparatus that *manufactures and sustains* the belief. Pasulka reads the whole complex through the history of religions, where a **contact event** (a hierophany, the perceived interface between humans and an intelligent nonhuman being from the sky) is followed by **interpretations**, then by **institutions** — including governments — with a stake in managing how the event is read.

The book marshals ethnography, religious-studies theory, cognitive science, and media analysis toward a layered argument. Its sub-claims escalate: perceived contact is **"simultaneously imagined and real"** — Pasulka makes "no ontological claim, that extraterrestrials are real in the sense that couches are real, although they could be," only that "perceived contact has very real effects with powerful social implications." The **human is a receiver and transmitter** of the phenomenon's information (a model her scientist-informants articulate through DNA-as-antenna and quantum-field theories), so that creativity, revelation, and even entire technologies are framed as "downloads" from a nonhuman source. **Media technologies function like the scribes and redactors of the first century CE**, embedding belief below the level of consciousness — imagination "exteriorized" into screens that then determine what is in our heads. And drawing on Heidegger's "The Question Concerning Technology" and Carl Jung's naming of the UFO as a "technological angel," she argues the deepest subject is not aliens at all but **humanity's quasi-religious, largely unreflective relationship to technology itself** — a relationship that, on Heidegger's warning, could birth a new epoch as thoroughly dominated by technology as the medieval world was by God. Her closing framing answer to the question posed throughout — "Is it real, or is it imaginary?" — is: **"it is both."**

## How the argument builds

The book is built as a **frame narrative** — "it began with a journey, and it ends with a journey" — that opens at a New Mexico desert "crash" site and closes at the Vatican, moving from ground zero of the UFO myth to ground zero of the Catholic faith. Around that spine, the argument escalates in roughly four movements.

**1. Framing and charter (Preface + Introduction, chs. 0–1).** The Preface installs the three load-bearing elements before any argument begins: Jacques Vallee as presiding theorist-informant (whose double "orthodox/unorthodox" biography models the theme of hidden histories), Silicon Valley as a field site where technology, secrecy, and the sacred converge, and the Heidegger/Jung frame that makes the UFO a *religious* symbol of a technological epoch rather than a hardware question. The Introduction converts this into a full program and methodological charter: the two revelations (scientist-believers; media-manufactured belief), the religious-studies method of bracketing, and Pasulka's own entry story (purgatory archives → orbs → a UFO conference → meeting the "meta-experiencers" → John Mack's "epistemological shock").

**2. The ethnographic anchor: the invisible scientists (chs. 2–3).** Pasulka delivers her two pseudonymous scientist-Experiencers and the desert fieldwork. Tyler D. (ch. 2) is the intuitive practitioner — space-industry entrepreneur, "meta-experiencer," heir to a lineage of Tsiolkovsky, Jack Parsons, and Colonel Philip Corso — who receives technology "downloads" via a bodily protocol but cannot explain the process. James (ch. 3) is "the consummate professor" who *can* articulate a formula: the phenomenon is real, interfaces with a genetically determined "brain receiver," possibly taps a quantum field of information, and leaves a traceable physiological "signature." Both chapters stage the New Mexico artifact recovery that the whole book will circle back to, and announce the thesis of "the religion of technology."

**3. The mechanisms of belief (chs. 4–5).** The ethnography widens from the invisible elite to the visible grassroots and then to the media machine itself. "In the Field" (ch. 4) embeds with debunker-believers who film anomalies while fighting the internet's "fabricated UFO," supplying the book's clearest mechanism — sighting → recording → viral amplification → virtual dogma — and Jung's "amplificatory interpretation" plus Pasulka's own "book encounter." "When Star Wars Became Real" (ch. 5) is the theoretical engine room: using her own stint consulting on *The Conjuring*, plus cognitive science (Zacks, Loftus, Landsberg, the Stanford VR lab), she shows how films and immersive media manufacture real belief, practice, and memory below consciousness — the "Total Recall Effect."

**4. The theoretical core and the receiver (chs. 6–7), then resolution (chs. 8–9).** "The Material Code" (ch. 6) is Pasulka's sustained reading of Vallee's *The Invisible College*: the phenomenon behaves like a technology, protected by a "logic of camouflage" whose engine is the absurd, and best exemplified not by a UFO case but by the 1917 Fatima apparitions. "The Human Receiver" (ch. 7) shows the receiver-model articulated from *inside* the experiencer community through Rey Hernandez's conversion and the founding of FREE with Apollo astronaut Edgar Mitchell. The two closing units resolve the frame: "Real and Imaginary" (ch. 8) takes Tyler to Rome, where his religious conversion dramatizes the thesis and the seventeenth-century bilocating nun Sister Maria of Agreda becomes his historical double; and the Conclusion, "The Artifact" (ch. 9), returns to the desert object — judged "so anomalous as to be incomprehensible" — and deliberately refuses to resolve it, turning finally to Heidegger's "Only a God can save us now."

## Chapter index

**[00-preface-a-tour-of-silicon-valley.md] — ch. 0 (Preface): "A Tour of Silicon Valley with Jacques Vallee."**
Pasulka opens with an ethnographic scene: Jacques Vallee — "the father of the modern study of UFOs and an early visionary of the internet" — drives her and colleague Robbie Graham through Silicon Valley, saying "There are many secrets in this valley." She sketches Vallee's "orthodox history" (helped NASA map Mars, early ARPANET engineer, venture capitalist, consultant on Spielberg's *Close Encounters* — the Truffaut character is based on him) and his "unorthodox history" adjacent to the Stanford Research Institute, whose declassified Stargate Project studied remote viewing, precognition, and ESP and "allegedly developed, intentionally or not, psychic cosmonauts." Reading Vallee through Heidegger's "The Question Concerning Technology," she argues the human relationship with technology is "religiouslike" — technology as sacred frame and "saving power" — and, via Jung, that "the symbol for this new epoch is the UFO." The Swedenborg example (claimed angel-assisted travel to Mercury, Mars, Venus, and the moon, published in *Life on Other Planets*, 1758) shows psychic travel predates SRI. Its role: it plants theorist, field site, and theoretical frame, and promises the "complex story," not Spielberg's "This is Hollywood" simple one.

**[01-introduction.md] — ch. 1: "Introduction."**
The book's thesis statement and methodological charter. Pasulka lays out the twin subjects — religion (with the UFO phenomenon as case study) and technology — and her religious-studies method of *bracketing* truth claims to study social effects. Her memorable frame is the printing press giving rise to Sola Scriptura, an "uncontroversial" example of a technology reshaping religion. She narrates her origin story (a prior multiyear study of the Catholic doctrine of purgatory turned up archival reports, 1300–1880, of orbs, luminous beings, and disclike aerial objects; inheriting Brenda Denzler's library; a local UFO conference where experiencers described the very phenomena she'd found in Catholic history). Strongest material: Robert Bigelow on *60 Minutes* ("I don't give a damn... It's not going to change the reality of what I know"); Hawking's 2008 "cranks and weirdos" line as "the lie"; physicist Eric Davis calling UFOs "craft of a supremely advanced technology"; belief polls (a 2008 Scripps poll: >50% of Americans believe in extraterrestrial life, 74% of 18–24-year-olds). It installs the Invisible College, meta-experiencers, contact event, epistemological shock (Mack's term), and "agents of information," closing "Is it real, or is it imaginary?... it is both."

**[02-the-invisible-tyler-d.md] — ch. 2: "The Invisible Tyler D."**
The ethnographic anchor and first numbered chapter. Under an unfinished *Fight Club* epigraph, Pasulka narrates the blindfolded drive with James Master to a no-fly-zone New Mexico site where believers hold a craft crashed in 1947, framing it as a **hierophany** (Moses's burning bush as classic example) whose consumerism (Roswell's museum and four-day festival) parallels Lourdes. She introduces her central informant, the pseudonymous "Tyler D." — space-shuttle engineer with 40+ patents who "get[s] paid to think," receives fully-formed "memories" he attributes to off-planet intelligence, and follows a bodily "protocol" (eight-plus-one sleep, no coffee — "Coffee really messes up the signal") because "our DNA is a receptor and transmitter... Humans are a type of satellite." Memorable material: the Challenger disaster and astronaut Judith Resnik's death; Tyler's Carl Sagan "book encounter"; the shielded "smart room"; the healing of "Jane"; and the lineage of Tsiolkovsky, Jack Parsons (Crowley/Hubbard rituals, died at 37), and Colonel Corso. Its role: it establishes the "code of silence" vs. academic transparency, "the invisibles," "meta-experiencers," and the phrase "ground zero of the new religion," and recovers the artifacts the book will circle back to.

**[03-james-master-of-the-multiverse.md] — ch. 3: "James: Master of the Multiverse."**
Pasulka's second informant: "James," a pseudonymous endowed-chair molecular biologist and the first scientist she met who was also "out" as an **experiencer**. She traces his lifelong anomalous encounters (childhood paralyzed night visitors — "I was not asleep. Oh, and I was paralyzed"; a soundless twenty-foot light formation over a boyhood paper route; a translucent bedside presence in his thirties) and his recognition of his own life in John Mack's *Abduction*. Strongest material: his conference declaration that "the phenomenon commonly referred to as UFOs exists... it interacts with humans, but we cannot as yet explain it"; his research program (a heritable "6th sense," a "brain receiver," a traceable physiological "signature," a quantum "spooky action at a distance" field of information); his word "bedeviled" and his goal of an "antidote" to let humans say "no"; the fully-human "men in black" (a university researcher with intelligence ties and an aerospace-firm scientist); and the foil-like desert artifact, a "donation," whose analysis made it "hard to believe they were made on Earth." Supporting cases: Ramanujan and the goddess Lakshmi; the 1953 Robertson Panel / Durant Report "training and debunking" campaign; the invisible-gorilla experiment; Jacobsen's *Area 51* vs. Corso on Roswell (Pasulka "tended to believe" Jacobsen while "living within" Corso). Its role: completes the scientist pair and announces "the religion of technology."

**[04-in-the-field.md] — ch. 4: "In the Field: The War Is Virtual, the Blood Is Real."**
Pasulka leaves the invisible scientists for the public, grassroots layer, embedding with Scott Browne's Facebook study group "In the Field" — trained videographers who film anomalies while debunking CGI and lens-flare hoaxes. Central paradox: "Scott is a debunker because he is a true believer," and his unpaid, harassed work is a religious **vocation**. The chapter gives the book's clearest mechanism of belief: sighting → viral video → "the fabricated UFO is the real UFO." Memorable material: Jung's "living myth" / "universal mass rumor" and **amplificatory interpretation**; Pasulka's coinage the **book encounter** (Tyler's Sagan, James's Mack, Scott's Blum, and — across centuries — St. Teresa of Avila's transverberation framed via Catholic angelology); Alison Kruse's "Murrysville UFO" (an FAA data disc confirming no aircraft) and her "punks"; David Stinnett's C140-sighting VHS synchronicity and his rule that a researcher must experience synchronicities "without being convinced"; the Marian Keech failed-prophecy study; and Scott's own faked UFOs recirculating as "real." Its role: stages the ontological pivot from Pasulka's rejected "only virtual" claim — Vallee's rebuke, "It is unwise to ignore the ontological aspects of the UFO" — to "I had given up the dualism of real and virtual."

**[05-when-star-wars-became-real.md] — ch. 5: "When Star Wars Became Real" (subtitled in the book "The Mechanisms of Belief").**
The theoretical engine room. Drawing on her own work consulting for the screenwriters of *The Conjuring*, Pasulka identifies the media techniques — the **mechanisms of belief** — that generate real belief, practice, and memory: the "based on true events" strategy, realist montage, cultural authorities, and splicing digital characters into historical footage. She names the **Total Recall Effect** ("They become us... in the form of memories") and grounds it in cognitive science: Zacks (the brain does not sort real, film, and novel events into separate "buckets"), the Stanford VR finding that "the brain often fails to differentiate between virtual experiences and real ones," Loftus's reconstructive memory ("a little like a Wikipedia page"), and Landsberg's prosthetic memory. Memorable cases: *The Great Martian War 1913–1917* specialist factual programming; the "If Star Wars Was Real" Photoshop project and the religion of **Jediism** (the 2002 UK census; a Jedi Knight at her community talk); Robert Ager's reading of Kubrick's monolith as the cinema screen ("human–monolith continuum"); the Betty and Barney Hill abduction case with the "wraparound eyes" that matched an *Outer Limits* episode aired twelve days before the hypnosis session; and Disney's 1995 *Alien Encounters from Tomorrow Land*. Its role: explains how belief is manufactured regardless of the bracketed reality question — "even if experiences are real, they are remembered 'with and through' media."

**[06-the-material-code.md] — ch. 6: "The Material Code: From the Disembodied Soul to the Materiality of Quantum Information."**
The book's theoretical core: a sustained engagement with Jacques Vallee built around his 1975 *The Invisible College*, which he gifted her after a 2014 Big Sur seminar. Vallee's thesis: the phenomenon "has a technological basis" yet generates "religious" emotions, and is protected by a **logic of camouflage** whose engine is the absurd ("It is sneaky, and time is on its side") — modeled on early Christianity as a subterranean counterculture that became Rome's state religion, and possibly working like a Zen **koan** ("a mass koan, working on millions"). Strongest material: the "two datasets" (witnesses tell authorities the empirical part, sympathetic listeners the psychic part); the parallel with Catholic devotional vs. doctrinal religion and private vs. public revelation; Vallee's **Morphology of Miracles** table; and the centerpiece reconstruction of **Fatima** (1915–1917) — the children Lucia, Jacinta, and Francisco; the enervating shining youth; the crowds hearing a buzzing "like that of a bee"; and the October 13, 1917 "Miracle of the Sun" before 30,000–100,000 people. It extends Vallee through Haraway, Andy Clark's extended cognition, Hayles's "new unconscious," and *2001: A Space Odyssey* ("The beings really are in our heads"), the **associative universe** ("space and time... a cultural artifact made possible by the invention of graph paper"), Pasulka's own "synthetic synchronicity" research (the Michelin Man targeted ad), and Tyler's DNA-as-biological-internet physiology (natural frequency ~150 MHz; "I like my quantum information physical"). Its role: supplies the framework that retroactively explains the earlier ethnography.

**[07-the-human-receiver.md] — ch. 7: "The Human Receiver: Matter, Information, Energy . . . Contact."**
The fullest portrait of the receiver thesis, told through Rey Hernandez — a Miami lawyer and "pure rationalist" atheist whose 2012 anomalous events (the healing of the paralyzed dog Niña; a called-down craft; conflicting timelines he later read as "missing time") converted him, "much like a religious conversion experience." His devout wife Dulce read the identical encounters as angels. Rey's directed "book encounter" (200+ NDE/consciousness books in four months) and a rush-hour "download" led him to cofound **FREE** (the Dr. Edgar Mitchell Foundation for Research into Extraterrestrial Encounters) with Apollo 14 astronaut Edgar Mitchell, Harvard astrophysicist Rudy Schild, and Mary Rodwell. Memorable material: Mitchell's **quantum hologram theory of consciousness** and **dyadic model** (samadhi as resonance with the "zero point field"; his own returning-from-the-moon *savikalpa samadhi* epiphany — "that's me"); his claim that extraterrestrials dismantled space-launched weapons; the "esoteric cosmonauts" lineage (Tsiolkovsky, Parsons, Tyler, Mitchell) who "may be on the fringe of our future"; and the Jungian "some UFOs are not photogenic." Its role: caps the mechanisms-of-belief thread with the **Hangar 1 "UFO Home Invasion" betrayal** — Rey's positive MUFON report morphed into a terrifying home invasion on the History Channel — closing "The truth is potentially 'out there,' but it's unlikely to be found in media productions."

**[08-real-and-imaginary.md] — ch. 8: "Real and Imaginary: Tyler D.'s Spiritual Conversion in Rome."**
The final numbered chapter closes the frame: Pasulka brings Tyler to the Vatican Secret Archives and the Vatican Observatory at Castel Gandolfo, mirroring his blindfolded New Mexico trip. Sent to analyze the canonization records of two seventeenth-century levitators — St. Joseph of Cupertino (canonized) and **Sister Maria of Agreda** (never canonized) — she instead witnesses "perhaps the most miraculous and strange event" of six years' research: Tyler's profound conversion, culminating months later in his becoming Catholic at a Mass with Pope Francis at Santa Sabina. Theoretically it delivers her most careful formulation: perceived contact is "simultaneously imagined and real"; "I am not making an ontological claim, that extraterrestrials are real in the sense that couches are real, although they could be." Memorable material: Maria's claimed **bilocation** to colonial New Mexico and the Jumanos' "lady in blue," "strategically politicized" by Benavides to justify Spanish colonization; Tyler and Maria as parallel "inadvertent colonists" who made imagined "first contact"; Tyler's classified fame opening Vatican doors Pasulka's professorship could not ("My archive badge is good for six months"); Father McDonnell's hospital last rites ("I get more from them than I give to them... It was God"); the carved door of Santa Sabina depicting levitations and ascensions; and Brenda Denzler's note that ufology is "overwhelmingly white and male" and over 90% Anglo-American (against which Pasulka sets the Nation of Islam's 1930s UFO narrative). Its role: dramatizes rather than describes the religion-in-the-making and hands off to the Conclusion.

**[09-conclusion-the-artifact.md] — ch. 9 (Conclusion): "The Artifact."**
The Conclusion returns to the desert object — or the one that "was perhaps planted" for her to find — and reports that research scientists judged it "so anomalous as to be incomprehensible," one saying "It could not have been made in this universe" (though they did not conclude extraterrestrial origin, remaining "comfortable, if amazed"). Under Vallee's epigraph "Credo quia absurdum, eh, Diana?", Pasulka reads the object as a relic: sacred artifacts are "objects of power. Part of their power lies in their mystery" (the Shroud of Turin as paradigm; "One cannot put an angel under a microscope"). Memorable material: James's method of steering graduate students toward anomalies, and Kuhn's advice to John Mack that scientific revolutions come through anomalies; the December 2017 *New York Times* / Luis Elizondo Pentagon-program article promoting the mechanism of "realism" and triggering "unofficial disclosure"; and Tyler's friend's dream of "a separate universe... turtles all the way down" carried in his backpack, which Tyler reads (via Whitley Strieber) as artifact–observer symbiosis. Its role: it deliberately refuses resolution and reframes the whole study around Shklovsky's "The prey runs to the predator" (our unreflective embrace of media and technology) and Heidegger's last-interview line, "Only a God can save us now."

## The evidentiary strategy

Pasulka's argument works by **braiding several distinct kinds of evidence** and asking that they be weighed not as proof of extraterrestrials but as documentation of how belief forms and functions. The recurring strands:

- **Participant ethnography, offered as testimony.** The backbone is Pasulka's own six years embedded with informants — the pseudonymous scientists Tyler D. and James, the grassroots researchers Scott Browne, Alison Kruse, and David Stinnett, the experiencer-convert Rey Hernandez, and astronaut Edgar Mitchell. She foregrounds her own entanglement ("reluctant bridge," "a pawn in the process of belief formation"), so the evidence is explicitly first-person and situated, not detached observation. She positions herself in the odd place of "almost confirming a myth."

- **The credible witness / testimony as evidence.** A structural move borrowed from both law and the history of religions: "the 'credible witness' — testimony accompanied by the witness's credentials — is an important credibility-lending feature shared by religious and UFO cultures." Two eminent scientists attesting to an unexplainable artifact is presented as "structurally the same as the founding documents of Christianity and Buddhism" — the Gospels as "the testimonies of the apostles." The weight requested is not "this proves the object is alien" but "credible people demonstrably have these experiences."

- **Historical and religious-studies parallel.** Contact event → interpretation → institution; hierophany; relic; devotional vs. doctrinal religion; private vs. public revelation. Pasulka repeatedly maps modern UFO material onto Catholic and biblical precedent (Fatima, Lourdes, the Shroud of Turin, Ezekiel's wheel, Samuel and Eli, St. Teresa of Avila, Sister Maria of Agreda), arguing the *logic* is not rational but patterned and socially consequential.

- **The named theorist (Vallee) and his method.** Vallee is the book's principal intellectual authority, supplying the bracketing method ("the belief in space visitors is independent of the physical reality of the UFO phenomenon"), the phenomenon-as-technology thesis, the "logic of camouflage," the Morphology of Miracles, the "two datasets," and the running ethic "Believe no one. Believe nothing" / "Trust no one. Do not even trust what you see."

- **Cognitive science and media studies, offered against her own data.** Pasulka deliberately juxtaposes external, internalist explanations without always adjudicating: Heather Berlin on the flow-state prefrontal shutdown that makes ideas *feel* external; Zacks on conflated event models; Loftus and Baker on reconstructive and pseudo-memory; Landsberg on prosthetic memory; Andy Clark's extended cognition; Hayles's technogenesis and "new unconscious." These are marshaled to explain how belief and memory are manufactured — while she notes that the physical artifacts "seem to solidify the external agent as real, not imaginary," keeping the tension live.

- **The scientists' own framing: physical evidence and quantum theory.** The "nuts and bolts" artifact analysis (James's molecular microscope, the New Mexico material, the Conclusion's verdict), and the quantum-physics vocabulary the informants reach for — "spooky action at a distance," non-locality, the zero-point field, entanglement, the quantum hologram theory — offered as the explanatory framework of choice in her research circles, which she relays but does not endorse ("I like my quantum information physical").

- **Declassified documents and institutional history.** The Stargate Project and SRI; the 1953 Robertson Panel and Durant Report's "training and debunking" recommendation; government "perception management" and "information operations"; the FBI file on the Nation of Islam; and the 2017 Elizondo disclosure — presented as evidence that powerful institutions have long monitored and shaped UFO interpretation.

Throughout, Pasulka is explicit that these are to be weighed as **social facts about belief**, not verdicts on ontology — her constant refrain that her interest is in effects "which are incontestably very real," and her insistence, even at the Conclusion's "It could not have been made in this universe," that "I can't solve the mystery of the artifact, but I have seen how its reality has inspired belief and, as Jung notes, rumors that spin mythologies."

## Reading it critically

The notes themselves record numerous tensions, hedges, and open questions — several of them raised by Pasulka on purpose, as constitutive of her subject rather than flaws to be resolved.

- **The deliberate ontological hedge.** The book's central methodological statement is a refusal: "I am not making an ontological claim, that extraterrestrials are real in the sense that couches are real, although they could be." "Is it real, or is it imaginary?... it is both." This is a feature, not a bug — but it means the book never adjudicates whether the phenomenon is anything more than a socially real belief, and even its most dramatic physical evidence (the artifact "could not have been made in this universe") is held at arm's length: the scientists "did not conclude extraterrestrial origin."

- **The disinformation / "pawn" problem, unresolved by design.** Pasulka repeatedly flags that she "stays open to the possibility that she and James were pawns in a disinformation plot," that "the message had been managed," and that the artifact "was perhaps planted" for her to find. She does not need to resolve this — but it undercuts any load-bearing reliance on the physical finds, and she says so.

- **Internal / external explanations left in tension.** She juxtaposes the "download" model (creativity from a nonhuman source) with neuroscience's internalist account (Berlin's prefrontal shutdown; Loftus's reconstructive memory) "without adjudicating," while noting the physical artifacts complicate the tidy internalist reduction. The reader is left holding both.

- **The self-implicating researcher.** Pasulka discloses she became a "cultural authority" for *The Conjuring*, a "reluctant bridge" for media, and "perhaps a pawn" in belief formation — and that she "fell headlong into Nietzsche's abyss." She narrates her own synchronicities (the New Year's *Gay Science* aphorisms; writing about *The Conjuring*'s screenwriters before meeting them) while invoking Stinnett's discipline of experiencing synchronicities "without being convinced." This candor is a strength but complicates her neutrality.

- **Speculation flagged as speculation.** Vallee's "schedule of reinforcement" reading of UFO flaps is offered "as speculation"; the koan / "mass koan" idea is a "wonders whether"; Tyler's DNA-antenna physiology and the quantum-field theories are relayed "without endorsement." The book's boldest mechanisms are consistently presented as its informants' claims, not Pasulka's conclusions.

- **Demographic and historiographic caveats Pasulka herself raises.** She records (via Brenda Denzler) that ufology is "overwhelmingly white and male" and over 90% Anglo-American, and that historians of ufology "with few exceptions ignore African American and indigenous UFO traditions" that predate the standard 1947 origin date (the Nation of Islam by the 1930s). She also notes the selective erasure of Maria of Agreda's cosmographies.

**Chapter-numbering discrepancy (transcription flag).** The unit-note frontmatter numbers the files sequentially 0–9, and this map follows those numbers per instruction. But the note texts record that the book's *own internal* chapter numbering differs from the note numbering: **[05-when-star-wars-became-real.md]** states it is "numbered 4 in the book, subtitled 'The Mechanisms of Belief,'" and **[07-the-human-receiver.md]** states it is "numbered 6 in the book's own chapter sequence." So note-chapter *n* does not reliably equal book-chapter *n* from ch. 4 onward. When citing to a physical copy of *American Cosmic*, verify against the book's table of contents rather than these note numbers.

**Other minor flags in the notes.** [05] spells the surviving-shark belief "Magaledon"/"Megalodon" inconsistently (an apparent transcription artifact). Several figure references carry the book's own possibly-inconsistent numbering (e.g., [04] cites "Figure 3.1"–"Figure 3.4" and [07] cites "Figures 6.1/6.2," reflecting the book's internal chapter numbers, not the note numbers). Names given only as first names or pseudonyms (Tyler D., James, James Master, Eddy, Jane, John) are anonymized informants per the book's confidentiality regime, and should not be read as fully identifiable individuals.

**Cross-book connections are marked speculative.** Every unit note carries a "Connections" section whose cross-book links (to *the-holographic-universe*, *my-big-toe*, *the-simulation-hypothesis*) are explicitly labeled **"speculative/thematic, NOT Pasulka's own references"** — reader-drawn parallels only. Pasulka cites none of those works. Those links belong to this knowledge base's thematic apparatus, not to *American Cosmic*, and should never be attributed to the author.
