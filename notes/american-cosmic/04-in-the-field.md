# In the Field: The War Is Virtual, the Blood Is Real

## In one paragraph
In this chapter D.W. Pasulka leaves the invisible scientists (Tyler and James) and enters the public, grassroots side of ufology, embedding with Scott Browne and his Facebook-based international study group "In the Field" — trained videographers, photographers, and graphic designers who film anomalous aerial objects while debunking the CGI, lens flares, and hoaxes that flood the internet. Through Scott, fellow field researchers Alison Kruse and David Stinnett, and experiencers like Eddy, she documents how media technologies inform UFO belief: an anomalous sighting becomes a video, goes viral, and mutates into a virtual, quasi-dogmatic object far removed from the original event. She retrieves Carl Jung's 1950s framework — the UFO as "living myth" and "universal mass rumor," and his concept of "amplificatory interpretation" — and adds her own mechanism, the "book encounter," by which experiencers (Tyler, James, Scott, and even St. Teresa of Avila) find the book that frames their anomalous experiences within a cultural narrative. She also examines synchronicity and the phenomenon's tendency to trick and deceive, arguing (with David Stinnett, Vallee, and George Hansen) that the disciplined researcher must experience synchronicities without believing them. The chapter ends with Pasulka reclaiming her once-"humiliating" thesis in a new key: within today's digital media ecology the real/virtual dualism has collapsed, so the "reality" of the UFO is virtual — without that making it unreal — and, like Kubrick's monolith, the phenomenon can be both the screen and the alien at once.

## Core claims
- Pasulka claims that today "the fabricated UFO is the real UFO" for all intents and purposes: physical and virtual worlds intersect and permeate one another, and the profitable, hoax-driven virtual UFO now dominates the phenomenon's public life.
- She argues that Scott Browne's debunking work is a religious vocation (Latin *vocatio*, "to be called"): a sacred, monetarily uncompensated task he cannot abandon despite threats and harassment — and that he is a debunker precisely *because* he is a true believer in a real phenomenon.
- The book says In the Field demonstrates, in real time, "the processes involved in the formation of a dogma": a witness's video gains momentum online, becomes virtual, orthodox, and far removed from the original event. This is how media technologies inform UFO belief; the members are anti-dogmatists trying to slow this tidal wave.
- Pasulka argues that Jung's 1950s claim that UFOs are "not photogenic" has been overturned — since the 1950s UFOs have become photogenic — but that Jung's methodological legacy (the UFO as living myth, universal mass rumor, and object of amplificatory interpretation) is prescient once the internet is added.
- She claims Jung called the UFO *apparently* impossible, not impossible — a methodological strategy to move study into the psyche — and that Jung missed the key point: it is the *potential physical reality* of the UFO that makes it a living myth. "Its realism is what gives it its bite. It is also what makes it religious."
- She argues that X-Files memes ("I want to believe," "The truth is out there") postpone rather than express belief: the postponed truth is not false but "future-real."
- Pasulka claims every experiencer she interviewed who linked their anomalies to UFOs had a "book encounter" — a seemingly serendipitous book that supplied an explanatory framework — and that this is one step in the process by which anomalous experiences get attached to a cultural narrative (UFO, or, as with Teresa of Avila, God and Catholicism).
- She says religious and UFO interpretations of anomalous experience are not mutually exclusive: a "biblical–UFO" hermeneutic (Eddy, Barry Downing, Michael J. S. Carter, the Nation of Islam) reads the experiences as both.
- Via David Stinnett, she reports that synchronicities are one aspect of the phenomenon ("If a researcher does not experience them, he or she is not really doing the research right") but that the phenomenon tricks and deceives (citing Vallee and George Hansen), so the right track is to refuse to be convinced by one's own synchronicities.
- She recounts that her early conclusion — that UFOs are real only in a virtual sense — earned Jacques Vallee's chastisement ("It is unwise to ignore the ontological aspects of the UFO"), and that her task became documenting the formation of a new religious form, not settling the mystery's ontological status.
- She argues that witnesses' attempts to correct the phenomenon's virtual lives (Scott's dogged debunking) are noble but ultimately futile: cultural narratives about the phenomenon have little or nothing to do with what witnesses actually captured, and amplification now runs through bots and algorithms, not just people.
- Her closing claim: within the digital landscape the modernist real/unreal distinction "ceases to be meaningful"; UFO theories (extraterrestrial, ultra-terrestrial, interdimensional) that presuppose that framework miss their own historical context. The reality of the phenomenon is virtual — and she has "given up the dualism of real and virtual." Media technologies have as much impact on human bodies as biotechnologies, perhaps more.

## Concepts
- **Debunker** — in ufology, a person who doesn't believe in the phenomenon and actively discredits witnesses, including trained observers like pilots; close to the worst name one can be called in the field. Pasulka inverts it: Scott is a debunker *because* he is a true believer.
- **True believer (debunker–believer paradox)** — Scott's stance: he fights the fabricated, virtual UFO precisely because he believes there is a real thing, which he and his group believe they have photographed.
- **In the Field (the group)** — Scott Browne's Facebook-based international study group of trained videographers, photographers, and graphic designers who pursue and film the phenomenon regularly; bound by a code of ethics and methodology (deviate and you're deleted). Three membership requirements: (1) shoot your own footage/stills of anomalous objects regularly and present them; (2) know the basics of the field; (3) keep an open mind with no preconceived notions (angels, aliens, demons, galactic federation). Its threefold work: observe and study; document and capture; share.
- **UFO ethic** — Scott's own phrase for his code: systematic study, no CGI, no false info or disinfo, nothing that "reeks of a bad sci-fi movie," common sense, and outing hoaxers.
- **Vocation** — from Latin *vocatio*, "to be called" to a special, usually sacred task; traditionally tied to religious orders or craftsmanship; typically uncompensated, with spiritual rewards. Pasulka, as historian of religion, identifies Scott's unquittable debunking work as one.
- **The virtual UFO** — the fabricated, internet-borne UFO (hoaxes, CGI, misidentified lens flares) whose rise Scott has watched; its disturbing consequence is that, for all intents and purposes, the fabricated UFO is the real UFO.
- **Trained observers** — the skilled videographers/photographers/graphic designers whose technical competence lets them distinguish truly anomalous objects from CGI and lens flares — "the skills of the new soldier" in a war waged partly in virtual space.
- **Internet refugees** — Pasulka's term for people like Alison Kruse who flee to In the Field from "the dark side of the virtual world of ufology," where harassment and denigration of its own members are rife.
- **"Punks"** — Alison Kruse's pet name for the starlike objects she films; she speculates they are vehicles that open "like a zipper" to release smaller objects, possibly remote-controlled probes like our Mars rovers, or even our future selves barred from contact lest they change history.
- **Living myth / universal mass rumor** — Jung's characterization of the flying saucer: not just a rumor or myth but a living one, a mass rumor "reserved for our enlightened, rationalist age." Pasulka adds that its potential physical reality is what keeps the myth alive.
- **Amplificatory interpretation** — Jung's concept: when confronted by an unknown (aerial) object, an individual or group must "complete" its meaning after initial confusion; the unconscious amplifies associations around the image and creates a meaningful framework attached to the event. Pasulka stresses it is not reductive: it admits a real, objective event while refraining from certainty about what the event is, focusing on the meaning projected onto it. It is partly how cultural narratives are produced.
- **"UFOs are not photogenic"** — Jung's remark about the surprising scarcity of "authentic" photos given American camera-mindedness; Pasulka says seventy years on, UFOs have become photogenic, as Scott's group attests.
- **Future-real** — Pasulka's gloss on "I want to believe" / "The truth is out there": belief and truth are postponed, not denied; a postponed truth is not false but future-real.
- **Book encounter** — Pasulka's coinage: at some point after their anomalous experiences, experiencers are given, come upon, or are seemingly miraculously directed to a book that puts the experiences into perspective and offers an explanatory framework, connecting them to a cultural narrative (UFO or religious). It differs from the library angel in that the book explains the reader's own experiences.
- **Library angel** — Arthur Koestler's term for the serendipitous arrival of the very book one needs when one needs it.
- **Biblical–UFO hermeneutic** — an interpretive strategy reading anomalous experiences as both religious and UFO-related (scriptural aerial phenomena = flying saucers); used by experiencer Eddy, minister Barry Downing, Rev. Michael J. S. Carter, and groups such as the Nation of Islam.
- **Transverberation** — "to be pierced through": the name for St. Teresa of Avila's famous ecstasy, in which a small, flaming angel plunged a golden, fire-tipped spear into her heart; the event that began her status as a mystic.
- **Synchronicity** — as defined by Jung: the coming together of inner and outer events that are not causally linked but are deeply meaningful to the experiencer. Per Stinnett, an aspect of the phenomenon that researchers must experience without accepting that it means anything — synchronicities are convincing and can lead you off the right track, which is "to not be convinced."
- **The phenomenon tricks and deceives** — Stinnett's warning, attributed also to Vallee and George Hansen: real encounters and meaningful synchronicities dupe researchers into believing the phenomenon is what they think it is — "Well, it's not."
- **Amore fati / "Personal providence"** — from Nietzsche's *The Gay Science*: the love of fate, and the aphorism warning that at life's high point the temptation of "personal providence" (reading every chance event as full of profound significance "precisely for us") threatens spiritual freedom; one should credit "beloved Chance" rather than an anxious Divinity. Pasulka read it mid-synchronicity as a lesson in restraint.
- **The monolith as donation** — James's reading (as a fan of Arthur C. Clarke's book behind Kubrick's *2001*): the monolith — like the New Mexico artifact — is a "donation," most likely from nonhuman intelligence, possibly a tool of control, whose benefit is "too early to tell."
- **The monolith as screen** — Rob Ager's interpretation (CollativeLearning.com): the monolith is the cinema screen (it even collapses into the screen in one scene). Ager thinks this excludes the alien reading; Pasulka argues the monolith can be *both* the cinema screen and the alien — the interpretations are not mutually exclusive. She also notes the monolith resembles a larger version of her iPhone.
- **Media ecology / digital infrastructure** — the framework in which Pasulka situates the phenomenon: the digital infrastructure determines and provides an environment ripe for the now-pervasive UFO belief system, erasing the boundary Scott's group tries to police.
- **Collapse of the real/virtual dualism** — her concluding move: within a digital landscape the real/unreal distinction ceases to be meaningful; this modernist framework gives way to a transcendent one that exceeds constructions of real and not-real (citing Baudrillard). "Unreal" things like movies and video games have real physiological and cognitive effects; media technologies impact bodies as much as biotechnologies, perhaps more.
- **Hypnotic regression and false memories** — Scott's reason for refusing hypnosis to recover his childhood events: regression can create false memories; "it was better to not know than to know something that wasn't true or never happened."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The threat against Scott Browne** — a social-media post ("Talk to me face-to-face, and I will show you what I think of debunkers!") escalating into specific descriptions of bodily and emotional harm, because Scott demonstrated that a claimed UFO photo was Photoshopped. Shows the vitriol debunkers face and the stakes of the virtual UFO war.
- **In the Field's operation** — the Facebook group's ethics code, three membership rules, and enforcement: hoaxers are removed on discovery and "always react with a whirlwind of vitriol." Pasulka joined not as a videographer but on the credential of working with Jacques Vallee. She uses the group to show dogma-formation in real time and its members as anti-dogmatists resisting the media wave.
- **Pasulka's coffee-shop interview with Scott (Northampton, Massachusetts)** — Scott vets her by asking her position on religion, fearing dogmatism; she describes herself as a churchgoing Catholic who began as a born-again Christian at eleven, open about what the truth is. Frames the chapter's demand for a nondogmatic approach.
- **Scott's childhood disappearance (age ~3)** — Scott vanished from home; his police-officer father filed a missing persons report and mounted a town-wide search; he was found many hours later in a nearby field saying he'd been talking with the cows. His mother's diary records him slipping out at 5:30 that morning and being found down the street coming from the field, "so excited that he talked to the cows even though they were horses!" Scott is careful: these events may or may not connect to his obsession, but he has an inkling they could.
- **The brothers' recurring dreams** — Scott's younger brother repeatedly dreamed of Scott on a table hooked to machines, examined by big-headed people; Scott dreamed of a giant praying mantis at his window (and later kept one as a pet).
- **1987 sleep paralysis and the bookstore** — after marrying and moving to a new apartment, Scott had repeated sleep-paralysis episodes with a menacing presence by the bed ("I think our house is haunted"); days later Howard Blum's *Out There: The Government's Secret Quest for Extraterrestrials* (blurbed by Whitley Strieber: "Absolutely essential reading") "popped out at him" in a bookstore, read like his biography, and launched his research. His book encounter.
- **Scott's refusal of hypnotic regression** — advised to be hypnotized to uncover his childhood, he researched regression, learned it can create false memories, and declined.
- **Alison Kruse (Pennsylvania)** — 2008: her daughter (no Star Wars/Star Trek fan) saw a glowing red "plane" hovering at early morning and drew it saucer-shaped. Months later Alison watched two "planets" that twinkled, moved, and faded out. Then children sledding saw a cigar-shaped object; sightings multiplied day and night. Researcher Bruce Cornett told her sightings would increase — they did. She spent thousands on night-vision and video equipment, cross-checked recordings with FAA flight records, and in November 2010 filmed a starlike object confirmed by the FAA to coincide with no aircraft; the FAA's own data disc was titled "Murrysville UFO." She observed the objects mimicking conventional airplane lights ("copying our lighting arrangements... so they can imitate us and not be recognized") and seeming shy/aware — disappearing when she grabbed her camera in front of invited witnesses. Worldwide reports of similar behavior lead her to reject the secret-military-craft theory ("they are coming in from everywhere—Australia, Europe, Pakistan, everywhere"). She was harassed online (called liar and hoaxer, computer targeted with viruses) and took refuge in In the Field. Her conclusion: "This is history. We are being visited by visitors from somewhere else" — though like Scott, Tyler, and James she admits she ultimately doesn't know.
- **The awareness effect** — witnesses and researchers commonly report that once you become aware of the phenomenon, it becomes aware of you: first sightings are followed by more, with the uncanny feeling the objects watch their watchers.
- **Scott's "best captures"** — two photographs of a metallic, roundish object partly hidden in clouds, blown up in stages (the book's Figure 3.1); triggers Pasulka's Jung discussion.
- **Jung's Guatemala anecdote** — Jung knew a man who watched a daytime UFO for an hour with hundreds of others in Guatemala, camera in hand, and forgot to take a photo — strengthening Jung's impression that UFOs are "somehow not photogenic."
- **Robert Bigelow's remark** — the billionaire, asked if he feared being thought crazy for believing in extraterrestrials: "I don't care... It's not gonna change the reality of what I know." Pasulka uses it to show religious truth-claims existing independent of belief or disbelief.
- **The book encounters compared** — Tyler: Carl Sagan's cosmos book appearing mysteriously in his luggage when he was lost and desperate, fusing space-program knowledge with biotech to form healing companies. James: John Mack's *Abduction: Human Encounters with Aliens*, picked up on a whim as supposed science fiction, reading like his biography. Scott: Blum's *Out There*. Each supplied the explanatory framework linking experience to the UFO narrative.
- **Edward Carlos** — art professor whose anomalous experiences featured in Mack's *Abduction*; he resisted the term "abduction" so strongly that Pasulka rewrote and published his account from his perspective. He said the light beings were sometimes like angels, sometimes like aliens, transcending both Catholic and UFO languages; Mack, though attentive, "never had a grasp of the real phenomenon." Yet after Mack's bestseller, his experiences became UFO-related for a wide audience — showing how framing, not the experiencer, fixes the label.
- **St. Teresa of Avila's transverberation** — the sixteenth-century nun's diary account: a short, beautiful, flaming angel plunged a golden spear with a fiery iron tip into her heart repeatedly, leaving her "utterly consumed by the great love of God," a spiritual pain of unbearable sweetness. Pasulka notes her *confusion* (unlike the serene Bernini sculpture and paintings with their little angel and small dart) and that Teresa turned to the books of her time — Catholic angelology, via her confessors — to frame it as religious: a sixteenth-century book encounter. (Pope Paul VI later made Teresa a "doctor" of the church.)
- **Eddy at the UFO conference** — an experiencer who regularly sees fifteen-plus saucers in formation, often as sole witness; after a year his wife finally saw them and took ten photos, which he showed Pasulka with a magnifying glass. His biblical–UFO reading: "Jesus went up in the clouds on a saucer, and he will come down again just as he went up."
- **The theologians' biblical–UFO books** — in the late 1960s Presbyterian minister Barry Downing made the same equation of scriptural aerial phenomena with flying saucers in his book *The Bible and Flying Saucers*; the Reverend Michael J. S. Carter (a Union Theological Seminary graduate) offers a contemporary version in *Alien Scriptures: Extraterrestrials in the Holy Bible*, arguing that the Bible is a history of human contact with extraterrestrials.
- **David Stinnett's C140 sighting and VHS synchronicity** — 30+ year researcher, director of the annual New Jersey UFO conference, 20+ year Bible student and Christian, Scott's mentor. On a central New Jersey back road he saw a silent, gun-metal-gray craft that looked exactly like a C140 transport creeping slowly; it would not show up in his camera's finder (the camera worked on other objects), then vanished. At home he inexplicably pulled from under a huge stack a VHS of Dr. Bruce Cornet lecturing to the New Jersey UFO Congress, describing that exact craft in perfect detail. He deadpans the "synchronicity" label and refuses to conclude anything except that the phenomenon is real and it deceives. His epigraph: "They've always been a reality; what they are is still a theory."
- **Stinnett verifies Alison** — after seeing her videos he visited her home: "Alison's phenomenon is the real deal. When I was out there, so was Homeland Security."
- **Pasulka's New Year's Eve Nietzsche synchronicity** — woken at midnight by revelers, she randomly opened *The Gay Science* to its only three New Year's aphorisms: "Sanctus Januarius" (the annually liquefying dried blood of St. Januarius as metaphor for Nietzsche's revival), then amor fati, then "Personal providence" — an aphorism about synchronicities read while she was having one. The stinging irony: mid-synchronicity, Nietzsche warns against ascribing it profound meaning. She has never forgotten the cognitive dissonance; Stinnett's restraint reminded her of it.
- **Pasulka's "foolish" conclusion and Vallee's chastisement** — presenting to Vallee, Jeff Kripal, and other academics, she argued (quoting Baudrillard) that UFOs were real only in a virtual sense; Vallee's swift, gentle rebuke: "It is unwise to ignore the ontological aspects of the UFO." She learned one cannot just "conclude" about such a phenomenon — like concluding about gravity or light — citing Vatican Observatory director Brother Guy Consolmagno: "Truth is a moving target."
- **The balloon–UFO exposé** — Scott blew up a viral series of circular-object photos and showed side by side that they were oddly shaped balloons (Figure 3.2). The posters accepted his exposé *and* kept believing: the UFOs, they said, had ingeniously disguised themselves as balloons. (Pasulka quips they must have read Vallee's *Messengers of Deception*.)
- **The Marian Keech study** — in the 1950s three Stanford University sociologists infiltrated a small UFO group whose leader, Marian Keech, predicted UFO arrival amid global cataclysm; when the prophecy failed, some followers reinterpreted events — their faith in Keech had averted the cataclysm — rather than disbelieve. Pasulka cites it as precedent for the balloon rationalization.
- **Scott's own fabricated UFO** — answering a website challenge to fake a believable UFO photo and explain the method, Scott built realistic UFOs from ordinary household items (Figure 3.3). For years afterward his admitted fakes recirculated as real UFOs, with posters even naming the (fake) locations where they were "photographed." The virtual world "eats up the conventionally real and spits it out as something entirely alien" to his intent.
- **Scott's despair about the technology** — his painful admission closing the interview: sophisticated faking tools now make it "almost impossible" for him and his videographers to tell real from fake. (Figure 3.4 shows him displaying phone UFO apps.)
- **The monolith / New Mexico artifact exchange** — during the New Mexico trip, James called the desert artifact a "donation," most likely from nonhuman intelligence; asked whether it is for our benefit: "It's too early to tell." Pasulka links it to Kubrick's *2001* monolith (dominance in the proto-hominid scene; possible tool of control; companion of human evolution via AI like Hal), Ager's screen reading, and her own iPhone resemblance — concluding screen and alien readings can both be true.

## Thread
This chapter widens the book's ethnography from the invisible elite scientists (Tyler and James, chapters 1–3, including the New Mexico desert artifact trip) to the visible, grassroots layer of the phenomenon — the public researchers whose work feeds and fights the internet's UFO narrative. It supplies the book's clearest account of a mechanism of belief: sighting → recording → viral amplification (now via bots and algorithms) → virtual dogma, with Jung's amplificatory interpretation and Pasulka's book encounter as the interpretive engines that attach anomalous experience to a cultural narrative — the same engine, she shows via Teresa of Avila, that once produced Catholic mystics. It also stages the book's ontological pivot: from her rejected "only virtual" claim, through Vallee's correction, to the non-dualist position that the phenomenon's reality is virtual *and* real — media technologies shaping bodies and belief as powerfully as biotechnology. The monolith coda loops back to James's "donation" and sets up the Conclusion (The Artifact), while the meta-lesson — researchers must experience synchronicities without believing them — prefigures her treatment of contact protocols and her own entanglement with the phenomenon.

## Connections
- Backward: the New Mexico "crash" site artifact and James's "donation" reading continue the desert expedition with Tyler and James from the opening chapters; Tyler's Sagan-in-the-luggage and James's Mack book encounters recall their origin stories told earlier.
- Forward: the artifact/monolith thread points to the Conclusion (The Artifact); the collapse of real/virtual and "media as belief infrastructure" underwrites the book's larger thesis of technology as a site of the sacred; Vallee's "believe no one" ethic recurs wherever Pasulka discusses the phenomenon's deceptiveness.
- Glossary (unit 10): amplificatory interpretation, book encounter, synchronicity, and the virtual UFO are this chapter's own key coinages/borrowings — none of them are among the terms Pasulka's dedicated Glossary chapter (unit 10) actually defines (experiencers, the Invisibles, meta-experiencers, the phenomenon, religion, contact event/hierophany, technology).
- Cross-book (speculative/thematic, not Pasulka's references): the chapter's collapse of the real/virtual dualism and the "monolith as screen" reading resonate with the-simulation-hypothesis (reality as computed/rendered) and with my-big-toe's virtual-reality ontology; the claim that "unreal" media have real physiological effects echoes the-holographic-universe's theme of image and reality interpenetrating. These are reader-drawn parallels only.
