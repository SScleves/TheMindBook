# American Cosmic: UFOs, Religion, Technology — Q&A

*Chapter pointers use the note chapter numbers. Legend: **Ch 0** = the Preface ("A Tour of Silicon Valley with Jacques Vallee"), the book's front matter; **Ch 1** = Introduction; **Ch 9** = Conclusion ("The Artifact").*

*Throughout, answers report what D.W. Pasulka and the figures she studies claim; nothing here is endorsed as established fact, and the author's own hedges are preserved.*

## Framing: Silicon Valley, Vallee, and Method

**Q: Who is Jacques Vallee, and what role does he play in the book?**
A: Pasulka presents Jacques Vallee as her presiding theorist-informant — a man she says has "perhaps done more for the field of ufology than anyone else in its short history," though he calls the study of UFOs his hobby. She sketches his "orthodox history" (astronomer who helped NASA map Mars, early ARPANET engineer, venture capitalist, and consultant to Spielberg on *Close Encounters of the Third Kind*, whose Truffaut character is based on him) and an "unorthodox history" tied to the Stanford Research Institute. The book opens with Vallee driving her and Robbie Graham through Silicon Valley, telling them "There are many secrets in this valley." (Ch 0)

**Q: What was the Stargate Project, according to Pasulka?**
A: She describes it as a classified program funded by the US military in partnership with SRI that studied remote viewing, precognition, and ESP, hoping to harness naturally psychic people for intelligence gathering. She reports that its psychic viewers allegedly uncovered unintended targets like UFOs and reported traveling to the moon and Mars — so the program, she writes, "allegedly developed, intentionally or not, psychic cosmonauts." (Ch 0)

**Q: How does Pasulka use Heidegger and Jung to frame technology?**
A: Reading Vallee through Heidegger's "The Question Concerning Technology," she argues humans misread technology as mere instrumentality when the human relationship with technology is actually "religiouslike" and can reveal it as "a saving power" birthing a new epoch. She then makes the UFO the symbol of that epoch, invoking Carl Jung's naming of the UFO as "a technological angel." (Ch 0)

**Q: How did Pasulka come to study UFO cultures in the first place?**
A: After finishing a multiyear study of the Catholic doctrine of purgatory — in which archival records from roughly 1300–1880 surfaced unexpected reports of orbs of light, luminous beings, flames penetrating walls, spinning suns, and disclike aerial objects — she shifted in January 2012 to studying UFO cultures ethnographically. A friend likened her purgatory reports to a Spielberg film and pointed her to a local UFO conference, where experiencers described the same phenomena she had found in Catholic history. (Ch 1)

**Q: What is the "parallel research tradition" or "Invisible College"?**
A: Pasulka claims there is, and always has been, an anonymous group of successful scientists and academics who study the phenomenon in secret — Allen Hynek's "Invisible College," written of by Vallee. Their work is never made public, yet it impacts society and its leaked rumors spawn the folklore of the UFO narrative. She says her participants remained anonymous because of the stigma of UFO belief and because classified government programs necessitate secrecy. (Ch 1)

**Q: What "epistemological shock" did Pasulka experience?**
A: Borrowing Harvard researcher John Mack's term, she describes a two-level shock: first, that respected elite scientists — not "cranks and weirdos" — believe in and study nonhuman intelligence; and second, that what is marketed to the public barely resembles their findings, being built instead on rumor, hoax, and disinformation. She cites Stephen Hawking's 2008 TED remark ("Why would they appear to only cranks and weirdos?") as the emblem of the "lie" that UFO belief is fringe. (Ch 1)

**Q: What is Pasulka's religious-studies method, and how does it relate to Vallee's UN address?**
A: Her method is "bracketing" — setting aside the metaphysical truth of phenomena to study their social effects, "which are incontestably very real." She models it on Vallee's 1979 statement to a UN special political committee that "the belief in space visitors is independent of the physical reality of the UFO phenomenon," noting that Vallee himself believes in the phenomenon while holding that mass belief does not depend on it. (Ch 1)

**Q: What does Pasulka mean by "agents of information"?**
A: She argues that screenwriters, television producers, and authors today play the role that first-century scribes and redactors played in the formation of scripture — interpreting, spinning, producing, and marketing the UFO story to the public. She says she witnessed "the dynamic genesis of a global belief system" and considers herself part of, perhaps even a "pawn" in, that process. (Ch 1)

**Q: How does Pasulka connect the printing press to her thesis about technology and religion?**
A: As her "uncontroversial" historical example that technology reshapes religion, she cites the printing press: mass-produced vernacular Bibles gave rise to *Sola Scriptura* ("Scripture Alone") and the Protestant Reformation. She extends this to argue technology is not just the infrastructure of UFO belief but is itself both the sacred medium and the sacred object of the new religiosity — while in some theological circles technology (especially the internet) is instead cast as "the Beast." (Ch 1)

**Q: How does the Introduction answer its own closing question, "Is it real, or is it imaginary?"**
A: Pasulka answers that "what follows suggests that it is both." That paradox becomes the book's framing answer, elaborated across the numbered chapters and stated most carefully in the Rome chapter as "simultaneously imagined and real." (Ch 1, Ch 8)

**Q: How does Pasulka use the Nation of Islam as an example?**
A: She offers it as a case of a contact event spawning a belief community and drawing institutional monitoring: early leader Elijah Muhammad (Elijah Robert Poole) believed UFOs would come to bring salvation to believers and punish nonbelievers, and the FBI established a file on him and his community. She later notes the Nation had articulated a UFO narrative by the 1930s — a tradition ufology's standard 1947 origin story tends to ignore. (Ch 1, Ch 8)

## The Invisible Scientists: Tyler D. and James

**Q: Who is "Tyler D."?**
A: Tyler D. is Pasulka's pseudonymous central informant — a space-industry scientist who joined the US space program at eighteen, worked as an engineer on nearly every space shuttle, holds more than forty patents, and says "I get paid to think." She casts him as a "meta-experiencer," a scientist who scoops data from primary experiencers, and as a contemporary, invisible version of Colonel Philip Corso. (Ch 2)

**Q: What is a "hierophany," and why does Pasulka call the New Mexico site one?**
A: A hierophany, in her framing, is a manifestation of the sacred — as when a nonhuman intelligent being descends from the sky or reveals itself (Moses's burning bush is her classic example). She reads the New Mexico desert site where a craft allegedly crashed in 1947 as a hierophany for millions who believe, explicitly flagging this as her scholarly interpretation, not a claim about the artifacts' actual origin. She adds that "where hierophanies appear, consumerism often follows," comparing Roswell's museum and festival to the pilgrimage economy at Lourdes. (Ch 2)

**Q: What happened on the blindfolded trip to the New Mexico site?**
A: Pasulka and the scientist James agreed to wear blindfolds for the final forty minutes of a drive to a no-fly-zone site (explicitly "not Area 51"). Tyler secretly photographed them blindfolded and explained that after the 1947 crash the government hid the craft and masked the area with tons of tin cans, now decomposed into powdery rubble; the metal detectors were specially configured to find the artifacts. Standing before an X-Files-familiar mesa, Pasulka had her realization that she was "standing on ground zero of the new religion." (Ch 2)

**Q: What is Tyler's "protocol," and what belief underlies it?**
A: Tyler's protocol is a bodily regimen for receiving "downloads" from off-planet intelligence: eight hours of sleep plus a forced "top-off" hour (the "eight plus one rule"), near-zero alcohol, no coffee ("Coffee really messes up the signal"), going to bed slightly dehydrated, then morning sun and water. It rests on his claim that human DNA is "a receptor and transmitter" operating at the frequency used to communicate with deep-space satellites — "Humans are a type of satellite." Pasulka and Kripal found the regimen reminiscent of yoga and meditation. (Ch 2)

**Q: How does Pasulka place Tyler in a lineage of space-program figures?**
A: She connects him to Konstantin Tsiolkovsky, the founding father of rocketry, who believed "ethereal beings" beyond our dimensions send messages through celestial symbols that a genius channels into technology; and to Jack Parsons, the American rocketeer who collaborated with Aleister Crowley and L. Ron Hubbard, performed desert rituals, believed himself in contact with extraterrestrial intelligences, and died at thirty-seven when a rocket exploded in his garage. She frames Tyler as the contemporary, invisible Corso, whose "The Day After Roswell" (1997) fueled a "modern Prometheus" myth. (Ch 2)

**Q: What is the "smart room," and where does Tyler claim innovative technology comes from?**
A: The smart room is a special facility where, Tyler says, the world's best scientists think in complete freedom and nothing is laughed at — even "a purple unicorn flying through space on a pickle." He claims some of the most innovative technologies in use emerged from it, gesturing at Pasulka's iPhone. He attributes his own ideas to "memories" that arrive fully formed of things he never learned, which he decided to "use for good." (Ch 2)

**Q: How does the Challenger disaster figure in Tyler's story?**
A: Tyler's mentor and friend, astronaut Judith Resnik, died when Challenger exploded in 1986. In a video filmed standing on the concrete slabs where the shuttle is buried, he recounts her last hug and going alone to the ocean to tell her "I'm sorry." Afterward his wife wanted a divorce and he was hospitalized with heart palpitations; a title-less black book left in his office turned out to be a Carl Sagan book on the cosmos, which became "my saving grace" and convinced him interstellar travel was possible. (Ch 2)

**Q: Who is James, and what makes him distinctive among Pasulka's informants?**
A: James is her second pseudonymous informant — a Silicon Valley-flavored scientist who holds an endowed chair in molecular biology at a top university and was, in her words, the first scientist she met who was also "out" as an experiencer. He is "the consummate professor" who can articulate a formula, in contrast to Tyler's intuitive practice. (Ch 3)

**Q: What is James's core scientific claim about the phenomenon?**
A: At Pasulka's conference James asserted that "the phenomenon commonly referred to as UFOs exists," interacts with humans, and cannot yet be explained — but that its effects and physical channels can be studied scientifically. He theorizes a genetically determined "brain receiver," notes that experiencers run in families, and holds that contact leaves a physical, physiological, traceable "signature" that science can identify. (Ch 3)

**Q: How does James explain anomalous cognition?**
A: Unable to explain how some subjects know things they shouldn't, James relies on quantum theory — "spooky action at a distance" — to suggest a "quantum field of information" his subjects somehow tap into, which then transfers into human neuronal hardware as a "recognition." He calls the interfacing ability "a 6th sense that is associated with a material component we already possess." (Ch 3)

**Q: What does James mean by "bedeviled," and what is his personal mission?**
A: "Bedeviled" is his most-used word for people tormented by unwelcome contact events, which he said would have been called demonic as recently as a hundred years ago and which can act "like a contagious agent" spreading from person to person. His mission is to develop a "medicine" or antidote that would shift the relationship 180 degrees, giving humans the right and ability to say "no," since contact currently happens on "their" terms. (Ch 3)

**Q: What childhood experiences did James report?**
A: He described "little people" appearing in his room around age five or six while he was awake and paralyzed; a soundless twenty-foot formation of lights that passed over his paper route in his early teens; and, in his thirties, a tall, thin, translucent presence at the foot of his bed that told him "Go to sleep." He recognized his own life in John Mack's *Abduction* after first mistaking it for science fiction. (Ch 3)

**Q: What were the "men in black" who visited James?**
A: After his televised artifact analysis, two grim men in black suits came to his office asking what he "really found out." James suspected he was being "punk'd," but they turned out to be fully human serious researchers — one a top university researcher with intelligence-agency ties, the other from a large aerospace firm — studying the phenomenon's radiation effects and biological interactions. Pasulka writes that "James had found his peers." (Ch 3)

**Q: What is the Durant Report / Robertson Panel, and why does it matter to the book?**
A: The 1953 Robertson Panel (recorded in the Durant Report) concluded UFOs were not themselves a security risk but recommended a mass-media "training and debunking" campaign — with the Jam Handy Co. and Walt Disney, Inc. suggested as producers. Pasulka uses it as documented proof of government "perception management," and later notes it recommended the very documentary-style techniques and authoritative experts Hollywood marketers would use. (Ch 3, Ch 5)

**Q: What did James find at the New Mexico site, and what did analysis suggest?**
A: James's metal detector flagged something between rocks, and after long digging he retrieved material that looked like crumpled tin foil yet was also a type of fabric. Months later his preliminary analyses made it "hard to believe" it was made on Earth — perhaps not makeable anywhere, and certainly not on Earth in 1947. Chuckling, he called such material a "donation" left by nonhuman beings, "a gift or donation for us to figure out." (Ch 3)

**Q: How does Pasulka use Srinivasa Ramanujan?**
A: The mathematician from Tamil Nadu, with only elementary training, became one of the great mathematicians of the early twentieth century and attributed his brilliance to the Hindu goddess Lakshmi whispering equations in his ear — an explanation that embarrassed his Oxford colleagues but which he never recanted. Pasulka offers him alongside Tyler and James as figures who attribute creativity to an external source. (Ch 3)

**Q: How does Pasulka weigh Jacobsen versus Corso on Roswell?**
A: Annie Jacobsen's *Area 51* affirms only top-secret military programs and no UFO reality, while Philip Corso's *The Day After Roswell* insists a real crash occurred and that his job was seeding alien debris to industry under a Russian/Chinese-technology cover story. Pasulka says she "tended to believe" Jacobsen's account yet felt she was "living within" Corso's — and, prompted by Tyler's maxim that the best place to hide the truth is in a mess of confusion, concluded both could be true on some level. (Ch 3)

## In the Field: Grassroots Ufology and the Virtual UFO

**Q: Who is Scott Browne and what is "In the Field"?**
A: Scott Browne runs a Facebook-based international study group called "In the Field" — trained videographers, photographers, and graphic designers who film anomalous aerial objects while debunking the CGI, lens flares, and hoaxes flooding the internet. Membership requires shooting your own footage regularly, knowing the field's basics, and keeping an open mind. Pasulka characterizes Scott as "a debunker and a true believer. He is a debunker because he is a true believer." (Ch 4)

**Q: Why does Pasulka call Scott's debunking a "vocation"?**
A: Because *vocatio* means "to be called" to a sacred task, and Scott's uncompensated debunking is work he cannot abandon despite threats and harassment (one post promised to show him "what I think of debunkers"). She frames it as a religious vocation performed in a war that is "virtual" but whose "blood is real." (Ch 4)

**Q: What does Pasulka mean by "the fabricated UFO is the real UFO"?**
A: She argues that in today's digital media ecology the physical and virtual worlds interpenetrate, so the profitable, hoax-driven virtual UFO now dominates the phenomenon's public life — "for all intents and purposes, the fabricated UFO is the real UFO." A sighting becomes a video, goes viral, and mutates into a quasi-dogmatic object far removed from the original event, which is how media technologies inform UFO belief. (Ch 4)

**Q: How does Pasulka revive Carl Jung's UFO framework?**
A: She retrieves Jung's 1950s characterization of the flying saucer as a "living myth" and "universal mass rumor," and his concept of "amplificatory interpretation" — the way an observer must "complete" the meaning of an unknown object, letting the unconscious build a framework around it. She argues Jung's claim that UFOs are "not photogenic" has been overturned since the 1950s, but that he missed the key point: it is the UFO's *potential physical reality* that makes it a living myth — "Its realism is what gives it its bite. It is also what makes it religious." (Ch 4)

**Q: What is a "book encounter"?**
A: Pasulka's coinage for the seemingly serendipitous book that supplies an experiencer with a framework for their anomalous experiences. Her examples: Tyler's Carl Sagan book appearing in his luggage; James's John Mack *Abduction*, read as if it were his biography; Scott's Howard Blum *Out There*, which "popped out at him" in a bookstore; and, historically, St. Teresa of Avila turning to Catholic angelology to frame her transverberation. (Ch 4)

**Q: Who is Alison Kruse, and what did she document?**
A: Alison Kruse is a Pennsylvania field researcher who, after her daughter drew a saucer-shaped object in 2008, spent thousands on night-vision and video equipment and cross-checked recordings against FAA flight records. In November 2010 she filmed a starlike object the FAA confirmed coincided with no aircraft — the agency's own data disc was titled "Murrysville UFO." She calls the objects "punks," believes they mimic airplane lighting to avoid recognition, and concluded "This is history. We are being visited by visitors from somewhere else" — while, like the others, admitting she ultimately does not know. (Ch 4)

**Q: What is David Stinnett's warning about synchronicity?**
A: Stinnett, a 30-year researcher, told Pasulka that synchronicities are one aspect of the phenomenon — "If a researcher does not experience them, he or she is not really doing the research right" — but that the phenomenon "tricks and deceives" (echoing Vallee and George Hansen). The disciplined researcher must therefore experience synchronicities without being convinced by them; the right track "is to not be convinced." (Ch 4)

**Q: How does St. Teresa of Avila fit into this chapter?**
A: Pasulka uses her transverberation — in which a small flaming angel plunged a golden, fire-tipped spear into her heart — as a historical "book encounter": Teresa, in confusion, turned to the Catholic angelology of her time (via her confessors) to frame the event as religious. Pasulka notes Teresa's confusion contrasts with the serene Bernini sculpture, and that Pope Paul VI later made her a "doctor" of the church. (Ch 4)

**Q: How did Vallee correct Pasulka's early conclusion about the UFO's reality?**
A: When she argued, quoting Baudrillard, that UFOs were "real only in a virtual sense," Vallee gently rebuked her: "It is unwise to ignore the ontological aspects of the UFO." She learned one cannot simply "conclude" about such a phenomenon (citing Vatican Observatory director Brother Guy Consolmagno's "Truth is a moving target"), and reclaimed her thesis in non-dualist form: the phenomenon's reality is virtual *and* real. By the chapter's close she says, "I had given up the dualism of real and virtual." (Ch 4)

**Q: What is the Marian Keech study, and how does Pasulka use the "balloon-UFO" exposé alongside it?**
A: When Scott showed that a viral series of "UFO" photos were actually oddly shaped balloons, the posters accepted his exposé yet kept believing — the UFOs, they said, had cleverly disguised themselves as balloons. Pasulka links this to the 1950s Stanford sociologists who infiltrated Marian Keech's UFO group: when Keech's doomsday prophecy failed, followers reinterpreted events (their faith had averted the cataclysm) rather than disbelieve. (Ch 4)

## The Mechanisms of Belief

**Q: What is the "Total Recall Effect"?**
A: Pasulka's coinage, drawn from her work consulting on films, for how movies about religion and the supernatural "produce real physiological effects, including practices and belief," and can "create and mimic real memories" — "In a very real sense, we incorporate these films into our minds and bodies. They become us." She says it goes beyond confabulation, being a form generated and nurtured by modern technology. (Ch 5)

**Q: What specific "mechanisms of belief" does Pasulka identify?**
A: She names the "based on true events" strategy (popularized by *The Blair Witch Project*), the "realist montage" (splicing fiction beside nonfiction to forge a cognitive link), reliance on "cultural authorities" commenting in media, the splicing of digital characters into iconic historical photographs, and depictions of ordinary-life scenes. She realized she herself functioned as a cultural authority for *The Conjuring*. (Ch 5)

**Q: How does neuroscientist Jeffrey Zacks support her argument?**
A: Zacks argues the brain does not sort real-life, movie, and novel events into separate "buckets" — cognitive event models get conflated, especially when fiction uses realism-creating techniques, and this happens unconsciously and probably cannot be trained away. His misinformation study found that a specific warning plus correction reduced but did not remove the effect of a factually inaccurate historical film. (Ch 5)

**Q: What is Jediism, and how does Pasulka categorize it?**
A: Jediism is a religious movement treating *Star Wars* as sacred "scripture," whose practitioners believe in a transcendent element (the Force) and hold that because Lucas drew on Daoism and Zen, the films point "like a finger pointing to the moon" to eternal truths. Pasulka engages Chidester's "fakelore," Cusack's "invented religions," and Davidsen's "fiction-based religions," but argues Jediism is powerful because it projects the realism of historical religions into a *future-real* material universe of potential nonhuman life. (Ch 5)

**Q: What is "specialist factual programming," and what example does Pasulka give?**
A: It is a media genre that uses digital technologies to make factual or historical events "special," by design employing the very techniques that mix the real and unreal. Her example is *The Great Martian War 1913–1917*, a "documentary" from the company Impossible Factual that simulates a WWI fought against extraterrestrial invaders using historians, aging "veterans," and fused archive and effects — and "took social media and television by storm." She notes some young viewers even believe the extinct megalodon shark survives from such programming. (Ch 5)

**Q: What was the Betty and Barney Hill case, and how does Pasulka reframe it?**
A: The Hills, an interracial New Hampshire couple, saw a star-like light on September 19, 1961; Betty later borrowed Donald Keyhoe's UFO book, and under hypnotic regression the pair related an abduction and examination aboard a craft. Pasulka cites David Halperin and Martin Kottmeyer's 1994 discovery that Barney's "wraparound eyes" description matched an alien in *The Outer Limits* episode "The Bellero Shield," aired twelve days before the session. She does not discount a real experience but asks, "What if what they remembered was not real but virtually real?" — arguing the hypnotic-regression tradition must be reassessed given media's influence on memory. (Ch 5)

**Q: How does Elizabeth Loftus's memory research figure in the chapter?**
A: Loftus holds that memory is reconstructive, not a video camera — "It's a little like a Wikipedia page, you can change it, but so can other people." Pasulka extends this: a movie or video game can change it too, so "Maybe the human being is like a Wikipedia page, and we are not the sole editors of our own pages." She pairs this with Robert Baker's account of implanted "pseudo-memories" and Alison Landsberg's "prosthetic memory." (Ch 5)

**Q: What does Robert Ager's reading of the *2001* monolith contribute?**
A: Ager decodes the monolith as a metaphor for the cinema screen — citing Kubrick's intent to bypass conscious rationalization, the stargate scene where Bowman's eye and the screen become indistinguishable, and the line "His feeling that he was inside a movie set was almost literally true." Pasulka concludes "there is a human–monolith continuum," noting that around 2007 ads superimposed the iPhone on the monolith, and that (against Ager) the monolith can be *both* the cinema screen and the alien. (Ch 5)

**Q: What was disturbing to Pasulka about Disney's *Alien Encounters from Tomorrow Land* (1995)?**
A: The program used edited testimonies of real abductees, "experts" like Budd Hopkins, and military-base footage for credibility, opening with the claim that "Intelligent life from distant galaxies is now attempting to make open contact." Most disturbing to her (and to Halperin, who said it stank "of dishonesty and manipulation") was the claim that many Americans would likely be abducted within five years and could acclimate via a theme-park ride where Hopkins showed children cards of aliens until they screamed — one rider reporting terrifying "flashbacks." (Ch 5)

**Q: What did the Stanford History Education Group study find?**
A: In 2015–2016, middle school, high school, and college youth largely could not distinguish sponsored content from legitimately sourced content, fake accounts from real, or paid ads from articles — "getting duped again and again" with "stunning and dismaying consistency." Pasulka uses it to argue that social-media fluency does not equal savvy, underpinning her claim that media manufacture belief below conscious awareness. (Ch 5)

**Q: What does Pasulka mean by "imagination exteriorized"?**
A: Her image for the current condition: "It's as if our imaginations have become exterior to ourselves, existing out there in our media, and our media then determines what is in our heads." She supports it with Andy Clark's "extended cognition" (cognition is not brain-bound) and N. Katherine Hayles's "technogenesis" (humans coevolve with their technologies), and argues the real fake is the human/machine binary, not new religious forms. (Ch 5)

## The Material Code: Vallee and the Technological Sacred

**Q: What is Vallee's "Invisible College," and where does the term originate?**
A: In Vallee's 1975 book of that title (which he gifted Pasulka in 2014), the "invisible college" is J. Allen Hynek's name for a small group of researchers who study the phenomenon under anonymity. Pasulka notes the term harks back to the scientists affiliated with Robert Boyle in the early 1700s — when science was considered suspect and potentially demonic — a group thought to be a precursor of the Royal Society of London. (Ch 6)

**Q: What is the "logic of camouflage"?**
A: Following Vallee, Pasulka argues that secrecy and absurdity are integral to the phenomenon's efficacy: its absurd elements get it rejected and ridiculed by society's "upper layers" while its symbols are absorbed unconsciously — so it operates "completely under the radar of consciousness," where it is most effective. Her model is early Christianity, an "absurd" subterranean counterculture (Romans called Christians cannibals) that eventually became Rome's state religion: "This is the logic of camouflage. It is sneaky, and time is on its side." (Ch 6)

**Q: What are the "two datasets" in UFO reports?**
A: Vallee's field finding, relayed by Pasulka, that witnesses report the empirical part of an event to authorities and confide the psychic part only to sympathetic listeners — so "when scientists and the military discuss UFOs, they are not talking about the same part of the phenomenon the public perceives." Fear of ridicule keeps the two datasets apart, mirroring the "nuts and bolts" versus subjectivist split in ufology. (Ch 6)

**Q: How does Pasulka parallel ufology's split with Catholicism?**
A: She maps ufology's materialist "nuts and bolts" school versus its subjective/psychic school onto Catholicism's doctrinal versus devotional traditions — formalized as "public revelation" (scripture, belief obligatory) versus "private revelation" (apparitions, belief optional). In both traditions, she notes, verification collapses onto the trustworthiness of the witness. (Ch 6)

**Q: Why does Vallee use the Fatima apparitions as his showcase example?**
A: Because, he argues, "the technology which 'she' uses is indistinguishable... from the technology surrounding the UFO phenomenon." Pasulka reconstructs the 1917 Portugal sequence — shining beings arriving in spheres, a buzzing "like that of a bee," children enervated after contact, and the October 13 "Miracle of the Sun" witnessed by tens of thousands (perhaps 100,000) who saw a spinning, staring-tolerable solar disc. She lists Vallee's "Morphology of Miracles": humming, shining discs, heat effects, healings, selective witnessing, absurd messages, and injunctions to silence. (Ch 6)

**Q: What is Vallee's "control mechanism" / "schedule of reinforcement" idea?**
A: Speculatively, Vallee suggests apparitions and UFOs are manifestations of a single control mechanism working like a psychological "schedule of reinforcement" (as in Pavlov's dogs). Graphs of UFO "flaps" over time appear, to him, to suggest such a schedule. He offers this as speculation while remaining committed to the idea that the phenomena appear technological. (Ch 6)

**Q: What is the "associative universe," and how does it naturalize synchronicity?**
A: Vallee argues that "the theory of space and time is a cultural artifact made possible by the invention of graph paper," and that if we live in an associative, informational universe (like a software scientist's, not a Cartesian space-time physicist's), then "miracles are no longer irrational events" and coincidences are simply how the universe retrieves information — "like putting a keyword into Google or Yahoo!" Consciousness becomes "the process by which informational associations are retrieved and traversed." (Ch 6)

**Q: What did Pasulka find about "synthetic" synchronicities?**
A: She reports that social bots and cookie-driven targeted ads can *manufacture* synchronicities, and that these remain profoundly meaningful even to experiencers who fully understand the mechanism. Her example: a Vallee fan who commented on Michelin Man images in the morning and that night received a targeted ad showing "Bibendum" giving a thumbs-up — "It really felt like a significant synch for me... DESPITE my knowing full well the likely advertising mechanisms." (Ch 6)

**Q: What is Tyler's "body as biological internet" model?**
A: Tyler claims DNA stores biophotonic particles as data, has a natural frequency of about 150 MHz (similar to satellite communication), and follows language-like syntax and grammar, making the body "programmable"; bone calcium acts as an antenna, and entangled twin photons transfer state instantaneously — together making "the human body and DNA a biological internet." He dismisses SETI for looking "in all the wrong places with the wrong sensors," arguing humans are the best detectors. Pasulka relays this without endorsement. (Ch 6)

**Q: What is the "materiality of information," the chapter's title claim?**
A: Against both the disembodied soul and the assumption that information is "fundamentally immaterial," Pasulka argues information is physical — epitomized by quantum physicist Nicole Yunger Halpern's line "I like my quantum information physical" and by Tyler's insistence that body and matter are indispensable to the interface. She traces this from her purgatory scholarship (scholastics debating how immaterial souls could leave burn marks on tables) to the contemporary claim that the human body is the receiver of a signal. (Ch 6)

## The Human Receiver

**Q: Who is Rey Hernandez, and what happened to him?**
A: Rey Hernandez is a Miami lawyer and self-described "pure rationalist" atheist whose worldview was, in his words, "totally shattered" by a 2012 series of anomalous events shared with his devout Catholic wife, Dulce. The first centered on their paralyzed sixteen-year-old dog, Niña: Dulce saw a metallic object and prayed, Rey saw a translucent "plasma-being" that he says "did control my mind," and the dog recovered with "puppy energy" — while the couple's timelines conflicted entirely, leading Rey to invoke "missing time." Pasulka reads it as one event received through two frameworks: Dulce's angels, Rey's entities. (Ch 7)

**Q: What is FREE, and who founded it?**
A: FREE — the Dr. Edgar Mitchell Foundation for Research into Extraterrestrial Encounters — was cofounded by Rey Hernandez with Apollo 14 astronaut Edgar Mitchell, Harvard-Smithsonian astrophysicist Rudy Schild, and researcher Mary Rodwell. Pasulka calls it the first comprehensive, multilanguage, data-driven study of contact with nonhuman intelligence; its motto is "Disclosure from the Bottom Up," and Rey says it was started "over a three-day period under the guidance of some unknown non-human intelligence." (Ch 7)

**Q: What is Edgar Mitchell's "quantum hologram theory" and "dyadic model of consciousness"?**
A: The quantum hologram theory (QHTC) holds that information consists of patterns of energy given off by matter, so all bodies of matter contain information; Schild told Rey it explains all the "contact modalities." The dyadic model explains remote viewing, telepathy, and mystical experience as information requiring interpretation — in *samadhi* the body/brain resonates with the "zero point field," the Self merging into a primordial field, with ecstasy as an evolutionary reward signal. Rey argued this bridges ufology's materialist/subjectivist rift with a "Consciousness Paradigm." (Ch 7)

**Q: What was Edgar Mitchell's epiphany returning from the moon?**
A: Seeing Earth, Moon, and Sun on the return from Apollo 14, Mitchell realized the molecules of his body, his spacecraft, and his partners "were prototyped, manufactured in some ancient generation of stars," experiencing an overwhelming oneness and ecstasy — "it wasn't them and us, it was 'that's me.'" Finding nothing in the science literature, he found the exact description in the ancient Sanskrit accounts of *savikalpa samadhi*, and later founded the Institute of Noetic Sciences. (Ch 7)

**Q: What did Mitchell tell Pasulka about extraterrestrials?**
A: At a Skype session where, notably, only Pasulka asked about extraterrestrials, Mitchell said that extraterrestrials had dismantled several US weapons launched into space (nuclear weapons damage their environment too), that there are good and bad alien species, that some people on Earth have secret missions in contact with the good ones, and that left-behind technologies have already benefited humanity. Pasulka notes he grew up in Roswell and, based on old-timers he had known most of his life, held the Roswell crash was real. He died several months later, on the eve of his moon-landing anniversary. (Ch 7)

**Q: What are "esoteric cosmonauts"?**
A: Pasulka's category for the hidden, unofficial history of the space program — cosmonauts and rocket scientists (Tsiolkovsky, Jack Parsons, Tyler D., Edgar Mitchell) who believed in nonhuman beings interacting with humans to help them achieve space travel, and, for Mitchell, peace on Earth. She says their ideas "might appear to be on the fringe, and are," yet "may be on the fringe of our future." (Ch 7)

**Q: What was the "Hangar 1" betrayal?**
A: Rey's family experience became an entire episode of the History Channel's *Hangar 1* — he recognized his own handwriting from his MUFON report, with his attorney letterhead blacked out — but the positive encounter was morphed into a terrifying "UFO Home Invasion," cutting Niña's healing and Dulce's prayers. Pasulka presents it as a concrete demonstration of the "mechanisms of belief," and notes FREE's first dataset of over three thousand reports showed experiencers overwhelmingly reported *positive* interactions. (Ch 7)

**Q: What is Pasulka's criticism of MUFON's "big data" approach?**
A: She notes MUFON reports become the organization's property (sellable to TV producers), and that non-local, untrained researchers taking a "big data" approach lump misidentified blimps, drones, and lightning under "UFO," skewing the data and inflating apparent unknown sightings. She contrasts this with rigorous field investigators — like the North Carolina state director who rules out all mundane explanations before granting "unidentified" status. Her verdict: "The truth is potentially 'out there,' but it's unlikely to be found in media productions." (Ch 7)

## Real and Imaginary: Rome and Conversion

**Q: Why does Pasulka take Tyler to Rome, and how does it mirror the book's opening?**
A: Sent by an organization to analyze the canonization records of two seventeenth-century levitators — St. Joseph of Cupertino (canonized) and Sister Maria of Agreda (never canonized) — Pasulka brings Tyler to the Vatican Secret Archives and the Vatican Observatory. She frames it as the mirror of the opening: "This book began with a journey, and it ends with a journey," from the "ground zero of the UFO myth" (where Tyler took her blindfolded) to "ground zero of the Catholic faith." (Ch 8)

**Q: What is the book's most careful statement of its central claim?**
A: In this chapter Pasulka writes: "I am not making an ontological claim, that extraterrestrials are real in the sense that couches are real, although they could be. I am arguing that perceived contact has very real effects with powerful social implications." Perceived contact, she says, is "simultaneously imagined and real." (Ch 8)

**Q: Who was Sister Maria of Agreda, and why does Pasulka call her a "meta-experiencer"?**
A: Maria was a cloistered seventeenth-century Spanish mystic who claimed that, aided by angels, she bilocated to colonial New Mexico, met indigenous Americans, and prepared them for baptism — while her sister nuns reportedly saw her rise off the floor in brilliant light. Pasulka came to view her as a "meta-experiencer" and as Tyler's historical double: both "inadvertent colonists" whose imagined "first contact," amplified by their eras' media, paved the way for real colonization — Spain's of the New World, and today's elite-driven colonization of space. (Ch 8)

**Q: What is the "both/and" framework?**
A: Following Jeff Kripal, Pasulka prefers a "both/and" framework over "either/or": Maria may genuinely have had experiences that cannot easily be explained away or reduced to political machinations, AND those experiences (via the "lady in blue" story the Jumanos told missionaries, carried to King Philip IV by Alonso de Benavides) were "strategically politicized" to legitimize Spanish colonization. (Ch 8)

**Q: How did Tyler gain access to the Vatican Secret Archives?**
A: Pasulka spent a year arranging her own access (requiring a PhD and expertise in Catholic culture), while Tyler — with over forty patents but no PhD — was held at security for nearly two hours. From the plane she first told him not to reveal who he is, then relented; his next text read, "I'm in. My archive badge is good for six months" — the maximum. Her gloss: "Apparently, Tyler was known to members of the Vatican," and his visibility opened doors, including to key postulators, that her credentials alone could not. (Ch 8)

**Q: What was St. Joseph of Cupertino known for?**
A: He was a seventeenth-century Italian priest said to have levitated so frequently that the priests handling his canonization stopped counting witnesses — with records of him soaring to a cathedral ceiling and, on at least one occasion, carrying another person up with him. Pasulka comments that the sheer number of witnesses suggests they were probably not fabricating: "seemingly something had happened." (Ch 8)

**Q: What experiences in Rome moved Tyler toward conversion?**
A: A cascade of episodes: Mass in St. Peter's Basilica near the incorruptible body of Pope John XXIII; hospital rounds with Father McDonnell administering last rites, after which Tyler emerged from a dying young man's bedside with his "heart broken"; and a golf-cart tour ending at the ancient basilica of Santa Sabina, where Pasulka found him kneeling, praying and crying. At a restaurant afterward he told her, "I have to go back and help people directly. I feel like I am a complete failure." (Ch 8)

**Q: What did Pasulka notice on the carved door of Santa Sabina?**
A: She noticed the early depiction of the crucifixion (Christ standing calmly, arms outstretched between two "expectant" thieves) and, alongside it, images of levitation — panels showing Christ's ascension above a mysterious globe- or disc-like object scholars cannot identify (which some websites associate with a UFO), plus Elijah ascending on a cloud and Habakkuk lifted by an angel. The historian showing her the door conceded, confused, "I suppose, yes, these are of levitations." (Ch 8)

**Q: What is the Vatican Observatory at Castel Gandolfo, and what does it demonstrate for Pasulka?**
A: It is one of the oldest Western observatories, directed by American Jesuit astronomer Brother Guy Consolmagno (an MIT- and Arizona-trained meteorite specialist), whose archive holds once-banned works of Kepler and Copernicus now prominently displayed. Pasulka stresses the Jesuits are *not* searching for UFOs or doing ufology; what they demonstrate is that science is compatible with religion — so effectively that Tyler, a Baptist scientist, chose to become Catholic. (Ch 8)

**Q: How did Tyler's understanding of "the beings" change after Rome, and how did his conversion complete?**
A: His connection felt "supercharged," yet he also felt he knew *less* about what and who the beings were — coming to believe they were, or were like, the angels Maria said carried her, a complete shift Pasulka compares to Rey Hernandez's move from atheist to agnostic. Months later Tyler returned to Rome to make his first communion as a Catholic at a small Mass with Pope Francis at Santa Sabina, on a St. Valentine's Day that was also, rarely, Ash Wednesday — "not an end, but the very beginning." (Ch 8)

**Q: What does Pasulka note about the demographics and hidden history of ufology?**
A: Citing her colleague Brenda Denzler, she observes that ufology is "overwhelmingly white and male" and over 90 percent Anglo-American, and that private space heads are billionaires — an elite endeavor in which Tyler's and James's work is largely invisible. She adds that historians of ufology, with few exceptions, ignore African American and indigenous UFO traditions that predate the standard 1947 origin (the Nation of Islam had a UFO narrative by the 1930s, with Wallace Fard Muhammad reportedly speaking of UFOs in the 1920s). (Ch 8)

## The Artifact (Conclusion)

**Q: What did scientific analysis conclude about the artifact?**
A: Pasulka reports that research scientists analyzed the object she found with James and Tyler (which, she allows, "was perhaps planted" for her to find) and judged it "so anomalous as to be incomprehensible" — one scientist saying "It could not have been made in this universe." She stresses this did not mean the scientists concluded extraterrestrial origin; they simply did not know how or by whom it was made, and were "comfortable, if amazed" with that ambiguity. Tyler called it "elegant beyond comprehension." (Ch 9)

**Q: Why does Pasulka open the Conclusion with "Credo quia absurdum"?**
A: The Latin phrase ("I believe because it is absurd") is Vallee's epigraph to the chapter — "Credo quia absurdum, eh, Diana?" Pasulka observes that absurdity "seems to have been written into the fabric of the artifact" itself, tying the ending back to Vallee's earlier themes of absurdity and deception. (Ch 9)

**Q: How does Pasulka read the artifact as a "relic"?**
A: She argues that as an object of mystery the artifact functions religiously, like the sacred relics of Catholic devotionalism — her paradigm case being the Shroud of Turin, an "artifact of faith, devotion, and belief" whose origin no theory conclusively explains and which leaves "a trail of miracles." Sacred artifacts, she says, "are objects of power. Part of their power lies in their mystery." (Ch 9)

**Q: What is Pasulka's teaching definition of religion, and how does it apply to the artifact?**
A: She teaches that traditional religions have "functional aspects" easily studied (churches, sacred texts, oral traditions) plus a "sacred" element — the mysterious object of belief that cannot be studied objectively: "One cannot put an angel under a microscope." It is this "mysterious sacred" that distinguishes religion from sports or fandoms — and for the scientist-believers, the artifact's impenetrable mystery compels reverence and belief. (Ch 9)

**Q: Why do anomalies matter, according to the Conclusion?**
A: Pasulka argues, via James's method and Thomas Kuhn's advice to John Mack, that anomalies are where scientific knowledge advances. James redirected graduate students toward data that did not fit their hypothesis — "the anomaly was there for a reason" — and Kuhn told Mack to focus on the raw data and keep collecting it even when it fit no conventional framework, which is "precisely how the scientists were proceeding." (Ch 9)

**Q: What is the "artifact–observer symbiosis," and what prompted it?**
A: When Tyler carried the part in his backpack, a friend later messaged that he had dreamt "there was a separate universe that you carried in it... very much the essence of turtles all the way down." Tyler, recalling Whitley Strieber's remark "that the artifacts we studied also studied us," proposed "some sort of symbiotic relationship between the artifact and those in its proximity. It generates information. Some people are able to pick up on that information. Don't ask me to explain it, because I can't." (Ch 9)

**Q: What was the December 2017 New York Times article, and why does it matter to the book's argument?**
A: The article published the testimony of Luis Elizondo, former director of the Pentagon's Aerospace Advanced Threat Identification Program, alleging a secret US program to study UFOs. Pasulka says it set off a firestorm of "disclosure" or "unofficial disclosure," prompted FOIA demands for government-held debris or "alloys," and promoted the "realism" that is one of her named mechanisms of belief — even converting some previously scoffing colleagues into interested parties. (Ch 9)

**Q: How do Shklovsky and Heidegger close the book?**
A: In the Vatican Observatory archive Pasulka found Sagan and Shklovsky's *Intelligent Life in the Universe* and was struck by Shklovsky's line "The prey runs to the predator" — originally about the risk that contacted extraterrestrial life might be unfriendly, which she reinterprets as humanity's "unreflective embrace" of media and technology. She ends with Heidegger's prediction that a technology-dominated era would be misunderstood and dangerous, and his final answer to *Der Spiegel*: "Only a God can save us now." (Ch 9)

**Q: Where does Pasulka finally position herself at the end of the book?**
A: As "an outsider" to the community of scientist-believers, unable to resolve the mystery but a careful witness to its effects: "I can't solve the mystery of the artifact, but I have seen how its reality has inspired belief and, as Jung notes, rumors that spin mythologies." Her deepest concern, she says, is not extraterrestrials but the quasi-religious power of media and technology over human belief. (Ch 9)
