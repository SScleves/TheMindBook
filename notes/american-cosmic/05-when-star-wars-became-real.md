# When Star Wars Became Real

## In one paragraph
In this chapter (numbered 4 in the book, subtitled "The Mechanisms of Belief"), D.W. Pasulka draws on her own field experience as a consultant to the screenwriters of The Conjuring to identify the media techniques — the "mechanisms of belief" — by which films and digital productions generate real belief, real practices, and even real memories in their spectators. She argues that the UFO is the best example of how these mechanisms work: a "fabricated UFO" era has arrived in which realist montage, "based on true events" framing, cultural authorities, and CGI splicing of aliens into historical footage together create the belief system of the UFO. She supports the argument with cognitive science (Jeffrey Zacks on event models, the Stanford Virtual Human Interaction Lab on virtual reality, Elizabeth Loftus on reconstructive memory, Alison Landsberg's prosthetic memory) and with a cascade of cases: specialist factual programming like The Great Martian War 1913–1917, the "Star Wars in ordinary life" trend, the religion of Jediism, Kubrick's monolith read by Robert Ager as the cinema screen itself, the Betty and Barney Hill abduction case, and Disney's 1995 Alien Encounters from Tomorrow Land. Pasulka's larger claim is that media technologies are not external to our minds and bodies — imagination has been exteriorized into our media, which then determines what is in our heads — and that this digital context is what makes the new, future-oriented UFO religiosity possible.

## Core claims
- Pasulka claims that movies about religion and the supernatural "produce real physiological effects, including practices and belief in things — even supernatural things," and that they "can also create and mimic real memories. In a very real sense, we incorporate these films into our minds and bodies. They become us." She names this the Total Recall Effect.
- She argues the Total Recall Effect goes beyond confabulation (the inability to distinguish fact from fantasy), though it could be considered a form of confabulation generated and nurtured by modern technology; her urban-legend research showed "the movie image trumped the historical record."
- Pasulka argues that the UFO phenomenon "offers the best example of how these techniques, the mechanisms of belief, work to inform and sustain religious belief and practice."
- She identifies specific mechanisms: the "based on true events" strategy, the realist montage, reliance on cultural authorities commenting in the media, the splicing of digital characters into iconic historical photographs, and depictions of scenes from ordinary lives.
- Citing neuroscientist Jeffrey Zacks, she says the brain does not sort real-life events, movie events, and novel events into separate "buckets"; cognitive models of events get conflated, especially when fiction uses realism-creating techniques — and this conflation happens unconsciously and probably cannot be trained away (warnings and corrections reduce but do not remove the effect).
- She notes that producers will likely resist disclaimers "because the confusion between fact and fiction has proven to be very lucrative," and argues immersive virtual reality is the real game-changer, citing the Stanford finding that "the brain often fails to differentiate between virtual experiences and real ones."
- Pasulka (agreeing with informant Scott Browne) declares "a new era is upon us, the era of the fabricated UFO, which is also the object of near-universal belief" — a perfect storm of belief-generating mechanisms based on faked videos, rumors of truth, and "the future-real — which is the potential reality of the UFO."
- She observes that The X-Files is not simply fictional: declassified documents show US and UK governments did cover up and manage information about reported UFO events, and the 1953 Robertson Panel recommended exactly the documentary-style techniques and authoritative experts later used by Hollywood marketers.
- The X-Files logline "I Want to Believe" is, per Pasulka, brilliant: it preserves believers' credibility by pairing belief with doubt, sidestepping Jung's point that no sensible person would admit to belief in UFOs — while belief in the possibility of extraterrestrial life seems sensible.
- She argues that Jediism and other UFO/ET belief systems are powerful because they "replace, supplant, or even . . . supplement and revise traditional religious beliefs," incorporating the realism of historical religions and projecting it into the future: potential nonhuman intelligent life exists in our material universe, "not in a past of questionable historical veracity and not in a nonmaterial postdeath reality."
- She contends the context that makes this new religiosity possible is digital: responding to historian of religion Robert Orsi's challenge to understand gods and sacred entities like saints as autonomous agents, she argues the "Star Wars in ordinary life" examples "offer a clue to an answer," and that this new religiosity must be understood "at its source — from within the screen itself," not through binaries of real/unreal, humans/gods, or body/mind.
- Pasulka claims the modern binary of "human" and "machine" is "the real fake, not new religious forms" — invoking Andy Clark's extended cognition and N. Katherine Hayles's technogenesis.
- On alien abduction: she does not discount that experiencers like the Hills had a real experience, but insists the hypnotic-regression tradition "does need to be reassessed given what is now known about how media technologies influence how humans think and what they remember"; abduction experiences are "remembered with and through the vast corpus of media products about abductions and UFOs."
- Her closing claim: research across cognitive science, history, and film studies reveals what Kubrick knew intuitively — "media technologies are not external to our bodies or minds, but inhabit them in specific ways"; Kubrick foresaw "the screen as a type of conduit to consciousness."

## Concepts
- **mechanisms of belief** — Pasulka's umbrella term for the media techniques that influence religious belief and belief in the supernatural: the "based on true events" strategy, realist montage, reliance on cultural authorities, splicing digital characters into iconic historical photographs, and depictions of ordinary life. In the Disney documentary she glosses them as "formal techniques that lack real-world substance."
- **Total Recall Effect** — Pasulka's coinage for how films about religion "don't just get in our heads; they become us, in the form of memories." It goes beyond confabulation, though it could be considered a technologically generated and nurtured form of it.
- **confabulation** — defined in passing as "the inability to distinguish fact from fantasy."
- **"based on true events" strategy** — a marketing/framing technique (popularized by the graduate-student writers of The Blair Witch Project) of pretending a fictional movie records a real event; historical religion films (e.g., about Jesus) begin with a preexisting assumption that the portrayed events are real, so viewers take them as historical accounts.
- **realist montage** — Pasulka's term for splicing different scenes together to create a narrative and a cognitive connection between them: fiction placed side by side with real life/nonfiction to create a realistic effect, grafting causally unrelated images into new meaning (also how internet memes are made).
- **cultural authorities** — authority figures (scholars, theologians, NASA spokespersons, CEOs) whose media commentary on a production generates buzz and lends it credibility; Pasulka realized she herself functioned as one for The Conjuring.
- **specialist factual programming** — a newish media genre focused on making factual or historical events "special" with digital technologies; by design it uses the very techniques that foster the mixing of the real and the unreal. Pasulka flags the irony of the name against Philip K. Dick's "extra-factual memory."
- **extra-factual memory** — the product of the evil company in Philip K. Dick's 1966 story "I Can Remember It for You Wholesale" (inspiration for Total Recall): implanted virtual memories.
- **fabricated UFO** — the object of the new era Pasulka announces: UFO belief built on faked videos and belief-generating media mechanisms, yet "the object of near-universal belief."
- **future-real** — "the potential reality of the UFO": belief anchored not in past events but in what is potentially real in the future (e.g., the tenet that we will find nonhuman life — "It is only a matter of time").
- **Jediism / the Force** — a religious movement claiming Star Wars as sacred "scripture"; practitioners believe there is a transcendent, supernatural element within Star Wars — the Force — and hold that because Lucas based the films on Daoism and Zen Buddhism, Star Wars points, "like a finger pointing to the moon," to eternal truths.
- **fakelore / fake religion** (Chidester) — religion that, "although invented, mobilized, and deployed by frauds, can produce real effects in the real world."
- **invented religions** (Cusack) — religions that openly advertise their invention; studying them enriches knowledge of traditional religions and shows how science fiction and new technologies inform belief and practice.
- **fiction-based religions** (Davidsen) — a proposed new category: unlike historical religions, they "draw their main inspiration from fictional narratives that do not claim to refer to the actual world, but create a fictional world of their own." Pasulka's own interpretation differs: Jediism sits in a milieu that posits the realism of ETs, if not now then in the future.
- **external world skepticism** — the philosophical idea, at least since Plato, that "we cannot assume that what we see or identify with our senses is real."
- **imagination exteriorized** — Pasulka's image for the current condition: "It's as if our imaginations have become exterior to ourselves, existing out there in our media, and our media then determines what is in our heads."
- **extended cognition** (Andy Clark) — the finding that the assumption that cognition is brain-bound (occurring only within the skull) is wrong; cognition occurs within a network that extends into the environment.
- **technogenesis** (N. Katherine Hayles) — the coevolution of humans with their technologies; technologies are not exterior to humans but merge with us in an ever more complicated, inextricable relationship (with speculation that this drives evolution toward a posthuman being).
- **postbiological intelligence** (Susan Schneider) — the claim that any nonhuman intelligence humans encounter would be in postbiological form — artificial intelligence — because that is the form "the most advanced alien civilization" would take.
- **monolith as cinema screen / human–monolith continuum** — Robert Ager's decoding of 2001: A Space Odyssey: the monolith is a metaphor for the cinema screen; Pasulka concludes "there is a human–monolith continuum, the boundaries of which are very vague."
- **hypnotic regression** — the practice of uncovering purportedly suppressed memories (of abduction) under hypnosis; established as a convention by the Hill case and central to John Mack's and Budd Hopkins's work; called into question by memory science.
- **wraparound eyes** — UFO-parlance term for the alien eyes Barney Hill described under hypnosis, trailing around the sides of the head — matching, per Martin Kottmeyer (1994), the alien in The Outer Limits episode "The Bellero Shield" broadcast twelve days before the session.
- **book encounter** — Pasulka's label for Betty Hill checking out Donald Keyhoe's UFO book from the library after her Air Force report (paralleling the book's recurring theme of pivotal book encounters).
- **reconstructive memory** (Elizabeth Loftus) — memory is not a video camera but a dynamic process: "Our memories are reconstructive . . . It's a little like a Wikipedia page, you can change it, but so can other people." Pasulka adds: so can a movie or a video game — "Maybe the human being is like a Wikipedia page, and we are not the sole editors of our own pages."
- **pseudo-memories** (Robert Baker, citing Loftus/Hilgard) — clinically and experimentally implanted false memories that come to be believed as veridical.
- **prosthetic memory** (Alison Landsberg) — memories that do not come from a person's lived experience; cinema "has long been aware of its ability to generate experiences and to install memories of them — memories which become experiences that film consumers both possess and feel possessed by."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Pasulka as consultant on The Conjuring (2012)** — hired for "a movie about Catholic culture"; screenwriters Chad and Carey Hayes, twin brothers from Malibu, had just made The Reaping (2007, starring Hilary Swank) about a woman scholar of religion — Pasulka's own profile; "we had written about each other prior to our meeting" (a synchronicity she, per David Stinnett's counsel, does not over-read). The Conjuring displaced The Exorcist as second-highest-grossing supernatural film. Its closing credits are her prime realist-montage example: photos and real names of Ed and Lorraine Warren and the Perron family placed alongside the costumed actors, letting spectators conflate real lives with fictional portrayals. Director James Wan's tweet that a consultant was hired made Pasulka herself a cultural authority.
- **The Blair Witch Project (1999)** — graduate-student writers increased sales by pretending the movie was based on a real event; origin of the strategy Pasulka studies.
- **Jesus films** — viewers take them as historical accounts, yet Jesus is shown as a white European (he was not) and Mary Magdalene as a reformed prostitute (no evidence she was one): the "based on true events" premise smuggling in fiction.
- **The Passion of the Christ (2004)** — marketing company Grace Hill Media invited scholars of religion and theologians to a prescreening; their published reviews created media buzz — cultural authorities as belief mechanism.
- **Zacks on Hitchcock's Saboteur** — a fictional Nazi-saboteur film with a real-world agenda; cultural authorities lauded it as accurate; Zacks bets many viewers integrated the film's factory bombing with newspaper/newsreel information and would later recount the fiction as fact — "just what makes such a movie effective as propaganda."
- **Zacks's misinformation study** — students shown a factually inaccurate historical film; a specific warning about bogus information plus correction when students accepted bogus facts reduced but did not remove the misinformation effect.
- **Stanford Virtual Human Interaction Lab** — scholars found "the brain often fails to differentiate between virtual experiences and real ones."
- **The X-Files (1993–2002)** — weekly series about a systematic government cover-up of UFOs; in a National Geographic poll, respondents chose it as the best representation of what actual UFOs and aliens would be like. NatGeo executive Brad Dancer conceded companies like his may bolster UFO belief: "As movies portraying aliens become increasingly convincing, they may subconsciously affect people's attitudes."
- **The 1953 Robertson Panel** — impetus for Project Blue Book; suggested a media campaign to manage public perception of the phenomena, recommending the very documentary-style techniques and authoritative experts later used by The Conjuring's screenwriters, Blair Witch's producers, and Grace Hill Media.
- **Impossible Factual and The Great Martian War 1913–1917** — a production company (clients: National Geographic, History Channel, Smithsonian) exclusively doing specialist factual work; its "documentary" uses realist montage to simulate a WWI fought against "a savage race of extraterrestrial invaders," with modern-day historians and aging veterans, fused historical archive and special effects. It "took social media and television by storm." The promo ranks it side by side with a factual WWII series (WWII's Greatest Raids: TV Series Documentary / The Great Martian War 1913–1917: 2013 TV Movie Documentary), solidifying the fiction–fact relationship.
- **Megalodon belief** — some young audience members believe the extinct prehistoric shark survives because they saw it portrayed in specialist factual programming (spelled "Magaledon" in the text).
- **If Star Wars Was Real (ISWWR) website** — iconic photos of the JFK assassination and the Hindenburg explosion with R2D2 and stormtroopers expertly Photoshopped in; a tongue-in-cheek mission statement soliciting "evidence" that Star Wars is real, claiming archives closed their doors and evidence disappeared "in the early 1970s." Pasulka contacted the creator, who insisted (and she believes) that participants know it is not real — her point is about how brains process such imagery regardless.
- **Thomas Dagg's "Star Wars" photo project** — photographer, twenty-four, recreated scenes of his youth with Star Wars characters ("If it was a blizzard outside I always thought of Hoth. If I saw a jogger I would imagine them with Yoda on their back"); the personal project unexpectedly "blew up"; Star Wars motivated him to become an artist.
- **Stormtrooper dash cam video** — a thirty-second video mimicking Russian dash cam crash footage, but the crash is a stormtrooper beside his crashed imperial TIE fighter; over two million views; credits link it to Lucasfilm, possibly franchise advertising.
- **"Death Star over San Francisco" by Michael Horn** — over 3.5 million views; the Death Star hovers over a San Francisco street demonstration, TIE fighters hover near beachgoers, no one takes notice; its realism earned Horn a Wired interview ("Lucas has not called me yet...").
- **The anonymous Oculus Rift programmer** — interviewed early in Pasulka's research (the headset company later sold to Facebook); working "in the set" much of the day, he experiences déjà-vu-like uncertainty: "was it a memory of something that really happened, or, did it happen in the set? I just can't remember."
- **The Jedi Knight at her community talk** — about fifty attendees laughed when Pasulka mentioned Jediism; afterward a man and his son approached; after she guessed Buddhism and Christianity, she realized he was a Jedi — he proudly confirmed he was a Jedi Knight. Pasulka calls him "a sign of how religious forms change over time and across material conditions."
- **The 2002 UK census joke** — people claimed Star Wars as their religion as a joke; Pasulka used it in class as a definitional puzzle, expecting it to be irrelevant within a year; instead it became part of an enduring movement with official religious groups.
- **Ellen Stofan's announcement** — NASA's chief scientist: strong indications of alien life within a decade, definite evidence within 20–30 years ("We know where to look. We know how to look...") — a cultural authority bolstering confidence; Pasulka also notes many UFO religions hold that alien entities left us "artifacts," and that such artifacts "inspire Tyler and James to create their innovative technologies" (and cites Eddy as a case of supplementing/revising traditional belief).
- **Robert Ager's monolith analysis of 2001: A Space Odyssey** — posted on YouTube in 2007 to hundreds of thousands of positive responses: the monolith is a metaphor for the cinema screen. Evidence Pasulka rehearses: Kubrick's stated intent to bypass conscious rationalization and "sink straight into the unconscious"; the stargate scene where Bowman's eye morphs to reflect the screen-like stargate until retina and screen are indistinguishable; the hotel-room television of Clarke's book replaced by the monolith in the film ("His feeling that he was inside a movie set was almost literally true"); the camera crew reflected in Bowman's helmet before he becomes the starchild. Around 2007, ads superimposed the iPhone on the monolith — for Pasulka, popular culture revealing the human–monolith continuum.
- **The monolith's dark side** — it appears at evolutionary shifts: teaching hominids the bone-tool, which (thrown skyward) match-cuts to a satellite — a modern tool of dominance; in a later Apple ad this whole scene plays on an iPhone screen ("Perhaps it reflects a truth"). Also dark: the film's Department of Defense program using hypnosis, drugs, and special effects to make subjects believe they contact alien intelligences, to acclimate humans to ET reality — Pasulka's framework for reinterpreting the abduction-hypnosis tradition (including John Mack's Abduction).
- **Whitley Strieber via Jeffrey Kripal** — the "alien abductee" author of Communion explained he was "perfectly aware" his visionary experience of the visitors was deeply informed by the bad sci-fi B movies he consumed as a kid in 1950s cold-war southern Texas.
- **The Betty and Barney Hill case** — interracial couple, civil rights activists, New Hampshire; September 19, 1961, on a rural road they saw a star-like light that hovered, then approached almost filling the windshield; Barney got out with a pistol; they went home. Two days later Betty called Pease Air Force Base; Major Paul W. Henderson's file logged a probable misidentified planet (Betty mentioned no beings). Betty then borrowed Donald Keyhoe's UFO book (head of the National Investigations Committee on Aerial Phenomena; thesis: advanced alien beings exist and the Department of Defense hides the evidence). Barney had headaches and nightmares; under hypnotic regression they related abduction, examination aboard a craft. David Halperin — a scholar of the Merkabah, the Jewish mystical tradition that arises from the visionary aspects of Ezekiel's wheel — highlights Barney's February 22 description of "slanted . . . [b]ut not like a Chinese" wraparound eyes — and Martin Kottmeyer's 1994 discovery that The Outer Limits episode "The Bellero Shield" aired February 10, 1964, twelve days before the session, featuring an alien with the same eyes. Pasulka does not discount a real experience but reframes it via the cognitive science of media: "What if what they remembered was not real but virtually real?"
- **The Hundredth Monkey and Other Paradigms of the Paranormal (1991)** — skeptical essay collection (contributors included Isaac Asimov and Carl Sagan) amid the Mack/Hopkins abduction-book flurry; Robert Baker, citing Loftus, described implanted pseudo-memories, including Hilgard's (1981) implanted memory of a bank robbery that never occurred — the subject even picked the "robber" from photographs.
- **Memory Studies journal issue on film and memory** — two decades of growing attention to cinema as repository "for representing, shaping, (re)creating or indexing forms of individual and collective memories"; a section on Landsberg's prosthetic memory. Application to abduction: recallers access both features of an experience and similar things they have seen — often movies about extraterrestrials.
- **Disney's Alien Encounters from Tomorrow Land (1995)** — TV program and theme-park exhibit, now defunct, featuring carefully edited testimonies of real abductees (explicit references to sex with aliens were cut); "experts" like Budd Hopkins; military-base footage for credibility. It opens: "Intelligent life from distant galaxies is now attempting to make open contact with the human race, and tonight we will show you the evidence." CEO Michael Eisner appears in a realist montage at what looks like a top-secret military installation; newspaper clippings include Jimmy Carter's sighting testimony; images of mutating cells imply alien genetic engineering; "compelling footage of home videos" shows what look like balloons — the type Scott Browne identified. Most disturbing to Pasulka (and to Halperin, who said the production stank "of dishonesty and manipulation"): the claim that many Americans would likely be abducted within five years and could acclimate via the "ExtraTERRORestrial Alien Encounter" ride, where Hopkins shows children cards of aliens and they scream in terror (Hopkins calls it "moving"). One rider: "I THOUGHT I DREAMT ABOUT GOING ON THE EXTRATERRORESTRIAL RIDE, BUT IT WAS REAL AND IT WAS TERRIFYING. I'M HAVING FLASHBACKS!"
- **Stanford History Education Group study (2015–2016)** — middle school, high school, and college youth largely could not distinguish sponsored content from legitimately sourced content, fake accounts from real, activist groups from neutral sources, paid ads from articles — "getting duped again and again" with "stunning and dismaying consistency" despite only a "reasonable bar" being tested; social-media fluency does not equal savvy.
- **Tyler's shuttle-launch media story** — Tyler D. told a prominent news reporter his story "reminded me of Scooby Doo"; the reporter answered, "That's OK. I only have three minutes," and declined Tyler's offer of an hour to make the three minutes profound. Tyler: "It's a deep flaw in the way things are presented to the public, and no one wants to change or fix it."

## Thread
This chapter is the book's theoretical engine room for the "religion-in-the-making" thesis: having introduced her scientist-Experiencers (Tyler D., James) and field sites in earlier chapters, Pasulka here explains how belief itself is manufactured and sustained — not by argument but by media mechanisms operating below consciousness. It supplies the machinery that makes sense of the UFO phenomenon's cultural power regardless of the reality question she keeps bracketed: even if experiences are real, they are remembered "with and through" media. It connects backward to Scott Browne's fabricated-video research and the recurring "book encounter" motif (here Betty Hill's Keyhoe book), and forward to the book's claims about artifacts (the alien "artifacts" that inspire Tyler and James), the human as receiver/conduit (the screen as "conduit to consciousness"), and technology as sacred object (the monolith–iPhone continuum). The Jediism material and the Chidester/Cusack/Davidsen engagement position the UFO belief system within religious studies as a new, future-real form of religiosity native to the digital age.
