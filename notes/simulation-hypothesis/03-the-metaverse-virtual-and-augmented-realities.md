# Ch 3 — The Metaverse: Virtual and Augmented Realities

## In one paragraph
Virk traces the metaverse from Neal Stephenson's coinage in *Snow Crash* (1992) through Active Worlds, *Second Life*, the Facebook-to-Meta rename, and *Ready Player One*, arguing that science fiction and technology drive each other in a "Sci-Fi Feedback Loop." He then lays out the next three stages of his roadmap to the "simulation point": Stage 4, full immersion via VR headsets (with a candid inventory of today's shortcomings — bulk, dedicated space, nausea, non-photorealism, power, missing haptics and 3D sound); Stage 5, photorealistic augmented and mixed reality, where rendered objects become indistinguishable from physical ones; and Stage 6, rendering in the real world without glasses at all, via light-field/volumetric displays and 3D printing (including bioprinting of living tissue). Each stage, he argues, progressively validates the book's underlying theme: that physical objects — and even biological bodies — can be reduced to information plus the processing (rendering) of that information, which is exactly what the simulation hypothesis proposes is already happening around us.

## Core claims
- Virk argues the metaverse is defined by an independent, persistent virtual world whose landscape is "built mostly by users," an idea that originated in MMORPGs (previous chapter) and was named by science fiction.
- Virk argues there is a "Sci-Fi Feedback Loop": science fiction influences real-world techno-scientific development, and real-world innovations influence the next wave of science fiction (space-age authors ↔ the space age; personal computers ↔ cyberpunk; *Snow Crash* → Second Life; Second Life → *Ready Player One* → Oculus → Meta). He says these visions "are influencing, both consciously and unconsciously, what innovators are creating."
- Virk argues metaverse hype runs in cycles: Second Life's boom collapsed after 2008; Meta's 2021 rename drove hype to new heights (a Time magazine cover); by 2022–2023 it died down again, replaced by AI hype — while the underlying technology "has continued to be realized."
- Virk claims *Second Life* was "the first real-world metaverse" to realize many aspects of Stephenson's vision and a major milestone on the road to the simulation point.
- Virk frames the Great Simulation ("our version of The Matrix") as a more sophisticated incarnation of today's MMORPGs, with better output methods, better input methods, better NPCs, and full immersion — "a fully immersive metaverse-like virtual world."
- Virk says the book will extrapolate forward on three dimensions: fidelity (how realistic games look), method of projection (2D screens → VR → AR → direct mind broadcast), and method of control (keyboards/controllers → voice, kinesthetic, and mind interfaces), drawing on computer science, human–machine interfaces, and neuroscience, and entering speculative territory (human-level AI, downloadable consciousness).
- Virk argues that if we can reach the simulation point in any reasonable time frame, then civilizations thousands or millions of years older may have already reached it — meaning "we are, in fact, already inside their virtual world" (argument developed in the final chapter of this part).
- Virk claims his own VR "conversion experiences" show the mind can be convinced a virtual scene is real after only minutes of real-time fusion between body movement and the rendered world.
- Virk argues that once photorealistic mixed reality is achieved, "it is not a leap" to suppose a hidden rendering engine is already at work creating the physical objects around us — physical objects "are truly revealed to be based on information, and only in our perception do they appear real."
- Virk claims the main obstacle to photorealistic VR/AR is no longer resolution but speed (of rendering engines) and size (of glasses), both improvable via optimization and better hardware; AI generation may be the real accelerator for photorealistic content at scale.
- Virk argues Stage 6 technologies (light-field displays, 3D printing) show computer-based rendering is not confined to screens but can operate in the physical world, "bolstering the idea that the physical world consists of information that is rendered for us."
- Virk claims physics supports the blurring of virtual and physical: solid objects are "really 99 percent empty space," and solidity comes from electrons absorbing light — so if "dancing electrons" make objects look and feel solid, that dance could be simulated with particles of light.
- Virk argues DNA shows living bodies are also information-based: genes act as instructions for building proteins, so "what we think of as a physical body can really be expressed as information that is interpreted by a biological printing process."
- Virk predicts ubiquitous bioprinters capable of repairing or replacing body parts from our own living cells are "probably less than a decade (or two, maximum)" away.

## Concepts
- **Metaverse** — an independent, persistent virtual world beyond any individual player or session, with the twist that the virtual landscape is built mostly by users. Term coined by Neal Stephenson in *Snow Crash* (1992); Stephenson envisioned a combination of an interactive video game and today's internet, hosting hundreds of millions of players with a full virtual economy and intelligent AI characters.
- **Avatar** — the in-world representation of a player. Stephenson's usage in *Snow Crash* popularized the term, now universal in the video game industry and beyond.
- **Sci-Fi Feedback Loop** — Virk's (informal) name for the process in which science fiction influences real-world techno-scientific development and real-world innovations influence the next wave of science fiction.
- **Simulation point** — the point at which the technical developments in these stages come together into the capability to build the Great Simulation. The exact date is unknown.
- **The Great Simulation** — "our version of The Matrix": a more sophisticated incarnation of today's MMORPGs with better output methods, better input methods, better NPCs, and full immersion.
- **Stage 4: Immersion Using Virtual Reality** (roadmap stage defined here) — moving beyond the 2D screen to full immersion via VR headsets; culminates in a fully immersive, photorealistic persistent virtual world (the merging of VR and MMORPGs, like the fictional OASIS).
- **Stage 5: Photorealistic Augmented and Mixed Reality (AR, MR)** (roadmap stage defined here) — rendering virtual objects into the physical environment in real time until they are indistinguishable from physical objects.
- **Stage 6: Real-World Rendering — Light-Field Display and 3D Printing** (roadmap stage defined here) — rendering objects in the physical world without glasses, via light-field/volumetric displays and 3D printers; "bleeding-edge research and speculative technology."
- **Virtual reality (VR)** — the state of the art in immersion: goggles with two small screens, one per eye, images offset by roughly the distance between the eyes; immersion comes from obscuring everything else in the user's environment so the user feels inside the scene.
- **Room-scale VR** — a room cordoned off for the VR experience, in which the player physically moves (the setting of Virk's table-tennis conversion experience).
- **Augmented reality (AR)** — closely related to VR (still requires glasses or a lens), but instead of fully immersing you, it shows you your physical surroundings and virtually adds things to them — e.g., putting chairs, spiders, or snakes on a real table.
- **Photorealistic mixed reality (MR)** — Virk's term for the point where virtual objects rendered in the physical world in real time are indistinguishable from actual physical objects — "a significant point on the road to the simulation point."
- **Spatial computing** — the term Apple popularized with the Vision Pro to encompass both AR and VR.
- **The dial-in effect** — the ability (seen in Apple Vision Pro and Meta's latest Quest) to dial the real world in or out, moving between AR and VR mode "like the dimmer on a light switch"; Virk uses it to question what distinguishes a "real world" object from an overlaid one.
- **Stereoscopy** — the technical term for presenting two images, one per eye, offset at the right distance so the mind assembles a single three-dimensional perspective; dates back to Victorian-era devices.
- **Rendering engine / virtual camera** — game scenes are stored as 3D models and textures, not pixels; the rendering engine converts models into pixels in real time based on where the character is and which way the avatar points — a "virtual camera." For VR it renders two offset versions of the scene via "a simple mathematical equation."
- **Persistent world / virtual economy / Linden dollars** — persistence plus user building enabled Second Life's economy: land ownership, structures that remain, jobs paid in Linden dollars (a virtual currency exchangeable for real-world dollars), and player-run virtual businesses.
- **Haptic suit** — a garment or wearable giving kinesthetic feedback; with physical-motion machines like (omnidirectional) treadmills, part of the accessory kit in *Ready Player One* for feeling immersed.
- **3D sound / spatial audio** — mimicking sound coming from different points in virtual space by simulating, through headphones, only the subset of sound each ear would hear from a given position; needed because in VR all sound physically comes from one place (the headset), while real ears triangulate.
- **Motion capture** — an actor wears a suit with sensors tracking key body points (facial motion capture does the same for the face); registered movements drive the CG animation for a character, letting nonhuman characters like Gollum "act" human.
- **CGI (computer-generated imagery)** — today's film special effects method, replacing physical models and puppets; unlike AR, film CGI need only be shot once rather than rendered in real time.
- **Deepfakes / AI image and video generation** — AI-generated realistic videos of people saying/doing things they never did; tools like Midjourney and OpenAI's Sora produce convincing photorealistic video, not yet in real time and with frame-level flaws (e.g., hands with more than five fingers).
- **Light-field display** — a way to render an object in the real world by calculating the exact amount of light that would be reflected off it and received by observers at different points in a room; initially simulated through headsets (e.g., Magic Leap), eventually using lasers to place particles of light in a room, requiring no headset.
- **Volumetric display** — displays using a combination of lasers to create holographic images placeable in the real world (e.g., on a table) that appear real from any point in the room, with light reflected off them as from a real physical object.
- **3D printing / physical pixels** — an evolution of 2D printing: printing an object precisely using pixels in three dimensions, where the pixels are physical dots of material (layers just 0.1 mm), driven by 3D-model information — "a rendering engine for the real world."
- **CMYK color model** — the cyan/magenta/yellow (plus black) ink model that made photorealistic digital printing possible; given as the 2D ancestor of 3D printing's material expansion.
- **Bioprinting / bio-inks** — printing living tissue with a 3D printer; bio-inks use live cells (mostly from plants) turned into an "ink"/material that can be assembled into living tissue or scale models of organs.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- ***Snow Crash* (Neal Stephenson, 1992)** — cyberpunk novel that coined *metaverse* and popularized *avatar*. Its metaverse: a 3D virtual world entered via VR goggles, extrapolating late-1980s computer graphics; hundreds of millions of players; highly realistic 3D rendered in real time on each device; user-built buildings and landscape; a full virtual economy (get and perform jobs, socialize in virtual bars and cafés, virtual sword fights); intelligent AI characters (then called "programs") serving drinks or giving virtual massages. Virk uses it as the template the industry has been chasing ever since.
- **Active Worlds (1995)** — the earliest attempt to build the metaverse, launched within a few years of *Snow Crash*; contained a replica of the novel's district "The Street." Limited by the bandwidth and graphics capabilities of the time; now defunct. Shows the Sci-Fi Feedback Loop starting immediately but ahead of the enabling tech.
- **Linden Lab / *Second Life* (Philip Rosedale; founded 1999, released 2004)** — enabled by two innovations that had reached widespread adoption: GPUs (fast math for rendering) and broadband (continuously connected computers) — the same keys behind the MMORPG wave of the time (e.g., *World of Warcraft*). Virk calls it "the first real-world metaverse." Players created avatars *and* parts of the 3D world itself; activities included dance clubs, building houses, concerts, getting married, shooting arrows or laser guns, and holding jobs paid in Linden dollars (exchangeable for real dollars). Linden Lab was among the first game companies ever to hire an economist to manage a virtual economy; at one point the Second Life economy was worth half a billion dollars a year. There were blogs and newspapers dedicated to its residents. What it shows: persistence + user creation ⇒ a real alternate world with a real economy.
- **The Second Life bust (after 2008)** — the hype died, the user base contracted, new users "couldn't figure out what to do in this open-world virtual landscape," and the boom in metaverse companies collapsed, relegating the metaverse back to science fiction. Virk uses it as the first hype cycle.
- **Facebook → Meta (2021)** — the largest social media company renamed itself as Zuckerberg announced they wanted to lead in building the metaverse; hype reached new heights, with the metaverse on the cover of Time magazine a few months later. In 2022–2023 the hype died again, replaced by AI hype — while the enabling technology kept advancing. Second hype cycle; also evidence of Hollywood–Silicon Valley intertwining.
- **Space-age vs. cyberpunk sci-fi** — Asimov, Clarke, and Heinlein wrote star-travel fiction when computers were room-sized enterprise machines; their work both influenced and was influenced by the space age. Cyberpunk authors (William Gibson, Neal Stephenson, Vernor Vinge) were similarly inspired by the birth of personal computers in the 1970s–80s. Virk's illustration that each sci-fi wave tracks the technology of its era.
- ***Ready Player One* (Ernest Cline, 2011; set in 2045)** — updated the metaverse with full immersion missing from Second Life: a lightweight VR headset plus accessories (haptic suit, omnidirectional treadmill), incorporating post–Snow Crash innovations (the web, instant messaging). During his doctoral research Virk found Cline had spent plenty of time in Second Life — the Feedback Loop across generations. In the novel's OASIS, kids attend school, people work, have relationships, change avatar looks, and play games; given the dystopian real world, many residents prefer life in the OASIS. When the book was released there were no consumer-grade VR headsets; within a few years Oculus released one.
- **Oculus VR (founded by Palmer Luckey, 2012; acquired by Facebook in 2014 for $2 billion)** — didn't necessarily invent new technology but optimized existing techniques and materials into a usable headset and developer tool kit; consumer headsets "started to take off" with Meta's Oculus Quest. CTO was John Carmack, an original creator of *Doom* — "the first highly successful game with a 3D perspective" — bringing years of 3D-rendering optimization experience. Employees were each given a copy of *Ready Player One* as the vision the company was building toward.
- **Spielberg's *Ready Player One* film (2018)** — amid real-world VR headsets from Sony and HTC, the blockbuster became "the new touchstone reference" for a fully immersive virtual world; a few years later Facebook became Meta.
- **Virk's VR table tennis "conversion experience"** — room-scale VR with an HTC Vive: he saw the table, an opponent, and (looking at his hands) only the paddle, which moved "in perfect concert" with his controller. "After a few minutes of this real-time fusing of my movements with the virtual world, my mind became convinced that I was playing a real game of table tennis!" (Figure 11: *Virtual Sports*, from HTC and Free Range Games.) Shows how quickly the mind accepts a rendered world as real.
- ***Dragonflight* prototype (2016, developer in Santa Monica)** — Virk rode a dragon over a fantasy Grand Canyon–like landscape with ruins of medieval castles; so realistic he felt he was flying, got motion sickness, and "needed to land my dragon." In another experience from the same company he stood in a cave complex by a great crevasse and after a few minutes was afraid to step to the right lest he fall in. Same lesson: VR realism overrides knowledge that the room is physical.
- **History of 3D glasses** — 1950s cinema 3D with cardboard red-and-blue-lens glasses; 1980s 3D TV broadcasts (glasses obtained from TV Guide, Virk recalls); Victorian-era stereoscopy devices with two offset pictures; modern 3D film glasses use polarization filtering instead of colors, enabling 3D versions of films like *Avatar*. Establishes that VR's core trick (two offset images = perceived depth) is old; the new part is real-time rendering.
- **Apple Vision Pro** — Apple's combined VR/AR headset, "released… accompanied by great media frenzy" (the chapter dates the release as "early 2023" in one passage and "early 2024" in a later one — flag for verification). Incredible resolution, enough to make early uses akin to projecting virtual screens onto your environment and to replace monitors; Apple popularized "spatial computing." Priced at $3,500 — an order of magnitude more than the Meta Quest 3 — so uptake was limited to early adopters, which Virk compares to the early Macintosh introducing GUIs. First release included an external battery pack for the pocket; shipped with built-in spatial audio; a natural application was watching 3D movies like *Avatar* on a giant virtual screen. If price and size come down, it could make VR a common household technology.
- **VR's current shortcomings (Virk's inventory)** — (1) dedicated, large glasses ("like having a toaster on your face," per an old joke; sunglasses-size goggles long anticipated); (2) dedicated space, since goggles block the real world — though not a barrier for business uses like training forklift operators; (3) nausea when only vision signals motion — mitigated by teleporting instead of continuous motion and image-blurring techniques; (4) lack of photorealism — VR games still look like computer games, though 360-degree video merging and the Vision Pro are progress; (5) power — phone-powered goggles proved "more of a gimmick than a sustainable model" ("people didn't want to put their phones on their faces"); (6) lack of kinesthetic feedback — haptic suits and treadmills exist but are rarely used due to cost and cumbersomeness; (7) 3D sound not yet fully integrated in most games and applications. Virk expects these addressed "within a decade at most," noting consumer VR is barely more than a decade old.
- **High Fidelity (Philip Rosedale)** — Rosedale's post–Second Life company focused on spatial audio effects. Example of a Second Life pioneer attacking a specific immersion gap.
- **Early AR headsets** — Google Glass and Microsoft HoloLens were among the earliest, later joined by Magic Leap (which uses a unique light-field technology), Meta, Apple's Vision Pro, and ambitious startups. At the first edition's writing, augmented objects looked "video gamey"; the HoloLens had a limited field of vision and was too cumbersome for everyday use, though valuable in business settings. AR and VR share underlying tech: 3D models, textures, real-time rendering — the same stack as MMORPGs and film effects.
- **History of film VFX/CGI** — early effects from *King Kong* (1933) to *Star Wars* (1977) used physical models and puppets. The first fully computer-generated movie sequence was the "Genesis Project" video in *Star Trek II: The Wrath of Kahn* (1982) [sic — chapter's spelling]. Spielberg was dubious CGI could produce realistic dinosaurs for *Jurassic Park* (1993) until ILM — Industrial Light & Magic, created by George Lucas while working on *Star Wars* — convinced him. Since *Toy Story* (1995), the first fully computer-generated major motion picture, CG characters have been common: Gollum in the final two *Lord of the Rings* installments; James Cameron's *Avatar* (2009) with the blue Na'vi on the fictional planet Pandora. Virk's point: film proves realistic virtual objects can blend with real footage — AR must do the same but in real time.
- **The realistic-human problem** — none of the early CGI characters were human; fully realistic humans have been difficult and expensive. Motion capture (body suits with sensors; facial capture with sensors on the face) lets nonhuman characters act human. Realistic hair movement proved particularly expensive, consuming much of the budget of *Final Fantasy: The Spirits Within* (2001). Since films are transmitted digitally at 2K (2,048 pixels), some at 4K (4,096), Virk concludes the obstacle to realistic artificial humans is not pixel resolution but how pixels move and are rendered in real time.
- **Objects are easier than humans** — in *Blade Runner 2049* and the Star Wars sequel trilogy (*The Force Awakens*, *The Last Jedi*, *The Rise of Skywalker*), CG flying cars, spaceships, and creatures blend naturally into scenes with human actors — e.g., a skyscraper cityscape augmented with realistic fleets of flying cars.
- **The dial-in effect (Apple Vision Pro + Meta's latest Quest)** — the ability to dial the real world in or out, moving between AR and VR "like the dimmer on a light switch." Virk uses it to destabilize the AR/real distinction: with ever-smaller glasses and eventually contact lenses, "you may be able to see all kinds of things that aren't there, including holograms of people you are talking to."
- **Meta's Orion demo** — as the book goes to press, Meta demoed next-generation AR glasses, code-named Orion, just slightly larger than ordinary sunglasses; commercial release may take a few years, but Meta promises AR experiences (virtual screens, holographic characters) that currently require much bigger headsets.
- ***Rainbows End* (Vernor Vinge, 2006)** — a sci-fi reference cited by Zuckerberg: everyone has wearable computers, including AR-type contact lenses; you can see a remote person as if projected in front of you or "overlay" the world. In one memorable scene a character wanders a bare-bones university library with blank walls, but sees and feels a medieval library with stone walls and torches. Virk: such visions are influencing innovators consciously and unconsciously.
- ***The Matrix Awakens* (2021, Warner Bros. + Epic Games, Unreal Engine)** — a short demo video game experience released just before *The Matrix Resurrections*, featuring a city so realistic "you'd think you were looking at live video from a city like Berlin or San Francisco, the actual cities used in shooting the Matrix films." Evidence that game engines (Epic's Unreal, with tools for realistic humans and cities) are closing the photorealism gap.
- **AI generation as the accelerator** — deepfake video generation has "exploded," raising worries the internet will flood with fabricated videos of individuals. Tools from Midjourney to OpenAI's Sora produce incredibly convincing photorealistic video — not yet real-time, with frame flaws like hands with more than five fingers — but the rate of improvement suggests AI will reach the milestone "over the coming months and years."
- **Light-field display research** — displays that calculate the exact light reflected off virtual objects toward observers anywhere in the room. Initial versions simulate the effect through headsets like Magic Leap; advanced versions will use lasers to place particles of light in the room, making objects look real without a headset.
- **MIT Media Lab & Brigham Young University volumetric displays** — researchers use combinations of lasers to create holographic images placeable in the real world (e.g., on a table) that appear real from any point in the room, with light reflected off them as off a physical object.
- **MIT real-time holography via AI (2021)** — MIT News revealed a group at MIT could use AI models to perform holographic computations in real time — potentially merging holography with AI and VR for real-time generation of realistic room-scale holograms viewable from any angle, shifting as needed.
- **Star Wars' Princess Leia hologram (1977)** — the famous hologram asking Obi-Wan Kenobi for help; the sci-fi vision that holographic displays move toward.
- **The physics aside** — physics reveals solid objects are ~99% empty space (electrons orbiting a nucleus); solid/gas/liquid is defined by relationships between atoms, and objects look solid because electrons absorb light. Virk's inference: if dancing electrons make things look and feel solid, that dance could be simulated with particles of light — collapsing "real" vs. rendered.
- **Star Trek holodeck + haptics** — light-field rooms don't yet match the holodeck (where simulated objects can be *felt*), but combining them with haptic gloves under investigation for VR (mechanical or electrical signals simulating touch) moves toward that vision.
- **From dot-matrix to 3D printing** — early "dot-matrix" printers showed visible dots in monospaced fonts; adding C, M, Y to black (CMYK) enabled photorealistic printing. A 3D printer prints objects with physical pixels in 0.1 mm layers (a cylinder = circles printed atop each other), driven by 3D-model information generated with the same modeling techniques as games and film VFX — and today automatically by AI tools, which in future may work from mere pictures without explicit 3D models.
- **3D-printable objects today** — early printers used one plastic epoxy material (everything plasticky and one color); today printers use different materials and can print almost any model. Open-source models exist for many common objects, "including a working gun"; printing metal and other substances is potentially near. As materials expand (glass, rubber, metal, composites), "we may soon be able to print any nonliving object" — realizing the Star Trek replicator (tea to clothes).
- ***Skyfall* Aston Martin** — the James Bond filmmakers 3D-printed a one-third-scale model of an Aston Martin sports car and destroyed it on film; it had to be painted to match the real car because printers of the time printed in a single color.
- **Bioprinting and the 2022 Texas ear** — bioprinters print biological materials such as organs using bio-inks (live cells, mostly from plants, turned into printable material). In an "eye-opening case," in 2022 a team in Texas bioprinted an entire ear using a patient's own cells and implanted it on the patient, who had been born with a small, misshapen left ear, using a 3D scan of the patient's other ear to guide the print. Virk: this "would have seemed like magic just a few years ago."
- **DNA as instructions** — DNA and genes were theorized before physically found; genes act as instructions for the body to build proteins, the building blocks of cells. Hence the body is information interpreted by a biological printing process into "larger abstractions called living organisms." Even how biological organisms are constructed "could be explained in terms of sophisticated miniature 3D printers."
- ***Westworld* (2016 HBO series; based on the 1973 film written and directed by Michael Crichton)** — a theme park with perfected AI and robots whose parts (including bones and skin) are printed on sophisticated 3D printers and assembled into artificial organisms ("hosts") so realistic tourists often can't tell them from humans; tourists talk to, fight, have sex with, or abuse them, and the premise is the robots' rebellion against this abuse. Virk: today's 3D printers "are not very far" from these fictional counterparts; ubiquitous bioprinters for repairing/replacing body parts from our own cells are probably less than a decade (or two, maximum) away — "not so far off at all" on the road to the simulation point.

## Thread
Chapter 2 ended the historical record at Stage 3 (MMORPGs: shared, persistent, online 3D worlds on 2D screens). This chapter carries the roadmap into technologies still being built: Stage 4 dissolves the screen (VR immersion), Stage 5 dissolves the boundary between rendered and physical objects (photorealistic AR/MR), and Stage 6 dissolves the need for glasses at all (light-field displays, 3D printing, bioprinting). The connective tissue is the Sci-Fi Feedback Loop — Snow Crash → Second Life → Ready Player One → Oculus/Meta → Orion — which Virk treats as evidence that the trajectory is real and self-reinforcing, not accidental. Each stage is then converted into an argument: if we can render convincing worlds, convincing objects in the world, and finally physical and even living matter from stored models, then "object = information + rendering" stops being a metaphor — and it becomes plausible that a hidden rendering engine already does this for the reality we inhabit. The chapter also plants the next moves: projection will continue toward "direct mind broadcast," control toward mind interfaces, and the speculative territory ahead includes human-level AI and downloadable consciousness, culminating in the simulation point — and in the final chapter of this part, the argument that older civilizations may already have reached it, with us inside their world.

  - **The Holographic Universe (Talbot)** — Virk's light-field/volumetric displays literally create holograms that "appear real from any point in the room"; Talbot's thesis that reality itself is hologram-like is the metaphysical mirror of Virk's "hidden rendering engine" analogy.
  - **My Big TOE (Campbell)** — Campbell's claim that reality is an information-based virtual simulation rendered to consciousness aligns almost one-to-one with this chapter's refrain that "the physical world consists of information… and the processing of that information."
  - **American Cosmic (Pasulka)** — the Sci-Fi Feedback Loop (fiction shaping technologists "consciously and unconsciously," e.g., Oculus employees handed Ready Player One) parallels Pasulka's account of belief and media shaping technological elites.
  - **Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance (Pirsig)** — Virk's question "what, then, is a 'real' object?" once form (information/model) is separated from substrate (material/pixels) echoes Pirsig's split between underlying form and surface appearance in how we relate to technology.
