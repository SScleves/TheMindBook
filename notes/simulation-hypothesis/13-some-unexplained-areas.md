# Ch 13 — Some Unexplained Areas

## In one paragraph
Virk closes Part III by turning the simulation lens onto phenomena that mainstream science "has been unable or unwilling to explain": UFOs/UAP, the Fermi paradox, Jungian synchronicity, déjà vu, out-of-body experiences, remote viewing, telepathy, and ghosts/visitations. His running move is the same throughout: each phenomenon is nonsensical inside a materialist, objectively shared physical reality, but maps cleanly onto standard video-game engineering — physics engines, rendering and partial rendering, conditional rendering ("narrowcasting"), procedural generation, virtual cameras, private messages, ghost mode, caching, and garbage memory. He argues these phenomena share three traits — reported by enormous numbers of people past and present, completely unexplainable in a materialist paradigm, and dismissed mainly for being "not possible" and non-reproducible in a lab (remote viewing excepted, which he says has been demonstrated in lab settings) — and concludes that the simulation hypothesis may explain them better than either current science or the older religious/occult doctrines, thereby offering a bridge across the religion–science gap.

## Core claims
- Virk argues that many scientists reject these phenomena via circular logic — "there is no reasonable mechanism for the phenomena to exist, therefore the people who report experiencing them must be mistaken, hallucinating, untruthful, or simply misinterpreting" — letting preexisting materialist beliefs make them "dogmatic."
- Virk argues that in a simulated universe there would be "a perfectly rational explanation and mechanism" for phenomena that seem absurd or impossible under materialism.
- Virk argues that three puzzling reported aspects of UFOs — maneuverability that defies physics, materialization/dematerialization (including moving through solid objects), and subjective visibility (one witness sees it, another doesn't) — "start to make sense" if physical reality is a computer simulation, via customizable physics engines, rendering/partial rendering, and conditional rendering (narrowcasting).
- Virk argues that wormhole-like travel or teleportation "would be akin to popping out of the simulation and popping back in at a certain point — behavior that is much easier in a simulated reality than in a physical one."
- Virk argues (crediting Vallée's cases) that UFO experiences have both an objective and a subjective/consciousness component, and that per-witness rendering differences only make sense "in the context of a distributed multiplayer simulation as opposed to a shared physical reality."
- Virk argues the simulation hypothesis is consistent with BOTH sides of the alien debate: with UFO believers (interdimensional/rendered craft) and with the Fermi paradox (no aliens at all — the stars and planets may be procedurally generated scenery we can observe but never visit). He explicitly declines to argue either side "too forcefully."
- Virk argues that synchronicity (Jung's "acausal connecting principle") fits a simulation in which our thoughts and actions are (1) monitored and (2) fed back into a loop that creates seemingly external events based on our game state and our specific quests and achievements — clues about "our next quest."
- Virk argues (via Pasulka's "technological synchronicity" and his own cookie/ad anecdote) that synchronicity seems like magic only until you posit a tracking mechanism, exactly as targeted ads seem acausal until you know about cookies.
- Virk argues (via Philip K. Dick) that déjà vu may be evidence that a variable in the computer-programmed reality was changed and a portion re-run — sensing "a parallel reality that had been rewound and rerun to the present with a different outcome."
- Virk argues that OBEs and remote viewing are "simply a matter of adjusting the virtual camera inside our shared video game reality" — the rendering client can place its camera at any x, y, z coordinate, including outside the character's body.
- Virk argues the etheric/subtle body of mystical traditions (light-blue mesh outline, template of the physical body) closely parallels the non-rendered 3D character model/mesh (often drawn in blue or green) that defines a game avatar's body.
- Virk argues telepathy is unexplainable materialistically but trivial in an MMORPG, where any player can send a private message to any other player "whether they are physically present in the scene or not."
- Virk argues ghost phenomena could be implemented in a simulation "either deliberately or accidentally, as an artifact or glitch": looping, place-bound ghosts as cached/garbage-memory artifacts replayed by the rendering engine; intentional, selectively visible apparitions as ghost-mode/reduced/transparent avatars using conditional rendering — possibly the "player" of the deceased, or a saved AI/NPC version of their consciousness (stage 10, downloaded consciousness).
- Virk argues all of the chapter's phenomena share three traits: mass reporting (thousands to millions, past and present), complete unexplainability in the materialist paradigm, and dismissal for being "not possible"/non-reproducible on demand — "with the exception of remote viewing, which has been demonstrated in lab settings."
- Virk argues the simulation hypothesis may be a better explanation than current science AND "a better, more techno-scientific explanation than the religious or occult doctrines of the past," and may bridge the seemingly insurmountable gap between religion and science.

## Concepts
- **UFOs / UAP** — Unidentified flying objects, "recently renamed in official circles as UAP (unidentified anomalous phenomena)": thousands of reports of saucer-, triangle-, and cigar-shaped craft by day and flying lights by night, from military officers and airline pilots as well as civilians, exhibiting maneuverability (hovering, instant acceleration/stops) that "defies our understanding of inertia and aerodynamics."
- **The unexplained 5 percent** — Virk's summary of official and unofficial commissions: a majority of sightings are explainable, some lack information either way, but "around 5 percent have provided enough information to rule out existing known objects or craft" — the residue puzzling skeptics and believers alike (includes the Tic Tac encounter).
- **Interdimensional vs. extraterrestrial hypotheses** — If UFOs are interdimensional, a world exists outside the physical and craft move in and out of our physical reality; if extraterrestrial, they would need wormholes or FTL technology to cross interstellar distances (Ch 9 territory).
- **Physics engines** — Video-game engines with physics "that can be customized easily by the programmers" — Virk's mechanism for impossible maneuverability.
- **Rendering / partial rendering** — Objects can be rendered anywhere in a game (appearing "out of nowhere"), and can be partially rendered — able to pass through physical objects in that state one minute and be fully physical (fully rendered) the next.
- **Conditional rendering / narrowcasting** — Because all rendering is done on individual computers, "it's possible for one player to see an object in a scene that is not visible to other players, a process we call narrowcasting." Virk's mechanism for subjective visibility of UFOs and ghosts.
- **Fermi paradox** — Proposed by Enrico Fermi (builder of the first nuclear reactor, basis for the Manhattan Project): if alien life existed on so many worlds, given the galaxy's age, aliens would have colonized or traveled to most of it — "So where are all the aliens?"
- **Procedural generation** — Content generated and rendered only as needed (the No Man's Sky model, Ch 2): the stars/planets we observe may not "actually exist" until a player visits; a galaxy of dead worlds "might actually make the simulation hypothesis more likely."
- **Synchronicity** — Jung's term, defined in his 1951 paper as an "acausal connecting principle"; casually, a "meaningful coincidence" — a psychic event (thinking/speaking of something) and an external event "fall together in time ('synchronized') in a meaningful way" with no definable cause and effect; one of the few concepts that is both scientific and psychological.
- **Glitch in the Matrix** — Virk's informal name (coined in his book *Treasure Hunt*) for individual synchronicity events; also his framing for déjà vu, and the phrase Curry Guinn used for ghosts and déjà vu as simulation evidence.
- **Technological synchronicity** — Concept Virk was introduced to by Diana Pasulka: synchronicity thought of in terms of association and computation — like a web cookie tracking behavior and serving a targeted ad, something tracking "all our internal thoughts and external actions" could "put a meaningful next step in front of us inside a simulation."
- **Associative information storage** — Vallée's idea (2011 TED Talk): ordering data by space and time is "the worst possible way to store data"; modern computers retrieve information associatively, and synchronicity may reveal that the universe stores information this way — "part and parcel of how the universe is assembled, not something miraculous."
- **Déjà vu** — The experience of feeling we have seen something/someplace/some event before; per Philip K. Dick, a clue that "some variable was changed — reprogrammed as it were" and an alternative world branched off; explicitly depicted in *The Matrix* (the repeating cat scene).
- **OBE (out-of-body experience)** — Perceiving the world from outside one's physical body; reported by NDE-ers, religious mystics, and explorers of consciousness "for hundreds, if not thousands of years"; a known benefit is diminished fear of death and a sense of well-being.
- **Etheric body / astral body / causal body** — In many religious traditions and new age philosophy, the physical body is surrounded by a subtler etheric body (a template defining the shape and vitality of the physical body's cells, often depicted as a mesh of light blue lines), which is surrounded by an astral body (the one that separates during an OBE or dying); the occult explanation of OBEs is astral travel in an astral realm mirroring the physical. Beyond the astral lies the "causal body" in the "causal" realm.
- **Koshas / three bodies (Vedic)** — In Yogic literature the body is surrounded by clear sheaths (koshas), which modern mystics liken to layers of electromagnetic fields; some of the five koshas correspond to the three bodies: gross body (sthula sarira, the physical), subtle body (suksma sarira — keeps the physical body alive, transmigrates with the soul across bodies, separates at birth), and causal body (karana sarira — the seed that creates the other two). The subtle body is considered a template for the physical body.
- **Kirlian photography / auras** — Photography developed to capture activity in the different levels of the aura (still controversial in the scientific community); a cut leaf's complete etheric template can sometimes be seen with it.
- **Character mesh (3D model)** — A game character's non-rendered 3D model: stored in memory/on disk, invisible to others in the rendered world yet indispensable to the character; drawn with polygons as a "mesh" of lines (often blue or green) outlining the avatar's body, then textured for the in-game rendering — Virk's analogue for the etheric template.
- **Remote viewing** — Sensing what is happening at a remote physical location; experimented with by the CIA and Soviets during the Cold War for espionage; researched at the Stanford Research Institute; the one phenomenon in the chapter Virk says "has been demonstrated in lab settings."
- **Virtual camera** — The camera used in most MMORPGs and 3D games showing what the player sees; because the client renders based on camera position, it can be placed anywhere — first person, over-the-shoulder, above the body, or at any x, y, z coordinate elsewhere in the world. Virk's single mechanism for both OBEs and remote viewing.
- **Telepathy** — Sending a mental message from one person to another (remote viewing can be thought of as a type of it); analogous to private messages between players in any MMORPG.
- **Ghost mode / reduced avatar** — Video-game modes letting you be in a scene without others seeing your avatar; a "reduced" avatar could be as simple as a single pixel, or a transparent avatar — "a 'visitor' in the game but... consciously controlled by another player." Virk's mechanism for intentional apparitions.
- **Garbage memory** — Old graphics data in memory not fully erased after scene updates; "orphaned data that isn't supposed to be used" until reused — Virk's mechanism for accidental ghost artifacts.
- **Caching** — Storing information "nearby" (client machine or nearby server) to avoid expensive recomputation or server calls over the internet; e.g., caching a rendered copy of a character who always logs in in the same place and clothes. If a simulation renders only what is local and cached data from previous runs persists, "the same scene could run over and over again in a loop" — explaining looping, place-bound ghosts.
- **Downloaded consciousness (stage 10 callback)** — A ghost avatar might be the "player" of the deceased or "a saved version (an AI or NPC) of their consciousness from when they were playing the game" — with intentionality but limited because no longer a current player.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
### UFOs / UAP
1. **Project Blue Book** — The US government's last official twentieth-century word on UFOs; a task force whose scientific consultant was J. Allen Hynek, a well-known astronomer from Ohio State University and Northwestern University. Shut down December 17, 1969; the air force and DOD afterward claimed to be out of the UFO-investigation business, citing Blue Book's conclusion that UFOs are not a threat.
2. **Hynek and Vallée continue** — Hynek kept studying the phenomenon with Dr. Jacques Vallée (who created the first computerized map of Mars for NASA in 1963). Both were rumored to be models for the French scientist in Spielberg's *Close Encounters of the Third Kind* (1977); Hynek defined the term "close encounters" and made a cameo in the film.
3. **AATIP revelation (2017)** — *The New York Times* revealed the DOD's denial of ongoing UFO research "wasn't quite true": Luis Elizondo revealed he was part of the Pentagon's Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program, with a $22 million budget, funded by senators including Harry Reid of Nevada. Virk says the Pentagon secretly taking these objects seriously while publicly denying it "underscores their interest in the strange aerodynamic properties of these objects."
4. **The "Tic Tac" encounter (2004)** — Fighter jets from the USS Nimitz reported a Tic Tac–shaped craft hovering over the ocean that descended from 80,000 to 20,000 feet in seconds, disappeared at significant speed, then reappeared at the pilots' intended meeting point. Both radar and visual sightings by the Nimitz carrier group off California. Pilots were amazed because current aerospace technology cannot perform such maneuvers. Virk places it in the unexplained 5 percent.
5. **Post–first-edition developments** — The first congressional hearings on UFOs in fifty years were held, and several UAP task forces plus a Pentagon office were established "to try to bring some semblance of order to the various unidentified reports."
6. **The 5 percent statistic** — In almost all summaries of sightings by official and unofficial commissions, a majority are explainable, some lack data, but ~5 percent rule out known objects/craft and defy the laws of inertia and gravity; this residue includes the Tic Tac and "many earlier sightings in the US, Europe, Australia, and South America."
7. **Vallée's selective-visibility cases (told to Virk, 2017 meeting)** — Vallée had documented cases in which several people were together and some saw the UFO and others didn't, leading him to believe "there was a consciousness aspect to UFOs." Virk: rendering the UFO on person A's consciousness but not person B's "makes sense only in the context of a distributed multiplayer simulation."
8. **Garry Nolan's car case** — Described to Virk by Stanford professor Garry Nolan (who has worked with Vallée): family members in the same car saw a UFO but gave completely different shape descriptions — "a disk in one case and a cigar shape in another" — despite sitting next to each other. Virk uses it as further evidence of per-observer rendering.
9. **Vallée's Pacific Northwest through-the-trees case** — Witnesses reported a UFO descending at a forty-five-degree angle into a clearing among tall, robust evergreens (some over a thousand years old). Vallée found that the reported angle would have taken the craft through the trees; the trees were undamaged, yet witnesses confirmed the craft had gone through them — withholding the detail from other investigators because it would sound "crazy," but standing by it. Virk: in a video game an object "can materialize but not be fully solid — you can even walk through walls" when partially rendered; a partially rendered UFO provides the mechanism.
10. **Sky-jumping UFOs** — Many witnesses recall UFOs jumping from one place to another in the sky — impossible for a fully physical/fully rendered object, but "for a partially rendered object, it would be like a projection that jumps against the sky"; Virk also floats a holographic-projection state for the craft.
### Fermi paradox
11. **Exoplanet abundance** — More than five thousand exoplanets confirmed, more discovered each year; "Some estimates put the number of habitable planets at more than one billion!" — sharpening Fermi's question.
12. **MIT Technology Review search-grid article** — A recent article found a search for extraterrestrial intelligence needs an eight-dimensional search grid; searches to date (through organizations like SETI) have covered "only a fraction of one of these dimensions."
13. **No Man's Sky (Ch 2 callback)** — The game "had eighteen quintillion planets" that the team never actually created: procedurally generated and rendered only when a player visits. Virk's model for a universe where none of the stars are real — observable from Earth but never visitable — making the simulation hypothesis compatible with there being no aliens at all.
14. **Parallel simulations (Ch 8 callback)** — Each universe as a different simulation on a different track: ours may be one where all worlds are procedurally generated with no other life, while other simulations have aliens who are "not just plentiful but have already made themselves known to us" — reconciling both sides of the debate.
### Synchronicity
15. **Jung's scarab-beetle case** — A patient stuck in therapy told Jung a dream from the previous night involving a scarab (the beetle of Egyptian reliefs). As she spoke, a beetle tapped on Jung's office window; he opened it, the beetle flew in — "probably the closest you could get to an Egyptian scarab in that part of Europe." Jung: "There is your scarab from your dream!" — which "seemed to loosen up the energy" and the therapy progressed rapidly. Virk's exemplar of psychic event + external event falling together meaningfully with no definable cause and effect.
16. **Jung–Pauli correspondence** — Jung corresponded heavily with Wolfgang Pauli, one of the founders of quantum physics, about synchronicity before writing his defining 1951 paper.
17. **Virk's *Treasure Hunt*** — Virk's earlier book on synchronicities in the business world, where he started calling individual events "glitches in the Matrix" before fully developing his simulation arguments. He catalogs two standard source-buckets — spiritual/religious (messages from God/guardian angels; new age clues for our life plan/karma) and scientific/quantum (future selves sending messages back so we make choices leading to one probable future, per Ch 8) — plus a third: synchronicity reveals an order we cannot see, made explicit in the simulation hypothesis as clues about our next quest.
18. **Vallée's 2011 TED Talk, "The Physics of Everything Else"** — Synchronicity and coincidence may reveal "part of the underlying structure of how the universe stores information." Analogy: a physical library stores books by physical location (book x, shelf y, slot 7), while sophisticated computer science stores information associatively and retrieves it statistically. If there is no time dimension as usually assumed, "we may be traversing incidents by association."
19. **Pasulka and the backpack cookie anecdote** — Talking with Diana Pasulka (professor of religious studies who has written about UFOs and synchronicities) about Vallée's ideas, Virk was introduced to "technological synchronicity." His illustration: while shopping for a backpack he browsed a maker's website on his computer, then later saw an ad for that exact brand on his phone's social media. A non-technologist might call it an acausal meaningful coincidence; Virk knows the cause was a cookie recording his click. Synchronicity in a simulation "works the same way."
### Déjà vu
20. **The Matrix cat scene** — In the movie, Neo watches a cat pass a doorway and seconds later the scene repeats — déjà vu explicitly depicted as a "glitch in the Matrix" (Virk returns to this scene again in the Guinn section on ghosts).
21. **Philip K. Dick's Metz speech (1977)** — At a famous speech at a science fiction convention in Metz, France, Dick — who "explicitly believed that we were living in a computer-generated reality" (referenced in the book's introduction) — said déjà vu is the clue that a variable was changed and reprogrammed, branching an alternative world; we sense a parallel reality "rewound and rerun to the present with a different outcome."
### OBEs, remote viewing, telepathy
22. **Anita Moorjani's NDE (*Dying to Be Me*)** — During an NDE in a Hong Kong hospital, after which she was "miraculously cured of lymphoma," Moorjani reported perceiving not only the room around her but what people hundreds of miles away were doing — including her brother, on his way from India to Hong Kong to visit her.
23. **Dannion Brinkley and other NDE-ers** — (Mentioned earlier in the book's NDE section.) Brinkley and others reported seeing what was going on in the hospital while they were "dead." Virk notes OBE-ers commonly gain a diminished fear of death and a sense of well-being.
24. **Robert Monroe's journeys** — In *Journeys out of the Body* and *Far Journeys*, Monroe raised the idea that OBEs are not just travel in the physical world but "might involve journeying to other realms that are not connected at all with our physical bodies" — leading into the astral/causal realm model.
25. **Kirlian leaf experiment** — When a leaf is cut, its complete etheric template can sometimes still be seen using Kirlian photography — Virk's evidence that the physical "has some kind of template, not unlike a mesh in the video game world, associated with it"; he pairs this with claims that seers of subtle bodies describe a light blue outline, matching the blue/green polygon mesh of game avatars.
26. **Cold War remote-viewing programs (Stanford Research Institute)** — The CIA and Soviets experimented with remote viewers to clandestinely see inside each other's secure facilities. In SRI research, subjects showed "an uncanny ability to 'look at' another physical site from a different point of view," including: **Ingo Swann**, who predicted a ring around Jupiter from his OBE journey to the planet; **Pat Price**, who helped locate Patty Hearst's car after her famous abduction; and **Uri Geller**, "famous (perhaps infamous) for his televised spoon-bending magic tricks."
27. **The virtual-camera explanation** — Because client software renders from the virtual camera's position, the camera can be placed anywhere — over the shoulder, above your character's body, or at any x, y, z point elsewhere in the rendered world. Virk maps OBEs (inner eye outside the body) and remote viewing (inner eye at a remote site) onto camera placement.
28. **MMORPG private messages as telepathy** — In every MMORPG a player can privately message any other player regardless of physical presence in the scene; "Video gamers do this all the time" — Virk's model for telepathy, unexplainable in the materialist worldview.
### Ghosts and visitations
29. **Deep history of ghost reports** — Reports go back to prehistoric times, prevalent across Mesopotamia, Greece, India, and Egypt; mentioned in Homer's *Iliad* and *Odyssey* and in the Bible. Per *Smithsonian* magazine, one of the oldest recorded instances may be a 3,500-year-old Babylonian tablet — with no reason to think reports didn't predate recording.
30. **Fictional depictions** — Virk selects the film *The Others* (2001) and the TV series *Medium* (2005) as representing the two commonly reported modes: visible apparitions in a house, and visits from the deceased inside dreams. He adds that dreams of recently deceased loved ones are relatively common and sometimes occur "just as a relative is passing hundreds or thousands of miles away, often unbeknownst to the dreamer."
31. **Six common characteristics of apparitions** — (1) spectral forms resembling actual people; (2) usually deceased, sometimes from decades ago; (3) appearing where the deceased spent much time (home, castle) or died; (4) sometimes repeating the same movements as in life, unaware of observers, "as if operating in a loop"; (5) sometimes trying to communicate; (6) sometimes visible only to certain people. Each is nonsensical physically but "could be easily implemented in a functional simulation, either deliberately or accidentally."
32. **Curry Guinn (2020)** — A computer scientist at the University of North Carolina Wilmington made headlines suggesting ghosts (along with déjà vu and other phenomena) could be evidence we live in a simulation, referencing "glitch in the Matrix" and the movie's cat scene.
33. **Garbage memory / caching mechanics** — Virk's technical excursus: everything on screen is already stored in memory; refreshed scenes overwrite values, but old data not fully erased persists as "garbage" memory. Caching saves rendered copies "nearby" to avoid expensive server calls over the physical internet. If the simulation renders only what is local and cached data from previous runs remains, the same scene could loop — fitting the "ghost in a loop" scenario and explaining why looping ghosts attach to specific places.
34. **The "Lois" case (Loyd Auerbach, in Leslie Kean's *Surviving Death*, 2017)** — A family near Oakland, California: for over a year the son, Chris, had been seeing and conversing with the "ghost" of a woman resembling the home's previous owner, who had lived there since her birth in 1917 and died before the family bought it in 1980. The parents saw the elderly apparition only sporadically (walking toward the stairs, waving, then disappearing), but "Lois" told Chris who she was and the origins of antique furniture acquired with the house. She was "most comfortable with him" to remain visible; she didn't always appear old — sometimes younger, in different clothes (per Auerbach, a sign of "self-awareness and consciousness"); and she told Chris she could "broadcast" herself to certain individuals, intentionally directing who could and couldn't see her. Virk: in a materialist paradigm this makes no sense (objectively present = visible to everyone and to instruments), but with conditional rendering one avatar in the room can easily see the deceased's avatar while others can't — and the ghost may be the deceased's "player" or a saved AI/NPC of their consciousness (stage 10, downloaded consciousness), intentional but limited because no longer a current player.

## Thread
This is the capstone chapter of Part III ("How Simulation Explains the Unexplainable — The Mystics"). Previous chapters showed the simulation hypothesis modeling modern physics and giving a scientific basis for Eastern and Western mysticism; this chapter extends the same lens to the phenomena science won't touch. The structure is a repeated four-beat pattern per phenomenon: (1) establish that it is widely and credibly reported; (2) show it is impossible under materialism; (3) supply the exact video-game mechanism that reproduces it (physics engine, partial/conditional rendering, procedural generation, monitored game state + quest clues, rewound variables, virtual camera, private messaging, ghost mode, caching/garbage memory); (4) decline to prove or debunk the phenomenon itself — Virk explicitly says proving/disproving is not his goal for UFOs, the alien debate, or ghosts. The chapter then closes Part III's arc: the hypothesis fits Eastern traditions, Western traditions, and the unexplained residue alike, positioning simulation as the bridge between religion and science.
