# Ch 12 — God, Angels, and the Simulation: The Western Traditions

## In one paragraph
Having argued in the previous chapters that Eastern descriptions of reality (Buddhism, Hinduism) are compatible with the simulation hypothesis, Virk turns to the Western religious traditions — Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, the Abrahamic line founded on Moses, Jesus, and Muhammad. His central move: religious scriptures originate with mystics who "peek out" beyond the physical world and then must translate ineffable experience into metaphors an ancient audience could grasp — metaphors that were often techno-scientific for their day (clothing, wheels, books, scrolls, messengers). If these faiths were founded today, he asserts, they would use the video game/simulation metaphor instead. He then walks through the core Western concepts — God speaking the world into existence, the afterlife (heaven, hell, purgatory), judgment via a scroll of deeds or book of life, recording and guardian angels, and prayer — and shows each maps naturally onto simulation machinery: procedurally generated worlds spoken into existence (holodeck, Meta's BuilderBot, Nvidia's Omniverse), gameplay recordings and scorecards stored "in the cloud" outside the rendered world, angels as computer programs or daemons, and God as (or aided by) a godlike AI tracking billions of players. The chapter closes with near-death experiences (NDEs) — Moody's core elements and Dannion Brinkley's "holographic panoramic 360-degree life review" — as a modern wave of the same afterlife reports, retold in modern technological metaphors, and argues the simulation hypothesis renders both scripture and NDE scientifically explainable.

## Core claims
- **Virk argues** that religious traditions evolve from mystics who have seen beyond the physical world and struggle to translate essentially ineffable experiences into words, because our vocabulary was built from experience of the physical world while religion concerns what is beyond it.
- **Virk argues** the same translation problem exists even for scriptures claimed to be channeled directly from beings outside the physical world (God, angels, ascended masters): the "sender" must still translate ideas into the language of the receiver and the public. It would not have made sense for ancient scriptures to talk about quantum physics, multiplayer video games, cloud servers, or computation.
- **Virk argues** scriptures therefore used metaphors that were often techno-scientific in nature even if they don't seem so to us — clothing (soul incarnation), the wheel (Buddha), the book/scroll (records of deeds), the messenger (angels).
- **Virk asserts** that if the Western faiths were founded today they would use up-to-date metaphors, and the video game or simulation metaphor may fit much better than the metaphors passed down from thousands of years ago.
- **Virk argues** the simulation hypothesis gives even die-hard materialists a question to ponder: what if the mystics of old were telling us exactly what they saw, but simply lacked the correct vocabulary and metaphors to make us understand it?
- **Virk argues** the Genesis creation account becomes plausible under the simulation hypothesis: a computer-programmed reality could come into existence in seconds if the programming were in place, though computational irreducibility means some things must be created by programs run over a period of steps (as in procedural generation in video games). The "six days" may be six clock units unrelated to Earth days, and since spinning up a computer program would use electromagnetic signals — i.e., light — "Let there be light" would be literally true.
- **Virk claims** the scroll of deeds (Islam) and book of life (Christianity, Judaism) are best understood as a record stored outside the rendered world, like a video game's quest manifest and character scorecard in the cloud: someone or something is watching our actions, recording them, and keeping score, including how they affect other people in the game. He says the simulation hypothesis "may be the only possible explanation" for actions being recorded and played back after death.
- **Virk argues** recording angels are as much functions as entities. He considers it unlikely — and, "the ultimate sin for a computer scientist, inefficient" — that fourteen billion separate conscious angels (two per human) watch over us; recording angels are most likely computer programs, like an intelligent screen recording of gameplay that also evaluates and keeps score. Guardian angels may be more sophisticated: conscious entities, advanced souls, master players leading "guilds" or "soul groups," or advanced AI.
- **Virk argues** that God, if He exists, is most likely represented by an intelligent computer program or AI or a conscious entity outside the simulation, helped by billions of individual "angel" computer programs — a godlike AI omniscient about the world's happenings; not that God *is* an AI, but that He would need an AI system to track and respond to everything (including billions of daily prayers).
- **Virk argues** NDEs are a modern body of knowledge about the afterlife: NDE-ers, like the prophets of old, return and struggle to describe the other side — but using modern language and metaphors (holograms, movies, screens) to describe ideas similar to those in the Bible and the Koran.
- **Virk argues** that from the simulation point of view, an NDE is the player starting to wake up in another reality when the character dies in the simulation — outside the rendered world of the shared video game, where other conscious beings await — turning the NDE from a mysterious, unexplainable thing into "a very explainable scientific process."
- **Virk notes** Brinkley's report of not being judged by anyone but himself seems inconsistent with Western religion but is actually supported by scripture (Koran 17:14, where your own self suffices as your reckoner).
- **Virk concludes** that simulation theory provides a bridge between sects of Eastern and Western religious traditions — a connective metaphor between religions and a bridge between ancient religious and modern scientific views of the world.
- **Virk claims** the model of the physical universe as information and computation provides reasonable, almost scientific explanations for concepts scientists dismissed as ludicrous because they were "imprisoned in an older, materialistic model of the physical universe."

## Concepts
- **Mystics' translation problem** — Mystics who peek beyond the physical world (and channeled senders such as God or angels) must translate ineffable, out-of-this-world experience into words and metaphors comprehensible to the receiver and the average person of their era; words are built from physical-world experience, so ancient scriptures could not use terms like computation or cloud servers.
- **Techno-scientific metaphors** — Scriptural metaphors that were technological for their time even if we no longer see them that way: clothing for soul-incarnation, Buddha's wheel, the book/scroll for deed records, the messenger for angels. The process continues today (Yogananda's film projector, NDE-ers' holograms and movie screens).
- **Western religious traditions** — Virk's grouping label for Judaism, Christianity, and Islam, all from the Abrahamic line, each founded by or based on an individual (Moses, Jesus, Muhammad), in contrast to the Eastern faiths of previous chapters. Their followers are the "people of the book" (Old and New Testament; Koran).
- **Mainstream vs. mystical traditions** — The division Virk finds most interesting: mainstream religions are full of rules and precepts for ordinary people, while mystical traditions within them (e.g., the Sufis in Islam) are primarily concerned with direct experience of God, seeking to validate, reexperience, and restate the older scriptures' metaphors.
- **Creation by spoken command** — All three Western religions agree God spoke, His commands were followed, and the world was created ("Let there be light"). Once incomprehensible except as divine magic, this now mirrors the holodeck and generative AI creating 3D worlds from verbal commands.
- **Computational irreducibility (applied to Genesis)** — Some things must be created by computer programs run over a period of steps rather than instantaneously — true of procedural generation in video games and possibly of our physical reality; hence a creation in six "clock units."
- **Al-akhirah / al-dunya** — Islamic terms: al-akhirah is the "hereafter" (the afterlife); al-dunya the "here and now" (the physical world). Life on Earth is temporary — a training ground or test for the day of judgment. Belief in the hereafter is one of the six pillars of belief for all Muslims.
- **Day of qiyamah** — Islam's day of judgment: it begins with an angel blowing a trumpet, the Earth's mountains are razed, and the dead are resurrected and judged before Allah based on good and bad deeds.
- **Jannah / Jahannam** — Islamic heaven (Jannah, literally "garden," depicted running with rivers of milk and honey) and hell (Jahannam, a place of fire, for those judged as doing more evil than good or who reject the "true faith" of Islam).
- **Scroll of deeds** — Each person's record of good and bad deeds in Islam; on judgment day people are not merely read to from it but *shown* their deeds and their impact on others visually — the first example of a "life review." Virk reads it as a highlight reel/replay stored outside the rendered world.
- **Book of life** — The Christian/Jewish counterpart to the scroll of deeds (Revelation 20:12); in traditional Judaism it exists in abridged form as a list of who goes to heaven or hell.
- **Purgatory / Hades / Gehinnom** — Intermediate afterlife states: Catholic purgatory, where a soul undergoes "purification" before heaven; the Eastern Orthodox "intermediate place" called Hades (borrowing the Greek underworld term; the Orthodox church doesn't explicitly recognize purgatory); Jewish Gehinnom (name closely tied to the Arabic), where souls may go only for a period to reflect on past misdeeds before entering the Garden of Eden.
- **Gan Eden / Olam Ha-Ba** — Names for the Jewish heaven (Garden of Eden / Olam Ha-Ba); Judaism goes into less afterlife detail than Christianity and Islam, and these concepts are debated among scholars.
- **Recording angels / Kiraman Katibin** — Angels who track deeds. In Islam, two angels per person — Raqib and Atid, collectively the Kiraman Katibin — record everything said and done, keeping two scores: sawab (good deeds) and haram (bad deeds). In Christianity, the guardian angel doubles as recording angel (Malachi 3:16); in Jewish tradition Gabriel is the recording angel with the "writing case at his side" (Ezekiel 9:2).
- **Guardian angels** — In Christianity, protectors and guides keeping us on God's path. Virk suggests they are more sophisticated than recording automatons: they make judgments, and in some traditions are advanced souls tasked with watching over souls — possibly conscious entities, master players leading "guilds"/"soul groups," or advanced AI.
- **Angels as functions** — Virk's reframing: recording angels are as much functions as entities — they have a specific purpose and rules to follow; computer science suggests they are some form of computer program recording (visually or otherwise) what happens in our lives.
- **Jinn** — In Muhammad's saying, everyone has a constant companion from among the jinn (origin of the word *genie*, depicted behind you) and one from among the angels (in front of you) — the origin, Virk suggests, of the cartoon shoulder angel/devil, which is not explicitly in the New Testament.
- **Daemon** — Computer-science term: a self-running process accomplishing some purpose, monitoring other programs, performing housekeeping on the machine — Virk's model for how angel-programs could operate.
- **Godlike AI** — An AI omniscient regarding all happenings in the world according to various rules — "a server with a very intelligent program keeping track of everything," a digital consciousness watching and responding to events. God would need such a system to handle billions of prayers and deed-records.
- **NDE (near-death experience)** — Term popularized (some say coined) by Dr. Raymond Moody in his 1975 bestseller *Life After Life*. A modern wave of afterlife reports, spiking in the West because of advances like CPR in the second half of the twentieth century that let people be resuscitated after clinical death.
- **Core NDE experience** — Moody's identified common elements: peace/well-being/painlessness; an out-of-body experience (seeing one's body, family, doctors); a tunnel experience (moving through a dark tunnel toward a light); beings of light (sometimes seen as angels or Jesus); a life review; a life decision (a barrier and a choice whether to cross or return); and the return to the body. Not every NDE-er reports all elements, but they are common enough to be recognized as the core.
- **Life review** — Being shown one's entire life and the impact of one's deeds; appears in Islam's judgment day, in the NDE core experience, and (in reverse) in *It's a Wonderful Life*. Brinkley called his a "holographic panoramic 360-degree life review."
- **Ripple effect** — NDE term for seeing how your actions affected others — not just those around you — echoing Islam's idea of being shown the full, possibly unknown, impact of your deeds.
- **Quest manifest / character scorecard** — Video-game concepts (carried from the previous chapter): a record of a character's actions and consequences stored outside the rendered world, in the cloud, invisible to the player but linked to the character — Virk's model for karma, the scroll of deeds, and the book of life.
- **Film projector metaphor for maya** — Yogananda's 1920s updated metaphor for the illusory nature of the world, chosen because modern Americans could understand it better than the old term *maya* — Virk's exemplar of mystics updating metaphors with current technology.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
1. **The mystic's translation problem (framing)** — Religious traditions usually evolve from a mystic or set of mystics who "peek out" beyond the physical world and return with words that become scripture; what they saw is powerful, profound, earthshaking, and/or divine, but they struggle to express ineffable experience in words built from physical-world experience. Even channeled scripture (from God, angels, ascended masters) requires the sender to translate into the receiver's language. Virk says this shows why scriptures couldn't mention quantum physics, multiplayer video games, cloud servers, or computation — the terms would have been meaningless to denizens of the Middle and Far East hundreds or thousands of years ago.
2. **Angels as "messengers of God" (Islam)** — Virk asks what an angel exactly is: wings? dressed in white? a horn? an indescribably bright being of light? In Islam angels are described as "messengers of God" — and he presses: what is a messenger, exactly? Shows angel-descriptions are themselves metaphors ripe for reinterpretation.
3. **The soul-as-clothing metaphor (Sufi Islam + Bhagavad Gita)** — Both the Islamic Sufi traditions and the Bhagavad Gita (the sacred text of Hindus) use the exact same metaphor: the soul incarnates into a body the way a person puts on clothes. Virk notes clothing was, in the long view of humanity, a technological innovation — evidence that scripture used techno-metaphors of its day. (Same for Buddha's wheel, from the previous chapter.)
4. **Yogananda's film projector and Autobiography of a Yogi** — In the 1920s, Swami Yogananda, one of the first Indian swamis to live in the US, explained the illusory nature of maya with the updated metaphor of the film projector — more effective for modern Americans than the old term *maya*. His bestselling *Autobiography of a Yogi* later influenced millions toward meditation and yoga in the 1960s–70s; one was Steve Jobs — when his biographer visited just before Jobs's death, it was the only book on his iPad. Virk points readers to his own book *Wisdom of a Yogi*, written on the autobiography's seventy-fifth anniversary. Shows mystics deliberately updating metaphors to current technology — exactly what Virk proposes doing with the simulation metaphor.
5. **Genesis 1:3 and creation in six days** — "God said, 'Let there be light' and there was light"; the world created in six days, God resting on the seventh. Scientists dismiss a creator making the world so fast, but Virk argues: a computer-programmed reality could exist in seconds if the programming were in place; computational irreducibility means some things must be created by programs run over steps (like procedural generation in video games); the "six days" may be six clock units having nothing to do with Earth days; and spinning up a computer program uses electromagnetic signals — light — so "Let there be light" would be "proven to be literally true."
6. **The Koran on the length of a "day"** — The Koran spells out explicitly, in several chapters, that six or seven days doesn't mean Earth days: in one case a day for the Lord is a thousand years from our point of view; in another, a day for angels is fifty thousand years for us. Virk says this reinforces that "six days" is metaphorical and could represent any arbitrarily, incomprehensibly large number of years.
7. **The Genesis creation sequence** — Roughly, usually by God speaking a command: (1) create the universe (with light); (2) create the waters; (3) create the land to divide the waters into seas; (4) create herbs, grass, trees, fruit, etc.; (5) create the animals; (6) populate it with beings in His own image. Virk maps this onto how a virtual world would be built up in stages.
8. **The holodeck (Star Trek: The Next Generation)** — Crew members of the Enterprise create convincing virtual worlds simply by telling the computer what to build; the computer/AI creates the landscape (oceans, land), then beasts, then characters, usually in the image of humans (sometimes aliens), storing the commands as a "program." Virk, a programmer from an early age, wondered about the word *program* since no code was written by humans — presumably the computer wrote and revised the underlying code to fit the spoken specifications. The holodeck played a role in his own interest in the simulation idea; here it models "speaking a world into existence."
9. **Meta's BuilderBot demo (early 2022)** — A video demo of Meta's AI tools creating a virtual world from verbal commands: Mark Zuckerberg tells BuilderBot to create an island in the water with a beach (not just a picture — a 3D model explorable by your 3D avatar), then clouds in the sky, then to change the cloud type, then palm trees and other objects on the beach. Graphics were primitive, but Virk says it signals that the decades entrepreneurs have hoped to build something like the holodeck may soon be ending.
10. **Nvidia's Omniverse** — Nvidia (maker of the chips powering both video games and AI tools) released its own metaverse, the Omniverse: tell it to create all manner of beasts and it automatically produces realistic-looking models of elephants, giraffes, and any other animals you like. Evidence of the clear line toward AI where "a creator simply speaks their desire" — making a literal reading of Genesis "completely understandable" in a simulated world.
11. **Newton, Einstein, and Bostrom on God** — It's a popular trend for today's scientists to be atheists, but it wasn't always so: both Newton and Einstein often talked about God in their writings. Nick Bostrom of Oxford, who first popularized the simulation hypothesis among academics, has said speculation about being in a simulation might cause some scientists to reconsider their position: beings "outside" the simulation might seem like angels or gods to the limited worldview of beings inside. (Virk flags this returns in the book's final chapter.)
12. **"Reaping what you sow" and the here/hereafter structure** — The Western religions don't explicitly support karma but believe in "reaping what you sow" (a Bible quote); the soul's final resting place depends on good and bad deeds on Earth. All three agree the physical world is a temporary residence ("here") and the afterlife is the "real world" we go to after death (the "hereafter").
13. **Islam: al-akhirah, al-dunya, and the day of qiyamah** — Al-akhirah ("hereafter" in Arabic, the language of the prophet Muhammad) vs. al-dunya (the "here and now"). Belief in the hereafter is one of the six pillars of belief of all Muslims; life on Earth is a training ground or test. On the day of qiyamah the soul is judged before Allah: an angel blows a trumpet, the Earth's mountains are razed, the dead are resurrected and judged. Heaven is Jannah ("garden," rivers of milk and honey); hell is Jahannam (a place of fire) for those doing more evil than good or rejecting the "true faith." Each person has a **scroll of deeds** — and because a person may not know the full extent or impact of their deeds, they are *shown* them visually while being judged: Virk calls this "the first example of a 'life review'" and says both are much easier to understand under the simulation hypothesis.
14. **The cloud-stored scorecard argument** — Virk asks: if there were a heaven or hell, where would they be located in our physical world? Where would the scroll of deeds be recorded? "Not in the rendered world around us, clearly, or we would be able to see it." He maps it to the quest manifest and character scorecard of a video game — stored outside the rendered world, invisible but linked to our character, "somewhere in the cloud." He notes it's common practice in today's video games, even shared MMORPGs, to record gameplay sessions and play them back visually afterward to review what was done well or badly — exactly the deeds-shown-to-us replay of Islam.
15. **Catholic particular judgment and purgatory** — The *Catechism of the Catholic Church* explains the soul receives "eternal retribution in his immortal soul at the very moment of his death" — entrance into heaven (through purification or immediately) or "immediate and everlasting damnation." The Catholic Church also introduces purgatory, a third destination where a soul undergoes "purification" before heaven.
16. **Eastern Orthodox Hades** — The Eastern Orthodox Church — from the first major schism in Christianity, when the church split into Roman Catholicism and the Eastern Orthodox churches — doesn't explicitly recognize purgatory but recognizes an "intermediate place" for certain souls called Hades, borrowing the old Greek term for the underworld.
17. **New Testament book of life** — Revelation 20:12 (the dead standing before God, books opened including the book of life, judged "according to their works") and 2 Corinthians 5:10 (all appear before the judgment seat of Christ to receive according to what they've done, "whether it be good or bad"). Virk: the book of life serves the same function as the scroll of deeds, and if someone records all our actions and plays them back after death, showing our score and next step, "this would be very similar to the idea of a video game—and the simulation hypothesis may be the only possible explanation!"
18. **Judaism's afterlife** — Judaism gives less detail; the concepts feature but are debated among scholars. The immortal soul goes to the Jewish heaven (Gan Eden, the Garden of Eden, or Olam Ha-Ba) or hell (Gehinnom — name closely tied to the Arabic). Gehinnom is a place souls might go only for a period of time to reflect on past misdeeds before being allowed into the Garden of Eden — "adding to the idea that what we do here is important for us to review." Judaism also has the book of life in abridged form: a list of who is going to heaven or hell.
19. **Recording angels: Raqib and Atid (Kiraman Katibin)** — From Virk's own upbringing in the Islamic tradition: two angels named Raqib and Atid (collectively the Kiraman Katibin) record everything we say and do, keeping two scores — sawab for good deeds and haram for bad deeds. Recording angels are more explicit in Islam but also present in Christianity and Judaism.
20. **Christian and Jewish recording angels** — In Christianity, guardian angels (protectors and guides keeping us on God's path) also serve as an individual's recording angel, so Islam's two recording angels aren't needed for the book of life (Malachi 3:16). In Jewish tradition, Gabriel is the recording angel, with the "writing case at his side" (Ezekiel 9:2).
21. **The shoulder angel/devil cartoon and Muhammad's hadith** — The popular cartoon of a good angel on one shoulder and a horned little devil on the other isn't explicitly in the New Testament. But Muhammad specifically says everyone has "a constant companion…from among the jinn and a constant companion from among the angels." The jinn (origin of *genie*) is depicted behind you, the angel in front — both kinds of "guardian angels."
22. **It's a Wonderful Life** — Jimmy Stewart's character George Bailey, considering suicide, is shown by his guardian angel Clarence (played by Henry Travers) what life would have been like for his family and acquaintances had he never been born. Virk calls it "kind of like the life review… but in reverse" — showing the results of a person's deeds by showing what would've happened had he not been there.
23. **The inefficiency argument: angels as programs** — If guardian and recording angels exist, where are they, and why can't our eyes or scientific instruments see them? (Science has been silent, calling angels hallucinations or ignoring them.) Virk reasons they'd be outside the rendered world, keeping watch and recording. He finds it unlikely — "and in the ultimate sin for a computer scientist, inefficient" — that fourteen billion separate conscious angels (two per human, not counting souls on other planets, which at least some religions account for) watch us at all times; they seem to follow well-defined rules. He notes the biblical traditions include a hierarchy of angels with different purposes and powers, and asks whether angels (i.e., messengers) are conscious entities or automatons that follow rules, record deeds, transfer messages, and intervene from time to time. Computer science's answer: recording angels are most likely computer programs — like an intelligent screen recording during a video game that also evaluates gameplay and keeps score, available for review at "judgment" in the afterlife.
24. **The book as a technological metaphor** — Even granting the core truth (actions recorded, deeds judged), the recording angels, scroll of deeds, and book of life are metaphors that made sense to the populace of the Middle East over a thousand years ago. The book is itself a technological metaphor — "perhaps, along with the wheel, among the most important technological developments in the history of humanity." We needn't believe literal angels with feather quills write in antiquated parchment; if it has ontological reality, a more sophisticated mechanism is at work.
25. **Guardian angels as advanced players** — Guardian angels may be more sophisticated than simple automatons: they must make judgments and guide/protect in complex ways. In some traditions they are advanced souls tasked with watching over a small or large number of souls — possibly conscious entities like us, master players leading "guilds" or "soul groups," or advanced AI (vs. recording angels as simpler mechanistic processes).
26. **Who judges?** — Virk asks who reviews the recording and rules on heaven: God? An angel like Saint Peter in Christian traditions? In the Islamic traditions, the angel whose first name Virk shares — Rizwan? Or do the judgments come from our own soul? (Answered partly by the NDE evidence and Koran 17:14.)
27. **Prayer at scale and the daemon/godlike-AI model** — Western adherents pray via rote prayers (Islam, Catholicism) or by asking God for help; sometimes God sends angels in response. Virk admits he never thought of prayer as a literal process with an underlying mechanism. With a billion-plus adherents each in Islam and Christianity praying daily, what entity could track *and reply to* billions of incoming requests? Under the simulation hypothesis: God is most likely an intelligent computer program or AI or a conscious entity outside the simulation, helped by billions of individual "angel" computer programs tracking, recording, and guiding each person. He introduces the computer-science *daemon* (self-running process monitoring programs, doing housekeeping) and the idea of a godlike AI — omniscient about the world per various rules, "a server with a very intelligent program keeping track of everything." Explicit caveat: this doesn't mean God *is* an AI, but He would need an AI system.
28. **Raymond Moody and the NDE core experience** — Modern medicine (CPR and resuscitation techniques in the second half of the twentieth century) produced many people clinically dead for a time and then revived, spiking NDE reports in the West (though NDEs occur spontaneously everywhere on Earth). Dr. Raymond Moody popularized (some say coined) the term in his 1975 bestseller *Life After Life*; after talking with more than a thousand experiencers, he became convinced NDEs were real. His biggest contribution beyond popularizing the term: identifying the common elements — peace/well-being/painlessness; out-of-body experience (seeing one's body, family members, doctors); the tunnel experience (dark tunnel toward a light); beings of light (spiritually advanced beings, sometimes seen as angels or a divine figure like Jesus); the life review; the life decision (a barrier and the choice to cross or return); and the return to the body. Not universal, but common enough to be the recognized "core experience."
29. **Dannion Brinkley's NDE (Saved by the Light)** — Brinkley, struck by lightning in 1975, was clinically dead for twenty-eight minutes; he approached Moody about his experience and later wrote (with Paul Perry) the 1994 bestseller *Saved by the Light*. He encountered a being of light who took him through a "panoramic life review": reliving his entire life one incident at a time from a second-person point of view, being simultaneously himself and every person he had ever interacted with. He has called it a "holographic panoramic 360-degree life review." He felt every situation not just from his own viewpoint but from others' — even feeling what they felt — a jarring experience that changed how he related to people afterward. Notably, he didn't feel judged by anyone but himself, saw nothing he would call hell (a surprise, having grown up in the Bible Belt), and the being of light simply reviewed his mistakes "like a schoolteacher or coach might do at the end of a test or game."
30. **Koran 17:14 reconciles the self-judged review** — Brinkley's no-external-judge report might seem inconsistent with Western religion, but Virk cites the Koran's Day of Judgment passage where each person's book is laid open: "Read your book; your own self is sufficient as a reckoner against you this day" — the self as its own reckoner.
31. **NDE-ers' modern metaphors and the ripple effect** — Other NDE-ers describe the review as an interactive movie, or being in a theater seeing thousands of scenes from their lives simultaneously on thousands of screens arranged around them. Virk: modern experiencers are clearly using current technological metaphors, updating the old book/scroll metaphor. Seeing how your actions affected others (beyond those around you) is sometimes called the **ripple effect**, harking back to Islam's being shown the unknown impact of your deeds.
32. **The VR re-render startup and haptics** — How could a 360-degree immersive replay of your whole life work? Video games already answer it: a video game startup recently took the 3D landscape of a multiplayer competitive game and could re-render any part of the game session in virtual reality from any coordinates in the virtual space. We can't yet reproduce other players' feelings, but we can already replay 3D scenes from other players' points of view, and haptic interfaces — gloves and suits letting us feel what happens inside the game, as depicted in *Ready Player One* — are being built.
33. **The Matrix and "insert another quarter"** — During the death of the character in the simulation, the player starts to wake up in another reality — like Neo waking in the pod after taking the red pill, eventually found by Morpheus in *The Matrix* — met by beings (of light, angels, or passed-on relatives) who have been watching us play. The NDE "life decision" to go back and continue the character's life "is a lot like the crude 'insert another quarter now to keep playing.'"
34. **Second-edition addendum and the bridge conclusion** — Since the first edition, Virk has found many more scriptural similarities and metaphors that, on examination, reasonably describe "some kind of illusory world where we play games and for which the results are recorded." His conclusion: simulation theory bridges sects of the Eastern and Western traditions — a connective metaphor between religions and between ancient religious and modern scientific worldviews.

- "God said, 'Let there be light' and there was light." (Genesis 1:3) — which Virk argues would be "proven to be literally true" if the world were spun up as a computer program using electromagnetic signals.
- "Each man receives his eternal retribution in his immortal soul at the very moment of his death…either entrance into the blessedness of heaven—through a purification or immediately—or immediate and everlasting damnation." (Catechism of the Catholic Church, on the particular judgment)
- "And I saw the dead, small and great, stand before God; and the books were opened: and another book was opened, which is the book of life: and the dead were judged out of those things which were written in the books, according to their works." (Revelation 20:12)
- "For we must all appear before the judgment seat of Christ; that every one may receive the things done in his body, according to that he hath done, whether it be good or bad." (2 Corinthians 5:10)
- "There is no one among you but he has with him a constant companion…from among the jinn and a constant companion from among the angels." (the prophet Muhammad — Virk's source for the shoulder angel/devil trope)
- "The simulation hypothesis provides the best answer: Someone or something is watching our actions, recording them, and 'keeping score'—not just what we do but how it affects other people in the game." — Virk, on the scroll of deeds and karma.
- "I began to relive my entire life, one incident at a time. In what I call the panoramic life review, I watched my life from a second person point of view. As I experienced this I was myself as well as every other person with whom I had ever interacted." — Dannion Brinkley, on his life review after twenty-eight minutes of clinical death.
- "Read your book; your own self is sufficient as a reckoner against you this day." (Koran 17:14) — the self-as-reckoner passage Virk uses to reconcile Brinkley's unjudged review with scripture.
- "It sure sounds like a server with a very intelligent program keeping track of everything—some kind of digital consciousness that can watch and respond to events as they happen." — Virk, on godlike AI (with the caveat that God would *need* an AI system, not that God is one).
- "It becomes both a connective metaphor between religious traditions and a way to create a bridge between the ancient religious and modern scientific views of the world." — Virk's closing conclusion about the simulation idea.

## Thread
This chapter completes the religious half of Part III's argument. Chapters 10–11 argued the Eastern traditions (Buddhism, Hinduism — maya, karma, Buddha's wheel, the quest manifest/character scorecard) map onto simulation machinery; chapter 12 makes the parallel case for the Abrahamic West: creation-by-command = procedural world generation; scroll of deeds / book of life = cloud-stored gameplay recording; recording angels = daemon programs; guardian angels = advanced players or AI; God = a conscious entity outside the simulation aided by godlike AI; the afterlife = the "real world" outside the rendered one. The NDE section then joins ancient scripture to modern first-person reports, showing the same afterlife content (life review, beings of light, self-judgment) re-expressed in each era's best technology metaphor — from parchment book to holographic 360-degree replay. Virk explicitly hands off to chapter 13, which will treat OBEs, synchronicity, and UFOs through the same information-and-computation lens, and to the book's final chapter, which revisits who is *outside* the simulation (Bostrom's beings that would look like angels or gods).

## Connections
- **Ch 10–11 (Eastern traditions)** — Virk repeatedly leans on the previous chapters: Buddha's wheel as techno-metaphor, karma and the character scorecard/quest manifest "mentioned in the previous chapter," maya and Yogananda's projector. This chapter's explicit thesis is that the Western traditions are compatible with the simulation hypothesis "just as it does with the Eastern religions," and the closing paragraph declares simulation theory the bridge between them.
- **Ch 13 (next chapter)** — Announced directly in-text: OBEs (previewed here inside the NDE core elements), synchronicity, and UFOs, "which also don't fit into the scientific worldview," continue the "unexplainable" thread using information and computation.
- **Part I (AI world-building)** — The BuilderBot/Omniverse material picks up Part I's discussion of AI creating 3D models and, soon, entire worlds from verbal description.
- **Final chapter** — Bostrom's point that beings outside the simulation would seem like angels or gods to those inside is explicitly deferred to the book's final chapter on implications.
- **Speculative cross-book links (collection; not claims of the chapter):**
  - *The Holographic Universe* (Talbot) — Brinkley's "holographic panoramic 360-degree life review" and the thousands-of-screens theater imagery resonate with Talbot's holographic model of reality and his own treatment of NDE life reviews.
  - *My Big TOE* (Campbell) — Campbell's larger consciousness system, in which physical reality is a virtual reality and death is a shift of the player's data stream, parallels Virk's "player wakes up in another reality" reading of the NDE almost one-to-one.
  - *American Cosmic* (Pasulka) — Pasulka's thesis that technology mediates and reshapes religious experience mirrors Virk's core claim here that each era's religion is expressed through its era's technological metaphors (book, projector, video game).
  - *The Power of Now* (Tolle) — Weaker link: Tolle's insistence that the deepest reality lies beyond mind-made form loosely echoes the mystics' translation problem — the ineffable "out there" that words built from the physical world cannot capture.
  - *Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance* (Pirsig) — Most speculative: Pirsig's struggle to name Quality — a reality prior to intellectual description — rhymes with Virk's mystics struggling to compress the ineffable into the vocabulary available to them.
