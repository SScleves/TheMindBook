# Ch 2 — Computer Games: From Pong to MMORPGs

## In one paragraph
Virk opens Part I by arguing that the simulation hypothesis is taken seriously today — rather than in earlier computing eras — in large part because of the sophistication and rapid advancement of video games and graphics technology, an argument he anchors in Elon Musk's 2016 Code Conference remarks about Pong evolving into photorealistic multiplayer simulations. He traces how Bostrom's simulation argument (we might be AI inside a simulation) and Chalmers's Matrix Hypothesis (we might be players inside a video-game-like virtual world) became conflated over time into the combined "simulation hypothesis," and introduces his own organizing concept: the **simulation point**, a kind of technological singularity at which a civilization can create hyperrealistic simulations with hyperrealistic AI beings. The chapter then walks the first leg of "the road to the simulation point" — a four-stage history of video game rendering technology, from stage 0 (text adventures) through stage 1 (arcade/console graphics), stage 2 (graphical RPGs), to stage 3 (3D-rendered MMORPGs and virtual worlds). Each stage is mined for the building blocks of a future all-encompassing simulation: the game loop, saved game state as information, NPCs, physics and rendering engines, persistent shared worlds, and procedural generation. Virk's conclusion is that most of the technical concepts needed for a simulated virtual world are already in place; what remains is computing power, new human-computer interaction, network optimization — and, crucially, players who believe the world is real.

## Core claims
- Virk argues that one of the main reasons scientists, philosophers, and technologists have started taking the simulation hypothesis seriously in the early twenty-first century is the sophistication and rapid advancement of video games and graphics technology.
- Virk relays Musk's conjecture that if video game technology keeps improving at any rate at all, games will become indistinguishable from reality — and Musk's conclusion that the chance we are in base reality (not in a simulation) is "one in billions."
- Virk argues that two originally distinct ideas — Bostrom's (we might be AI inside a simulation, i.e., NPCs) and Chalmers's (we might be players inside a video-game-like virtual world, i.e., PCs/avatars) — have become conflated over time into what is now called the simulation hypothesis.
- Virk claims his definition of the simulation point covers the capabilities required by both Bostrom's simulation argument and Chalmers's Matrix Hypothesis.
- Virk argues that video game rendering technology can be classified into four historical stages (0 through 3), and that projecting these stages forward leads to the simulation point.
- Virk argues that today's video games may already be providing the underlying infrastructure for the future stages that reach the simulation point.
- Virk argues that alongside rendering, the sophistication of control mechanisms (keyboards, joysticks, specialized controllers, haptics, voice, eventually mind interfaces) is a key factor in the stages.
- Virk argues that "the history of video games is the history of optimizing very limited resources," and that without those optimization techniques neither computer graphics nor progress down the road to the simulation point would have been possible.
- Virk notes that arcade games introduced the term *video game* (because of the way they drew graphics onto a video screen), and calls the new form of entertainment as significant as the introduction of motion pictures or television in the twentieth century.
- Virk argues that text adventures used "the most powerful graphics engine available—that of our minds," and that the idea of using the mind's imagination rather than an external screen will return in more advanced stages of the road.
- Virk argues that the ability to capture the state of the player and the entire world as information — stored somewhere outside the rendered world — is a key precursor to the simulation hypothesis.
- Virk claims the technique of rendering only the portion of the world the player's character is observing has a direct parallel with quantum physics (to be explored in Part II as conditional rendering).
- Virk argues that the persistence of persistent worlds is an illusion: it lasts only as long as the information on the server is saved and unchanged.
- Virk suggests that a suspiciously round number in a game world can be a tell of computer-generated reality: No Man's Sky's exactly eighteen quintillion planets is the largest number representable in exactly 64 bits.
- Virk claims we are not yet at the simulation point: today's games cannot support billions of individual agents of consciousness, nor is immersion at a level where anyone would confuse game world with real world — but he argues closing that gap is "a matter of adding computing power, adding new forms of user human-computer interaction, and further optimization of network traffic."
- Virk claims the video game industry is now as big as the traditional entertainment industry, with revenue over a quarter trillion dollars a year and climbing.

## Concepts
- **simulation-hypothesis** — The merged idea, formed over time from two once-separate notions, that we might be AI inside a simulation (Bostrom) and/or players in a video game (Chalmers). At the core of both is that some technological civilization is capable of creating highly realistic simulations that cannot be distinguished from physical reality.
- **simulation-point** — Virk's term (a kind of technological singularity) for the theoretical point in a civilization's development when it can create hyperrealistic simulations with hyperrealistic AI beings: raw computing power to track a large number of seemingly individual points of consciousness, record all their experiences, and render the environment indistinguishably from the "physical world"; plus the ability to beam players' consciousness into a shared world, record their responses, and run AI beings (NPCs). A precise definition, building on Vernor Vinge's original definition of the technological singularity, is promised for Chapter 6.
- **matrix-hypothesis** — David Chalmers's idea, from his essay "The Matrix as Metaphysics," that we are in fact players inside a video game–like virtual world; concerned with being inside a virtual reality as a player (PC/avatar).
- **simulation-argument (Bostrom)** — Bostrom's 2003 argument, less concerned with video games and VR than with the computational power and memory needed to create simulated AI beings and what that means for a technologically advanced society; detailed treatment deferred to Chapter 6.
- **posthuman** — Bostrom's term for a species that is able to build these hyperrealistic simulations.
- **base-reality** — The condition of not being in a simulation; Musk put the chance that we are in base reality at "one in billions."
- **road-to-the-simulation-point** — Virk's name for the process traced in Part I: video game history from the 1960s–70s to MMORPGs, then projecting forward through virtual reality, augmented reality, direct mind broadcast, artificial intelligence, and downloadable consciousness, ending with the capability to create something like the Great Simulation.
- **stage-0: text adventures and the "game world" (1970s to mid-1980s)** — Single-player text adventures that described locations in text and took typed commands; developed in parallel with stage 1 but split off for its different characteristics and technical underpinnings. Introduced the big game world, player game state, world game state, NPCs, textual input, and the basic game loop.
- **stage-1: early graphical arcade and console games (1970s–1980s)** — Games about controlling movement and action on screen; pioneered computer graphics, real-time motion controls, level-based worlds, multiple lives, physics engines, and hints of an expanded world.
- **stage-2: graphical adventure role-playing games (1980s–1990s)** — Games combining the graphical capabilities of arcade games with the expanded worlds of text adventures; introduced the big graphically rendered world, graphical player state and inventory, world game state with off-screen history, graphical NPCs, and quests/storylines. Often (though not always) set in a medieval European fantasy world reminiscent of D&D, with storylines that evolved over multiple game releases over many years, giving these fictional game worlds a level of reality previously reserved for fantasy worlds such as Middle-Earth and Narnia.
- **stage-3: 3D rendered MMORPGs and virtual worlds (1990s–today)** — Massively multiplayer online role-playing games built on first-person perspective and 3D modeling; introduced 3D avatars, persistent shared worlds on servers, player state stored outside the rendered world, user-generated content, questing engines, evolving storylines, distinct physics/rendering engines, and procedurally generated worlds.
- **basic-game-loop** — The cycle introduced by text adventures and maintained to this day: the computer presents the existing state of the game world and your character; the player issues a command; the program changes the game state based on the command and other factors; repeat. Virk notes it mirrors the dungeon master's role in Dungeons & Dragons.
- **game-state / player-game-state / world-game-state** — The player game state is the player's metadata (XP, level, character information), artifacts (gold, weapons), and location; the world game state is the state of the world itself, changed by player actions (e.g., where the sword was dropped, the location of the ogre). Initially held only while running, later savable to disk, eventually to cloud servers.
- **persistent-shared-world / persistent-world-state** — Introduced by MMORPGs: a world that exists beyond a single gameplay session or a single player's computer, its game state maintained on a server with information sent to individual machines for local rendering; there could be multiple versions of the world hosted on different servers, each with different states. Its persistence "is an illusion; it lasts only as long as the information about the game world on the server is saved and doesn't change."
- **non-player-characters (NPCs)** — Computer-controlled characters; the term originated as "non-playable characters" in tabletop games like D&D, where the dungeon master ran them. Text adventures introduced the first computerized NPCs (merchants, bartenders, ogres), whose conversational engines were "the humble beginnings" of today's chatbot industry and among the very first examples of AI.
- **player-characters (PCs) / avatars** — The player's own character; "avatar" is the term for a character's on-screen representation, coined by the makers of Habitat (one of the first shared virtual worlds, built by Lucasfilm Games), and customizable by race, profession, skin color, gender, and clothing.
- **physics-engine** — Whatever set of rules the graphical objects on screen have to follow — a scaled-down version of Newton's laws of classical mechanics (e.g., momentum in Asteroids). Skill at these games meant understanding the underlying physics engine.
- **rendering-engine** — The component that actually creates the pixels (based on clothing and texture of objects) so the player can see the scene, distinct from the physics engine that figures out where objects should end up; the two came into full play with MMORPGs.
- **questing-engine** — The code to keep track of tasks, challenges, and achievements assembled into storylines; the informal name that emerged as quests multiplied in MMORPGs.
- **textual-input** — Interaction by typed commands with a very specific grammar ("go north," "drop knife"); Virk says textual input — vocal or keyboard — remains essential in sophisticated simulations and will remain so.
- **real-time-motion-controls** — Joystick or trackball control of on-screen movement in real time; real-time feedback allowed a feeling of immersion even standing at a cabinet game in a pizza parlor.
- **multiple-lives** — The fixed number of lives per quarter in arcade games; dying that many times meant restarting at level 1.
- **wraparound-geometry** — The beginning of a geometry of the virtual world: in Asteroids, a ship leaving the top of the screen reappears at the bottom, giving the illusion of a spherical world.
- **inventory** — The "magical place" off-screen where a player's objects are stored, from which they can be deposited into or taken from the rendered world.
- **assembly-language** — The native language of any processor: hexadecimal codes varying by CPU that tell the processor what to do physically (put a value in a register or memory location); very fast, hence optimal for early graphics, but with limited commands and very time-consuming to write.
- **compilation / interpretation** — The process by which most higher-level languages are transformed down into assembly language to run on a particular computer.
- **interactive-fiction** — The subgenre that keeps the text-adventure tradition alive today; MMORPGs, with storylines evolving through guilds, later became "the first true graphical interactive fiction."
- **side-scrollers** — Term that eventually arose from limited game worlds viewable by scrolling left or right.
- **MUDs** — Multiuser dungeons: early shared spaces (along with chat rooms) using dial-up services such as CompuServe, preceding graphical multiplayer gaming.
- **MMORPG** — Massively multiplayer online role-playing game; first wave included Ultima Online, EverQuest, and World of Warcraft. Simultaneous players log in to the same persistent world from anywhere; today's MMORPGs have millions of players.
- **first-person-perspective + 3D modeling** — Underlying the stage-3 leap: designers used 3D models and maps to lay out worlds and placed the player's character at a point in the modeled world, rendering only what is visible from where the character stands and looks.
- **user-generated-content** — Objects or structures players "leave" that become part of the persistent world (Second Life, many MMORPGs) — which Virk notes is not so different from dropping a bicycle in a real park.
- **procedural-generation** — A technique that uses algorithms to create data (rather than creating it manually), as with each unique planet and its inhabitants in No Man's Sky; gets around the finite size of hand-designed worlds.
- **fractal-geometry** — Algorithms discovered from the realization that natural structures are hard to reproduce with old Euclidean geometry; fractal techniques can draw a coastline that looks natural, nearly indistinguishable from the real thing.
- **large-language-model (LLM)** — In the postscript on text adventures: generative AIs like ChatGPT can dynamically generate room descriptions, offering an almost infinite set of rooms, though continuity is lost because today's early AI chatbots lack the logic or memory for player/world game state.
- **state-as-information** — The requirement that the state of the player and entire world be captured concisely as information, stored outside the rendered world (today on cloud servers invisible to those inside the world) and rendered as needed; Virk calls this ability paramount.
- **conditional-rendering** — Rendering only the portion of the world the player's character is observing; Virk flags a direct parallel with quantum physics, to be explored in Part II.
- **smart-NPCs** — AI-driven simulated characters that may become indistinguishable from player characters; modern state deferred to Chapter 5.
- **downloadable-consciousness** — The concept, often lumped in with the singularity, that even biological beings could be downloaded to silicon; a later stage before the simulation point (Chapter 5).

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Elon Musk at the Code Conference (2016)** — The founder of Tesla and SpaceX reflected on the forty years since Pong ("two rectangles and a dot") to today's photorealistic 3D simulations with millions of simultaneous players; he conjectured that with any rate of improvement games become indistinguishable from reality, and concluded that the chance we are in base reality is "one in billions." Virk uses this as the emblem of why the hypothesis is now taken seriously.
- **Nick Bostrom's 2003 paper** — Oxford's Nick Bostrom, best known for his work on AI, popularized the simulation argument in this paper, which refers to a species able to build these hyperrealistic simulations as becoming "posthuman"; it concerns the computational power and memory needed for simulated AI beings rather than video games/VR per se. Detailed in Chapter 6.
- **David Chalmers, "The Matrix as Metaphysics" (2003)** — Chalmers was invited by the creators of The Matrix to write one of several essays about philosophy and the film; his essay explored the "Matrix Hypothesis" — that we are players inside a video game–like virtual world. Virk pairs it with Bostrom's paper as the two ideas that fused into the simulation hypothesis.
- **The Matrix (film)** — Described as one of the first Hollywood blockbusters that asked serious philosophical questions and, for many, spurred serious conversations; the benchmark for the "all-encompassing" simulation Part I asks whether we could build.
- **Colossal Cave Adventure (Will Crowther, 1976)** — The first text adventure game, built on a PDP-10 mainframe, based partly on the Mammoth Caves in Kentucky where Crowther had spent much time. Shows the origin of stage 0 and of the game world explored via typed commands.
- **Don Woods's port and the fantasy elements** — Woods (from Stanford) and many other programmers took Crowther's original code, ported it to various systems, and added the multiple fantasy elements that made Adventure a precursor to the adventure games that followed.
- **Dungeons & Dragons (tabletop)** — Used as the offline analogue of the basic game loop: the DM tells players the state of the world, each player declares actions, and the DM — with dice and the master adventure map — reports outcomes; the computer plays the DM's role in text adventures. D&D also originated the term non-playable characters and later lent its character role-playing model and medieval fantasy flavor to RPGs and MMORPGs.
- **Infocom, Zork I and Zork II (early 1980s)** — A group of MIT grads started Infocom, which produced the extremely popular Zork games for PCs and Apple computers, plus a whole suite of games on its text engine ranging from original adventures like Planetfall to licensed properties such as The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy. Virk reproduces a hand-drawn map of Zork to show how expansive imagined worlds were.
- **Saving game state to media** — Early text games reset when exited; as text games reached PCs, game state could be saved on disk (initially cassettes, then floppy disks, later hard disks) and play resumed — the birth of persistable state.
- **Interactive fiction and emulators today** — Text games are rarely played by today's generation, but the "interactive fiction" subgenre keeps the tradition alive, and emulators run the old games on Mac, Windows, and Linux. Some industry purists feel all later games lost something because no rendering matches imagination — akin to feeling no film of The Lord of the Rings could match the book.
- **ChatGPT/LLM text adventures (postscript)** — Generative AIs have been used to provide a seemingly infinite set of rooms, with the LLM generating descriptions dynamically instead of a designer authoring each room. It works but is primitive: continuity is lost because current chatbots lack the logic/memory for player and world game state. Virk projects a near future where an AI dungeon master runs coherent adventures forever, indistinguishable from human-authored ones.
- **Spacewar! (MIT, 1962)** — A graphical game built on the PDP-1, preceding even Pong; because it ran on a mainframe it wasn't widely available outside universities and lacked the controls that made Pong a success.
- **Pong (Atari, 1972)** — The first arcade game most people remember and the first widely available: a few dots on a monitor built into a cabinet. Virk credits it with ushering in the modern era of video games.
- **Assembly language era + Virk's Tic Tac Toe anecdote** — Early games were programmed in assembly (vs. today's C#, JavaScript, Python and engines like Unity or Unreal) because pixel updates needed near-instant speed. Virk recalls programming Tic Tac Toe in BASIC as a kid, being horrified by rows of hexadecimal graphics code in Byte magazine, then copying the hex codes onto his Apple II and being amazed at their speed. Shows the resource-optimization theme firsthand.
- **Steve Jobs, Steve Wozniak, and Nolan Bushnell at Atari** — A well-known Silicon Valley anecdote: Jobs, working for Atari founder Bushnell, promised he could build a certain game quickly with limited memory; the skeptical Bushnell gave him the project, and Jobs brought in the far more technical Wozniak, who created the game at night after his full-time engineering job. Illustrates the pioneers squeezing every bit of performance from limited hardware.
- **Pac-Man and Space Invaders** — More sophisticated arcade games in a direct line from Pong, developed by Japanese companies in what some call the golden age of Japanese video games.
- **Atari VCS / Atari 2600 (home console)** — How many first experienced video games at home; it carried a graphical version of Adventure, plus Space Invaders and Pac-Man. In Atari's Adventure, the avatar was a single dot — Virk's baseline in the avatar-evolution lineage.
- **Pole Position** — An early racing game on the Atari that suggested a "graphical world" beyond the track you couldn't leave; it was the game that raised the questions in teenage Virk's mind about the world "in there," beyond the racetrack.
- **Asteroids** — Example of an early physics engine (your ship had momentum it kept while slowing or turning) and of wraparound geometry (exiting the top of the screen re-appearing at the bottom — the illusion of a spherical world).
- **Space Invaders (mechanics)** — Cited as a single-player multilevel arcade game whose physics rules allowed only left-right movement at constant speed and shooting upward at aliens.
- **Joust** — You rode a flying ostrich that could land only on certain parts of the screen; the physics rules were visible in how the ostrich rose and slowly floated down. Skill = internalizing the physics engine.
- **Raiders of the Lost Ark and E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial (Atari crossover games)** — Early media crossovers; in the Raiders game (which Virk played for many hours) the player explored a landscape of mesas to find treasure — a middle step between arcade games and graphical RPGs. The poor reception of the E.T. game is credited by some with not only the downfall of Atari but the end of that phase of the industry.
- **Video game industry revenue** — Today the industry is as big as traditional entertainment: revenue over a quarter trillion dollars a year and climbing.
- **Virk's 1987 Japan trip (Mazda program)** — Mazda opened a plant in Flat Rock, Michigan, near Virk's Detroit-suburb high school, and each year paid to send seven Michigan high schoolers to Japan for the summer; Virk went on one of the first trips. In Japanese high schoolers' living rooms he saw a console with buttons rather than joysticks, made by Nintendo (makers of Donkey Kong) — foreshadowing the next phase of the industry.
- **NES and Sega Genesis** — The consoles that marked the next phase in the US market (raved about by Virk's younger brother's generation while Virk was at MIT): 16-bit systems capable of much better graphics than the 8-bit Atari-era systems.
- **The Legend of Zelda** — Stage-2 graphical RPG: playing Link, you had to rescue Princess Zelda and the kingdom; an example of backstory and quest. The franchise continues to evolve today (as does Final Fantasy).
- **King's Quest** — First entry: you play Sir Graham, a knight of the kingdom of Daventry, who must find three legendary treasures; success makes your character King of Daventry. Followed by many sequels (I–VI cited for continuing backstory); shown as the exemplar figure of first graphical RPGs and of 2D sprites in the avatar lineage.
- **Ultima** — Stage-2 RPG whose storyline continued across sequels (Ultima I–III); it later got a multiplayer version, Ultima Online, which also appears among the first wave of MMORPGs.
- **FarmVille** — Resource-management example of world game state evolving while you're away: crops grow in your absence, so the world renders differently on return, reflecting an (accelerated) history — important for realistic simulations.
- **Doom (id Software)** — Landmark first-person shooter: proved physical 3D worlds could be represented with models and rendered in pixels in real time on a client machine, influencing not just games but movie special effects and computer-aided design/engineering. One of the first truly popular multiplayer games via two-player "deathmatch mode" over the just-spreading internet; so popular at launch that it ate up network bandwidth on college campuses nationwide. Significant on the road not for the game itself but for its rendering techniques and multiplayer mode — precursor to both MMORPGs and FPSs.
- **MUDs and CompuServe** — Before graphical multiplayer, there were shared chat rooms and multiuser dungeons over dial-up services such as CompuServe; the onboarding of the game-playing population to the internet in the 1990s made multiplayer graphical gaming possible.
- **Habitat (Lucasfilm Games)** — One of the first shared virtual worlds; its makers coined the term "avatar" for a character's on-screen representation.
- **First wave of MMORPGs** — Ultima Online, EverQuest, and the biggest and most popular of the era, World of Warcraft; players chose and customized avatars (race: human, elf, dwarf, gnome; profession: thief, barbarian, warrior, wizard; plus skin color, gender, clothing), and interactions affected both player and world game state on a server.
- **World of Warcraft** — The most prominent desktop MMORPG: fantasy-themed 3D world that looked like it came out of Lord of the Rings; so popular it was lampooned on TV shows like South Park; estimated over a hundred million subscribers over its lifetime, the most popular MMO of its era. With EVE Online, its in-world storylines were followed by online blogs — the first true graphical interactive fiction.
- **EVE Online** — Science-fiction-themed desktop MMORPG of the same era with a much more finely developed virtual economy, built on outer-space trade and battles via spaceships and different planets.
- **No Man's Sky (Hello Games, 2016)** — Considered the largest universe inside a video game: supposedly eighteen quintillion planets, each with a unique ecosystem of fauna, flora, and landscapes, explorable on foot or by jet pack. The game itself disappointed many gamers ("many found it boring—perhaps too much like real life!") but changed how large universes could be created, via procedural generation and fractal algorithms. Virk's kicker: exactly eighteen quintillion is the largest number representable in exactly 64 bits — inside that universe, this could be a clue you were in a computer-generated reality; games evolved from 8-bit (Atari) to 16-bit (late-1980s consoles) to 64-bit lifelike worlds.
- **Fractal coastline comparison (Figures 8–9)** — Two island coastlines, one natural and one generated with fractal algorithms; "it's very difficult to tell the difference unless you know what you are looking for." Fractal algorithms arose because natural structures are hard to reproduce with Euclidean geometry (more in later chapters and in Virk's The Simulated Multiverse).
- **Second Life and Minecraft** — Cited for persistence of objects and user-generated content in virtual worlds even after individual players log out; you can "leave" objects or build structures that join the persistent world — like dropping a bicycle in a real park for other "players" to pick up. Second Life and non-game persistent worlds are promised for the next chapter.
- **Einstein's "very persistent illusion"** — Virk borrows Einstein's phrase to state the missing requirement: for the world around us to be that illusion, players must believe it is not a game or simulation but real — something no current game achieves.

## Thread
Chapter 2 opens Part I's engineering case: before asking whether we live in a simulation, Virk asks whether we could build one, and answers by showing that four decades of video game history (stages 0–3) have already produced the core machinery — game loop, state-as-information, NPCs, physics/rendering engines, persistent shared server-side worlds, procedural generation. The chapter converts the simulation hypothesis from metaphysics into a technology roadmap, defining the simulation point as its destination. It ends by naming exactly what is still missing — billions of conscious agents, total immersion, belief — which the following chapters on VR/AR, mind interfaces, and AI are structured to supply.
