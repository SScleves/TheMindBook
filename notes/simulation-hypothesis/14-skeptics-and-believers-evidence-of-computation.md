# Ch 14 — Skeptics and Believers: Evidence of Computation

## In one paragraph
Virk tackles one of the questions he says he is asked often: can we prove, or at least detect, by physical experiment whether we live in a simulation? He first organizes the skeptics' case into two families — consciousness-based objections (consciousness is primary, so we can't be "just" simulated beings) and negation/resource arguments (simulating the universe would take impossible or pointless amounts of computing) — and answers both: the consciousness objection only hits the NPC flavor, not the RPG flavor where conscious players inhabit avatars, and the resource argument assumes today's classical computing with no optimization, ignoring conditional rendering and quantum computing. He then surveys the believers' evidence in two corresponding families: evidence of conditional rendering (delayed-choice experiments such as the Italian MLRO satellite test and Tom Campbell's CUSAC program, which suggest the world is rendered only when observed by a player) and evidence of pixels/computation (Beane's cosmic-ray lattice and the GZK cutoff, Gates's error-correcting codes inside supersymmetry equations, Vopson's second law of infodynamics, quantum error correction defining space-time at the Institute for Advanced Study, entanglement explained as shared memory addresses, fractals as nature's algorithm, Wolfram's simple programs, and Hoffman's evolutionary case against veridical perception). His conclusion: no single experiment is definitive, but everything points toward the universe being a type of computer — what he calls the Great Simulation — in which the physical is really information and computation.

## Core claims
- **Virk argues** that while some believe we can no more detect our simulation than Mario could detect his (and so we "should just get on with playing the game"), there may in fact be ways to detect that we live in a computer simulation, or at least a computational universe rendered for us based on information.
- **The skeptics' case is** presented in two categories: (1) *evidence of consciousness* — scientists from Max Planck to Amit Goswami hold that consciousness is at the heart of the universe, so Bostrom's argument "cannot be true" because it makes us simulated beings only; (2) *evidence of negation* — resource arguments/experiments purporting to show a simulation of the universe would require infinite resources, or at least as many atoms as physical reality contains, "so what would be the point?" Virk notes these skeptics will admit some probability (less than 100 percent) that we are in a simulation, and that their case turns out to be "more arguments than evidence."
- **Virk counters the consciousness objection** by saying it is not really an argument against the simulation hypothesis but against the NPC flavor (lifeless AI). The RPG flavor — conscious players outside the game playing characters/avatars inside it — accounts for consciousness; if consciousness is downloaded at birth and uploaded at death, the objection falls away, and the requirement for consciousness in some experiments may actually *support* the RPG flavor.
- **Virk counters the resource argument** by noting it assumes today's computers, no optimization algorithms, and computing-intensive methods (Monte Carlo); he compares it to early-20th-century royal astronomers who "proved" a moon rocket impossible on fuel weight, and to those who declared 3D video game worlds impossible before conditional rendering. Video games render only what is needed; the rest exists in informational form.
- **The believers' evidence falls into two categories**: (1) *evidence of conditional rendering* — the physical universe operates like a video game that draws only what the player sees (variations of the double-slit/delayed-choice experiments, some emphasizing the need for an actual observer, implying players); (2) *evidence of pixels or computation* — clues that a computer generates reality (pixels, blurring, coding artifacts, digital physics treating the universe as computing information).
- **Virk claims** quantum indeterminacy by itself is evidence of a type of conditional rendering, and that this "unexplainable behavior seems to make sense only if the simulation hypothesis is true."
- **Virk concedes** that whether the observer must be a *conscious* one is debatable (could an AI or a measuring device be the observer?), but holds that multiple experiments have confirmed the need for an observer and that the findings of quantum physics thus far are "pretty conclusive that reality doesn't follow the old Newtonian model"; in the conclusion he still reads the various versions of Wheeler's delayed-choice experiment as seeming "to show that consciousness (or a player) is required for quantum determinacy to work."
- **Bostrom's position (as reported)**: glitch reports are most likely errors of perception; there would be no clear, obvious experiment giving 100 percent certainty about which of his three possibilities holds — though even he admits there might be ways to detect evidence making a simulation more (or less) likely. Virk is "not so dismissive" of strange phenomena and holds that science advances by looking at its fringes.
- **Chalmers's position (as reported)**: even accepting Bostrom's statistical argument, the revelation is not futile — the world remains real from the point of view of those inside it; in *Reality+* he admits it may be impossible to tell if the simulation is "perfect."
- **Noack's position (as reported)**: it is impossible to test the simulation hypothesis as a whole; the best we can do is look for ways the simulation might work and detect artifacts — perhaps clues left by lazy designers.
- **Virk argues** the existence of quantum computers may prove the opposite of the skeptics' claim: needing as many qubits as particles doesn't show the universe isn't a computer — "it might just show that the universe is one big computer system after all!" — even before counting optimization.
- **Virk argues** that quantum entanglement — confirmed but unexplained (the "why" and "how" are missing) — is best modeled computationally: entangled particles are like two pixels sharing the same memory address, and the *why* is optimization/compression.
- **Virk argues** that fractal geometry, cellular automata, and Wolfram's simple programs show evidence of computation in nature at the macro level: nature may be a fractal-generating computer, and matter may be created by following encoded instructions outside the rendered world.
- **The chapter's verdict**: neither resource-based nor consciousness-based objections stand up to scrutiny; no single experiment proves (or conclusively disproves) the hypothesis, but collectively the evidence "strongly point[s] to the idea that the universe is a type of computer" — the Great Simulation.
- **Caveat Virk himself flags**: the statements and experiments here "are by no means conclusive," and the chapter moves into speculative territory; more definitive answers should come as such experiments multiply and our own technology approaches the simulation point.

## Concepts
- **Glitches in the simulation** — everyday-parlance strategy of spotting simulation artifacts: objects disappearing, people appearing/vanishing, strange cloud formations. More realistic detection would be subtler than a sign in the sky reading "Error: sky not found."
- **Evidence of consciousness (skeptic category)** — the objection that Bostrom's argument makes us simulated-only rather than conscious beings; really an objection to the NPC flavor, not simulation itself.
- **Evidence of negation (skeptic category)** — experiments/arguments purporting to prove we cannot live in a computer-generated simulation, usually by calculating that the required resources would be infinite or equal to the universe itself.
- **Evidence of conditional rendering (believer category)** — experiments looking for signs the universe renders only what is directly visible, the optimization technique of all 3D video games; typically variants of the (delayed-choice) double-slit experiment, some requiring an actual observer rather than just a measurement — implying players.
- **Evidence of pixels or computation (believer category)** — searching the physical structure of the universe for pixels, blurring of pixels, or other coding artifacts; includes digital physics, which treats the universe as computing information.
- **Digital physics** — the class of arguments that look at the universe as computing of information.
- **Epistemology** — questions about how we know things; Virk notes the West is conditioned to treat only repeatable-experiment scientific knowledge (a small fraction of human experience) as valid, dismissing the rest as subjective.
- **GZK (Greisen–Zatsepin–Kuzmin) limit** — a well-known cutoff in certain cosmic rays, a point at which nothing can be smaller; its source is unknown to high-energy physicists. Beane et al. theorize it reveals the "geometry" of the simulation — constraints imposed by lattice spacing.
- **Lattice** — the structure Beane proposes to look for: 3D pixels of a 3D simulated reality; lattice spacing would reveal the arrangement of the pixels of the real world.
- **Checksum** — bits added to a transmission indicating information about the original signal (e.g., a count of 1s), letting the receiver verify no errors occurred; the simple explanation for how computers check transmissions.
- **Doubly-even self-dual linear binary error-correcting block codes** — codes commonly used to remove errors in computer transmissions, which Gates found "quite unexpectedly" within the equations of supersymmetry.
- **Information entropy** — Shannon's measure of "disorder" in a set of bits: 8 bits of all 0s carry little information; random, incompressible bits carry more. The basis of compression and error detection, and of how the internet transmits information.
- **Mass-energy-information equivalence principle** — Vopson's 2019 proposal that information might have mass, so adding information to a device slightly increases its mass; he also calculated the amount of information in the universe.
- **Information as a fifth state of matter** — Vopson's 2022 proposal, alongside solids, gases, liquids, and plasma.
- **Second law of infodynamics** — Vopson's 2023 finding that, contrary to thermodynamic entropy increasing, information entropy in systems he analyzed (data storage devices, RNA genome sequences) *decreased* as the systems evolved — systems, including atomic arrangements, put themselves into symmetry to reduce information entropy.
- **Classical computing** — traditional digital computing on bits (zeros and ones) via transistors and Boolean logic gates (AND, NOT, OR) that take in bits and return a bit.
- **Qubits (quantum bits)** — superpositions of bits holding both 0 and 1 (like Schrödinger's cat both alive and dead) until observed; a string of qubits takes on every possible value simultaneously, enabling faster problem-solving than serial classical processing.
- **Quantum error correction** — because qubits are notorious for "flipping," error-correcting code was needed to make quantum computers possible; the simplest way to do it relies on quantum entanglement, so a flipped qubit can be found out and reversed.
- **Quantum entanglement** — the mysterious, unexplained correspondence of two or more particles no matter where they are located; confirmed to exist (non-locality proven), used in quantum cryptography, but the why and how are missing.
- **Quantum cryptography** — field relying on the property that collapsing the quantum field (reading/observing a particle or qubit) affects its entangled partner, revealing that a secret message has been "read" — like a sealed envelope whose broken seal betrays tampering.
- **De Sitter space** — the scaled-down "toy universe" model used by the Institute for Advanced Study researchers to show quantum error-correction codes may define the fabric of space-time.
- **Shared-memory pixel model of entanglement** — Virk's explanation: two pixels based on the same memory address change together when the value in memory changes; if pixel A starts using a different spot in memory they are "no longer entangled." De-coherence maps to pixel B getting its own value in memory.
- **Fractals** — per the Fractal Foundation, never-ending, infinitely complex patterns self-similar across scales, created by repeating a simple process in an ongoing feedback loop; they exhibit expanding/unfolding symmetry and describe rivers, trees, coastlines, neurons, and other natural processes better than Euclidean geometry. In the conclusion Virk notes fractal geometry has been called the "geometry of nature."
- **Recursion** — the computing technique behind fractals: a program calls itself to implement the solution at a smaller level of complexity.
- **Simple programs** — Wolfram's proposal (A New Kind of Science, 2002) that nature is a collection of computer programs and that simple programs are a basic building block of all science; science becomes the study of computation and information (recursion, registers, etc.).
- **Computational irreducibility** — the idea, popularized by Wolfram among others, that you have to "compute" in order to figure out the result — the whole basis for running simulations.
- **Hypergraphs** — diagrams in Wolfram's 2020 Physics Project which, with cellular-automata rules, he claims can derive fundamental aspects of the physical universe, including Einstein's relativity.
- **Conscious agents** — Hoffman's framework for how consciousness works, formulated after The Case Against Reality.
- **Desktop / VR-headset metaphors** — Hoffman's images for perception: we see file icons, not the bits on disk — useful representations, not reality; his later VR-headset metaphor (seeing a representation, not the bits) aligns closely with the simulation hypothesis — what Virk calls a "rendering of the information."
- **The Great Simulation** — Virk's name for the overall computer program driving the universal computation.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
1. **Marcus Noack (Lawrence Berkeley National Lab) — untestability position.** Believes it is impossible to test the simulation hypothesis as a whole; the best we may do is look for ways the simulation might work and detect artifacts, perhaps because lazy designers left clues. Virk uses him to open the "glitch-hunting" framing.
2. **"Error: sky not found" TV episode.** Producers of a TV series contacted Virk about an episode where a small town realizes it's simulated when that sign appears in the sky — a riff on video game graphics-loading errors. Virk's point: real detection would be far subtler.
3. **The Arizona lemon video (Today show).** A viral video of a woman cutting a lemon and finding it whole again; Virk was contacted by the Today show. His response: impossible to know if the (digitally manipulable, possibly AI-generated) video is genuine, but in a simulated world there could be one-off glitches that render "out-of-date" data. (Also: a whole subset of YouTube "glitch" videos — disappearing cars and planes, appearing/disappearing people, strange cloud formations.)
4. **Bostrom vs. Virk on glitches.** Bostrom dismisses such glitches as "hallucinations, psychiatric problems, visual illusions, self-deception, fraud, and so forth." Virk is not so dismissive: science advances by examining its fringes and trying to understand dismissed phenomena (per his previous chapter, the simulation could explain them).
5. **David Chalmers (NYU) — simulation ≠ unreality.** Even accepting Bostrom's statistical argument, the revelation isn't futile and doesn't make the world unreal: everything is still real from the inside. In *Reality+* he admits it may be impossible to tell if the simulation is "perfect." Bostrom likewise expects no experiment with 100 percent certainty, only evidence shifting likelihood.
6. **The metaphysical/consciousness aside.** The Buddha told followers to verify his teachings through their own meditation; Carl Jung believed psychic entities exist independent of physical consciousness and communication with them is possible; Tom Campbell participated in OBE research at the Monroe Institute, which pioneered the field. Many people have told Virk that DMT/psychedelic experiences convinced them we're in a simulation — including the son of a well-known Hollywood director (at a lunch in Hollywood, months after the first edition) who said he could see the grid lines of the simulation on DMT. Virk notes these echo near-death experiencers' claims of a world "more real" than this one, and critiques Western epistemology for dismissing non-repeatable experience.
7. **Ringel & Kovrizhin (Oxford) — the Hall effect resource argument.** Running simulations of the Hall effect (particles with magnetic fields), they reported in *Science Advances* that memory and information required grow exponentially with particle count, making large-particle simulation impractical for today's computers. Virk's rebuttals: they used Monte Carlo simulations (computing-intensive, not optimization-friendly), today's computers, and no allowance for optimization algorithms. Even lead author Ringel leaves the door open: "Who knows what are the computing capabilities of whatever simulates us?"
8. **Andrew Masterson (former Cosmos editor) — atoms-in-the-universe claim.** Reported that simulating even a few hundred particles would require more atoms than exist in the universe, concluding "fears that we might be unknowingly living in some vast version of The Matrix can now be put to rest." Virk treats this as premature.
9. **Rudy Rucker (computer science professor, SF writer) — "why bother?" argument.** A virtual Earth would require the same number of particles as the real Earth, so simulation is pointless; he too considers the simulation argument put to rest. Virk's reply spans the moon-rocket analogy (royal astronomers' fuel-weight impossibility claim, overturned by the Saturn V and modern rocketry) and pre-3D-graphics skepticism overturned by 3D modeling, textures, and conditional rendering.
10. **MLRO (Italian Space Agency's Matera Laser Ranging Observatory) — delayed choice at satellite distance.** Conducted a version of Wheeler's delayed-choice experiment in which a photon traveled thousands of miles to a satellite and back after passing the initial slits. Result: consistent with Wheeler's conclusion that observation of a particle — even clearly in the future — influenced what the particle did in the past. Virk counts this as one of the most interesting conditional-rendering experiments.
11. **Tom Campbell (former NASA physicist), Houman Owhadi (Caltech), et al. — the Kickstarter experiments.** Crowdfunded experiments to test the simulation hypothesis; their 2017 paper says it can certainly be tested, speculating they could show that a simulated universe was a system that, "as in a video game, render[s] content (reality) only at the moment the information becomes available for observation by a player and not at the moment of detection by a machine." Two proposed test routes: (1) test the moment of rendering; (2) exploit the conflict between logical-consistency preservation and detection avoidance to force rendering discontinuities or a measurable signature event. Experiments involve a quantum "eraser" or quantum "inserter" after the double slits; the aim is to show consciousness is fundamental to quantum theory, supporting Campbell's version of the hypothesis (we are all players in a computation-based world).
12. **CUSAC status update.** Virk visited Cal Poly Pomona (post first edition), where researchers were still setting up equipment for the first of the five proposed experiments. Campbell has since established CUSAC (Center for the Unification of Science and Consciousness); as of 2024 the first experiment is underway — results are proving complicated, but Campbell told Virk they're going in the direction he anticipated, with updates promised at conferences.
13. **QBism and per-player rendering.** Campbell et al.'s paper discusses QBism, an alternate interpretation of quantum mechanics allowing individual subjective waves rather than a single objective wave function. Further experiments might show the world is rendered per player — each player rendering on their own device (or "consciousness") — for which the video game/RPG framework may be the best model.
14. **Beane, Davoudi & Savage — "Constraints on the Universe as a Numerical Simulation" (2012, University of Bonn).** Proposed looking for a "lattice" representing 3D pixels and watching how it changes over time; in a simulation time would go in discrete steps (clock speed) versus physics' continuous equations. Certain cosmic rays display a small lattice with a cutoff — the GZK limit — whose source is unknown; they theorize it reveals the simulation's geometry (lattice-spacing constraints). Studying angular distribution of the smallest lattices, they found energy flowing between lattices in certain directions — reminiscent of adjacent pixels in a rendered image.
15. **James Gates (University of Maryland) — error-correcting codes in supersymmetry.** Studying string theory and supersymmetry, claims to have found the equivalent of "error-checking codes" in the physical universe — specifically "doubly-even self-dual linear binary error-correcting block codes" within supersymmetry equations (2016 video). These are the codes (of the Shannon/Hamming lineage) used to remove transmission errors on the internet. Gates: this suggests the codes "may be ubiquitous in nature and could even be embedded in the essence of reality," giving us "something in common with The Matrix." Virk supplies the checksum walkthrough: send `0100 1001`, count the 1s (3 = binary 11), append a 4-bit checksum `0011` for a 12-bit message; context: dial-up modem errors and TCP/IP packets crossing many lines and computers.
16. **Melvin Vopson (University of Portsmouth) — the information trilogy.** 2019: mass-energy-information equivalence principle (information has mass; calculated the universe's information content). 2022: information as a fifth state of matter. 2023: experiments formulating/validating the "second law of infodynamics and its implications for the simulated universe" — while thermodynamic entropy increases, information entropy in data storage devices and RNA genome sequences *decreased* as systems evolved, with systems arranging into symmetry. Vopson believes this is very strong evidence we could be living in a simulation. Virk's illustration of information entropy: a Game of Thrones winter scene is mostly predictable white pixels, so it compresses — the basis of internet streaming.
17. **Quantum computing as counter-evidence to the skeptics.** Feynman first proposed quantum computers, suggesting that to simulate the physical universe we should use quantum particles, not classical computers, as computing elements; early working models now exist. Since nature already has particles with quantum properties (photons, electrons), building qubits means measuring them. Virk's inference: because quantum indeterminacy is a feature of reality, a simulation must process uncertainty and probability waves and force collapse on observation; quantum computers show fundamental particles are actually information, and even a particle-for-particle simulation would suggest "the physical universe is most likely a supersophisticated quantum computer." (Also: just as we built GPUs for rendering, a civilization nearing the simulation point would build processors optimized for ancestor simulations.)
18. **Almheiri, Dong & Harlow (Institute for Advanced Study, Princeton — Einstein and von Neumann's former center) — error correction as space-time.** Virk introduces this as the possible overlap between Einstein's general relativity (which gives an explanation for gravity) and quantum mechanics (the behavior of subatomic particles) — a century-old mystery with no evidence uniting the two until recently. Discovered that quantum error-correction codes not only exist but, in their simulated worlds, may define the fabric of space-time itself. Using a scaled-down de Sitter space model, entanglement across particles let them reconstruct particle locations without needing all the information — optimization built into space-time, "very similar to how computer graphics are processed and optimized." Extended to our universe, error-correcting codes could be the "smoking gun."
19. **Entanglement confirmations.** Einstein (with Rosen and Podolsky) coauthored the original paper yet derided entanglement as "spooky action at a distance." Since then: quantum cryptography exploits collapse-detection (sealed-envelope analogy); Norbert Kalb et al. (2017) demonstrated enhanced entanglement between an actual particle and a qubit, confirmed at two meters' distance; the 2022 Nobel Prize in Physics went to researchers who explored entanglement and essentially proved "non-locality." Whether information travels faster than light, and the mechanism, remain hotly debated — but entanglement and non-locality are no longer in question. What's missing is the why and how.
20. **The shared-memory-pixel explanation of entanglement (Virk's model).** Rendered pixels reflect values in memory; video games and image compression let many pixels (e.g., the black pixels of a night sky) share one memory location, keeping a tally rather than a per-pixel grid. Pixels A and B sharing an address change together; A switching to a different memory spot = no longer entangled; de-coherence (interaction with environment, or measurement) = pixel B getting its own independent value. The *why* is optimization: streaming Game of Thrones is only possible because redundant pixels are compressed. Vopson's related explanation: entangled particles must both be the same distance from a CPU or other processing unit for a change in one to affect the other.
21. **Fractals and the coastline.** Mandelbrot found self-similarity across scales in telephone-line error codes, commodity prices, and coastlines. "How long is a coastline?" depends on measurement scale — nooks and crannies all the way down (until atoms). Fractal iteration feeds each equation's output into the next run; the Mandelbrot set wasn't fully fleshed out until after the personal computer — not coincidental, says the Fractal Foundation, since fractals need thousands or millions of iterations and are ideally suited to computers. Straight lines rarely exist in nature; Euclidean geometry describes it poorly, while rivers, trees, neurons, and other biological processes follow fractal patterns (Figure 36). On-screen fractals are only approximations, limited by pixel resolution. Virk's question: could fractal geometry be the best evidence nature itself is undergoing computation — a fractal-generating computer?
22. **Stephen Wolfram — A New Kind of Science (2002) and the Physics Project (2020).** The chief designer of Mathematica proposed, from studying cellular automata's similarity to nature, that nature might be a collection of "simple programs" — making science the study of computation and information. Controversial then, less far out now; computer science may become fundamental the way mathematics was. He popularized computational irreducibility (you must compute to get the result — the basis of simulation) and, in the Wolfram Physics Project, claims hypergraphs plus cellular-automata rules can derive fundamentals like Einstein's relativity. Virk: the implication is strong that we may be inside a universal computer program — the Great Simulation. (He notes he goes deeper on cellular automata, fractals, and quantum computing in *The Simulated Multiverse*.)
23. **Donald Hoffman — The Case Against Reality (2019).** The UC Irvine cognitive scientist (formerly at MIT) argues natural selection does not necessarily favor the species that can see actual reality; we evolve to perceive information useful to survival. His desktop metaphor: file icons don't exist in nature — a file is bits as electrical signals on disk — but icons and word processors are useful representations. Virk interviewed him for his podcast; Hoffman has since formulated "conscious agents" and upgraded the metaphor to a VR headset: you see not the bits but a representation — Virk's "rendering of the information" — aligning closely with the simulation hypothesis. Builds on the idea that space-time doesn't really exist but represents some fundamental reality.

## Thread
The chapter runs a courtroom structure: first the question (can we detect the simulation at all? — Noack says no full test; Bostrom expects no 100-percent experiment; Chalmers says it wouldn't make things unreal anyway), then a taxonomy — two argument families against (consciousness-based, resource-based) mirrored by two evidence families for (conditional rendering, pixels/computation). Virk pauses to bracket the mystical route (meditation, Jung, Monroe Institute, DMT grid lines) as verifiable-by-experience but out of scope, keeping this chapter scientific. He then dismantles the skeptics: the consciousness objection only kills the NPC flavor (the RPG flavor absorbs it via downloadable consciousness), and the resource objection repeats the moon-rocket fallacy by assuming no optimization and no quantum computing. The believers' evidence then escalates from rendering (MLRO delayed choice, Campbell/CUSAC, QBism per-player rendering) to pixels (Beane's GZK lattice) to computation (Gates's error codes, Vopson's infodynamics), then into the quantum-computing deep end (qubits, error correction as the fabric of space-time at IAS, entanglement as shared memory), and finally zooms out to the macro world (fractals, Wolfram's simple programs, Hoffman's evolutionary interface). The verdict withholds proof but stacks the inference: everything physical is information and computation — the Great Simulation.

  - **My Big TOE (Campbell)** — the Tom Campbell featured here (CUSAC, Monroe Institute OBE research, consciousness-as-fundamental) is the same author; his "render at the moment of observation by a player" claim is the experimental arm of My Big TOE's virtual-reality cosmology.
  - **The Holographic Universe (Talbot)** — the IAS finding that some particles' information reconstructs the rest of space-time echoes Talbot's holographic principle of the whole encoded in parts; non-locality/entanglement is central to both.
  - **American Cosmic (Pasulka)** — Virk's "science advances by looking at its fringes" stance and his refusal to dismiss anomalous experience parallels Pasulka's serious treatment of dismissed phenomena.
  - **Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance (Pirsig)** — Virk's critique of Western epistemology (repeatable-experiment knowledge as "a small fraction of the human experience") rhymes with Pirsig's challenge to the subject–object split in Western rationality.
  - **The Power of Now (Tolle)** — the consciousness-is-primary camp (Planck, Goswami) that Virk folds into the RPG flavor sits close to Tolle's claim that consciousness precedes form.
