# Ch 11 — Multiple Lives and Karma as Quests in Video Games

## In one paragraph

Virk argues that the Eastern doctrines of reincarnation and karma — shared by Hinduism, Buddhism, and Jainism — map with striking precision onto the architecture of modern MMORPGs, and that the simulation hypothesis therefore supplies a "modern explanation for how the doctrine of reincarnation and its underlying mechanism of karma might actually work." Just as a player outside the rendered game world enters it to play a persistent character across many gameplay sessions, a consciousness outside the simulation is "downloaded" into successive bodies, carrying with it karma — information stored outside the rendered world, like character data in the cloud. The chapter's centerpiece is a theoretical "quest engine for karma": an AI-driven algorithm that scans the karmic rosters of players whose characters share a scene, generates possible quests to resolve past karmic debts, offers them to the characters (who may accept or reject, preserving free will), and feeds the results back into each player's quest manifest as new or resolved karma. On this reading, Buddha's endless wheel of samsara is literally an algorithm, the Lords of Karma are "a sophisticated AI," and — Virk's strongest claim — the workings of karma as the Eastern traditions describe them "may only be possible in a simulated world."

## Core claims

- Virk argues that the video-game metaphor of a player outside the rendered world who enters it to play a character "fits very well with the ancient traditions": each character played is like a life lived "in-world," real and singular from the character's own point of view, and the metaphor works in both frameworks precisely because part of each character exists outside the game world — the player.
- Virk argues that the simulation hypothesis "does a very good job of providing a modern explanation" for how reincarnation and its underlying mechanism, karma, "might actually work" — specifically, that the RPG flavor of the simulation hypothesis "may provide a techno-scientific basis for the worldview expressed in Hinduism and Buddhism."
- Virk claims that ancient texts routinely used technological metaphors of their era (e.g., the wheel — a foremost ancient technology — for samsara) to explain complex, seemingly supernatural ideas to general populations, and that we can and should do the same today, updating those metaphors with algorithms, AI, and cloud servers to "add more nuance, mechanism, and complexity to a rational understanding of processes that previously have been dismissed as being beyond our comprehension."
- Virk argues that karma functions as "a storage of the results of our actions in the past, whether in this life or in previous lives," a "vault" or "information bank" drawn upon to create present and future experiences — which raises two standard computer-science questions: what information is stored and where, and how/when is it used or processed?
- Virk argues that no metaphysical beings ("Lords of Karma") are needed for karma to work: the same process could be accomplished with algorithms and artificial intelligence — an intelligent algorithm that is "really just a collection of rules—so, very manageable through computer code."
- Virk claims consciousness, as described in the Buddhist tradition (Ricard's "radio wave" that transmits information without being concrete), "is a type of information" — paralleling how computer science stores and transmits information as electromagnetic waves.
- Virk argues that entering a life resembles character creation: consciousness transmits from "base reality" outside the simulation into "a somewhat artificially generated body," whose nature — family relations, ethnicity, etc. — is chosen and "encoded in its DNA." He names this "life selection," part of / precursor to incarnation or "ensoulment."
- Virk claims the purpose of playing another life is to finish the quests (karmic elements) built up over time, leveling up the character according to a map or tree of quests "selected for us by our particular karma over multiple lives."
- Virk claims that although a simulation tracking seven billion humans (plus potentially other worlds and souls) sounds beyond any game built to date, "recent advancements in both information processing and artificial intelligence show that it is not out of our grasp."
- Virk's strongest claim (stated as such): "we can go beyond the idea of metaphors of gods and say that the only way they could be implemented would be in some form of simulation that we download consciousness into and become a part of" — i.e., karma as the Eastern traditions understand it "may only be possible in a simulated world" driven by a questing engine or algorithm.
- Virk notes that free will survives in this model: each player might put off a particular interaction or choose to go elsewhere in the world, bringing up alternate quests from previous karma or entirely new karma.

## Concepts

- **Reincarnation (rebirth / transmigration)** — Doctrine shared by the Indian religions (Hinduism, Buddhism, Jainism) that each soul or consciousness goes through multiple lives, "learning lessons and having adventures (as well as misadventures), creating and fulfilling its karma during each life." Virk notes a more literal translation of the Sanskrit would be "rebirth" or "transmigration," but "reincarnation" has stuck in English.
- **Karma** — Literally "action" or "deeds" in Sanskrit; the consequences of one's thoughts and actions. Every karmic action is "like a seed that is planted and will then grow to eventually be resolved"; karma is the source of future experience and the force that keeps the wheel of samsara turning. Per the Bhagavad Gita, karma is "literally the force of creation, which brings all things to life." Expressed in modern terms as the law of cause and effect.
- **Samsara (Wheel of Life)** — Buddhist representation of reincarnation as an endless wheel; Sanskrit for "wandering," representing the soul's wandering across many lifetimes (Figure 32 in the book). Virk reads the wheel itself as a technological metaphor of ancient times.
- **Moksha** — Traditional Hinduism's goal: liberation from the endless cycle of rebirth, breaking the spell of maya (discussed in the previous chapter); "the purpose of life is to transcend our karma."
- **Nirvana / enlightenment** — The equivalent process in Buddhism, which Virk notes sprang out of Hinduism based on the teachings of Siddhārtha Gautama at some time between the fourth and sixth centuries BCE: "waking up," realizing the interdependence of all things and of karma, and finally transcending it. Per Capra, equivalent to moksha, "a state of consciousness beyond all intellectual concepts" that "defies further description" — hence ineffable, hence the critical role of metaphor.
- **Tulku** — In Tibetan traditions, a higher, more evolved being who chooses to come into the world in order to help others evolve — the exception to karma-driven rebirth.
- **Lords of Karma** — The texts' metaphorical name for "abstract spiritual beings responsible for the creation of these future situations to help us resolve our past karma." In Virk's model they are "reduced to a sophisticated AI."
- **Akashic records** — A repository of information existing outside the individual and outside the rendered world, storing "not just our karma but the actual events of every single past life." Per Twitchell, "these soul records consist of the past incarnations on the physical, astral, causal planes."
- **Subtle body** — The nonvisible analog of the physical body; per Ashley-Farrand, one candidate location where karma is stored (a "distributed architecture" in Virk's computer-science reading). In many Eastern traditions and new-age updates, subtle bodies containing consciousness leave the physical body during sleep — which Virk maps to being AFK.
- **Bardo** — The "other side" from which the Eastern mystics believed consciousness is downloaded into a physical, biological entity (our bodies).
- **Soul vs. "bundle of cause and effect" ("bag of karma")** — Major Hindu/Buddhist difference: Hinduism posits an indivisible soul that goes through multiple lives seeking learning and liberation; in Buddhism the "thing that reincarnates" is not a soul but "a bundle of cause and effect," akin to the information of the reincarnated consciousness. Virk uses "soul" to cover both scenarios.
- **Consciousness as information** — Virk's reading of Ricard's radio-wave description: the wave of consciousness contains information — our experiences to date — serving as seeds of future experience; "consciousness is a type of information."
- **Multiple lives (video games)** — One of the first concepts in modern video games, introduced initially for economic reasons (a quarter bought only so many lives of Pac-Man or Space Invaders); later superseded by persistent characters in RPGs.
- **Character sheet / leveling up** — RPG characters (from Dungeons & Dragons onward) defined by attributes (intelligence, constitution, strength), race (elf, dwarf, human), and class (rogue, wizard, fighter), recorded on a character sheet and updated as the character levels up; the character persists across gameplay sessions and campaigns.
- **The cloud** — Silicon Valley term for "a server that you cannot see," possibly not even a single server, where character and game-state information persists outside the rendered world across server instances and sessions.
- **In-world / IRL / AFK** — States of play: "in-world" (in the simulated virtual world / in-game); "IRL" ("in real life"), a gamer acronym conveying information about the player rather than the character; "AFK" ("away from keyboard"), logged in but absent, with the avatar in a special state signaling others not to engage.
- **Quests and achievements** — Concrete tasks that guide the player ("fight an orc and win," "build a house," "find the treasure map"); evolved from simple lists into treelike structures with prerequisites (can't kill the goblin king until you've completed "find the goblin map"). In multiplayer games, accepted quests might involve working with a group of other players to accomplish a goal (beat a boss, find a treasure, go on a raid).
- **Quest manifest** — The per-character instance of the quest tree: a running list of which quests the player has accomplished, "signed up" for, and not yet reached; a subset of all possible quests, run by a questing engine.
- **Quest engine (questing engine)** — A subset of the game code defining the player's path through the game world; even seemingly open worlds only allow possibilities "encoded into the game and allowed by its underlying quest engine." In Virk's model, it is "the elusive link" between Eastern ideas of reality-creation and how video games already work.
- **Life selection** — Virk's coined term for the process by which the character (avatar / body), family relations, ethnicity, etc. are chosen and encoded in DNA before play; "both part of and a precursor to the playing of the game," analogous to incarnation or ensoulment.
- **Karmic debt / karmic roster / karmic friends** — A pending obligation between players (possibly from a different lifetime) that the quest engine detects by scanning players' karmic rosters (quest manifests); "karmic friends" are the group of souls we encounter again and again across lives (who may or may not be actual friends).
- **The Great Simulation** — Virk's name for the sophisticated game that would need to track not just a generic quest database but individual accomplishments per character-life and a store of past karma (quests involving other specific players).
- **Lila** — The "dramatic stage play" of the Eastern traditions, "a magical play that creates our illusion," the lila of the Vedas — to which the simulation hypothesis gives "new color and meaning," alongside Shakespeare's stage line.

## Experiments, examples & case studies

1. **Yogananda's multiple-lives epigraph** — The chapter opens with Paramahansa Yogananda's sequence: a man born in France as a powerful king, reborn in India traveling by bullock cart into the forest to meditate, then reborn in America as a successful businessman, then reincarnated in Tibet as a devotee of Buddha spending his life in a lamasery — "Each existence is a dream within a dream, is it not?" Sets the frame that Virk will translate into player-and-character terms.
2. **Reincarnation's reach across traditions** — Doctrine shared by Hinduism, Buddhism, and Jainism, with Western adherents including Plato in ancient Greek times and Ralph Waldo Emerson "among many others" in more modern times. Virk uses this to establish reincarnation as a widespread doctrine, not a fringe idea.
3. **The wheel of samsara as ancient technological metaphor (Figure 32)** — The Buddhist Wheel of Life; Virk stresses that the wheel was one of the most important technologies of ancient times, so the "wheel of samsara" was itself a technological metaphor for something complex and hard to envision — evidence for his thesis that ancient texts used period technology as metaphor and that we can update those metaphors today.
4. **Two modern definitions of karma** — Virk quotes two: (a) "In Hinduism and Buddhism, the sum of a person's actions in this and previous states of existence, viewed as deciding their fate in future existences"; (b) "Karma refers to the spiritual principle of cause and effect where intent and actions of an individual (cause) influence the future of that individual (effect)." He uses them to distill karma's shared purpose: a storage ("vault" / "information bank") of past results drawn upon to create experience.
5. **The Lords of Karma** — The ancient texts' metaphorical answer to how future circumstances get created: abstract spiritual beings who build future situations to help resolve past karma. Virk's stated project in the chapter is to test whether any metaphysical beings are needed at all, or whether algorithms and AI could do the same job.
6. **The tulku exception** — Per Buddhism and Hinduism, one is reborn because of karma; without karma there would be no reason for rebirth — "unless as a higher, more evolved being who wishes to come into the world in order to help others evolve (referred to as a tulku in Tibetan traditions)."
7. **Fritjof Capra, *The Tao of Physics*** — Cited as a precedent for using "different modern metaphors to explain complicated ancient concepts"; quoted equating nirvana and moksha as the state of total liberation beyond all intellectual concepts. Virk draws from this the ineffability point: because moksha/nirvana can't be put into words, "metaphors are so critical in conveying the essence of these ideas, both to ancient humans and to modern humans."
8. **Paul Twitchell / Eckankar's school metaphor** — Twitchell, founder of the modern-day religious movement Eckankar (which he says is based on ancient Indian and Tibetan practices), expresses the philosophy in contemporary terms: "We are like children who attend school to get simulated experiences to prepare ourselves for places in society." Virk calls the classroom-of-simulated-experiences metaphor powerful and notes it "fits within the framework of the simulation hypothesis quite closely" (pointing to the book's FAQ for possible purposes of the simulation).
9. **The Bhagavad Gita on karma** — "According to the Bhagavad Gita, karma is literally the force of creation, which brings all things to life." Used to introduce the storage/mechanism question the ancient texts leave underexplained.
10. **Thomas Ashley-Farrand, *Healing Mantras* (trigger mechanism)** — Quoted: "As we go through life, the people we encounter or circumstances we are met with will trigger individual bits of karma, releasing them and bringing them into play. When this happens we are presented with an opportunity to work off that particular portion of our karma." Virk uses this as the closest textual account of karma's runtime behavior.
11. **The gross Western revenge example** — "If person X kills person Y in one life, then Y will get revenge on X in a future life." Virk's point: even this simplistic view implies information stored somewhere plus a future situation engineered so Y can resolve the karma — i.e., a goal-bound interaction, which he later reframes as a quest.
12. **Figure 33 — a theoretical model for reincarnation** — Built from "standard parts" of the philosophies (which Virk notes are remarkably consistent in structure, purpose, and mechanism despite doctrinal differences): a cycle of death and rebirth (samsara) in which one enters the ongoing simulation with a different character/personality but carries traces from previous lives — karma — which becomes the basis of experience in the new life. Virk then poses the two computer-science questions: what information is stored and where; how and when is it processed?
13. **Ashley-Farrand on karma stored in the body (distributed architecture)** — *Healing Mantras* stipulates karma is stored in the physical body or its nonvisible analog, the subtle body. Virk: "This would be akin to a distributed architecture and might make sense when we think about billions of souls, each responsible for keeping track of its own karma."
14. **The Akashic records (centralized architecture)** — The alternative: a repository outside the individual and outside the rendered world, storing our karma and "the actual events of every single past life." Twitchell quoted: "these soul records consist of the past incarnations on the physical, astral, causal planes." Together with #13 this gives Virk the centralized-vs-distributed storage duality (later: "centralized or decentralized ledger").
15. **Kurzweil parallel — downloading consciousness** — "Just as Kurzweil and others in modern times believe that we can download consciousness from our brains into a silicon device, so the Eastern mystics who pioneered the reincarnation philosophy also believed in a downloading of consciousness from the 'other side' (the bardo) to a physical, biological entity (namely, our bodies)." Virk notes his model visualizes this download while steering clear of defining what consciousness actually is.
16. **Matthieu Ricard's radio-wave description of consciousness** — Ricard, "a well-known Buddhist monk who served as translator for the Dalai Lama," describes the succession of states a consciousness passes through as comparable to a radio wave "which transmits information but without itself being concrete," an individual's future lying in the transformations of this wave, which "contains all of our experiences in this life and in our past lives in an infinitely complex web." Virk: computer science stores and transmits information as electromagnetic waves — "this sounds remarkably similar to what the Buddhists are describing: In other words, consciousness is a type of information."
17. **Hindu soul vs. Buddhist "bag of karma"** — Doctrinal difference over what transmigrates: Hinduism's indivisible soul on a quest for learning and liberation vs. Buddhism's "bundle of cause and effect." Virk declares he will use "soul" to cover both an actual indestructible soul and a temporary entity associated with a bag of karma.
18. **Arcade multiple lives — Pac-Man and Space Invaders** — Multiple lives entered video games "well after" the Eastern doctrine, initially for economic reasons: "a quarter in an arcade would buy you only so many 'lives.'" Virk explicitly says it's not clear the original gaming moniker had any connection to the Eastern traditions — the analogy is structural, not historical.
19. **Dungeons & Dragons and the RPG character** — Tabletop RPGs established the persistent character: attributes (intelligence, constitution, strength, etc.), race (elf, dwarf, human, etc.), class (rogue, wizard, fighter, etc.), recorded on a character sheet, updated on leveling up; a wizard learns more sophisticated spells, a warrior new weapons (e.g., a crossbow). "The whole point of an RPG was that you would play the role of the character," persisting across sessions and campaigns.
20. **Second Life and World of Warcraft — persistent virtual lives** — As RPGs became MMORPGs, characters and their avatars became more persistent: large inventories of possessions, accomplishments, backstories, friends lists — "with information about them stored outside the rendered world." Virk's key structural parallel to karma stored outside the rendered world.
21. **Character state in the cloud; queued messages** — When not logged in, your character is not rendered but "still exists…somewhere": in the cloud, possibly on a different server each session, with information persisting across server instances. Private messages sent to an offline character queue outside the rendered world and are read when back "in-world." Virk itemizes the separate data sets about a PC: player/account info, character/avatar, attributes and possessions, and quest/accomplishment progress.
22. **AFK and IRL as mystic states** — AFK avatars appear in a special state signaling others not to engage; "IRL" asks about the player rather than the character. Later, Virk maps AFK to the Eastern/new-age belief that subtle bodies containing consciousness leave the physical body during sleep — "This is how mystics explain many unanswered aspects of dreams—interactions with the departed, with angels, or with other entities that are not in the rendered world."
23. **Second Life's flexibility problem vs. WoW's exclamation-point NPC** — Virtual worlds like Second Life were "almost too flexible"; new players who could do anything didn't know what to do next. In World of Warcraft, a newly created character meets an NPC with an exclamation point over its head offering the first quest. Virk uses this to show why quests structure experience — the design rationale that karma plays in a life.
24. **Quest trees and prerequisites — the goblin king** — Quests evolved from simple lists into treelike structures with prerequisites ("you can't go kill the goblin king until you have completed 'find the goblin map'"); quests feed into other quests; larger game worlds give the player agency over which region to visit next, changing the set of available quests. Each game stores a per-character instance: the quest manifest, run by a questing engine.
25. **Open worlds as illusions of endless possibility** — Second Life, World of Warcraft, and Grand Theft Auto "may have the illusion of endless possibilities," but the only real possibilities are those encoded into the game and allowed by its quest engine — Virk's bridge to how reality could feel open while being karma-constrained.
26. **Figure 34 — the consciousness/karma simulation model** — Virk's simulation-spin version of Figure 33: consciousness transmits from base reality into an artificially generated body via "life selection" (character, family relations, ethnicity chosen and encoded in DNA); player and character are associated for the length of the character's life; purpose is to finish quests/karmic elements and level up per a quest tree selected by one's karma over multiple lives. He argues today's designer-limited quest lists could scale, via AI-generated quests, to "as many different quests or accomplishments as there are players," tracked in a cloud server outside the rendered world (achieved quests, pending quests, and future quests already committed to, alone or with other players), flexible enough to create new entries from the player's experiences, in-game actions, and even thoughts and feelings. The ledger would be "the computer science equivalent of the karma of a given player" — with player-level karma (lessons across many lifetimes) distinguished from character-level karma (to be resolved within this life).
27. **Figure 35 — the quest-generation engine walkthrough (the chapter's central mechanism)** — Characters X1, X2, X3 are present in the Current Scene, played by players A, B, C. Each player has karma storage from previous lives plus this-life tasks (quest manifest). The engine scans player A's and player B's karmic rosters for a karmic debt or a possible interaction to their karmic benefit (or detriment, "depending on how you interpret interactions in the real world") — the debt may be from a different lifetime but still involves A and B, even though they now play X1 and X2. Using an intelligent algorithm ("really just a collection of rules—so, very manageable through computer code"), it generates a possible quest and offers it to X1 and X2, either of whom can accept or reject. If both accept, the karma-mandated situation is created: "a friendship, a violent interaction, a sexual interaction, or a casual meeting of minds and ideas," or something as fleeting as "a simple smile from one character on a day that the other character was feeling down." Results are recorded and loop back into both players' quest manifests — eliminating fulfilled karma and/or creating new karma as the seed of future actions in this or a future life.
28. **Buddha's endless wheel as algorithm; Lords of Karma as AI** — In this scenario samsara "is now a computer algorithm that stores karma created in-game and unceasingly creates additional situations in future gameplay sessions to satisfy past karma with other players who are in the vicinity"; the Lords of Karma become an AI tracking billions of players, generating quests "in real time, dynamically, depending on where in the rendered world they are." Player combinations are so numerous you might go several lifetimes without re-encountering a given player — or keep playing with your "friend group" (the karmic friends of the Eastern traditions). Free will is preserved: players can defer interactions or relocate, surfacing alternate quests.
29. **The astute reader's refinement — third-party karma resolution** — After Virk published this theoretical model, "one astute reader pointed out that not all existing karma need be resolved with the exact same player": karma accumulated between A and B might be resolved in a future life between A and C. The same framework works — the possible quest is generated with C (who is in A's scene) instead of B — "This makes it much easier to both create and resolve karma more quickly than might otherwise happen."
30. **Scale feasibility — seven billion players** — Virk anticipates the objection that storage and processing for a simulation of seven billion humans (plus "potentially numerous other worlds and souls out there in the universe") exceeds any video game built to date, and counters that recent advances in information processing and AI "show that it is not out of our grasp."
31. **Lila and Shakespeare's stage** — Closing example: the simulation hypothesis "gives new color and meaning to both the idea of a magical play that creates our illusion, the lila of the Vedas, and Shakespeare's famous line 'All the world's a stage, and all the men and women merely players.'"

## Thread

The chapter runs a sustained two-column translation exercise. It opens with Yogananda's dream-within-a-dream epigraph and the observation that "multiple lives" in video games postdates — and has no clear historical connection to — the Eastern doctrine, yet the player/character structure fits it exactly because part of every character exists outside the game world. Virk then lays out the Eastern column: reincarnation across Hinduism/Buddhism/Jainism, samsara as an (already technological) wheel metaphor, karma as seed and stored cause-and-effect, moksha/nirvana as the ineffable exit, and the underspecified mechanism question — where is karma stored and how does it create situations? (candidate answers: body/subtle body = distributed, Akashic records = centralized; what transmigrates: Hindu soul vs. Buddhist bag of karma; Ricard's radio wave → consciousness is information). He then builds the gaming column: multiple lives, D&D character sheets, MMORPG persistence, cloud-stored character state, AFK/IRL states, and the quest system culminating in the quest manifest and quest engine. The synthesis is Figures 33→34→35: life selection as character creation, karma as the player's ledger, and a worked quest-generation engine (scene scan → karmic roster match → offered quest → accept/reject → feedback into both manifests), refined by a reader's point that karma with B can be resolved via C. The conclusion escalates from "good metaphor" to "may only be possible in a simulated world," recasting the wheel as algorithm, the Lords of Karma as AI, and life as the lila / Shakespeare's stage.
