# The Blanche DuBois Rule

## In one paragraph
In this chapter (numbered 9 in the book), Tom DeLonge & Levenda argue that conventional war planning is useless against the Alien because — unlike every terrestrial adversary — it has no discernible origin, homeland, supply lines, or known vulnerabilities, leaving humanity able only to invoke "the Blanche DuBois rule (from 1947)" and "depend on the kindness of strangers." Opening under a Paulo Freire epigraph about domination-as-invasion, the authors run the Aztec/conquistador analogy to show how total the asymmetry is, then work through a series of headed sub-sections — Death, The Alien Invasion, Treaties, Territory, Communication, Probes — each testing a standard category of warfare against the Alien and finding it inapplicable. They call for thinking "outside the box": integrating consciousness studies (as the SRI programs of the 1960s and Russian, Chinese, and American efforts once did), mapping the planet geophysically and culturally, and looking "perpendicular" to our three dimensions, per Vallée's projector metaphor and the Flatland analogy. The chapter's stakes are cultural as much as military: the mere presence of the Other, without any invasion, would devalue human identity and everything we know. It closes with the authors' conclusion that the UFOs/UAPs are not probes gathering information — they already know everything — but are training us, accustoming us to their presence: "Mystification machines. Sekret machines."

## Core claims
- The authors claim no earthly government or military can plan for conflict with an alien force because, unlike every human enemy, the Alien has no identifiable origin, language, culture, ideology, or location — we cannot invade it, disrupt its supply lines, spy on its bases, intercept its communications, or seize its assets, and "it's entirely possible we can't even kill them."
- They argue that fictional victories (The War of the Worlds, Independence Day) rest on a fallacy: even if a battle were "won," we could never know whether the defeated force was merely a reconnaissance mission for vastly larger forces waiting in the wings.
- The book says the Aztec encounter with the conquistadores is the closest historical analogue — but the one saving grace then (both sides were human, equally vulnerable) is absent with the Alien, whose vulnerability to weapons, weaknesses, or earthly elements is unknown.
- The authors argue we cannot "catch up" to Alien science by ordinary methods; they hesitate even to call it "advanced," suggesting it may simply reflect a different approach to the science we already study, and note the Alien seems untroubled by the grand unified theory we still lack.
- They claim the problem requires integrating consciousness studies — resuming the kind of paranormal-oriented research Russia, China, and the United States have all funded (from the 1960s SRI programs onward) — but this time without the "medieval horrors" of demons and 'spirits of the air' that sabotaged such studies repeatedly.
- They argue this is "an intelligence problem" and may not be a science problem: if the Phenomenon is a "control mechanism," scientists are studying the projected images rather than the projector — "Plato's cave, but for the twenty-first century."
- The book claims the real stake is the devaluation of human culture: an actual invasion is unnecessary; the mere presence of the Other (citing the Wendt and Duvall sovereignty paper) would undermine confidence in our identity, values, and sense of what it means to be human.
- The authors ask readers to stop and look at their own surroundings — the streets, buildings, sky, trees — and ask how that perception will change once the Alien's presence is unequivocally known: will we see our environment as "ashen, gray, and hopelessly primitive," our cities as prison camps, or will we one day look back on this life with nostalgia, wondering that anything so perfect could have existed? They say how we will see the past depends on what we do now.
- The Alien is a threat "even if the Alien doesn't know it or intend it," the authors write — it causes us to question our values, our reality, and our sense of self and of what it means to be human.
- The authors argue the Tic Tacs are already forcing a rethink of military, political, and corporate strategy into a global context — an argument not for one-world government but for universal collaboration.
- They claim the Alien has no need to invade because humanity is "doing its work for it" — the planet is already a battlefield of wars, genocide, displaced peoples, and weapons of mass destruction.
- On death, the authors ask whether the Alien knows death as we do, whether it has knowledge of "life after death," whether its lifespan is shorter or longer than ours, whether it decays after death or "evaporates" its dead somehow, and why no alien bodies have ever been found; they argue that if the Alien does not experience death, loss, and its horror as we do, it cannot be expected to value our lives; alternatively it may possess knowledge about death and survival (reincarnation, heaven and hell) that we lack — or be as ignorant as we are, which is potentially more disturbing.
- Under "The Alien Invasion," the book asserts the invasion has already happened: "They're here. They've been here" — and suggests our gaze is the problem; we should look not up or down but "across," perpendicular to the familiar three dimensions.
- They claim no peace treaty with the Alien is possible: treaties assume communication, mutual concepts (peace, war, agreement, conditions), and a degree of equivalence, none of which we share with the Alien; an "invasion" might instead resemble contamination — a virus, a bacterium, or insects living unnoticed in the walls of our home.
- On territory, they argue national boundaries are meaningless to the Alien; the conflict must be reframed planetarily — mapping ocean floors, fault lines, the Ring of Fire, magnetic anomalies, weather correlations, and every nuclear installation (including submarines) against the record of alien appearances to find patterns.
- They propose the Alien may be an organic Phenomenon peculiar to Earth — an "ultraterrestrial" coresident or natural Phenomenon — a possibility that transcends both the nuts-and-bolts approach and the purely speculative "outer space" approach; alternatively it may be a species from elsewhere with unknowable intentions, requiring exhaustive study by experts from diverse fields and backgrounds, including the humanities — a diversity the authors insist on "not as a gesture of 'wokeness'" but because no one racial or ethnic group has a monopoly on understanding the Alien.
- Since no hidden terrestrial city has ever been found and the Alien seems "rather helpless on land" (no wheels, no driving, only "crashes" bring it to solid ground), the authors conclude these machines do not come from the Earth's surface.
- On communication, they claim military interactions (US East/West Coast pilots, former Soviet Union reports) show the Alien is conscious of us, interacts primitively to demonstrate that consciousness, and — most dangerously — can know our intentions in advance.
- They argue the probe hypothesis fails: after centuries (at minimum since the twentieth-century "foo fighters") of watching our energy, ballistics, aeronautics, atomic bombs, and wars, there is nothing left for the Alien to find out — an adversary that knows our F-16s' CAP rendezvous points and can switch our nuclear missile silos off and on at will is not gauging our technology.
- Their conclusion: the Alien is not probing us but training us — accustoming us to its presence — though with no schedule or timeline, since it has always been here.
- They claim the "benign, angelic" reading fails on the record: not a single instance of an Alien saving an abused child, stopping a war (rather, coming alarmingly close to starting one), or sparing anyone famine or disease; benign only in the sense of not overtly hostile, its sole interaction with humans being "increasing mystification."

## Concepts
- **the Blanche DuBois rule** — The authors' coinage (dated "from 1947") for humanity's only available posture toward the Alien: since we cannot fight, invade, or negotiate with it, all we can do is "depend on the kindness of strangers."
- **the Alien** — The authors' term for whoever or whatever pilots or controls the observed vessels; an adversary with no discernible origin, unknown vulnerability to human weapons or earthly elements, and no shared humanity, history, or experience of reality (including birth and death).
- **the Phenomenon** — The broader UFO/UAP reality under discussion, which the authors say has a strong consciousness/paranormal aspect recognized at various times by Russia, China, and the United States; it may be a "control mechanism," an organic phenomenon peculiar to Earth, or a species from elsewhere.
- **control mechanism** — The hypothesis that the Phenomenon controls or projects what we observe; if true, scientists "are studying the images and not whatever is projecting those images."
- **Plato's cave, but for the twenty-first century** — The authors' analogy for our scientific predicament: courage is required to step away from the wall's shadowy images, turn around, and look directly at the source — something "science as we know it" will not do.
- **Tic Tacs** — The famous UAP craft, used throughout as shorthand for the Alien's visible vessels; "floating and teasing," they are already forcing a rethink of military, political, and corporate strategy.
- **perpendicular dimension / interdimensionality** — The authors' proposal (explicitly not string theory or M-theory) to add one more dimension to our repertoire and "look perpendicular" to the familiar three; the Tic Tacs may be "the tip of some other reality that is inserting itself into our dimension and then suddenly disappearing," as in Flatland.
- **ultraterrestrial** — The possibility that the Alien is an organic coresident of this planet (or a natural Phenomenon) rather than a visitor from outer space.
- **nuts-and-bolts approach** — The conventional physical-craft framing of the Phenomenon, which the authors say the ultraterrestrial research program would transcend, along with the "purely speculative 'outer space' approach."
- **probes vs. training** — The authors' analysis of UAP incursions into restricted airspace: the probe hypothesis (gauging our technology before invasion, or benign curiosity) makes no sense after centuries of observation; the only remaining conclusion is that "they are not probing us, but rather they are training us" — getting us accustomed to their presence.
- **mystification machines / Sekret machines** — The chapter's closing characterization of the Alien craft: machines whose only interaction with humans has been "increasing mystification"; ties the chapter to the series title.
- **peace treaty (impossibility of)** — A treaty acknowledges the binary, zero-sum outcome of victory/defeat and assumes equivalence and mutual concepts; since communication with the Alien is impossible or "insanely problematic," there will be no peace treaty.
- **universal collaboration** — The authors' prescription in place of one-world government: global cooperation forced into being by the Alien's presence (they note the Alien could impose world government "with little effort, with itself as our president and parliament").
- **theory of everything / grand unified theory** — The still-missing integration of the four known physical forces (strong and weak nuclear forces, gravity, electromagnetism); the Alien "does not seem to have a problem with that."
- **consciousness studies** — The field the authors say must be integrated into the Alien problem without arguing about what consciousness is or where it comes from, resuming SRI-style paranormal research minus "demons" and "spirits of the air."
- **perception of reality** — The authors' call to look at one's own surroundings — the streets, buildings, sky, trees — and ask how that perception changes once the Alien's presence is unequivocally known: will the world seem "ashen, gray, and hopelessly primitive," our cities prison camps, or will we look back on this life with nostalgia?
- **unintentional threat** — The authors' point that the Alien is a threat "even if the Alien doesn't know it or intend it," simply by causing humans to question their values, reality, and sense of self.
- **America as a haunted house** — A callback to the authors' earlier writing: America is haunted by the ancient civilizations that preceded the Europeans; here extended to ask whether the entire planet is a haunted house — and whether that is our status among the Aliens.
- **geophysical and human mapping** — The authors' proposed intelligence program: map ocean floors, fault lines, tectonic plates, the Ring of Fire, magnetic anomalies, weather, and every nuclear installation, collating them with alien appearances since the 1950s; equally, "map" the human aspect of the planet — cultural, linguistic, religious, and historical data from all groups, since no one racial or ethnic group has a monopoly on understanding the Alien.
- **foo fighters** — The famous twentieth-century aerial phenomena cited as the minimum baseline ("best case") for how long the Phenomenon has been observing us.
- **CAP (Combat Air Patrol)** — The rendezvous point of fighter aircraft; the authors note the Alien knows in advance where our F-16s' CAP will be, proof it is not gathering intelligence.
- **infantry** — The authors parenthetically note the term originates from "child soldiers," in their catalogue of human self-destruction.
- **samsara / nirvana** — Invoked as questions about the Alien's relation to death and rebirth: does the Alien experience samsara? Nirvana?
- **experiencers** — People claiming direct contact; cited for the claims that the Alien communicates telepathically and in images, and for reports of the Alien struggling with land-based existence.
- **sovereignty (Wendt and Duvall)** — The threat an alien presence poses to human ideas of sovereignty, as represented in "the by-now famous paper by Wendt and Duvall," previously noted in the trilogy.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The War of the Worlds and Independence Day** — H. G. Wells's Martians are destroyed by an ecological force; the aliens of Independence Day by human ingenuity and determination. The authors use both to expose a fallacy: we could never know whether the defeated forces were merely reconnaissance for "much larger, more devastating forces waiting in the wings."
- **The Spanish conquistadores and the Aztecs** — The Aztecs were surprised by beings partly human-like with unheard-of weapons who came "from elsewhere" on ships; the Aztecs could not know how many were coming, what their resources were, or even that Spain existed, and could not invade it. The authors present this as the model of total strategic asymmetry — then remove its one saving grace (shared humanity and vulnerability) for the Alien case: "imagine those same Aztecs, but instead of the Spanish arriving on sailing ships they witness Aliens arriving on Tic Tacs. It's game over."
- **European arrival in North America** — The same asymmetry held for the indigenous peoples of North America when the English, Dutch, and French arrived; again mitigated only by the fact that both sides were human.
- **Blanche DuBois (1947)** — The chapter's title case: with no other option, humanity can only invoke the rule "from 1947" and "depend on the kindness of strangers" (endnote 200 in the source).
- **The 1960s SRI programs** — Cited as existing studies "already on the books" addressing the paranormal aspect of the Phenomenon; Russia, China, and the US have all at some point funded this consciousness/paranormal approach, which the authors say must be resumed.
- **The Wendt and Duvall paper** — The "by-now famous" paper (endnote 201) on the threat an alien presence poses to sovereignty, referenced earlier in the trilogy and re-invoked here for the claim that mere presence, not invasion, undermines human identity.
- **Vallée's projector metaphor and Flatland** — Vallée said we should stop looking at the movie screen and shift our gaze to the room with the projector; the authors extend this with Flatland's creatures to argue the Tic Tacs are the tip of another reality inserting itself into our dimension and disappearing — "This may be their only advantage, but it is a monstrous one."
- **The insects-in-the-walls scenario** — The authors' image for a non-invasion invasion: like homeowners discovering a colony of insects has lived unnoticed in their walls for years, we may already be at the mercy of the Alien — perhaps literally in our walls, perhaps in a perpendicular dimension; possibly a virus or bacterium as the weapon or as the Alien itself.
- **The proposed mapping program** — A concrete intelligence agenda: map ocean floors (mostly unmapped), reevaluate fault lines, tectonic plates, and the Ring of Fire for defensibility; test whether pole shift, magnetic-field variation, or weather (rain, snow, hurricanes, typhoons, temperature) affects alien "craft"; map every nuclear power and missile installation, including nuclear submarines and surface craft, and collate them with alien visitations by date, time, and number to find patterns — since "It is understood that the Alien has an interest in our nuclear programs."
- **No hidden city; helplessness on land** — Aerial and online mapping has found no city where there shouldn't be one, so the machines do not come from the Earth's surface; various experiencer reports show the Alien struggling on land — no driving, vehicles without wheels, and "crashes" as "the only time the Alien meets solid ground."
- **US pilot observations, East and West Coasts** — Recent reports from pilots on both North American coasts, plus reports from the former Soviet Union, are cited as traces of possible alien communication: the Alien is conscious of us, interacts primitively, and can know our intentions in advance.
- **The Soviet "summoning" reports** — The authors recount Soviet reports that the Alien could be summoned by actions that had attracted it before, such as big troop movements; when the "craft" appeared, troops waved their arms in a certain pattern, which the craft imitated. The authors say this implies, but does not prove, consciousness — noting we cannot even rule out AI in our own communications networks.
- **Foo fighters and the 1945 atomic bombings** — The Phenomenon has been present most likely for centuries, at minimum since the twentieth century's foo fighters; it witnessed the destruction of two Japanese cities in 1945 and knows exactly how we react to threats — hence there is "nothing to find out" and the probe hypothesis fails.
- **F-16 CAP anticipation and nuclear silo shutoffs** — The Alien knows ahead of time where our F-16s' Combat Air Patrol rendezvous will be and "can shut off and on our nuclear missile silos at will" — the authors' clinching evidence that these are not technology-gauging probes.
- **The failure of the benign-angelic thesis** — For all the experiencer statements and military reports, not one recorded instance of an Alien saving an abused child, stopping a war (they have come alarmingly close to starting one), or sparing anyone famine or disease; the authors conclude the Alien is benign only in the sense of not being overtly hostile.

## Key quotes
- "All domination involves invasion—at times physical and overt, at times camouflaged, with the invader assuming the role of a helping friend." — the chapter's epigraph, from Paulo Freire, The Pedagogy of the Oppressed (1970).
- "It's entirely possible we can't even kill them." — capping the list of conventional war measures that fail against the Alien.
- "It's game over. All we can do is invoke the Blanche DuBois rule (from 1947) and 'depend on the kindness of strangers.'" — the title claim, after imagining Aztecs facing Tic Tacs instead of sailing ships.
- "This is like Plato's cave, but for the twenty-first century." — on scientists studying the projected images rather than the projector, if the Phenomenon is a control mechanism.
- "We speak blithely of an alien invasion scenario, but an actual invasion is not necessary. The mere presence of the Other will call into question everything we know and everything we value about our planet and about our human race." — the cultural-devaluation thesis.
- "The Alien has no need to invade—if that was ever its intention. We are doing its work for it." — after the catalogue of human warfare, genocide, and weapons.
- "Are our ghosts mingling with their ghosts?" — from the Death section's chain of "childish" questions the authors insist will one day be critical to species survival.
- "It's already happened. They're here. They've been here." — opening of "The Alien Invasion" section.
- "There will be no peace treaty with the Alien." — the Treaties section's conclusion, since treaties assume equivalence and shared concepts.
- "The only conclusion we can come to is that they are not probing us, but rather they are training us. They are getting us accustomed to their presence." — the chapter's pivotal inference from the failure of the probe hypothesis.
- "Their only interaction with humans has been that of increasing mystification. They are machines, perhaps, as we have been suggesting all along. Mystification machines. Sekret machines." — the closing lines, naming the series.

## Thread
This chapter is the book's systematic demolition of conventional war-fighting categories as applied to the Alien — the analytical hinge of the "War" theme. It follows the trilogy's earlier work (the sovereignty threat via Wendt and Duvall, "America is a haunted house," and the running suggestion that the craft are machines) and operationalizes the war-room framing of the Introduction: taking treaties, territory, communication, and probes in turn and showing each fails. Its opening line explicitly sets up the following chapter, which will address why no earthly government can plan for conflict with a force of unknown origin. Its positive program — consciousness studies, planetary geophysical and human mapping, perpendicular/interdimensional thinking — carries forward the authors' central thesis that this is an intelligence problem and possibly a control mechanism, feeding directly into the book's concluding argument (the Afterword's "Alien in the Panopticon") about presence, observation, and control. The closing coinage "Mystification machines. Sekret machines." ties the chapter's conclusion — training, not probing — to the series' title and thesis.
