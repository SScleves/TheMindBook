# Sekret Machines Book 3: War — Q&A

*Chapter-pointer legend: pointers use the note chapter numbers from each unit's frontmatter, not the book's printed chapter numbers. Ch 1–3 = front matter (dedications and acknowledgments); Ch 4 = Preface; Ch 5 = Introduction ("Welcome to the War Room"); Ch 6–16 = the main chapters; Ch 17 = Afterword ("The Alien in the Panopticon"); Ch 18–19 = bibliography and endnotes. All answers state what Tom DeLonge & Peter Levenda claim in the book; none are endorsed as fact.*

## Front Matter and Framing

**Q: Who are the two authors, and how do they position themselves relative to the defense establishment?**
A: The book is by Tom DeLonge and Peter Levenda, writing the third and final nonfiction volume of the Sekret Machines trilogy. They describe themselves as neither scientists nor defense insiders nor adversaries of the defense establishment, but "reasonable fellows" able — thanks to Tom's access — to ask questions of it; Levenda discloses that he worked c. 1973–1979 for the defense contractor Bendix in a Manhattan sales office without a clearance. In the acknowledgments DeLonge thanks unnamed "collaborators in the shadows, from deeper places within the government that know a lot more than we do." (Ch 2, Ch 4)

**Q: What slogan does Peter Levenda propose the UFO field should adopt, and why?**
A: In his acknowledgments Levenda urges the next generation to rise above the community's "factionalism and paranoia" and to replace the old slogan "I Want to Believe" with "I Want to Know" — reframing the subject as a matter of sober inquiry rather than faith. He declares the roughly ten-year Sekret Machines project succeeded in bringing the conversation into national discourse, news media, academia, and "the halls of Congress." (Ch 3)

**Q: What was Christopher Mellon's 2023 Debrief article, and why do the authors dwell on its publication date?**
A: The Preface opens with Mellon's November 22, 2023 Debrief article "Disclosure and National Security," which argues that secrecy about UAPs is a national-security matter and that blunt disclosure risks both existential panic and exposing US defenses. The authors note the article — which opens by asking the reader to imagine being newly elected president told the military "has recovered advanced extraterrestrial technology" — appeared on the sixtieth anniversary of JFK's assassination, joking that "some weird force in the world stage-managed this little nexus of themes." (Ch 4)

**Q: What is the authors' central complaint about how disclosure is being handled?**
A: They argue the Phenomenon has been captured entirely by the national-security frame — "war, the preparation for war, and the calculations of the battlefield" — so that the people best equipped to challenge views of reality (clergy, artists, philosophers, mystics) and the "soft sciences" are never "read in." They also name a "catch-22": "We want the government to disclose; we don't trust the government's disclosure." (Ch 4)

**Q: According to the Preface, why does the book claim the military has privileged access to the Phenomenon?**
A: The authors argue that if the military has specialized access, it is because the Alien "is operating along the same lines as they do" — it reacts to human weapons systems (nuclear capabilities, aircraft, submarines, missile silos) and not to culture, religion, politics, museums, or art galleries. "The Alien is reacting to our weapons systems," they write, "not to our culture." (Ch 4)

## Welcome to the War Room (Introduction)

**Q: What is the book's core corrective claim about seventy years of government secrecy?**
A: DeLonge & Levenda argue the strange official behavior toward UFOs is best explained not as "a conspiracy against their own citizens" but as "helplessness in the face of the inexplicable" — technological societies each confronting the same "metatechnical Phenomenon: sekret machines," machines whose "provenance and purpose are unknowable, unfathomable, and hence sekret." A century-long conspiracy among hostile governments, they say, is wildly improbable. (Ch 5)

**Q: What do the authors say are the trilogy's three explicit takeaways?**
A: They state them as: (a) the Phenomenon is real; (b) there is no cabal of magi withholding the truth "at least not the way you think"; and (c) the tension between informing citizens and denying intelligence to rival governments is so absolute that "we, the people of the world, have already lost the ability to defend ourselves from an alien invasion." (Ch 5)

**Q: What is the "Plato's Bunker" image the Introduction closes on?**
A: Adapting Plato's cave allegory, the authors cast the world's military leaders in their war rooms as the chained prisoners, their sensors as the wall, and their threat-assessment traditions as the chains — "an almost Neanderthal conception of threat assessment" — while "the Alien is making shadow puppets on that very wall." They conclude: "Like the generals in Plato's bunker, we are mistaking the presence for the threat." (Ch 5)

**Q: What does the book propose in place of "mutually assured destruction"?**
A: The authors propose that thinking in terms of alien invasion "may be the only avenue we have left" to unify a divided Earth into a planetwide defense posture — what they call "mutually assured protection." Even if it turned out to be unneeded, they argue, achieving that global unity "would be a tremendous accomplishment." (Ch 5)

**Q: How do the authors want the Phenomenon studied, and what do they mean by holding two aspects "at once"?**
A: They call for a "multivalent," Renaissance-style, multidisciplinary and multicultural approach rather than a single-discipline or purely extraterrestrial reading, invoking the blind-men-and-the-elephant figure ("We're gonna need a bigger room"). They insist both the physical evidence (FLIR videos, radar, artifacts) and the consciousness aspect must be held simultaneously and synthesized into a third: "the Phenomenon as (competing?) worldview." (Ch 5)

## Steampunk Dreams: The Phenomenon Before the Saucers

**Q: What were the Great Airship sightings, and why do the authors emphasize them?**
A: The authors describe a wave of "Great Airship" sightings that swept America and reached Russia in 1896–1897 — beginning in Sacramento in November 1896 and ending near Veliky Ustyug, Russia in September 1897 — six years before the Wright brothers flew. They argue hundreds of witnesses (including the governor of Kansas) saw craft performing impossible maneuvers, proving UFOs long predate flying-saucer media culture; "They behaved like UFOs," the authors write. (Ch 6)

**Q: How do the authors explain why the 1897 craft looked like "steampunk dreams" while later ones looked like saucers?**
A: They propose the Phenomenon works through the consciousness of observers, using "the psychological content of each culture as the 'material basis' that allows it to manifest in some visible form" — so it appeared as fantastic gasbag-and-propeller airships in 1897 and as saucers later. They call the brain's clothing of an unprecedented sight in familiar imagery "a kind of poetry of the cortex." (Ch 6)

**Q: What did Nikola Tesla claim to have received, and how do the authors connect it to modern science?**
A: The authors say Tesla, working on wireless energy at Colorado Springs in 1899, picked up a regular rhythmic signal pattern he concluded "must be of planetary origin." They note he also warned that an extraterrestrial's "sign language" would be nearly unreadable ("a message from Mars which might be a triangle to them would appear as some other form to us") — anticipating, they argue, NASA's own 2014 expert study on the near-impossibility of alien communication by over a century. (Ch 6)

**Q: How do the authors read Bram Stoker's *Dracula* (1897) against later abduction reports?**
A: Published the same year as the airship wave, *Dracula* is mapped point-for-point onto 1970s–80s abduction scenarios: a nocturnal foreign being who can fly and entrances victims, coaxes them from their beds, takes bodily fluids, and returns them before dawn. Lucy Westenra's not-asleep/not-awake testimony ("my soul seemed to go out from my body") is offered as the model of the abductee's state of consciousness, which the authors say John Mack's Harvard work found accompanied by genuine post-traumatic stress. (Ch 6)

**Q: What is Henri Bergson's contribution to the chapter's argument, and how does it connect to the Golden Dawn?**
A: Via Bergson the authors argue mechanism and mysticism are intertwined — "mechanism should mean mysticism" — glossing his static vs. dynamic religion as "religion is a building and mysticism is a machine." They tie this to the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn, whose leader S. L. MacGregor Mathers claimed physical contact with superhuman "secret chiefs" weeks before the first airship sighting; Mathers was brother-in-law to Bergson through his wife Mina. Bergson's closing image is of the universe as "a machine for the making of gods." (Ch 6)

## To The Stars: Rockets, Parsons, and the Occult

**Q: Why do the authors make Jack Parsons the hinge figure of the chapter?**
A: They argue there is "no way" to discuss UFOs without the space race, JPL, science fiction, and ultimately Aleister Crowley — and that rocket scientist Jack Parsons (1914–1952) "combines all of these various disparate threads of the Phenomenon in one person." A JPL co-founder, pioneer of solid rocket fuel and JATO, and founder of Aerojet (with a Moon crater named after him), Parsons was also deep in Crowley's Ordo Templi Orientis. (Ch 7)

**Q: What was the Babalon Working, and what claim rests on it?**
A: The Babalon Working was Parsons's own occult ritual, performed with L. Ron Hubbard in the Mojave Desert in January–March 1946, aimed at contacting nonhuman entities to create a "hybrid" creature — "a bit like an alien abduction scenario but in reverse." The authors present it as the root of the claim that Parsons opened "a hole in the ether, a kind of stargate, through which the contemporary UFO Phenomenon would enter our world"; they call this fanciful but treat Parsons as "an important icon of the event." (Ch 7)

**Q: How do the authors describe Scientology's origins?**
A: They argue "Scientology was as much a creature of the OTO as it was of Hubbard's own imagination," built from Parsons's occult training, Sara Northrup's influence, and Thelemic doctrine. They read OT VIII's Xenu "space opera" as the Gnostic descent of spirit into matter combined with ancient-astronaut theory "retold from a science fiction perspective," and say the Sea Org was templated on the classified-military milieu around Parsons. (Ch 7)

**Q: What does the declassified Browning memo of October 1947 say, and how do the authors read it?**
A: The memo (Major Earl S. Browning Jr., October 25, 1947, relaying Wright Field) states "The Air Materiel Command is of the opinion that some sort of object, such as the flying saucer, did exist," notes wind-tunnel models were being built, and points at the Horten brothers and the Soviets. The authors read this juxtaposition as suggesting the official Mogul-balloon line on Roswell "was smoke and mirrors," and raise whether Wright Field thought the saucer was of Nazi origin. (Ch 7)

**Q: How did Parsons die, and what surrounding coincidences do the authors note?**
A: Parsons died on June 17, 1952, when he dropped a vial of fulminate of mercury in his garage — six months to the day after his security clearance was finally revoked on January 17, 1952 — an event variously called accident, suicide, or assassination. His mother killed herself with sleeping pills the same afternoon; the authors note that one month later came the UFO overflights of Washington, D.C. (Ch 7)

**Q: What "two-way street" concept do the authors say is at the heart of the project?**
A: Using the Babalon Working as illustration, they claim contact with alien or extramundane intelligences is a two-way street: "'They' can make contact with us; more importantly, perhaps, we can make contact with 'them.'" They frame Parsons's occultism as mapping directly onto Ufological ideas — hybrids, initiation as staged travel to the stars, and "reengineering of human consciousness to enable a form of travel that science so far understands to be theoretically impossible." (Ch 7)

## Weaponizing the Wizards: Nazi UFOs

**Q: Do the authors think the "Nazi UFO" theory explains the Phenomenon?**
A: No — they argue a Nazi/human origin cannot be a general solution because sightings have been recorded worldwide for millennia, and the proposition reads as "a transparent attempt to dismiss all UFO sightings." But they take it seriously as a live possibility for some 1947-era sightings, noting US military documents of the period took Nazi "flying saucer" technology seriously — "one gets the impression that they actually hoped it was a Nazi superweapon," because the alien alternative was unacceptable. (Ch 8)

**Q: What do the authors say about the expatriated Nazi infrastructure after the war?**
A: They describe the Maison Rouge meeting (Strasbourg, August 10, 1944), where SS officers told German industry to expatriate wealth and know-how through front companies, and trace the ratlines (ODESSA, Die Spinne, Kameradenwerke) and Argentine institutions (CAPRI, the Huemul Project). Counting Paperclip alone misses the scale, they argue: in aggregate more than ten thousand Nazi scientists and specialists were reemployed worldwide — "there was a postwar Nazi space program. Just not in the way most of us think about it." (Ch 8)

**Q: Where does the Antarctic "Hitler survived" saucer myth come from, per the authors?**
A: They attribute it largely to Canadian Holocaust denier Ernst Zündel, who began the story in the 1970s "as a fictional device" — Hitler taken by U-boat to an Antarctic base where engineers built saucers — boosted by a misleading Spanish-to-English translation of an Admiral Byrd interview in Chile's El Mercurio. They separate this fabrication from documented facts like the 1938–39 New Swabia Expedition and Operation HIGHJUMP. (Ch 8)

**Q: How do the authors use Barney Hill's abduction account in this chapter?**
A: Under hypnosis with Dr. Benjamin Simon in 1964, Barney Hill described an abductor as "an evil face... He looks like a German Nazi." The authors — noting Hill was a Black man married to a white woman in 1960s New Hampshire — read this as the consciousness side of the Phenomenon: the closest reference points available to Hill were "a white cop with a German shepherd" or "a Nazi landing in a V3." (Ch 8)

**Q: What is the authors' thesis about the Alien's indifference, illustrated by the Auschwitz meditation?**
A: They argue any Alien interest in humans is predicated on the Alien's self-interest and on technology, not morality: "As long as we were killing each other, the Alien was cool. It's only when we began to invade their airspace and their sea space that they became a little concerned." In the "Auschwitz orchestra" meditation they ask whether an Alien watching the "Nazi technology miracle" noticed the SS sorting victims to Strauss waltzes — and whether "the Alien does not sing." Their moral inversion: if the Tic Tac is not human-made, "we are all of us... black children living in Mississippi in 1960." (Ch 8)

## War of the Worlds: Cold War Secrecy Is Born

**Q: What theft frames this chapter, and why does it matter?**
A: Over the July 4 weekend of 1948, 1,885 classified pages were removed from Theodore von Kármán's Columbia office safe by his protégé and Soviet spy William Perl, photographed by the Rosenberg ring, and delivered to the KGB. The authors use it to argue that the man at the center of the emerging military-industrial complex — which von Kármán himself effectively proposed — was the leak point to Moscow, even as Paperclip Nazis like Dornberger arrived at Wright Field the same month as the Roswell wreckage. (Ch 9)

**Q: What did one of the authors' sources claim about Roswell, and how do they handle it?**
A: They report — explicitly attributing it to one of their "otherwise impeccable and unimpeachable" sources — that the Roswell crash was a man-made flying disk of neither American nor Soviet manufacture, but "a craft designed by the Nazis." They stress their own initial doubt and treat it as a hypothesis to stress-test, reasoning that the very fact UFOs crash implies human manufacture: "the UFOs that have crashed—all of them—were made by us." (Ch 9)

**Q: What was the suppressed "Estimate of the Situation," and what template did it set?**
A: It was Project Sign's report (credited chiefly to Alfred Loedding) concluding the UFOs were extraterrestrial; General Vandenberg rejected it and all copies were destroyed ("literally incinerated," per Ruppelt). The authors argue the earlier official Sign statement dismissing the ETH in favor of a foreign nation's craft "formed the template for everything to follow" — debunkery, ridicule, and misidentification claims that would define the next seventy years. (Ch 9)

**Q: What is the single explanation the authors say accommodates all the evidence?**
A: Faced with the Arnold/Rhodes sightings matching Horten flying-wing designs but no known human propulsion (jet, rocket, nuclear) matching the observed behavior, they arrive at the proposition that "the craft may have been built by human beings, but the technology derives from a nonhuman source." (Ch 9)

**Q: According to the "Politics of Secrecy" section, from whom is UFO information really being kept?**
A: The authors argue UFO secrecy cannot be justified as keeping secrets from enemy states, since every state sees the Phenomenon; the secrets are being kept from citizens, and the cleared form "a secret society entrusted... with secret knowledge." They add a closing diagnosis: "there is a demonstrably uneven development of our bicameral minds, and it may be this very vulnerability that the Phenomenon exploits." (Ch 9)

**Q: Why do the authors find Operation HIGHJUMP suspicious?**
A: They describe HIGHJUMP as a 13-ship, roughly 5,000-man Antarctic convoy launched amid drastic postwar demobilization (defense spending collapsing from 37% of GDP in 1945 toward 5.5% by 1947), with seven lives lost and murky results. "It made no sense then, and makes no sense now," they write — "which means it was probably not what it was claimed to be," possibly triggered by whatever the earlier Operation NANOOK encountered. (Ch 9)

## 1952: The Nine and the Cold War Occult

**Q: How do the authors use the film *Red Planet Mars* to frame 1952?**
A: They argue media anticipates what happens behind the scenes: *Red Planet Mars* (opened May 15, 1952) features a Nazi scientist secretly manipulating "Martian" radio contact for the Soviets from an Andes mountaintop, and world-shaking messages. They note a transmission time repeated in the film — 11:22 — and read it against November 22, 1963, saying the film "sets the stage for what actually will occur in 1952." (Ch 10)

**Q: What are "the Nine," and how did contact with them allegedly begin?**
A: The Nine were entities first channeled through Dr. D. G. Vinod at Andrija Puharich's Round Table Foundation in Maine on December 31, 1952, announcing themselves as "Nine Principles and Forces, personalities if you will, working in complete mutual implication." They were later identified with the Egyptian Ennead and with Aliens aboard a spacecraft in near-Earth orbit; a June 27, 1953 séance had nine human attendees who "mirrored" them. (Ch 10)

**Q: Who were some of the wealthy, connected figures in the Round Table Foundation's circle?**
A: The authors list Alice Bouverie (born Ava Alice Muriel Astor, daughter of John Jacob Astor IV), DuPont heiress Marcella DuPont, Arthur Young (inventor of the first Bell helicopter), Ruth Forbes Paine Young (best friend of Mary Bancroft, mistress of Allen Dulles), Aldous Huxley, and former Vice President Henry Wallace — a circle they say tied together Theosophy, the CIA, the Pentagon, Bell Aerospace, Nazi scientists, "and Lee Harvey Oswald." (Ch 10)

**Q: What connection do the authors draw between the Nine and the Kennedy assassination?**
A: They claim "a very real and very tangible link" running from séance participants Arthur and Ruth Young to their daughter-in-law Ruth Paine, hostess of Marina Oswald, who got Lee Harvey Oswald his Texas School Book Depository job. They allege Allen Dulles derailed Ruth Paine's Warren Commission testimony just as she was about to describe a pre-assassination visit to the Youngs, and say there is arguably "more documentation to suggest a serious relationship between the Nine and the assassination than there is to show a conspiracy by the Cubans, or the Soviets, or the Mafia" — while framing dismissal of it as cognitive dissonance. (Ch 10)

**Q: What "unofficial classification process" and "C.P. file" do the authors describe?**
A: Citing Ruppelt and their own government contacts, they say US policy was to release only what the press already knew — an "unofficial classification process" where "you pretty much have to know what you want before you even know it exists." Any report of direct contact with an Alien was automatically filed as "C.P." ("crackpot"), consigning decades of abduction reports to "bureaucratic oblivion." (Ch 10)

**Q: What warning does the chapter say it exists to raise?**
A: The authors state that a purpose of the chapter is to raise "the possibility that ongoing contact with the Phenomenon may not be in the best interests of humanity and may, in fact, be detrimental in some way" — suggestive of "some hostile force at work in the world but not necessarily of the world." They describe the Nine's communications as a "seduction" designed to erode resistance to "being used by invisible, unidentified Forces," while declining to come down on either side of the Nine controversy. (Ch 10)

## Collateral Damage: The Casualties of the Field

**Q: What is the "memorial wall" conceit that frames this chapter?**
A: The authors propose an imagined memorial wall — like the CIA's stars at Langley — for people who died as a result of investigating the Phenomenon, arguing "there is something lethal about this field." They contend that since 1947 UFO research has accumulated violent deaths at a rate unmatched among other arcane researchers, and that the very first casualties were seasoned military personnel, not unstable saucer fanatics. (Ch 11)

**Q: How do the authors characterize the Maury Island incident?**
A: They call it "a hoax within a hoax," neither a straightforward hoax by Harold Dahl and Fred Crisman nor a straightforward genuine sighting. Citing the FBI's own investigation, they note Dahl did not say it was a hoax but said that if anyone asked he would *say* it was a hoax. They also link Maury Island to the Kennedy assassination through personnel — Crisman (subpoenaed by Jim Garrison) and FBI agent Guy Banister. (Ch 11)

**Q: Who were the first military deaths the authors count in "the modern UFO era"?**
A: Air Force intelligence officers Captain William Davidson and Lieutenant Frank Brown, who died on August 1, 1947, when their B-26 crashed near Kelso, Washington while carrying Maury Island debris. Adding reporter Paul Lantz (dead ten days after filing a Maury Island story) and Captain Thomas Mantell — who died January 7, 1948 pursuing a UFO over Kentucky — the authors count three Air Force officers dead in UFO-related duty within just over five months. (Ch 11)

**Q: How do the authors treat Wilhelm Reich's death?**
A: They argue Reich (who died in Lewisburg penitentiary in 1957) cannot be dismissed as merely paranoid, because the US government really was arrayed against him — the FDA destroyed his orgone accumulators, and six tons of his books were burned in 1956, "the biggest single act of censorship in American history." They add recent scholarship that Reich appeared on an NKVD death-list, and quip: "But is it paranoia if they really are out to get you?" (Ch 11)

**Q: What is the "control mechanism" or "mind game" hypothesis the chapter endorses as a live option?**
A: Following Jacques Vallée, the authors treat as a serious possibility that UFOs are intelligently controlled but not spacecraft as we understand them — "a kind of mind game being played on the chessboard of human experience... by an unseen player." They connect this consciousness-focused reading to the madness and suicides of otherwise sane investigators like Morris Jessup and James McDonald, arguing the Phenomenon's cross-linking of science, religion, politics, and the paranormal multiplies dangerous coincidences for those who study it. (Ch 11)

**Q: Do the authors believe there was a cover-up, and what motive do they assign it?**
A: They say yes, there was a cover-up, grounded in solid evidence of denial and blocked access — but they characterize its motive as "a mixture of fear, ignorance, and confusion rather than a cynical desire to fool the American people." Fear because the Phenomenon defied sovereignty; ignorance because leaders knew little more than anyone; confusion because credible trace and radar cases left authorities unable to assess intent. On McDonald they add that the response was "not a scientific solution... but a political one." (Ch 11)

## The Journey to the East: Russia and China

**Q: What do the authors mean by "Alienism," and how does it relate to Said's "Orientalism"?**
A: Modeling the term on Edward Said's "Orientalism," they define "Alienism" as the Ufological habit of making the "alien" into a screen for projection — "a projection of our worst fears, our most hopeful dreams, or an expression of our colonial anxieties," from bug-eyed monsters to Grays to angelic Venusians. The chapter turns from America-centric Ufology to Russia and especially China. (Ch 12)

**Q: Who was Qian Xuesen, and why do the authors call his expulsion a self-inflicted catastrophe?**
A: Qian was a Caltech protégé of von Kármán, a "Suicide Squad" member and JPL co-founder who was stripped of his clearance in the 1950 McCarthy panic, arrested, and in 1955 traded to Mao's China, where he built the Chinese missile and space programs (designing the Silkworm missile and Long March rockets). The authors note America purged Parsons, Qian, Malina, and even Oppenheimer, "and they were all replaced by Nazi rocket scientists. Actual Nazis." (Ch 12)

**Q: What did the authors find about who was studying the paranormal in Russia and China?**
A: They report that by the 1970s American intelligence noticed the paranormal was being studied in both countries — "Not the civilians. The generals." They argue Marxist states, having stripped religion away, could pragmatically study ESP, psychokinesis, and remote viewing as "somatic science" or "life science," pursued under the PLA with Qian Xuesen "leading the charge," documented in declassified CIA/DIA files. (Ch 12)

**Q: How do the authors describe Falun Gong, and why was it crushed?**
A: They present Li Hongzhi's Falun Gong (founded 1992) as an unintended consequence of the state's qigong revival — a "UFO religion" in the lineage of Mormonism and Scientology, whose doctrine holds that aliens are manipulating humanity ("The aliens have introduced modern machinery... The ultimate purpose is to replace humans," per Li's 1999 Time interview). The state tolerated UFOs as apolitical science but not "an entire population of uncontrolled and unregulated remote viewers," banning the movement from 1999. (Ch 12)

**Q: What does the book say is the unchanging rationale for UFO secrecy across nations?**
A: The authors argue the rationale has not changed in over fifty years: keep what you know from rivals, which means keeping it from your own citizens, because you cannot know what would be valuable to another nation's scientists. They add that no government can fully come clean because admitting what it does not know "would challenge their right to govern": "A government that cannot protect its people is not worthy of governing." (Ch 12)

## The Russia House: Information Warfare

**Q: What American cultural "vulnerability" does this chapter argue Russia weaponized?**
A: The authors claim American culture has a basic vulnerability to paranoia and mass hysteria running unbroken from the Salem witch trials (1692) through the Illuminati and Masonic scares, the Red Scare, satanic-ritual-abuse panic, birtherism, Pizzagate, and QAnon. They argue Russia exploited it: the 2016 WikiLeaks dump of John Podesta's emails was "the result... of a Russian disinformation project," continuing a lineage from the Tsarist-forged *Protocols of the Learned Elders of Zion* through Soviet *disinformatsiya*. (Ch 13)

**Q: How do the authors connect the degraded information environment to the Phenomenon?**
A: Following Vallée's "control mechanism" hypothesis, they argue a society that has surrendered fact-checking is soft terrain the Phenomenon can exploit: "This is the perfect set of conditions for the UFO to reveal itself to the world, for we have so devalued the idea of 'fact' and 'truth' that a UFO appearance is equivalent to the 'confession' of a known liar." They add that cyber-warfare infrastructure lets the Phenomenon "slip into our increasingly interrelated and interdependent systems." (Ch 13)

**Q: What was the Voronezh incident, and what parallel do the authors draw?**
A: In late September–early October 1989, TASS reported a series of UFO sightings near Voronezh, about 300 miles from Moscow, including a landed sphere whose tall humanoid occupant and small "robot" walked through a park; Vallée went to Moscow and concluded the sightings occurred as described. The authors note the USSR handled it exactly as the USA handled Roswell — official confirmation, then walk-back — and ask "What type of intelligence exercise can continue for more than seventy years, virtually unmodified... in widely divergent — even hostile — regimes?" (Ch 13)

**Q: What is the Belokoroviche incident, and why do the authors cite it?**
A: On October 4, 1982, at a nuclear missile base in Soviet Ukraine, a disk-shaped UFO roughly 900 feet across allegedly hovered for nearly an hour and lit up the control panels signaling imminent launch of all missiles, with officers helpless, before the sequence reset and aborted "evidently by the UFO." All the codes, keys, and safeguards were "bypassed at once by an outside source." They compare it to the 2004 Nimitz "Tic Tac" case, where UFOs seemed to know the F-18s' encrypted rendezvous point in advance. (Ch 13)

**Q: What did the Soviet "summoning" experiment reportedly show?**
A: Per KGB Major General Vasiliy Yeremenko, the Soviet military found UFOs appear in areas of "heightened tension," so at a range in Astrakhan Region they staged tension with increased combat-aircraft flights: "Then UFOs appeared with a probability of nearly 100 percent." Soldiers waved arms in a pattern (twice right, twice left) and the craft imitated it — which the authors liken to the climax of *Close Encounters of the Third Kind*. (Ch 13)

**Q: What does Dr. Walt LaBerge's outburst illustrate for the authors?**
A: When retired Army colonel John Alexander briefed Pentagon specialists on what UFO sightings might imply about unconventional aeronautics, aerospace engineer Dr. Walt LaBerge slammed the table and shouted, "You're not supposed to know that. That's what you learn when you die!" The authors read this as proof that even elite scientists "harbor medieval emotional responses and Inquisition-era paranoia," producing a "UFO gap" and "remote viewing gap" alongside the missile gap. (Ch 13)

## The Blanche DuBois Rule and The Art of War

**Q: What is "the Blanche DuBois rule"?**
A: It is the authors' coinage (dated "from 1947," the year of A Streetcar Named Desire) for humanity's only available posture toward the Alien: because we cannot fight, invade, or negotiate with it, all we can do is "depend on the kindness of strangers." They argue no earthly military can plan for conflict with a force that has no discernible origin, homeland, supply lines, or known vulnerabilities — "It's entirely possible we can't even kill them." (Ch 14)

**Q: Why do the authors conclude the UAPs are "training" us rather than probing us?**
A: They argue the probe hypothesis fails because, after centuries of watching our energy, ballistics, aeronautics, atomic bombs, and wars — at minimum since the foo fighters — there is "nothing to find out." An adversary that knows our F-16s' Combat Air Patrol rendezvous points and "can shut off and on our nuclear missile silos at will" is not gauging technology. The only remaining conclusion: "they are not probing us, but rather they are training us... getting us accustomed to their presence." The closing line names them "Mystification machines. Sekret machines." (Ch 14)

**Q: What "positive program" does the Blanche DuBois chapter propose?**
A: The authors call for integrating consciousness studies (resuming SRI-style paranormal research minus the "demons" that sabotaged earlier efforts); mapping the planet geophysically (ocean floors, fault lines, the Ring of Fire, magnetic anomalies, every nuclear installation) and culturally against the record of alien appearances; and thinking "perpendicular" to our three dimensions, per Vallée's projector metaphor and the *Flatland* analogy. They insist on diversity of disciplines and cultures "not as a gesture of 'wokeness'" but because no one group has a monopoly on understanding the Alien. (Ch 14)

**Q: What is Chris Mellon's greatest fear as relayed in "The Art of War"?**
A: The authors report that Mellon's greatest concern is that decades of increased sightings indicate the craft are probes designed to test "our preparedness and our defense posture," in advance of a possible attack or invasion. The chapter then tests classical military theory (Clausewitz, Jomini, Sun Tzu) against the Phenomenon and finds none of it maps cleanly, because the Alien has no identifiable territory, home base, ideology, or predictable pattern. (Ch 15)

**Q: How do the authors reinterpret alien abductions and cattle mutilations in "The Art of War"?**
A: They argue the "medical experiment" explanation makes no scientific sense for a technologically superior species, and propose instead that abductions and mutilations may function as psychological terrorism — unpredictable acts aimed at destabilizing a population's sense of security. Citing Vallée, they say cattle mutilations "may be just what they seem to be: acts of terrorism." They add the puzzle of target choice: "What would the Alien want with Whitley Strieber... when the killing fields of Cambodia were available?" — implying the choice is about propaganda value, not biological material. (Ch 15)

**Q: What speculative CIA/AFOSI arrangement do the authors float, and how do they flag it?**
A: Labeling it "wholly speculative," they suggest the CIA — barred from domestic operations — could operationalize domestic scenarios through the Air Force's AFOSI, which has no such restriction, mirroring the alleged NSA/Army Cyber Command/CIA division of labor in the Stuxnet ("Operation Olympic Games") cyberattack. They offer it to explain how a disinformation agent like Richard Doty could function relatively unscathed, and why any such program's records would be un-declassifiable. (Ch 15)

## The Alien Invasion

**Q: What does "intersectionality" mean in this chapter's argument?**
A: Borrowing the academic term, the authors argue the UAP Phenomenon must be studied "intersectionally" — across military, scientific, technological, biological, religious, racial, and economic disciplines — rather than through the narrow, DoD-integrated lens that dominates US study. They argue that since the 20th century, war itself has been "the intersection of science, technology, religion, race and genetics, gender, and money," with WWII as its culmination. (Ch 16)

**Q: Why do the authors use European colonization as a model for alien invasion?**
A: They argue roughly 500 years of European colonization of Africa and the Americas is the best available real-world model for what an alien invasion and its cultural aftermath could look like — including forced religious conversion, destruction of oral and land-based spiritual traditions, and the creation of rootless post-slavery cultures. Because race is a social construct with no genetic basis, they argue, an alien invader would draw no distinction between human "races": all would be "ripe for colonization, abduction, enslavement, and destruction." (Ch 16)

**Q: What is the chapter's central speculative thesis about the Phenomenon as PSYOP?**
A: The authors float the possibility that the Phenomenon may itself be a sophisticated psychological-warfare campaign — introducing the Nazi term Weltanschauungskrieg ("worldview warfare") — waged simultaneously against Earth's civilian, military, and intelligence sectors. They ask: "What if the Phenomenon is itself a psywar campaign being waged against the world by a civilization that is much weaker, much more vulnerable, than we can imagine?" (Ch 16)

**Q: What is the "selective witnessing" pattern, and how do the authors interpret it?**
A: Selective or partial witnessing is when some observers among many perceive the Phenomenon while others standing beside them see nothing — as in the 1994 Ariel School case in Zimbabwe, where only about 62 of 250 children reported seeing a craft and beings, and no adults saw anything. The authors argue this pattern "is not a bug, it's a feature," pointing to the Phenomenon's still-unexplained nature rather than discrediting the testimony. (Ch 16)

**Q: Why do the authors treat the 2004 Nimitz "Tic Tac" incident as decisive?**
A: They present it as real-world evidence rather than hallucination because it combined pilot testimony (including Commander David Fravor), radar, and ATFLIR video, with the wingless Tic Tac craft performing extreme maneuvers and associated ocean turbulence. Most strikingly, the craft was found waiting at the F/A-18s' classified, computer-generated CAP coordinates before they arrived — implying "something bordering on consciousness and precognition." They call the underlying single, not-yet-understood science "the science of the Sekret Machine." (Ch 16)

## The Alien in the Panopticon (Afterword)

**Q: What is the panopticon, and why do the authors make it the book's master metaphor?**
A: The panopticon is Jeremy Bentham's 1791 circular-prison design, made famous by Michel Foucault, in which an unseen watcher in a central tower can observe every backlit cell while inmates never know if they are being watched. The authors argue alien abduction and UFO surveillance behave exactly like panopticon logic: "the 'aliens' behave as if we are laboratory specimens in a panopticon, being observed, being experimented upon, and perhaps even being 'corrected.'" (Ch 17)

**Q: How do the authors treat the "benevolent aliens" hypothesis in the Afterword?**
A: They reject it as an emotionally understandable but strategically dangerous coping fantasy that "increases our vulnerability." Even the "benevolent" hybridization theory, they argue, amounts to a form of genocide because human genetic material is being altered "without our permission," and they compare the "to save the human race from itself" rationale to the social-Darwinist logic of 1930s–40s eugenicists and Nazis, citing Josef Mengele. (Ch 17)

**Q: What recurring "elect / bloodline" theme do the authors trace across traditions?**
A: They argue that mythic invisible kingdoms — Shambhala and Agarttha in Buddhist/Theosophical lore, Thule for the Nazis, Asgard for the Norse — all encode the same requirement: some special quality (genetic, spiritual, or religious) is needed to be admitted among "the elect." They add the Christian resurrection of the dead as another instance, and read the abduction literature's claim of multigenerational, bloodline-based abduction as the same structure applied to an alien hybrid program, tracing the eugenic strand back through Blavatsky's Theosophy and Nazi racial policy. (Ch 17)

**Q: How does the Afterword apply Foucault's history of sexuality to abduction accounts?**
A: The authors argue abduction accounts represent a shift from *ars erotica* (pleasure-centered) to *scientia sexualis* (clinical, reproductive, state/science-controlled) discourse, describing a "strong S&M element" of bondage and discipline in which the restrained human is the slave and the Alien the master. They map the hypnotic-regression recovery process onto Foucault's history of "confession" (religious, then torture-extracted "dark twins," then psychotherapeutic), pointedly noting the DSM "initially was created at the request of the US Department of Defense." (Ch 17)

**Q: What parallel do the authors draw between alien abduction and the 1980s satanic-ritual-abuse panic?**
A: They note that in the satanic-ritual-abuse cases no organized satanic cults were ever proven to exist, yet the underlying trauma was judged real — implying "another cause." They apply the same logic to abduction: hypnotic regression is legally unreliable and inadmissible due to suggestibility, but the demonstrable trauma still points to a real, unexplained precipitating experience. (Ch 17)

**Q: How does the book end?**
A: On the panopticon image turned to defiance: the authors call for a global, "total war"–style, multidisciplinary defense effort regardless of the Others' true intent, and close, "The Alien is indeed in the panopticon. The prisoners are starting to bang on the walls of their cells. If all the prisoners bang at the same rhythm at the same time, the walls of the panopticon will come tumbling down and the prisoners will rush the tower." (Ch 17)
