# The Journey to the East

## In one paragraph
Opening with Hermann Hesse's novel of the same name, DeLonge & Levenda turn from America-centric Ufology to the East — Russia and above all China — arguing that Western "Orientalism" (Said's term) has a modern twin, the "Alienism" of Ufology, in which the alien becomes whatever we project onto it. The chapter's spine is a biography of Qian Xuesen: Caltech protégé of Theodore von Kármán, Suicide Squad member and JPL co-founder, stripped of his clearance in the McCarthy panic of 1950, arrested, and in 1955 traded to Mao's China, where he built the Chinese missile and space programs — and then, in later decades, led military-backed research into qigong, "somatic science," and paranormal abilities, documented in declassified CIA/DIA files. The authors show that Soviet and Chinese generals studied ESP, psychokinesis, and remote viewing in parallel with military UFO analysis, mirroring Puthoff and Vallée in the West, and that the rationale for UFO secrecy — never let a rival learn what you know — is identical on both sides. The chapter then follows the state's qigong revival into the unintended birth of Falun Gong, Li Hongzhi's mass movement whose core doctrine holds that aliens are manipulating humanity, which Beijing crushed from 1999 even as a "UFO boom" swept China. The authors conclude that China's approach — treating UFOs as apolitical scientific phenomena studied by vetted academics — was in some ways an improvement on American "UFO denial," though both sides sidelined the consciousness aspect of the Phenomenon.

## Core claims
- The authors argue that Said's "Orientalism" has a Ufological parallel they call "Alienism": the "alien" is a screen for projection — "our worst fears, our most hopeful dreams, or an expression of our colonial anxieties."
- They claim that Russia and China each have their own Ufology and Ufologists, and that — despite cultural, religious, and political distance — their governments' approach to the Phenomenon has been "remarkably similar" to that of the US and other Western countries.
- They note a catch-22 in American UFO discourse: the UFO community demands disclosure, then distrusts whatever disclosure it gets — the same suspicion Americans apply to Soviet/Chinese data.
- They claim paranormal studies (ESP, psychokinesis, remote viewing) in the USSR ran roughly parallel to its military analysis of UFO sightings, and that the US quietly did the same (Puthoff, Vallée as touchstones of "intersectional" research).
- They argue that Qian Xuesen's expulsion from the US — driven by baseless Red Scare suspicion — was a self-inflicted catastrophe: America purged Parsons, Qian, Malina, even Oppenheimer, "and they were all replaced by Nazi rocket scientists. Actual Nazis."
- They claim that by the 1970s American intelligence noticed that in both Russia and China it was "Not the civilians. The generals." who were studying the paranormal.
- They argue that Marxist states, having stripped religion away, could pragmatically study paranormal phenomena as science ("Let's try works without faith and see how that goes"), rescuing them from "cultic hucksters" and weaponizing them against the West.
- They claim Chinese "somatic science" / "life science" — embracing qigong, ESP, PK, remote viewing, and qi-measurement technology — was framed within Marxist "spiritual civilization" doctrine and pursued at the highest levels of the military-industrial complex under the PLA, with Qian Xuesen "leading the charge."
- They claim the rationale for UFO secrecy has not changed in fifty-plus years: keep what you know from rivals, which means keeping it from your own citizens, because you don't know what would be valuable to another nation's scientists.
- They argue the UFO–paranormal connection "has always been there" in the historical record: sky gods have paranormal powers, abductees report mental-image communication, and General Ramey linked saucers to Biblical episodes and psychic phenomena in 1952. Psychic abilities may be "convenient media for the Others to employ" — and the Others "seem to employ those media better than we do," a sobering national-security implication.
- They contrast East and West on psychic ability: in Asia it is cultivable through technique (yoga, pranayama, tantra, alchemy, qigong); in the West it is considered inherent in some people and not others.
- They argue the state's rehabilitation of qigong "inadvertently opened the door" to exactly what Marxism sought to eradicate: a competing belief system, Falun Gong.
- They claim Falun Gong is a UFO religion in the lineage of Mormonism and Scientology, with alien manipulation of humanity central to Li Hongzhi's philosophy — closer to Scientology's Xenu than to anything in Daoist scripture.
- They state that both sides of the "alien invasion" theory — evil aliens with designs on Earth, and benevolent aliens saving us from ourselves — "are equally wrong," which "may be much closer to the truth than we realize."
- They argue China's tactic — vetted, credentialed, officially sanctioned UFO study — elevated the subject to respectability and beat the American approach of ridicule and "UFO denial," though in both countries the consciousness aspect lost out to "nuts and bolts."
- They claim governments cannot come clean about the Phenomenon because admitting what they don't know "would challenge their right to govern": "A government that cannot protect its people is not worthy of governing."
- They note Christian Evangelical elements in the US government and Pentagon link UFOs to demons — a factor absent in antireligious China and the former USSR, though Russia has since moved from October Revolution Marxism-Leninism toward an officially embraced Russian Orthodoxy under Putin (alongside occultist gadflies like Aleksandr Dugin), while still tending to separate superstition from science; the authors conclude "the jury is still out" on which attitude is most reliable for understanding the Phenomenon.
- They observe that after 2017 (AATIP, Navy videos), American Ufologists' "fantasy world became the real world," leading some to conclude "that other fantasies might be real, too" — fertile ground for Epoch Times–style alt-right conspiracy culture.

## Concepts
- **Alienism** — The authors' coinage, modeled on Said's Orientalism: the Ufological habit of making the "alien" into anything we need it to be — "a projection of our worst fears, our most hopeful dreams, or an expression of our colonial anxieties," from bug-eyed monsters to Grays to angelic Venusians.
- **Orientalism** — Edward Said's characterization, cited by the authors: a Western fantasy of the East as "mystical, barbaric, and sensual: the realm of the 'Magic Asian.'" The authors trace its furniture to Marco Polo, Helena Blavatsky's Theosophy, Rudyard Kipling's "white man's burden," Richard Burton's Thousand and One Nights, the Kama Sutra and the Prayer Mat of Flesh, the I Jing and The Serpent Power, and figures like Lobsang Rampa, Alexandra David-Néel, Fu Manchu, and Suzie Wong — travelers and works that romanticized or fictionalized the East, shrouded in racist tropes.
- **Foreign devils / white ghosts** — Imperial China's terms for Europeans, literally regarding them as "not quite human, a being somewhere between the world of humans and the world of spirits" — the East's own version of alienizing the Other.
- **Project Bluebird** — CIA special access project approved April 20, 1950 (three years into the agency's existence) to understand and combat brainwashing, prompted by the Mindszenty show trial; it lasted "in one form or another for the next twenty-five years."
- **Brainwashing** — The "targeted form of psychological manipulation" the West encountered in Mindszenty and in Korea: "the wiping of an individual soldier's mind and replacing it with something else, turning a patriotic citizen into a turncoat," as against the "shotgun approach" of ordinary propaganda.
- **Suicide Squad** — The rocketry group of Jack Parsons, Frank Malina, Ed Forman, Qian Xuesen and others at Caltech; its members later founded JPL, and it was "broken up and scattered to the winds" by the Red Scare.
- **Somatic science / life science** — The Chinese government's official English terms for a research field spanning qigong, meditation, ESP, PK, and remote viewing, emphasizing the physical (somatic) over the spiritual; per a CIA-released document it "applies Systems Theory to study of the human body and environmental interactions" and examines three inter-related areas: Paranormal Capabilities, Traditional Chinese Medicine, and Qigong. Also called "Enhanced Human Body Functions."
- **Functional states / paranormal state** — Somatic-science doctrine that the body can assume different "functional states," one of which is the "paranormal state."
- **Multi-state physics** — Liu Yicheng's proposed "multi-state matrix for physics" in which matter has two or more states, "normal and extraordinary, that branch away from each other, yet under a fixed condition, be interchanged"; the normal state is stable but "very easy to move to the extraordinary state."
- **Spiritual civilization** — Chinese Marxist doctrine (quoted from Guangming Ribao) that a high degree of material civilization is impossible without a high degree of spiritual civilization; studying "life science" both requires and serves its construction.
- **Qi / prana** — The life-force itself; qi (Chinese) is "considered to be roughly equivalent to prana in the Indian system," and Chinese practice teaches its control.
- **Qigong** — "The Chinese art of the cultivation of qi," an aspect of traditional Chinese medicine; Qian Xuesen insisted it was "the key to understanding powerful abilities locked within the human body," with military and aerospace applications. The CIA separately tracked decorated military scientists in China and Russia growing involved with UFO groups and research agendas even as their militaries studied the paranormal, and noted Qian "openly discussing applying traditional Chinese medicine and Chinese philosophy to enhance the ability of the Chinese army to conduct a form of psychic warfare."
- **Siddhis** — "The Sanskrit term for magical abilities" attained alongside spiritual states through yoga, pranayama, tantra, and Chinese/Indian alchemical traditions — the Asian view that paranormal powers can be produced and magnified by technique.
- **Psychic Magnification Effect** — Subject of a Chinese study of psychics who could "magnify the cells at the acupuncture point to the size of cherries," determine the existence of a cell membrane, and watch material exchange in and out of cells at the acupuncture point.
- **The UFO Gap** — Title of J. Allen Hynek's Playboy article, a play on the Cold War "missile gap": the fear that the Russians might solve the UFO mystery — or make first contact — before America.
- **Falun Gong (Dharma Wheel Work)** — Li Hongzhi's 1992 qigong school: precise practice steps plus a philosophy of mental and spiritual cultivation including paranormal abilities (even remote viewing); initially state-praised, later banned (books 1996, declared a cult July 1999) once it became "a competing belief system" with millions of adherents outside party control.
- **Cultivation practice** — Li Hongzhi's aim, per his Time interview: not health or longevity but higher consciousness — "one can achieve a divine status through cultivation practice while one is still alive."
- **Supersoldiers** — The PLA's early-1990s program cultivating soldiers with qigong and martial arts in their curriculum (e.g., breaking bricks with their hands) — paranormal ability under state control, as opposed to Falun Gong's "entire population of uncontrolled and unregulated remote viewers."
- **Alien invasion theory** — The two-sided popular theory: aliens as evil/hostile with designs on Earth, versus aliens as benevolent saviors; the authors say "both positions are equally wrong."
- **Xenu / thetan** — Scientology's alien overlord and the "soul of a being slaughtered by Xenu to reduce overpopulation" — the authors' closest analogue to Falun Gong's evil-alien doctrine.
- **UFO religions** — The authors' category for Mormonism, Scientology, and now Falun Gong: religions in which alien worlds or beings are doctrinally central.
- **UFO denial** — The American approach of ignoring the problem altogether, lumping UFO groups with New Agers, Wiccans, crystal gazers, and Tarot readers, producing "a collective devaluation of all these systems in public opinion."
- **Mutually assured destruction (MAD)** — The Cold War doctrine defining the paranoid era of proxy wars, U-2 flights, the Bay of Pigs, and the Cuban Missile Crisis, when the world seemed destined for "a centuries long nuclear winter."
- **507 Institute (ISME)** — The military-run Institute of Space Medico-Engineering in Beijing, which produced the 1987 Zhang Baosheng PK film; its Spaceflight Department prize signaled military support of PSI research.
- **AATIP** — The program whose 2017 revelation, with the US Navy UFO videos, made American Ufologists' "fantasy world" real — with the side effect of validating other fantasies too.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Qian Xuesen's American career (1911–1950)** — Born Hangzhou, December 11, 1911; mechanical engineering degree, Jiao Tong University, Shanghai, 1934; MIT on scholarship 1935; Caltech doctorate 1939 under von Kármán; Suicide Squad member; one of three rocket scientists who in 1943 formed what became JPL in response to the German V2 threat; accompanied von Kármán to Germany to debrief von Braun's Peenemünde/Mittelwerk scientists; held top-secret clearance and rank equivalent to a US Army Air Force colonel; "widely considered a genius on the same level as Goddard, von Braun."
- **Qian's fall and expulsion (1950–1955)** — Clearance revoked June 6, 1950 after a confrontation with LAPD's Red Squad; questioned by FBI; told Navy under secretary Dan Kimball he would leave rather than build weapons to be used against the Chinese; Kimball refused to let him go; arrested August 1950, roughly five years of house arrest; October 1955, exchanged for Korean War POWs and welcomed in Beijing. DeLauer called it "a disaster... a tremendous loss for us, and a great gain for China"; Kimball called it the "stupidest thing this country ever did." Qian went on to the Chinese missile/space programs, designing the Silkworm missile "used so effectively by Saddam Hussein's Iraq" and developing the Long March rockets.
- **The Mindszenty show trial (1949)** — Cardinal Josef Mindszenty televised confessing to crimes he had not committed, with "weird appearance, glassy eyes, and general lack of affect" indicating psychological conditioning; the CIA took note and approved Project Bluebird on April 20, 1950 — six months before the Korean War.
- **McCarthy and the Red Scare** — February 1950 West Virginia speech claiming a list of "known Communists" in the State Department; earlier support for commuting sentences of SS officers responsible for the Malmedy massacre of American POWs; anti-Semitic remarks earning KKK admiration; a roughly five-year era of blacklists, hunts for Communists and homosexuals (deemed blackmail-vulnerable), and Hollywood purges. The authors' point: no one waved lists of "the names of hundreds of Nazis working for the Department of Defense."
- **The purge-and-replace pattern** — Parsons out (suspended from JPL September 1948 over Crowley/OTO involvement; died in the 1952 Pasadena explosion, having been about to do for Israel what Qian did for China), Qian out, Malina fled to Europe, Oppenheimer stripped of clearance on "mere baseless suspicion" — while von Braun, Dornberger, Strughold, "actual Nazis," were brought in.
- **The 1947–1949 timeline** — Project Sign begins 1947 after the Pacific Northwest sightings, with von Kármán a member "as extant memos demonstrate"; January 1948 Captain Mantell dies pursuing a UFO over the Midwest; July–August 1948 the Rosenbergs arrested; 1949 Project Sign shut down "largely because its members believed the UFOs to be real, and to represent a nonterrestrial technology," replaced by Project Grudge, tasked with discrediting sightings; May 22, 1949 Forrestal's suicide from a hospital window ("numerous raised eyebrows"); August 29, 1949 first Soviet atomic bomb; September 21, 1949 Mao defeats Chiang Kai-shek.
- **The von Braun quote (January 1, 1959)** — The same day Castro took Havana, West German paper Neues Europa published a von Braun interview: "We find ourselves faced with powers which are far stronger than hitherto assumed and whose base is at present unknown to us. More than that I cannot say at present."
- **WWII occult precedents** — The British employed astrologers and magicians not from belief but for insight into the adversary's mindset; Rudolf Hess, mysticism devotee, flew solo to England before the invasion of Russia, after which German occult societies were banned and leaders sent to camps (imprisoned in Spandau, Hess claimed telepathic communication with his wife); Himmler's Ahnenerbe; Andrija Puharich openly pitching weaponized paranormal to the US Army during the Korean War.
- **The Guangming Ribao article** — Beijing article placing "life science" in a Marxist "Socialist spiritual civilization" frame; its English translation "appears in an unclassified CIA document, approved for release on August 10, 2000."
- **Liu Yicheng's paper (November 1989, Beijing)** — "Preliminary Query into Extraordinary Human Body Functions and Multi-State Physics," Academica Sinica High-Energy Physics Research Institute, prepared for the China Society of Somatic Science; proposes multi-state physics. Related literature includes chapter headings like "The Effects of the Practice of Qigong on the EEG" and "Effects of Practicing Qigong on Cerebral Hemograms."
- **CIA "highly evaluated" source (May 1992)** — Confirmed announcement of a "New Chinese Academy of Parapsychology" and submitted "procedures for correspondence with Chinese parapsychologists."
- **CIA chronology of Chinese PSI interest** — "Chronology of Recent Interest in Exceptional Functions of the Human Body in the People's Republic of China," 1978–1990, thirty-six entries over four pages; includes Dr. Stanley Krippner (Saybrook Institute) visiting China in October 1981, and a 1985 experiment by Hal Puthoff to duplicate tests conducted by Chinese parapsychologists in 1982.
- **The Zhang Baosheng PK film (1987)** — The military-run ISME/507 Institute in Beijing filmed Zhang Baosheng as PK subject, showing "a marked medical pill passing through glass"; the film won a second-class "Scientific Research Achievement Prize" from the Spaceflight Department — evidence of military support for PSI research.
- **The CIA "Parapsychological Research in the PRC" presentation** — Undated, PowerPoint-like document: West "more skeptical and less supportive," East "generally ahead of West in achieving credible results"; names the field Enhanced Human Body Functions / Somatic Science; includes Qian's photo captioned "Led effort to develop application of system science to study of parapsychological phenomena" and his prediction that somatic science may bring a 21st-century revolution greater than quantum physics and relativity. By the early 1990s, funded PSI research was confirmed to be under the control of the Chinese army, dating back to the mid-1970s when the subject was still controversial in China; by 1990 it had become almost respectable, not least through the backing of the man they called "China's Einstein": Qian Xuesen himself.
- **Qian's death and the Moon landing symbolism** — Qian died October 31, 2009 — Halloween, and the anniversary of the Suicide Squad's first rocket test in the Arroyo Seco outside Pasadena. On January 2, 2019 China landed its first rocket on the far side of the Moon — the side with the crater named after Jack Parsons — specifically on the crater named after Theodore von Kármán, Qian's old mentor. The authors: because Qian was "ostracized and condemned—without evidence—as a Communist... China was able to achieve the impossible within fifty-five years."
- **The rise and suppression of Falun Gong** — Li Hongzhi founds his school 1992; initially praised; reasonable fees, later free (funded by book sales), charity donations; rival schools accuse him of creating a "qigong monopoly"; he leaves the state-run China Qigong Research Society as Party involvement is demanded; books banned 1996; declared a cult July 1999; arrests, torture-deaths, executions; open demonstrations including in Tiananmen Square "barely ten years after the infamous crackdown on students in 1989"; by 2000–2001, rumors that Falun Gong was an American-intelligence creation to destabilize China; Jiang Zemin (president from 1993) believed it was infiltrating the Party and ordered mass roundups with conversion-type methods; at its height an estimated 60–100 million practitioners in China.
- **Li Hongzhi's alien doctrine (Time, May 10, 1999)** — Li: "I did not want to cure illnesses or to help people keep fit"; aliens "have begun to invade the human mind and its ideology and culture" since the start of the century, come from other planets and undiscovered dimensions, "introduced modern machinery like computers and airplanes," aim "to replace humans," make earthlings have wars, some look human, and "U.S. technology has already detected some aliens."
- **Li in exile** — Touring Europe and the US from the mid-1990s, absent during the suppression; set up in upstate New York; founded the Epoch Times ("an organ for far-right politics and extreme conspiracy theories," involved in the 2016 Trump campaign); Shen Yun, the touring performance company, is also his organization's creation.
- **The Chinese "U.F.O. Boom" (1999–2000)** — New York Times article, January 11, 2000: dozens of sightings across China from about September 1999, recorded and studied by official government research institutes, drawing PLA and especially Chinese Air Force attention — at the very height of Falun Gong suppression. Chinese UFO organizations were officially sanctioned, with membership requiring "a college degree and published research."
- **China's recent official UFO program** — Announced, per the authors, perhaps "inspired... or even threatened" by the US government's tentative new embrace of Ufology; stops short of "aliens," admits unknown phenomena, and uses advanced AI to track, analyze, and identify them.
- **The 1952 Washington, D.C. press briefing callback** — The authors recall the famous US Air Force briefing where "the generals were invoking Biblical episodes and psychic phenomena in relation to flying saucers" — General Ramey connecting UFOs to the paranormal before "alien abduction" was a household term.
- **The crash-retrieval hypothetical (endnote-sourced block quote)** — A quoted hypothetical: if major adversaries had "access to crash-retrieved craft or materials," each would close-hold its findings and press to learn the other's, since any revealed tidbit could hand a rival "a disruptive technology breakthrough" — "a sin of the first water," met with "draconian steps."

## Thread
This chapter widens the book's lens beyond America-centric Ufology, delivering on the authors' recurring insistence that "we all will have to learn from each other where 'aliens' and 'invasions' and colonialism are concerned in order to prepare ourselves for contact with the others." It picks up threads laid earlier in the book — Russian UFO interference with missile bases, the Voronezh incident and Vallée (Chapter 8 per the text), Qian's first mention (Chapter 4), von Kármán's ties to Parsons and the Rosenberg ring, Puharich's Korean War pitch, the 1952 Washington briefing, and the first volume Gods' "resilient web of ideas" linking sky gods to paranormal powers — and braids them into a single East–West argument: the paranormal and the UFO have always been studied together, in secret, by militaries on both sides, for the same national-security reasons. The Qian arc doubles as an indictment of the Red Scare's self-harm (Nazis in, homegrown geniuses out), a theme of the book's WWII chapters. The Falun Gong story dramatizes the book's central thesis about secrecy, control, and governance: a state can tolerate UFOs as apolitical science but not an unregulated mass movement of aspiring psychics with an alien-manipulation theology. The chapter also plants a forward pointer — that both poles of the alien-invasion theory are "equally wrong" — to be explored later (the text says "as we will explore in Chapter 11"), and its closing image of post-2017 Ufology sliding toward conspiracy politics sets up the Afterword's "Alien in the Panopticon" concerns about secrecy, belief, and control.
