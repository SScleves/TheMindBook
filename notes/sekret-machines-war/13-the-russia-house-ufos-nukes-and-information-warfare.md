# The Russia House: UFOs, Nukes, and Information Warfare

## In one paragraph
In this chapter (numbered 8 in the book), DeLonge & Levenda pivot from individual consciousness to *group* consciousness, arguing that America's recurring vulnerability to mass hysteria and conspiracy panic — from Salem in 1692 through Pizzagate and QAnon — has been weaponized by Russian "information warfare," and that this degraded information environment is exactly the opening a UFO "control mechanism" would exploit. They then survey the Soviet/Russian side of the UFO story: the 1989 Voronezh landing reported by TASS, Colonel Boris Sokolov's revelations of a decade-long secret Soviet UFO study, KGB Major General Vasiliy Yeremenko's account of "summoning" UFOs at a test range, and the October 4, 1982 Belokoroviche incident in which a UFO allegedly initiated (then aborted) a launch sequence at a Ukrainian nuclear missile base. The authors' central puzzle is that the USSR and the USA handled UFO incidents in eerily identical ways — publicize, then discredit with the same tactics — for over seventy years, which they say makes no sense unless both powers faced a common threat transcending even nuclear confrontation. They close by linking Soviet ESP/"biological fields" research to the Phenomenon's apparent control over human perception, and by using Superman as a parable for an Alien that controls how it is seen.

## Core claims
- The authors claim American culture has a "basic vulnerability... to paranoia and mass hysteria," running in an unbroken line from the Salem witch trials (1692) through the Illuminati scare, the Masonic scare, anarchist panics, the McCarthy-era Red Scare, the satanic-ritual-abuse scare, birtherism, Pizzagate, and QAnon — and that this vulnerability "is capable of exploitation and manipulation" in the age of mass communication.
- They claim Russia deliberately exploited this vulnerability: the 2016 WikiLeaks dump of John Podesta's hacked emails was "the result — we now know — of a Russian disinformation project to destabilize the democratic systems of the United States," continuing a lineage that runs from the Tsarist-fabricated *Protocols of the Learned Elders of Zion* (1903) through Soviet *disinformatsiya*.
- They argue the Internet has become "a moral substitute" and "the new 'book'": the abdication of personal responsibility to it "has opened a door in the human psyche through which anything at all might fly in," making identity malleable and enabling a "group mind."
- They claim the devaluation of fact and truth is "the perfect set of conditions for the UFO to reveal itself to the world," letting the control mechanism (per Vallée's hypothesis) "enter a new phase of activity" — and that cyber-warfare infrastructure lets the Phenomenon "slip into our increasingly interrelated and interdependent systems" like the Joker in a deck of cards (a callback to *Sekret Machines: Man*).
- They claim the Voronezh incident (1989) was handled by the USSR exactly as Roswell (1947) was handled by the USA — official confirmation, then walk-back to mundane explanations — and pose the question: "What type of intelligence exercise can continue for more than seventy years, virtually unmodified... in widely divergent — even hostile — regimes?"
- They reject the "paranoid" answer of an international Illuminati-style cabal ("would be like trying to herd cats"), and also flag as "equally incredible" the alternative that national leaders are controlled by extraterrestrial intelligences — yet insist the US/Russia parallelism "does not make sense" without a common threat "that transcended even the very real danger of nuclear confrontation."
- They claim the same two nuclear superpowers were also the two nations that most heavily researched parapsychology (CIA's Bluebird, Artichoke, MK-ULTRA; the military's Stargate remote-viewing programs; Soviet equivalents), and hypothesize that Ufology and psychic research "may be linked."
- They claim the physical and psychological evidence are mutually dependent: "the physical evidence is not decipherable without the psychological, and vice versa."
- They claim that if the human body radiates readable/writable fields (as Soviet and Chinese research asserted), the Phenomenon may possess "psychotronics" as advanced as its aeronautics — "light-years ahead of us" — which would explain contactee experiences and the Phenomenon's control over how it appears ("They want us to see what they want us to see"). "This is how you win a war without firing a shot."
- They claim US progress is hampered by "medieval emotional responses and Inquisition-era paranoia" — religiously tinged resistance in the Pentagon and Congress that equates studying UFOs or remote viewing with studying the occult — producing a "UFO gap" and a "remote viewing gap" alongside the missile gap.
- They note an unintended consequence of the Podesta dump: it "did demonstrate to the world that DeLonge was truthful about his claims to be working with high-ranking individuals in the US government and military" on UFOs, preceding his stronger 2017 organization and the December 2017 New York Times AATIP revelations.

## Concepts
- **Information warfare** — the term the Internet Research Agency itself used (per the March 2019 report epigraph on Russian election interference) for its social-media campaign "designed to provoke and amplify political and social discord in the United States"; the chapter's frame for state manipulation of group consciousness.
- **World view warfare** — a "sophisticated approach to propaganda" the authors attribute to the Germans: "It was only necessary to convince an enemy that 'everything they knew was wrong' to completely demoralize and disarm its people."
- **Disinformatsiya / disinformation** — the Soviet honing of world-view warfare; later defined as "a term created by the Russians during the Cold War to categorize deliberately planted false data to confuse an enemy or to trace the path of a false story to see where it lands and through which people or organizations, as a counterintelligence measure."
- **Deep state** — what suspicious citizens call "the unelected officials and workers of the federal government and its agencies (without which there would be no functioning government)"; to post-2016 QAnon conspiracists, "the sekret machine is the deep state itself."
- **Pizzagate** — the 2016 conspiracy theory, born of "kabbalistic" decoding of Podesta's hacked emails, that a Washington pizzeria owner ran a baby-sacrificing satanic cult from a basement (which the pizzeria did not have), attended by Clinton and Podesta; the authors present it as the latest iteration of an old American panic template.
- **Control mechanism** — Jacques Vallée's suggestion (endorsed conditionally by the authors) that the UFO Phenomenon functions as a mechanism of control over human belief; the Internet-degraded information environment "inadvertently provided it with a sophisticated means for advancing its designs upon us."
- **Internet as moral substitute** — the authors' coinage for outsourcing judgment: "Unsure if a given statement is true or not...? Ask the Internet"; personal responsibility gives way to a "seemingly impartial judge."
- **Group mind / group consciousness** — the chapter's shift of focus from individual consciousness to collective: permeable mental borders and eroding liminality between online tribes create "larger and larger communities based on the lowest common denominators between them. A lynch mob of 'shamans' and 'light workers.'"
- **Simulacrum** — the "clever, near-perfect" replacement of the physical world by prefabricated images, deep fakes, and photoshopped creations as collective consciousness adapts to digital flows.
- **Glasnost** — Gorbachev-era "openness" that allowed the Voronezh sightings to be reported by TASS even while Soviet media censorship "was still a reality."
- **UMMO** — a 1970s–80s European/Latin American movement claiming verified alien contact through "a steady stream of suspicious documentation"; "That it was a hoax seems certain," yet its symbol (similar to the astrological sign for Uranus) appeared in witness drawings at Voronezh — leading Vallée to conclude he faced "a genuine phenomenon but one that was strangely tainted with hoaxed material."
- **Psywar exercise** — the suspicious-observer reading of Voronezh/Roswell-style handling: incidents "orchestrated" as psychological-warfare exercises; the authors' problem with it is its seventy-year, cross-regime invariance.
- **International cabal hypothesis** — the "paranoid response" that a cabal with access to the highest levels of both governments manages the disinformation campaign; the authors reject it as fantasy ("like trying to herd cats").
- **Extraterrestrial hypothesis (ETH)** — the standard alien-origin explanation; the authors say their consciousness/psychic linkage "is not to deny the ETH but to raise other questions."
- **Nuts-and-bolts vs. consciousness aspect** — the book's running dyad, here glossed as the "machine" versus the "sekret": physical craft, debris, and nervous-system effects are "inextricable" from the consciousness aspect.
- **"Summoning" UFOs / heightened tension** — the Soviet military finding (per Yeremenko) that UFOs appear in areas of "heightened tension" (weapons tests, massed hardware), so staging apparent tension baited them: "Then UFOs appeared with a probability of nearly 100 percent."
- **Vortex rings** — the propulsion concept the 2007 Pravda article claimed the USSR used to build its own version of supposedly American-made UFOs.
- **Vertical takeoff and landing / Zimmer Skimmer** — Chance Vought's design interest (aircraft flying from ship decks) and Charles Zimmerman's disk-shaped, propeller-driven experimental concept behind the V-173 "Flying Pancake" and XF5U "Flying Flapjack."
- **Ziegel Readings** — a biannual Moscow conference on paranormal phenomena/UFOs, named for astronomer-cosmologist Felix Ziegel (1920–1988), "the founder of Ufology in Russia."
- **Biological fields / ESP mechanism** — the Soviet (and Chinese) theory that the body generates infrared, magnetic, acoustic, bioluminescent and other fields carrying readable information, making the body "not only a receiver of data but a transmitter"; the proposed physical basis of ESP.
- **Psychotronics / psychotronic devices** — Soviet devices claimed to act on or read these fields; "the side effects were such that their use was either discontinued or dramatically reduced." The authors extend the term to the Phenomenon's hypothesized perception-control technology.
- **UFO gap / remote-viewing gap** — the authors' coinage, by analogy with the "missile gap," for America falling behind because conservative Christian congressmen deemed such research "demonic."
- **Übermensch / Superman parable** — the "super man" idea revered by the Nazis versus DC's patriotic undocumented Alien; the authors use Clark Kent as the model of an Alien species "in total control over how they appear and therefore in control of our perceptions of them."
- **Blood libel** — the weaponized lie against the Jews by the Tsarist government and Christian communities, cited as precedent for state-manufactured fantasy.
- **AATIP** — the Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program, the Pentagon's buried UFO investigative agency revealed by the New York Times in December 2017.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The Podesta email hack and Pizzagate (2016)** — WikiLeaks dumped emails hacked from Clinton campaign manager John Podesta in late summer 2016; conspiracy theorists "decoded" them into claims that a Connecticut Avenue NW pizzeria owner ran a satanic, infant-sacrificing cult from a nonexistent basement, attended by Clinton and Podesta. The authors use it as the model modern panic and as verified Russian disinformation output; its unintended consequence was confirming DeLonge's claimed government contacts (Podesta among them).
- **The American panic lineage** — Salem witch trials (1692), where accusations reached the Massachusetts governor's wife before the trials were terminated and even Tituba was released; the Illuminati scare of the late 1700s (Washington himself — a Freemason — addressed the rumors in correspondence); the Masonic scare of the early 1800s (today's GOP descends from a party formed to elect an anti-Mason president); anarchist/"mad bomber" panics; the McCarthy-era Red Scare (Parsons, Oppenheimer — per earlier chapters); the late-1970s/early-1980s satanic-ritual-abuse scare (claims inflated from thousands to millions of children with "no evidence... ever unearthed"); Tea Party birtherism against Obama; and QAnon. Shown as one recurring exploitable pattern.
- **Post–Civil War grievance and assassinations** — the authors compare unreconciled Confederates to post-WWI Germans; conspiracy theory thrived and "led to the assassination of Abraham Lincoln in 1865"; the JFK assassination in Dallas, "almost one hundred years later," founded the modern rejection of government secrecy.
- **The Protocols of the Learned Elders of Zion (1903)** — Tsarist secret-police fraud used to justify pogroms; brought by Alfred Rosenberg to Hitler as justification for the Third Reich's worst excesses; cited as factual by Henry Ford; "still believed by millions."
- **Cardinal Mindszenty show trial** — recap of the earlier chapters' brainwashing example: he was made to confess to crimes he did not commit, "thereby convincing a wider audience of his guilt" — Soviet manipulation of consciousness at its apotheosis.
- **The Voronezh incident (Sept 22–Oct 2, 1989)** — in the ten days after the autumnal equinox, TASS reported a series of UFO sightings about three hundred miles southeast of Moscow, including a landed sphere whose occupants (a humanoid "three or four meters tall" with a disproportionately small head, plus a small "robot") walked through a local park. Vallée went to Moscow, met the Soviet Academy of Sciences, verified accounts and physical ground traces, and "came away... with the firm conviction that the sightings did take place as described" from no identifiable Russian technology. The New York Times (Oct 11, 1989, sec. A p. 6, "U.F.O. Landing is Fact, Not Fantasy, the Russians Insist") relayed TASS's insistence: a glowing red sphere, a nine-foot-tall, three-eyed humanoid and robot seen by three children and gathering adults; the humanoid pointed a device at a screaming boy, who vanished and reappeared after the humanoid returned to the ship. Also reported in Sovetskaya Kultura (official line). Then Russian scientists waffled and the matter was dropped — the same arc as Roswell (flying saucer → weather balloon → Operation Mogul balloon). The authors add the parallel Heilongjiang sighting in China ~five years later with three-meter-tall Aliens.
- **The UMMO symbol at Voronezh** — witnesses' drawings included the infamous UMMO symbol (resembling the astrological sign for Uranus) on an alien vehicle, despite UMMO being an almost certainly hoaxed contact movement; Vallée's verdict: genuine phenomenon "strangely tainted with hoaxed material. For what purpose?"
- **Pravda's "American-made UFOs" article (March 31, 2007)** — an English-language piece claiming US-built UFOs "invaded" the USSR in the 1980s, that the Soviets built their own based on "vortex rings," and that human manufacture renders alien intelligence moot. It traced the American saucer to the Vought "Skimmer" lineage: Chance M. Vought (1890–1930), aviation pioneer who worked with the Wright brothers, whose VE-7 was the first aircraft to take off from a ship, and whose company later built Zimmerman's V-173 (1942, "Flying Pancake") and XF5U (1943, "Flying Flapjack" — only two ever made). The article leapt from these to submarine-launched UFOs with "impulse electromagnets" (too heavy to fly from the US), explaining sightings of UFOs rising from the waters. The authors call this the same pretzel-rationalization the US government uses.
- **Boris Sokolov's revelations (1993/1996) and reversal (2000)** — the Ministry of Defense colonel, interviewed in Moscow by journalist George Knapp in 1996, revealed a genuine Soviet UFO study dating to the late 1960s/early 1970s: Russian warplanes engaged the craft, one fighter collided with a UFO and was damaged, and thousands of sighting reports accumulated over his ten years in charge, kept secret until 1993. Slammed by officials for saying too much, he later refused to discuss it and in 2000 coauthored an article with Dr. Yulii Platov dismissing sightings as natural causes and rocket tests.
- **Smetanina's Russia Beyond the Headlines article (April 2013)** — "Former KGB agent reveals Soviet UFO studies": the KGB took sightings seriously and knew not all were explicable; decades of official "paranormal phenomena" study included UFOs; quotes retired Major General Vasiliy Yeremenko, head of the KGB (now FSB) division for aircraft manufacturing — the same unit collating UFO sightings — who revealed the official protocol: avoid anything that would "invite aggression from a UFO."
- **The Astrakhan "summoning" experiment (early 1980s)** — per Yeremenko: having realized UFOs appear at areas of "heightened tension," the military staged tension (increased combat-aircraft flights) at a range in Astrakhan Region; "Then UFOs appeared with a probability of nearly 100 percent." Soldiers attempted communication by waving arms in patterns (twice right, twice left); the UFO responded by "swinging twice to the right and then twice to the left." Yeremenko said they had no idea why or how. The authors note the resemblance to Close Encounters of the Third Kind's climax with colored lights, music, and Kodály hand-signing of tones.
- **The Belokoroviche incident (October 4, 1982)** — at a nuclear missile base in Soviet Ukraine (near Belokoroviche, west of Kyiv, locatable in a heavily redacted CIA document on Soviet ICBM sites), a disk-shaped UFO — about nine hundred feet in diameter, "the size of a five-story house," seen by roughly a thousand officers and men — hovered for nearly an hour and lit up the control panels signaling imminent launch of all missiles, with officers helpless. After fifteen seconds the controls reset and the launch aborted ("evidently by the UFO, not by the missile base commander") as the UFO disappeared. All the codes-keys-and-devices safeguards were "bypassed at once by an outside source." Context: Ukraine then hosted roughly 30 percent of all Soviet missiles; after the fall it denuclearized, and a museum with a hardened control center and miles of tunnels commemorates the era.
- **The Nimitz "Tic Tac" parallel (2004)** — the authors compare Belokoroviche to Commander Fravor's USS Nimitz incident, where UFOs knew in advance the F-18 Hornets' rendezvous point — "chosen by computer algorithm and obviously also heavily encrypted" — implying capability beyond "the most sophisticated hacker," or "another method... for which we are hopelessly unprepared."
- **The Dalnegorsk joint study (May 1990)** — per a declassified FBI report dated May 21, 1990 ("USSR, PRC Scientists in Joint Study of UFO's"), the USSR and China met at the seaside town of Dalnegorsk (northeast of Vladivostok, on the Sea of Japan), chosen for its high incidence of UFO sightings, to develop a joint strategy for investigating "anomalous phenomena."
- **The John Alexander / Walt LaBerge episode** — Alexander, a retired US Army colonel, formed a committee to discover whether a UFO "cabal" existed within the US government (prompted by the Cold War discovery that the Soviets took UFOs seriously). Briefing Pentagon specialists on military space use, he discussed what UFO sightings might imply about unconventional aeronautics when Dr. Walt LaBerge (1924–2004) — aerospace engineer, under secretary of the Army 1977–1980, co-developer of the Sidewinder missile, later a Lockheed executive — erupted, slammed the table, and shouted: "You're not supposed to know that. That's what you learn when you die!" The authors read this as proof that even elite scientists "harbor medieval emotional responses and Inquisition-era paranoia."
- **The Soviet ESP laboratory (revealed June 8, 1987)** — five years after Belokoroviche, a BBC radio interview revealed a special laboratory at the Institute of Radio Engineering and Electronics, USSR Academy of Sciences, studying "what is popularly known as ESP" — phenomena hinging on "the physical fields that are believed to be generated by biological objects." The Russians identified a spectrum of fields (infrared, magnetic, acoustic, bioluminescent, others), built psychotronic devices (curtailed for side effects), and scoured the country for individuals with demonstrable psychic abilities.
- **US mind-research programs** — Operations Bluebird, Artichoke, and MK-ULTRA (CIA missions "to uncover the secrets of the human mind using any means available, from hypnosis and suggestion to psychoactive drugs, invasive psychological conditioning, and occultism") and the military's Stargate remote-viewing programs — mirrored by Soviet programs of the same era; American versions faced pushback from conservative Christian congressmen who deemed them "demonic" even knowing the Soviets persisted.
- **Casualties of the "game"** — the authors cite ruined fortunes and credibility among duped Americans, "and in some cases (such as those of Dr. James McDonald and Morris Jessup) even suicide," as the human cost of the decades-long UFO disinformation pattern.
- **The Superman parable (from June 1938)** — Clark Kent as "the perfect spy, the ultimate agent provocateur": superhuman on Earth, ordinary on his home planet, in total control of how he appears. His enemy Lex Luthor — "a mad scientist and a businessman, the epitome of the military-industrial complex." Introduced months before WWII while Hitler ruled Germany; despite Nazi reverence for the Übermensch, DC's version defended "Truth, Justice, and the American Way." The authors ask whether a more clandestine struggle between "the Alien and the complex" is now underway — "If so, where do the rest of us stand? Better yet, where can we hide?"
- **The pragmatic third bloc** — the authors observe that countries outside the US/Russia blocs — "France, Belgium, Sweden, Chile, etc." — have governments "more reasonable and pragmatic in their approach to the Phenomenon."

## Thread
This chapter executes the book's announced turn from individual to group consciousness: earlier chapters established the consciousness aspect of the Phenomenon through individual cases (Parsons, Oppenheimer, Mindszenty's brainwashing, China's somatic science and the Heilongjiang case from the previous chapter); here the authors show the same dynamics at the scale of nations. It welds the book's information-warfare strand (Protocols, disinformatsiya, the 2016 election, Pizzagate/QAnon) to the UFO strand via the "control mechanism" hypothesis: a society that has surrendered fact-checking is soft terrain for both hostile states and the Phenomenon itself. The Soviet material — Voronezh, Sokolov, Yeremenko, Belokoroviche, Dalnegorsk — supplies the mirror image of the American cases (Roswell, the Nimitz Tic Tacs), producing the chapter's central anomaly: seventy years of identical publicize-then-discredit handling across hostile regimes, which the authors say implies a shared threat above nuclear war. The closing ESP/psychotronics section sets up the book's forthcoming paradigm — announced here as extending Vallée and Keel into genetics, AI, quantum mechanics, and "psychobiological" technologies — in which physical and psychological evidence must be read together. The Superman/Panopticon-adjacent ending (an Alien in control of how it is perceived) points directly toward the Afterword's "Alien in the Panopticon" thesis.
