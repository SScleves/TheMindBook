# Steampunk Dreams: Writers, Wizards, and the Alien

## In one paragraph
DeLonge & Levenda take the reader back to 1896–1897, when the "Great Airship" sightings swept from California across America and over the Atlantic to Russia — six years before the Wright brothers flew — and use that wave to argue that the Phenomenon predates flying-saucer media culture and manifests through the psychological content of each era. The airships looked like "steampunk dreams" (fantastic gasbag-propeller-wing hybrids appropriate to their time) yet maneuvered exactly like modern UFOs, so the authors propose the Phenomenon works through the consciousness of observers, using each culture as the "material basis" for its visible form. The chapter then walks through the figures who clustered uncannily around that same moment: Jules Verne and H. G. Wells imagining the technology and the invasion; Nikola Tesla receiving what he believed were planetary radio signals at Colorado Springs in 1899; Bram Stoker's Dracula (1897) prefiguring alien-abduction scenarios; H. P. Lovecraft's "Call of Cthulhu" (1926) describing an alien presence sensed first by artists and poets; and the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn, whose leader MacGregor Mathers claimed physical contact with superhuman "secret chiefs" weeks before the first airship sighting. Via Henri Bergson's "mechanics and mysticism," the authors argue that the UFO experience is simultaneously technology and mysticism — a spiritualized machine — and that consciousness is an active, missing element in our physical equations. The chapter closes by setting up the book's turn toward technology and magic together, quoting Bergson's line that the universe is "a machine for the making of gods."

## Core claims
- The authors claim the Great Airship sightings of 1896–1897 were real anomalous events, not mass fantasy or hoax: hundreds of witnesses at a time (including the governor of Kansas) saw craft that hovered, stopped suddenly, shot off at speed, and climbed vertically — maneuvers "still, even in the twenty-first century, impossible" — six years before heavier-than-air flight and three years before the first Zeppelin.
- They argue the airships "behaved like UFOs": the two constants across 1897 and modern sightings are impossible aeronautical maneuvers and a wide variety of vehicle designs, which they treat as two basic elements of the Phenomenon.
- They reject the academic skeptics' claim (in a modern text they quote) that witnesses "fantasized chimerical combinations," countering that there is no evidence for that assertion and that in 1897 there were no "real pilots" or Zeppelins for witnesses to model their reports on.
- Their central proposition: the Phenomenon works through the consciousness of observers, using the psychological content of each culture as the "material basis" that lets it manifest visibly — hence steampunk airships in 1897 and saucers later. The brain puts visual "clothing" around an observation with no prior context ("a kind of poetry of the cortex").
- Against the media-priming critique of saucer sightings, the authors say the 1897 wave proves UFOs existed long before flying-saucer stories and films; reports of unidentified aerial vehicles are "truly ancient" and always consistent with their time and place.
- They note the irony that Roswell 1947 was explained as a weather balloon and later a Project Mogul balloon — "maintaining a dismissive narrative from one generation to the next focusing on balloons as UFOs."
- They argue Tesla genuinely believed he received intelligent, regular signals of "planetary origin" in 1899, and that his caution about the near-impossibility of communicating with an extraterrestrial biology anticipated NASA's own 2014 expert study by over a century.
- They point out that NASA's study ignored an entire demographic: direct experiencers (Hynek's "close encounter of the third kind"), who consistently report communication — telepathic, image-based, mechanical-voiced, or awkwardly fluent — usually one-directional and usually carrying apocalyptic warnings, just as Biblical prophets did; this, they suggest, indicates religion and the Phenomenon are "the same type of event."
- They endorse Lovecraft's fictional premise as literally suggestive: if the Phenomenon is a fact of consciousness as much as of physics, its psychological effects should overflow into society at large, first through the sensitive — artists, musicians, writers.
- They claim Dracula (1897) shares the structure of later alien-abduction reports (nocturnal foreign being, flight, trance, bodily fluids taken, victims returned to bed), and that Lucy Westenra's not-asleep/not-awake state matches abductees' consciousness better than the "night terrors" explanation; John Mack's Harvard work showed abductees exhibit genuine post-traumatic stress.
- They argue consciousness is not merely brain function: memory has no dedicated brain site, so consciousness may be more like "the cloud" than a local air-gapped computer; the mechanical "wet computer" view describes pieces of consciousness, not the whole.
- On the Golden Dawn: they explicitly do NOT claim the secret chiefs are Aliens in spaceships or vice versa, but that a common thread runs through these ideas about how humans perceive the Other, and that consciousness is "an active component" of reality, making the humanities as important as the hard sciences for understanding the Phenomenon.
- Via Bergson: mechanism and mysticism are intertwined ("mechanism should mean mysticism"); ritual magic is Bergsonian mechanization applied to world and consciousness at once, and "the sekret machines themselves may be products of this work."
- The sightings' historical power comes from being a "perfect combination of physicality and 'spirituality'": purely physical, they would be mere invaders or secret weapons; purely spiritual, they would be dismissed as hallucination.

## Concepts
- **Great Airship sightings** — the 1896–1897 wave of sightings of strange airship-like vehicles ("a kind of hybrid" of steamship and dirigible) across North America and Europe, beginning in Sacramento in November 1896 and ending near Moscow in September 1897; the authors treat it as the Phenomenon manifesting in period-appropriate dress.
- **Steampunk** — a term coined in the 1980s for the aesthetic of elaborate piping, gauges, wheels, flywheels, gears, valves, and boilerplate representative of industrial-revolution steam machinery, familiar from Wild Wild West and Brazil; the 1897 airship designs were "steampunk dreams" before the genre had a name.
- **The Phenomenon** — the authors' umbrella term (carried through the series) for the UFO/contact reality; in this chapter defined as something that is "as much a fact of consciousness as it is of physical reality," working through observers' minds and producing cultural overflow beyond direct sightings.
- **The searchlight motif** — the one design element common to all 1897 airship reports regardless of configuration: a massive light switched on and aimed at the ground from rooftop or cornfield height; present from the very first Sacramento sighting.
- **Foo fighters** — WWII aerial anomalies seen by American pilots; the authors note regional design differences: colored spheres and strings of red/white/orange lights over Europe, but uniformly "red fireballs" over Japan.
- **Poetry of the cortex** — the authors' phrase for the brain's manufacture of images around an unprecedented experience: forced to describe something with no prior context, the mind puts visual "clothing" on it, like love-song lyrics equating eyes with "limpid pools," not meant literally.
- **Cultural filtering / material basis** — the proposition that the Phenomenon uses "the psychological content of each culture as the 'material basis' that allows it to manifest in some visible form," which is why vehicle descriptions are always consistent with their time and place.
- **War of the currents** — the struggle beginning around 1888 between Edison's direct current (DC) and Westinghouse's alternating current (AC) for the American electrical grid; Tesla's polyphase induction motor design tipped it to AC, declared the winner in 1892.
- **Interspecific communication problem** — Tesla's (and later NASA's) recognition that beings evolved under different gravitational, chemical, and electromagnetic conditions would perceive and signal so differently that communication would be nearly impossible; even a shared genetic code would produce vastly different beings in different environments — the authors note in passing that "at least one modern scientist has suggested an off-world origin for our own genetic code."
- **Close encounter of the third kind** — Dr. J. Allen Hynek's term, cited by the authors, for direct experience of alien contact.
- **Apocalyptic utterances** — the uniformly negative warnings (coming conflagration, world war, ecological damage) reported by contactees, which the authors compare to Biblical prophets' predictions as evidence that religion and the Phenomenon are the same type of event.
- **Secret chiefs** — the invisible, superhuman-yet-somewhat-human spiritual leaders of the Golden Dawn, believed to guide the destiny of the planet through sufficiently illuminated humans; only founder MacGregor Mathers claimed to have seen them.
- **Masters** — Madame Blavatsky's earlier version of the concept: spiritually advanced beings overseeing the planet who communicated with her (including by materializing letters from thin air); researchers have since identified many as actual persons she met during "Great Game"-era travels in Central Asia, India, Egypt, and Tibet.
- **Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn** — a secret initiatory society established in Great Britain in 1888 by influential Freemasons acting on coded instructions from a mysterious German lodge; its membership spanned artists, writers, mystics, and scientists, and its highest degrees were held by the secret chiefs, not by human leaders.
- **The Great Game** — the era of political adventurism in Central Asia and India during which Blavatsky met many of the figures later mythologized as Masters.
- **Static religion** — Henri Bergson's term for organized religion: a fixed, dogmatic, unchangeable set of beliefs and practices around which a society forms. The authors gloss: "religion is a building."
- **Dynamic religion** — Bergson's term for mysticism, the religious instinct in action and motion — what Mina and MacGregor Mathers practiced. The authors gloss: "mysticism is a machine."
- **Duration** — Bergson's concept combining causality and free will through a reinterpretation of time and consciousness; his philosophy also moved William James to abandon logic as the only metric of reality.
- **Mechanics and mysticism / spiritual impulsion** — Bergson's argument (from the section of that name) that machines extend the body's reach and therefore consciousness's reach, so that "the body, now larger, calls for a bigger soul, and that mechanism should mean mysticism"; mechanization has mystical origins.
- **Ritual magic** — in the authors' framing, "the application of the Bergsonian concept of mechanization to the real world and to consciousness simultaneously (these are not seen as separate in magic)"; it has its own internal logic and proceeds from a mystical impulse.
- **Sekret machines** — the series' term for the craft of the Phenomenon: otherworldly technology that is simultaneously technology and mysticism ("spiritualized machines"), possibly from a fixed point in space-time or from a parallel world penetrating ours unpredictably, thwarting scientific study; the authors add they "may be products of" magical work.
- **Lam** — the secret chief depicted in a painting by Aleister Crowley when he later claimed direct contact; the authors say the depiction is "virtually identical to that of the standard 'grey Alien.'"
- **Consciousness as the cloud** — the authors' analogy: since memory (key to self-identity) has no dedicated brain site, consciousness resembles cloud computing more than "a local, unconnected (air gap) personal computer" — against the "wet computer" model fashionable in AI circles.
- **Consciousness as orchestra** — a contemporary philosopher's metaphor the authors adopt: science analyzes individual instruments, but consciousness is the whole orchestra playing in concert — "a steampunk band" — detectable only by the unmeasurable factor that makes us human. The Golden Dawn magicians fancied themselves conductors; the secret chiefs supplied the sheet music.
- **Machine for the making of gods** — Bergson's closing image: fulfilling "the essential function of the universe," which he calls a machine for the making of gods, is the extra effort humanity must decide to make.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Sacramento, November 17, 1896 (~6 p.m.)** — first Great Airship sighting, reported by the Sacramento Bee: hundreds saw a strange light hovering and moving erratically east to southwest, seemingly avoiding obstacles, as if under intelligent control; speculation about a balloon with an "electric arc lamp" beneath, but its careful vertical and horizontal motion suggested otherwise. It also triggered deliberate hoaxes — "setting the pattern" for the mid-20th-century UFO situation.
- **Omaha, Nebraska, January 1897 and Topeka, Kansas, March 1897** — credible follow-on sightings; the Topeka object, described as a square red light, was witnessed by the governor of Kansas along with numerous citizens.
- **April 1897 midwestern wave** — Chicago and Evanston, Illinois; St. Louis; Minneapolis; Milwaukee; back to St. Louis: the airship seen by hundreds at a time, sometimes hovering at rooftop level, occasionally with occupants described in detail and conversations about current events such as the imminent war with Spain. Voices sometimes seemed loudspeaker-borne or electronically enhanced.
- **The Atlantic crossing** — by July 1897 sightings reached Sweden and Norway; the last of the period was September 26, 1897, at Veliky Ustyug, Russia, a few hundred miles northeast of Moscow.
- **The skeptical academic analysis (quoted)** — a modern academic text claims 1897 claimants "fantasized chimerical combinations of gas bags, propellers, and bird wings" and that later witnesses saw only airworthy designs, while conceding the implausible searchlight persisted; the authors rebut that the book's hoax-plus-Venus thesis assumes what it should prove, and that hundreds of coordinated fantasists strains plausibility more than the sightings do.
- **Foo fighters (WWII)** — regional variation as a case of the Phenomenon's cultural/geographic plasticity: colored spheres and strings of lights in the European theater, uniformly red fireballs over Japan.
- **Roswell, 1947** — cited for the irony of the Army Air Force's weather-balloon (later Project Mogul balloon) explanation: balloons used dismissively across generations.
- **Fin-de-siècle futurist artwork** — steampunk-era illustrations (included in the book) imagining the year 2000: personal aerial vehicles boarded like pleasure boats and "aerial houses" in the clouds; optimistic, naive, reassuring. By the 1920s, after WWI, the genre turned ugly and threatening — the sky became "the province of weapons" (Sopwith Camel, Fokker, Albatross).
- **Jules Verne's oeuvre as anticipation** — Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea (1870): Captain Nemo, an Indian of mixed Hindu-Muslim ancestry, builds the Nautilus from parts fabricated worldwide and assembled secretly (anticipating supply-chain manufacturing), living free of European imperialism — a "living criticism" of colonialism and of Christian ruling-class Europe. From the Earth to the Moon (1865): a rocket fired from a "space cannon," inspirational to generations of rocketeers — in 1969 US astronaut Frank Borman told Verne's grandson in Paris, "In a very real sense, Jules Verne is one of the pioneers of the space age." Around the World in Eighty Days (1873): balloon travel. Thus Verne contains "all the elements of the Great Airship mystery."
- **Nikola Tesla's biography** — born 1856, son of a Serbian Orthodox priest in Croatia; excelled at the University of Graz until overwork and a gambling addiction consumed his energy and tuition; struggled in Prague; joined Continental Edison in France; emigrated to America in 1884 to work for Edison; died broke and alone in a New York City hotel room in 1943. Monuments to him stand on both the US and Canadian sides of Niagara Falls.
- **The war of the currents (1888–1892)** — Edison (DC) vs. Westinghouse (AC); Tesla's polyphase induction motor made AC attractive; his Westinghouse royalties nearly bankrupted the company; J. P. Morgan engineered the merger that forced Edison out of power distribution; AC declared winner in 1892.
- **The Astor/Morgan investments** — John Jacob Astor IV (urged by his first wife, Ava Lowle Willing) invested in Tesla's fluorescent lighting plan; Morgan invested in Tesla's wireless transmission system (a rival to Marconi's radio). Tesla diverted Astor's money to his own research, causing a years-long break healed around 1908 with talk of collaborating on aircraft propulsion; Astor died on the Titanic in 1912. His daughter Ava Alice Muriel Astor is flagged for later discussion in the book.
- **Colorado Springs, 1899 / Colliers, 1901** — while working on wireless energy transmission, Tesla picked up a regular, rhythmic signal pattern he concluded "must be of planetary origin" — impossible as a natural phenomenon, hence intelligent; reported in letters of 1899 and his own 1901 statement carried in Colliers and other national publications. He simultaneously warned that an extraterrestrial's "sign language" would be unreadable: "a message from Mars which might be a triangle to them would appear as some other form to us."
- **NASA's 2014 communication study** — NASA commissioned leading anthropologists, archaeologists, and linguists on communicating with an alien intelligence; consensus: nearly impossible, for Tesla's reasons (divergent gravity, chemistry, electromagnetism yield beings with different senses, organs, and perception). The authors fault the study for ignoring the experiencer demographic, who consistently report communication — telepathic images, imperfect speech, or mechanical voices — largely one-directional and typically bearing apocalyptic warnings.
- **H. P. Lovecraft, "The Call of Cthulhu" (1926)** — quoted passage describing artists and poets worldwide dreaming bizarre things, a London suicide, a South American fanatic's visions, a California theosophist colony donning white robes for a "glorious fulfillment"; the authors say the atheist, science-loving Lovecraft "was right" as a description of how an alien presence would first register in sensitive minds. "The Dunwich Horror" features contact-seeking cults; "The Horror at Red Hook" links its cult to the Yezidis (recently targeted for extermination by the Islamic State group in Iraq). Lovecraft anticipated the god-as-extraterrestrial comparisons of Sitchin and von Däniken. Biographical parallel with Tesla: both recluses, both devotees of science; Lovecraft born 1890, died 1937 (six years before Tesla).
- **Bram Stoker's Dracula (1897)** — published the same year as the airship wave: a foreign, polyglot, night-bound Count who can fly and shapeshift, feeds on human blood, entrances victims and coaxes them from their beds to underground places — mapped point-for-point by the authors onto 1970s–80s abduction scenarios (taken from bed at night, operated on, fluids removed, implants inserted, returned before dawn). Lucy Westenra's testimony ("I didn't quite dream, but it all seemed to be real… my soul seemed to go out from my body") is offered as the model of the abductee state of consciousness — neither waking nor dreaming.
- **John Mack's abduction work** — the Harvard professor found alien-abduction experiencers exhibited evidence of post-traumatic stress regardless of the cause one credits, and that they were neither normally awake nor asleep/dreaming during the experience.
- **Wells's Martians as vampires** — half-machine, half-biological, telepathic, leaderless, reduced to brains in "steampunk exoskeletons," feeding on human blood; the authors cite James Gunn's introduction to a recent edition: "They were vampires." Wells's invasion premise prefigured Lovecraft by almost forty years; the 1898 novel later fed several films and the famous 1938 Orson Welles broadcast.
- **The Golden Dawn milieu** — founded 1888; rumored (unproven) membership of Bram Stoker; actual members included Arthur Machen, W. B. Yeats, and Algernon Blackwood; influence stretching "from the sedate drawing rooms of Victorian London to the crash pads of 1960s hippie culture in Southern California to today's venerable halls of academe in Leiden." Two Nobel laureates in literature orbit the circle: member Yeats and Mina Mathers's brother Henri Bergson.
- **Mathers's contact, October 29, 1896** — weeks before the first airship sighting, S. L. MacGregor Mathers affirmed in writing that he had contacted the secret chiefs, confirming his leadership of the Order; he compared the experience to the continued effect of a nearby lightning flash. Mathers is portrayed as an eccentric (walking London in highland attire), a British Library habitué, and the era's only English translator of the Keys of Solomon, The Book of Abramelin, and selections from Knorr von Rosenroth's Kabbala Denudata. "What Yeats and Bergson wrote about, Mathers did." The authors note it is unknown how much Henri Bergson influenced his sister Mina and MacGregor Mathers (or vice versa), but the couple did move from London to Paris shortly after Mathers's claimed contact, putting them near Henri Bergson's home in the same city.
- **Blavatsky and the Theosophical Society (New York, 1875)** — origin of the modern secret-chiefs concept: her "Masters," advanced beings communicating by materialized letters, many since identified as real persons from her Great Game-era contacts or as leaders of occult groups in Egypt, India, and Tibet.
- **Aleister Crowley, 1899** — took his first Golden Dawn initiation; later claimed his own direct contact with the secret chiefs and painted one — "Lam," virtually identical to the standard grey Alien. The authors flag his later profound influence on a founder of the Jet Propulsion Laboratory and a contributor to the military-industrial complex, to be discussed later in the volume.
- **The telescoped-timeline exercise** — the authors' rereading of history: 120 years from imagined contraptions to Moon landings and Pluto probes; sixty years from building-sized computers, Bakelite phones, and vacuum-tube TVs to smartwatches, smartphones, and the web — evidence that "historical periods are now much shorter than the average lifetime" and that technology has outstripped the evolution of human consciousness.

## Thread
This chapter is the book's deep-history pivot: the authors first push the record back before Roswell, before the foo fighters, to 1896–1897, to break the skeptics' "media first, sightings later" sequence. It advances the series' signature thesis — carried over from the series' first volume (on the history of religions) and from Sekret Machines: Man (the science/consciousness volume, explicitly cited here for its critique of the brain-as-wet-computer model) — that the Phenomenon is jointly physical and consciousness-borne, manifesting in each culture's idiom. The chapter closes by turning toward "the war of the rational versus the irrational" and "the political and military response to the Phenomenon," the tension that gives the book its title. The parade of writers (Verne, Wells, Stoker, Lovecraft), the wizard-circle of the Golden Dawn, and Tesla the "mad scientist" all cluster around the same few years as the airship wave, which the authors read as the Phenomenon's "overflow" into sensitive minds. The chapter ends by announcing the book's next move: examining technology and magic together — UFOs as "spiritualized machines" satisfying Arthur C. Clarke's dictum that advanced technology is indistinguishable from magic — and it plants a forward hook in Crowley, whose influence on a Jet Propulsion Laboratory founder ties Victorian occultism to the military-industrial complex discussed later in the volume.
