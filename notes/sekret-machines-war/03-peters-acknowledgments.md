# Peter's Acknowledgments

## In one paragraph
This short front-matter unit is Peter Levenda's personal acknowledgments for the Sekret Machines project, which he describes as a "nearly ten-year journey." He thanks his co-author Tom DeLonge — credited as the one "who started it all and kept it all going" despite unforeseen obstacles — along with the To The Stars team and a roster of named collaborators. Levenda claims the project achieved its goal: bringing "this conversation front and center" in national discourse, the news media, scientific and academic circles, and "the halls of Congress." He closes with a charge to the next generation to rise above the community's factionalism and paranoia, ask the right questions, and be ready for belief-challenging answers — replacing the slogan "I Want to Believe" with "I Want to Know."

## Core claims
- Levenda says the Sekret Machines project was a nearly ten-year journey, kept going by Tom DeLonge "even after tremendous, and often unforeseen, obstacles."
- The book says the authors "did what we set out to do": they "helped bring this conversation front and center in our national discourse."
- Levenda claims the project "created an environment in which this subject could be discussed openly and soberly" — in the news media, in scientific and academic circles, and "in the halls of Congress."
- Levenda argues the new generation "have the tools at your disposal to enable you to solve this ancient problem once and for all" — if they know how to use them and rise above the "factionalism and paranoia that have divided the community for so long."
- He counsels asking "the right questions" and being "prepared for the answers you receive, even if, and when, they challenge your beliefs."
- Levenda argues the guiding attitude should shift from "I Want to Believe" to "I Want to Know."

## Concepts
- **"I Want to Know"** — Levenda's proposed replacement slogan for the field: "forget 'I Want to Believe.' It's about time for 'I Want to Know.'" It reframes the subject as a matter of inquiry rather than faith.
- **"I Want to Believe"** — the older attitude Levenda tells readers to forget; presented as the belief-oriented stance the new generation should move beyond (the text names it only as the slogan to discard).
- **The "ancient problem"** — Levenda's term for the subject the series addresses, which he says the new generation can "solve... once and for all" with the tools now at their disposal.
- **Factionalism and paranoia** — the forces Levenda says "have divided the community for so long," which the next generation must "rise above" to make progress.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The Sekret Machines project itself ("nearly ten-year journey")** — Levenda's central concrete example: a collaboration begun by Tom DeLonge that, per Levenda, succeeded in bringing this conversation into national discourse, news media, scientific/academic circles, and Congress. He frames this outcome as the fulfillment of what the authors set out to do.
- **The To The Stars team and named figures** — Levenda names A. J. Hartley, Jim Semivan, Hal Puthoff, and "the whole To The Stars team" as companions; Chris Mellon, Lue Elizondo, and Steve Justice as sources of "enlightening conversations"; and Jacques Vallee, thanked simply "because of course." No further detail on each is given in this unit, but the list maps the project's collaborator network.
- **Family and friends as supporters** — thanked for backing "what must have seemed like tilting at windmills most of the time," Levenda's own characterization of how quixotic the effort looked from outside.

## Key quotes
- "To Tom DeLonge, my indefatigable co-author and inspiration, who started it all and kept it all going even after tremendous, and often unforeseen, obstacles" — crediting DeLonge as the project's originator and engine.
- "To Jacques Vallee, because of course." — a terse, self-evident thanks to Vallee.
- "who've supported what must have seemed like tilting at windmills most of the time" — on family and friends backing an apparently quixotic effort.
- "It's been quite a ride, but we did what we set out to do." — Levenda's verdict on the ten-year project.
- "We helped bring this conversation front and center in our national discourse... in our news media, and in scientific and academic circles, but also in the halls of Congress." — the claimed achievement.
- "You have the tools at your disposal to enable you to solve this ancient problem once and for all" — the charge to the new generation.
- "rise above the factionalism and paranoia that have divided the community for so long" — the condition Levenda attaches to that charge.
- "Ask the right questions. And be prepared for the answers you receive, even if, and when, they challenge your beliefs." — his method-and-mindset advice.
- "In fact, forget 'I Want to Believe.' It's about time for 'I Want to Know.'" — the unit's closing reframe.
- "Let's do this." — the final line, a call to action.

The "I Want to Know" reframe and the warning against factionalism and paranoia preview the book's insistence on sober inquiry over belief, and the roster of names (To The Stars, Mellon, Elizondo, Justice, Puthoff, Vallee) introduces the disclosure-movement network that recurs in the main text.
