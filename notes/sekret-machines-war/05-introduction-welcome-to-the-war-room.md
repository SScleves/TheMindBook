# Introduction: Welcome to the War Room

## In one paragraph
This Introduction frames the third and final nonfiction volume of the Sekret Machines trilogy around the theme of war: war as "a constant condition of humanity," war as the lens through which governments have engaged the UFO Phenomenon, and war as a possible mode of contact itself. DeLonge & Levenda argue that the secrecy of militaries and intelligence agencies over the past seventy years reflects not a grand conspiracy against citizens but "helplessness in the face of the inexplicable" — technological societies each confronting the same "metatechnical Phenomenon." They reject a single-discipline or purely extraterrestrial reading, calling for a "multivalent," Renaissance-style, globally inclusive approach that holds the nuts-and-bolts and consciousness aspects of the Phenomenon simultaneously, while still recommending a defensive posture ("a war footing, at least until all the facts are in"). The chapter surveys the terrain the book will cover — US/Russia/China responses, Nazi/Soviet reverse-engineering claims, WWII and Cold War context, and the wisdom or folly of threat analysis — and closes with the governing metaphor of the volume: the generals of the world as prisoners in "Plato's Bunker," mistaking sensor shadows for reality while the Alien makes shadow puppets on the wall.

## Core claims
- The authors open by noting the US spends more on armaments and defense than on any other area, and that globalization has made conflict global too: a civil war in South America can hit North American coffee, a Middle East war can hit US oil, and a terrorist bombing in Europe or America turns "a regional conflict in the Middle East" into "a global conflict," an effect "exacerbated by the use of mass communications, which can send the message of conflict around the world in less than a second."
- The authors claim war has "replaced virtually every other preoccupation of humanity," and that even the world's religions are built on it — citing the Sumerian creation epic (Tiamat vs. Marduk) to argue humans "are quite literally the children of war."
- DeLonge & Levenda argue that governments and the military are the only institutions with the resources to fund scientific R&D, producing the "military-industrial complex" Eisenhower named — and that this complex may circumscribe governmental power itself, carefully monitoring control of information about the Phenomenon.
- The authors say serious late-twentieth-century paranormal research in the US, Russia, and China was conducted under military/intelligence auspices interested in "the weaponization of the paranormal," and that this and the UFO issue were often seen as interrelated — hence their umbrella term "the Phenomenon."
- They claim the American military-industrial complex "was foreshadowed by the Third Reich, which provided the template and inspiration" for it, urged on by the same man who mentored Jack Parsons, Qian Xuesen, and Rosenberg spy William Perl ("as we shall see").
- The book's central corrective claim: seventy years of strange official behavior toward UFOs is best explained not as conspiracy against citizens but as "the result of technological societies confronting the same metatechnical Phenomenon: sekret machines" — machines whose "provenance and purpose are unknowable, unfathomable, and hence sekret."
- The authors argue a century-long, multi-national conspiracy of hostile governments to hide "the truth about UFOs" (the strong "national security state" thesis associated with Richard Dolan) is wildly improbable; what is likely instead is that each government jealously guards its own "slender pieces of the overall puzzle" for national-security reasons — a situation that would outlast regimes.
- They claim there is evidence of deliberate American military disinformation (the Richard Doty/AFOSI case) that works by reductio ad absurdum in both directions: linking secret aircraft to UFOs discredits sightings of real secret weapons, and linking UFOs to secret aircraft devalues real UFO sightings — "Win-win."
- Because "No one is telling us the truth," the authors argue the full unvarnished truth about UFOs is nearly impossible to reach — but governments, while hiding hardware, left "in plain sight" evidence of supreme importance: the traces in the minds, bodies, "and perhaps even in the DNA" of experiencers.
- Drawing on John Mack's work, they claim experiencers suffer genuine trauma (PTSD, "the same malady suffered by combat veterans") — "Something had happened to these people" — even if the literal content of the memories cannot be verified; they pose a "third — and ultimately more terrifying — possibility" beyond both the FMSF implanted-memory view and the CIA's dismissal of brainwashing research.
- They argue Sagan-style dismissals of the ETH (star travel is impossible under Einstein's light-speed limit) rest on unexamined assumptions: the Others could have arrived eons ago, be hiding in the solar system or under the oceans, possess cloaking technology, or command a quantum "metascience" in which "consciousness is part of every equation."
- Reiterating volume two, they float the claim that "our brain is an emerging property of consciousness and not the other way around," and that a culture with such a science would see our failure to grasp consciousness as "a vulnerability to be exploited."
- The authors insist both aspects of the Phenomenon must be held at once — the physical (FLIR videos, photos, radar, artifacts) and the consciousness aspect — and synthesized into a third: "the Phenomenon as (competing?) worldview." "The Phenomenon is physical. That's the whole point. But it is also… 'supernatural,' an artifact of the collision of two separate realities."
- They claim no single discipline or expert can approach the subject ("no Einstein or Aristotle" of the field); Ufology needs every discipline, every culture, and especially the experience of colonized peoples — "Ufology in general cannot afford to be the Western world's hobby or pastime only."
- Against critics who call To The Stars fearmongering, they argue that because TTS founders are former government/intelligence/military people, threat analysis is their natural frame — and that we "have to hope for the best… but we have to prepare for the worst," while admitting "we have no idea what the worst might be."
- The military, they claim, "has always been one step ahead of the general population" only in data, not understanding — "that is like having more data on a dream" — and has shown reluctance to engage the Phenomenon partly out of "fear of demons" (as with 1980s remote-viewing programs defunded by Congress as "unholy").
- The trilogy's three takeaways, stated explicitly: (a) the Phenomenon is real; (b) there is no cabal of magi withholding the truth "at least not the way you think"; (c) the tension between informing citizens and denying intelligence to rival governments is so absolute that "we, the people of the world, have already lost the ability to defend ourselves from an alien invasion."
- Final proposal: thinking in terms of alien invasion "may be the only avenue we have left" to unify a divided Earth into a planetwide defense posture — "mutually assured protection" — and even if it proves unneeded, that unity would be "a tremendous accomplishment."

## Concepts
- **The Phenomenon** — the authors' general rubric grouping the UFO issue with the paranormal, since military and intelligence engagement historically treated them as interrelated; capitalized throughout as the book's subject.
- **Sekret machines** — the trilogy's title concept, restated here: UFOs/UAPs are machines whose "provenance and purpose are unknowable, unfathomable, and hence sekret"; official secrecy is downstream of this unknowability, not of conspiracy.
- **Metatechnical** — the authors' descriptor for the Phenomenon as something beyond human technology that technological societies nonetheless confront as technology ("the same metatechnical Phenomenon: sekret machines").
- **Military-industrial complex** — Eisenhower's coinage (quoted from his farewell address) for the conjunction of an immense military establishment and a large arms industry; the authors add it may circumscribe governmental power and control Phenomenon-related information, and claim it was foreshadowed by the Third Reich.
- **Weaponization of the paranormal** — the Cold War military/intelligence interest (US, Russia, China) that funded serious paranormal research; also the epigraph's description of Qian Xuesen's advocacy.
- **National security state** — Richard Dolan's term (cited by the authors) for the thesis that militaries and governments cynically hide the truth about UFOs; the authors reject its strong conspiratorial form as requiring an "improbable, century-long understanding" between hostile nations.
- **Extraterrestrial hypothesis (ETH)** — the explanation that UFOs are vehicles from other planets/star systems; the authors call the UFO communities' focus on it "partisan" and inadequate to a Phenomenon that "may be more holistic — in terms of physics, biology, and consciousness."
- **Multivalent** — the authors' description of this work: deliberately involving "discrete realms of knowledge that many may consider unrelated," with repetition and cross-referencing "designed to challenge our accepted paths to knowledge."
- **Cartesian habit / Renaissance understanding** — the contrast the authors draw: the Cartesian diversification of knowledge into separate realms of matter, mind, and God versus their proposed return to a Renaissance view in which these realms are "interdependent and permeable."
- **War footing / defensive posture** — the authors' recommended stance toward a possibly non-benign Phenomenon: "the only sensible approach may be to take a defensive posture: to assume a war footing, at least until all the facts are in."
- **Disinformation by reductio ad absurdum** — the alleged AFOSI tactic (Doty case): linking real secret aircraft to UFOs consigns all such sightings "to the tinfoil hat realm," while linking UFOs to secret test aircraft devalues genuine UFO sightings; both directions serve concealment.
- **Experiencers** — people bearing the traces of the Phenomenon "in the minds and the bodies (and perhaps even in the DNA)"; the evidence governments left "in plain sight," analogized to a 1960s serial killer unknowingly leaving DNA everywhere.
- **Recovered memories / False Memory Syndrome** — recovered memories: allegedly repressed traumatic memories made conscious via hypnosis or triggers; the False Memory Syndrome Foundation (est. 1992) countered that therapists could implant such memories "deliberately or accidentally" through suggestion, producing "a confabulation of fact and fantasy."
- **Satanic ritual abuse** — the 1970s–80s US phenomenon of recovered memories of abduction and abuse by Satanists in occult rituals, with claims of thousands of sacrificed children; investigation showed "no thousands of murdered children, no national (or global) networks of Satanists" — leaving the question of where the memories came from.
- **Brainwashing / mind-control programs** — the fear arising from Korean War POW experiences that inspired US military and CIA programs "Operations Bluebird, Artichoke, Often, and the more notorious MK-ULTRA"; per Richard Helms's congressional testimony, abandoned as "twenty years of pointless research."
- **The third possibility** — the authors' suggestion that beyond FMSF's implanted-memory view and Helms's dismissal lies "a third — and ultimately more terrifying — possibility": both FMSF and the CIA "were groping in the dark after the same Phenomenon."
- **Metascience** — a hypothesized science behind our science, "one in which consciousness is part of every equation," possibly mastered by the Others and reflected in their observed technology.
- **Flying Rorschach tests** — the authors' speculation that the Other may deploy the craft to probe human reactions, "trying to understand" our consciousness by observing our responses.
- **Mandala / individuation** — Carl Jung's proposal (cited by the authors) that "flying saucers" are externalizations of the mandala, the circular symbol of psychic integration he called individuation; the authors ask "What if both ideas are true?" — machine and point of connection between consciousnesses at once.
- **Nuts-and-bolts vs. consciousness aspects / Phenomenon as worldview** — the two idea-sets to hold simultaneously: (a) the physical evidence (FLIR videos, photos, radar traces, artifacts) and (b) the emotional/psychological responses and implications; their synthesis yields "a third: the Phenomenon as (competing?) worldview."
- **Blind men and the elephant** — the authors' figure for the field's epistemic condition: every discipline touches part of the Phenomenon, none grasps the whole; "We're gonna need a bigger room."
- **Colonization analogy / invader mindset** — the argument that colonized peoples' experience ("being invaded by beings who do not speak our language… who exploit us and our resources") and colonizers' experience alike are essential data for modeling human–Alien contact.
- **Ur-contact** — recalled from Sekret Machines: Gods: a hypothesized event "in dim prehistory" that "jump-started human ideas about gods, angels, and demons."
- **Cargo cult** — the series' opening frame, recalled here: human civilizational development as a cargo-cult mentality "focused on the stars and on the gods and immortality," now reaching "the limit of its logical conclusion."
- **Government of the sky** — the authors' phrase for the Other conceived as a ruling power of the heavens, with whom human–alien consciousness contact could become "a new battleground."
- **Weapons of consciousness** — nonlethal weapons "of consciousness, of perception, communication, and perspective… with the ability to manipulate our reality like the proverbial hypnotist makes a man think he's a chicken"; potent because "we have not been able to harden our consciousness."
- **Politics as continuation of war** — the authors' inversion of Clausewitz ("War is a continuation of politics by other means"): since the end of WWII, "Politics is a continuation of war by other means."
- **Disclosure parameters** — the authors' critique that the public has "already decided how it should happen" (living Alien, dead Alien, saucer, crash debris) and deems anything else a dodge — while believing itself scientific.
- **Special Access Programs** — the compartmented files at the end of the chain of not-knowing: "The advisors want to know what the Special Access Programs have in their files, files to which even they do not have access."
- **Fear of demons** — the authors' explanation for official reluctance: in the 1980s remote-viewing projects were denied congressional funding "because it was believed to be somehow unholy," and UFO study "suffered the same fate."
- **Plato's cave / Plato's Bunker** — the chapter's closing metaphor (with the Republic Book VII passage quoted at length): the world's military leaders in their war rooms are the chained prisoners, their sensors the wall, their threat models the chains ("an almost Neanderthal conception of threat assessment"), while "the Alien… is making shadow puppets on that very wall."
- **Mutually assured destruction / mutually assured protection** — the Cold War doctrine invoked via Dr. Strangelove, versus the authors' proposed inversion: a planetwide defense posture uniting humanity, "something we might call mutually assured protection."
- **Threat analysis** — the national-security frame TTS's ex-government founders bring to the Phenomenon; the volume promises "a long, hard look at… the wisdom or folly of interpreting the Alien from a threat analysis perspective."
- **Tic Tacs** — the authors' shorthand for the modern observed craft ("the infamous Tic Tacs," "the lozenges"), the contemporary endpoint of a sighting record they trace back to ancient Egypt.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Thutmose III stela (c. 1460 BCE)** — from Jacques Vallée's Wonders in the Sky: the earliest known sighting, a stela describing a star hovering over the Nubian enemy army during a war with Egypt, illuminating it so the horses fled and the army fell "upon their own blood"; Thutmose attributed the star to the god Amon. The authors present it as the first recorded instance of the Phenomenon intervening in armed conflict, tied simultaneously to a deity and a political leader — and note that throughout Vallée's catalogue it is sightings "as observed by kings" that make the history books.
- **Eisenhower's farewell address** — quoted directly on the "conjunction of an immense military establishment and a large arms industry… new in the American experience," whose influence is "economic, political, even spiritual." The authors use it to establish the military-industrial complex, then claim it was foreshadowed by the Third Reich.
- **The unnamed mentor** — the authors assert one man mentored rocket scientist and mystic Jack Parsons, Chinese Silkworm missile inventor Qian Xuesen, and Rosenberg spy William Perl, and urged the Nazi template on America ("as we shall see") — a setup for later chapters.
- **Richard Doty / AFOSI disinformation** — "the infamous example": Doty claimed the Air Force Office of Special Investigations targeted individual Ufologists with UFO disinformation; the authors note the CIA is legally barred from domestic operations but "the Air Force evidently suffers from no such prohibition," and read the campaign as protecting secret aircraft via reductio ad absurdum.
- **Foreign openness** — the authors note some countries have created federally funded/approved UFO study commissions and that statesmen of various nations have publicly acknowledged UFOs and promoted open study — evidence that nations lacking crash retrievals have little to conceal.
- **John Mack's abduction research** — the Harvard psychologist "did the world a service by taking the 'alien abduction' experience seriously," finding common elements among experiencers and forms of PTSD indicating "'something' had happened to them" — being taken in the night and abused, usually physically — though independent verification was rare.
- **The satanic ritual abuse panic (US, 1970s–80s)** — recovered memories of sexual and ritual abuse by Satanists, with claims of thousands to tens of thousands of murdered children; investigation demonstrated no such murders or networks existed, leaving the origin of the memories unexplained.
- **False Memory Syndrome Foundation (1992)** — established in part to counter the recovered-memory argument, insisting therapists could implant memories through leading and suggestive language.
- **CIA mind-control programs and Helms's testimony** — Operations Bluebird, Artichoke, Often, and MK-ULTRA, created out of fear of Korean War "brainwashing"; CIA Director of Intelligence Richard Helms testified before a congressional committee around the Watergate era that the programs were abandoned as unfruitful — "twenty years of pointless research." The authors juxtapose FMSF and Helms as contradictory and propose both were "groping in the dark after the same Phenomenon."
- **Sagan's ETH critique** — scientists including Carl Sagan invoked Einstein's light-speed limit to call star travel from, e.g., Zeta Reticuli ridiculous; the authors counter with hypotheticals (ancient arrival, hiding under the oceans, cloaking technology, superior quantum mechanics).
- **Jung's flying-saucer mandala** — Jung's proposal that flying saucers externalize the mandala/individuation symbol familiar from Himalayan and South Asian religions; the authors entertain machine and psychic symbol being true simultaneously.
- **Remote viewing defunded (1980s)** — military remote-viewing study projects denied funding by Congress "because it was believed to be somehow unholy"; UFO study met the same fate — the authors' evidence for "fear of demons" in official reluctance.
- **Harry Reid and the reopened door** — Senator Harry Reid of Nevada and two colleagues "crack[ed] open the door once again in Congress — and from there to the Pentagon" for official UFO investigation; further action by influential former defense department and intelligence-community personnel widened it until data and "the grudging admission of the DOD" reached American media.
- **Dr. Strangelove war room** — the series' closing reference point (paired with the cargo cults that opened it): governments helpless to stop nuclear holocaust, spying on each other and calculating post-exchange survival; "We are living that movie right now" — swap Cold War paranoia for possible alien invasion and "the players stay the same."
- **Plato's Republic Book VII** — the cave allegory quoted at length under the heading "Welcome to Plato's Bunker," mapped onto the world's military leaders: bunkers as the den, sensors as the wall, threat-assessment tradition as the chains, the Alien as the shadow-puppeteer.
- **Sumerian creation epic** — invoked twice: the cosmos created by war between the gods of Tiamat and Marduk, humans fashioned as slaves to the victors — "humans are quite literally the children of war."
- **Biblical warfare and Matthew 10:34** — the Bible characterized as "a record of warfare between tribes, between nations, and between religions, often at the express encouragement of God," capped with Jesus's "I have come to bring not peace, but a sword." The authors add further examples of religious violence: "holy wars, such as the Crusades. Wars against heretics. Against witches. Even Christians slaughtering Christians."
- **Twentieth-century transformation of war** — war moved from cavalry charges, saber rattling, and trumpets to chemical weapons, armored units replacing horses, rockets, aerial bombardment, and "the bomb," and now to "electronic warfare. Drones. Cyber-attacks. Nuclear missile installations that can destroy a city within minutes of a launch halfway around the world" — culminating in "Mutually assured destruction," the immediate lead-in to the "sekret machine" amid this "mechanized and digitized armory."
- **Egypt-to-modern sighting span** — the authors' evidence for the military's data-not-understanding advantage spans "the earliest sightings in ancient Egypt to the modern video release of UAPs teasing naval aircraft off the coast of California, and including the now infamous Roswell incident in 1947."
- **Contact cinema** — H. G. Wells's novel War of the Worlds as the earliest invasion-conditioning reference, and films like The Day the Earth Stood Still and Independence Day; Close Encounters of the Third Kind and E.T. as "feel good" counterparts; the authors call the cuddly/evil tension useful for gaming out contact scenarios.
- **Epigraphs** — Qian Xuesen ("Consciousness is the machinization of the cerebrum"), Whitley Strieber (every voice in his experience has been "Like a machine"), and Wilhelm Reich's Ether, God and Devil (mechanist and mystic alike ruled by "a contradictory and murderous mixture of machines and gods") — three framings of the machine/consciousness collision the volume explores.

## Thread
This Introduction is the hinge of the trilogy: it recaps what Gods (religion, Ur-contact) and Man (science, consciousness-first reality) established, and declares that this volume moves from groundwork to "analysis… recommendations and suggestions." It sets the book's method (multivalent, multidisciplinary, multicultural), its explanatory thesis (secrecy as helplessness, not conspiracy), and its posture (threat analysis without assuming hostility). It also lays out the volume's roadmap explicitly: how the US, Russia, and China have handled the problem; the Nazi/Soviet reverse-engineering claims in WWII and Cold War context; the personalities involved; a hard look at "alien invasion" and threat analysis; and a closing sociological summary "that may owe more to science fiction than most would be comfortable entertaining." The Plato's Bunker image introduced here — generals chained to their sensors while the Other casts the shadows — prefigures the book's Afterword territory of surveillance and control, and the teased unnamed mentor of Parsons, Qian, and Perl plants a thread the following chapters promise to pull.
