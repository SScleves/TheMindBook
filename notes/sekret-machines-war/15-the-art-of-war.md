# The Art of War

## In one paragraph
This chapter turns from religion and science — lenses the authors say they have "previously" applied earlier in this book — to war as the framework for evaluating the UFO Phenomenon, opening with Chris Mellon's fear that increased UAP sightings represent probes testing human defense readiness ahead of a possible invasion. The authors apply classical military theory — Clausewitz's "trinity" of war and his dictum that war is "the continuation of politics," Jomini's concept of the "theater of war," and Sun Tzu's teaching on spies — to the Phenomenon, and find that none of it maps cleanly because the Alien has no identifiable territory, home base, ideology, or predictable pattern of appearance. The chapter then works through three military-theory lenses in turn: the UFO as a weapons platform (citing a declassified Australian Ministry of Defense report categorizing "weapon" effects from Vallée and Hynek's database), terrorism (arguing that alien abductions and cattle mutilations function as psychological warfare rather than science), and espionage (contrasting the Alien's apparent superiority in SIGINT/ELINT with its clumsy HUMINT attempts, illustrated by Levenda's own Men in Black encounter in Rhode Island and Singapore). It closes by speculating that "believers" in a benign Alien may function, wittingly or not, as a psychological "fifth column" softening humanity for contact, invasion, or colonization.

## Core claims
- The authors argue that from a purely military point of view, humanity is "completely vulnerable" to the UFO Phenomenon because we do not know its origin, its intentions, or the extent of its forces.
- The authors report that Chris Mellon's greatest fear is that decades of increased sightings indicate the craft are probes designed to test "our preparedness and our defense posture," in advance of a possible attack or invasion.
- The authors argue that classical military theory (Clausewitz, Jomini) fails to map onto the UFO Phenomenon because it presupposes territory, a home nation, and identifiable political motives — none of which the Alien demonstrably has.
- The authors compare humanity's position to the Arawaks facing Columbus: able to resist the "sailing ships" (UFOs/"Tic Tacs") that appear but unable to locate, understand, or retaliate against their home base.
- The authors apply Clausewitz's "trinity" (the irrational/violent emotion, the nonrational/chance, and the rational/politics) to the Alien and conclude none of the three is verifiable in the Alien's case — there's no evidence of alien "emotion," no evidence of alien "political ideology," and the Alien's unpredictability represents chance only from the human side, not the Alien's.
- The authors argue that if humans could predict when and where the Alien appears, this would reveal that it is constrained by a timeline, environment, or strategy, and would be a major step toward understanding its motivations.
- The authors suggest, as an open possibility, that the Alien may already have "invaded and colonized the Earth a long time ago and we are only just now waking up to the fact."
- The authors argue that dismissing pilot reports (e.g., Nimitz) as equipment malfunction, or invoking Einstein's relativity to declare interstellar travel impossible, is a "straw man" argument equivalent to denying we could reach the Moon because space is a vacuum, and that "inadequate science is as bad as no science" when discussing the Phenomenon.
- The authors argue that the common assumption that the Alien comes from another planet or star system is "pure conjecture without any meaningful context," resting on thin and possibly uncredible contactee testimony such as George Adamski's, and propose that the Alien or "Visitor" may instead originate "from somewhere within our own environment in a way we do not yet understand."
- The authors cite the Australian Ministry of Defense's 1970s-era report as evidence that a national government, evaluating the Phenomenon strictly militarily and without ideological or skeptical bias, concluded that UFOs demonstrate real weapon-like effects, used so far only defensively, and that Australia ultimately abandoned serious study of the topic, likely under American pressure.
- The authors argue that the US Air Force / Pentagon mindset revealed in the 1969 "Science, Technology and Warfare" symposium — rigid hierarchy, distrust of outside experts, comfort with ex-Nazi scientists over suspect figures like Oppenheimer — explains why the military apparatus has never been capable of seriously investigating the Phenomenon, from Project Sign onward.
- The authors argue that terror attacks and UFO sightings "share a lot in common": both unpredictable in time/place/perpetrator identity, and both aimed at destabilizing a population's sense of security rather than achieving a conventional military objective.
- The authors argue that the classic "medical experiment" explanation for alien abductions makes no scientific sense for a technologically superior species, and propose instead that abductions may function as a form of psychological terrorism/warfare, whether performed by the Alien or by a human agency (e.g., CIA/AFOSI) exploiting alien mythology as cover.
- The authors speculate, explicitly labeling it "wholly speculative," that the CIA — barred by charter from domestic operations — could use the US Air Force's AFOSI (which has no such domestic restriction) as an operational arm, analogous to the NSA/Army Cyber Command/CIA arrangement alleged in the Stuxnet ("Operation Olympic Games") case, and that this could explain how a disinformation agent like Richard Doty operated within the UFO community, and why any such program's records would be un-declassifiable (each agency could block the other's release).
- The authors argue that the alien abduction narrative's persistent "absurd" elements (fluid/ova/sperm extraction, anal probes, passage through solid walls) more plausibly originated as a human-engineered psychological-warfare "story" than as literal alien medical procedure, following Vallée's observation that a technologically superior species would not need to abduct humans to experiment on them — the authors suggest such a scenario "would have been cooked up" in the "fevered imagination" of MK-ULTRA's Sidney Gottlieb.
- The authors argue the UFOs' observed "transmedium" capability (flying from air into ocean without a wake, as seen off the Nimitz) is consistent with abductees' reports of passing through solid walls, suggesting a genuine (not merely implanted) shared physical phenomenon rather than pure suggestion.
- The authors argue, citing John Mack, that alien abductees typically show no other psychiatric pathology, undermining the theory that the abduction narrative is purely a symptom of mental illness or hypnotic suggestion.
- The authors argue cattle mutilations and human abductions share a structural pattern (abduction, extraction of fluids/organs, return to point of origin) and that the key difference — cattle are killed and displayed, humans are returned alive — may function as a deliberate warning of what could be done to humans.
- The authors argue that implants, if they function as tracking devices, would be unnecessary for cattle (domesticated, herd-bound, always locatable) but necessary for humans, who "travel far," "go on vacations," and "relocate for work" — offered as a reason abductees but not mutilated cattle are described as implanted.
- The authors cite Jacques Vallée's opinion that cattle mutilations "may be just what they seem to be: acts of terrorism," intended to demonstrate total control and terrify the population, without any further discoverable purpose.
- The authors argue that whoever or whatever is responsible for mutilations/abductions is engaged in a long-running (potentially decades- or century-long) psychological warfare operation that has recently intensified, evidenced by increased sightings, DOD acknowledgment, videos, and officers going on the record.
- The authors argue that Sun Tzu's teaching on spies exposes the fundamental asymmetry of the situation: humans have zero intelligence (no HUMINT/SIGINT) on the Alien's structure, numbers, or intentions, whereas the Alien appears vastly superior at SIGINT/ELINT-type surveillance of humanity.
- The authors argue the Alien struggles at HUMINT — infiltrating human society directly — evidenced by recurring reports of "off" details (archaic language, ill-fitting clothing, odd behavior) among Men in Black-type encounters, comparable to a WWII spy who doesn't know who won the World Series.
- The authors present Levenda's personal 1990 Rhode Island encounter (and its Singapore echo years later) as a first-person case of apparent MIB surveillance/interference, offered as evidence for the "clumsy alien HUMINT" pattern rather than as proof of any specific explanation.
- The authors argue civilian society is a softer HUMINT target than a military base because of America's cultural heterogeneity, making "odd" behavior less conspicuous; hence probing missile bases/carriers uses UAPs to test military response while probing civilian populations only requires "showing up and acting a little goofy."
- The authors raise, as speculation, the possibility that some humans who insist the Alien is wholly benevolent function as an unwitting "fifth column," lowering humanity's guard in a manner consistent with the aims of an invader or colonizer.
- The authors argue it "doesn't make sense" that an Alien wanting human test subjects would abduct middle-class Americans (e.g., Whitley Strieber) rather than exploit the effectively limitless supply of victims available from war zones, genocides, and refugee crises across the twentieth century — implying the choice of targets is not about biological material but about location and propaganda value.
- The authors propose that the specific choice to stage mutilations/abductions in politically stable, technologically advanced nations (rather than war zones where such acts "would be lost in a sea of similar events") suggests the stability itself, or the shock value achievable only in a secure society, is the actual target/purpose.
- The authors argue that the spread of the Internet, AI, and the Internet of Things now gives whatever entity is behind the Phenomenon a global vector for propagating awareness of its existence, contributing to a new "alien" paradigm taking shape following official acknowledgment of Nimitz, FLIR, and Tic Tac footage.

## Concepts
- **Clausewitzian trinity** — Carl von Clausewitz's framework describing war as composed of three factors: the irrational (violent emotion), the nonrational (chance/luck), and the rational (war as continuation of politics); the authors test whether each applies to the Alien and find none clearly does.
- **War as continuation of politics** — Clausewitz's dictum, quoted by the authors, that political realities and concerns guide and lead to war between states; the authors ask whether this formula can apply to a nonhuman entity with no known political structure.
- **Theater of war** — a concept from Baron de Jomini's The Art of War (taught at West Point) describing the geographic ground — enemy soil, allied territory, or one's own — on which conflict occurs; the authors argue the concept collapses for the UFO Phenomenon because there is no identifiable "enemy soil."
- **The Alien as Columbus / humans as Arawaks** — the authors' analogy that just as the Arawaks could resist Columbus's ships but not locate or attack his home country, humans can observe and even resist UFOs ("Tic Tacs") but have no way to identify or reach their point of origin.
- **Ghost in the machine / the house the Alien haunts** — the authors' metaphor that while the UFO seems like a "chimera" or "phantom," almost supernatural, to a fighter pilot, it is in fact the Alien that behaves as the "ghost in the machine" haunting us — because we, unlike it, are "reliably three-dimensional" and physically vulnerable; Earth itself is "the house the Alien haunts."
- **Alien origin conjecture** — the authors' argument that the popular Ufological assumption the Alien hails from another planet or star system is "pure conjecture without any meaningful context," resting on thin contactee testimony (such as George Adamski's); they propose it may instead come from "somewhere within our own environment in a way we do not yet understand," and call the "interstellar travel is impossible" rebuttal to Nimitz-type sightings a straw-man argument.
- **Weapons platform hypothesis** — the popular and military-analytic idea (examined via the Australian MoD report) that UFOs function partly as weapons systems, deploying rays or beams for mapping, illumination, disabling engines, or causing physical effects on people and equipment.
- **Residual effects** — a term from the Australian MoD report referring to physical traces left on the environment or on people/vehicles after a UFO encounter (e.g., stalled engines, burns, paralysis); the report identifies these as the most promising avenue for scientific study.
- **Operational analysis** — military/bureaucratic term (from the 1969 USAF Academy symposium "Science, Technology and Warfare") for integrating new scientific and technological discoveries into military operational structure; the authors use it to illustrate the Pentagon's institutional resistance to novel phenomena like UFOs.
- **Terrorism (as defined by the authors)** — "a technique and a strategy" with no inherent ideology or religion, practicable by state or individual actors, whose goal is to unnerve a civilian population (not achieve a military objective) and to make the perpetrator appear more powerful/popular than it actually is.
- **Alien abduction as psychological warfare** — the authors' hypothesis that the abduction narrative (fluid extraction, implants, sexual violation, passage through walls) may function as a terror campaign rather than literal alien medical experimentation, since the latter makes no sense for a technologically superior species.
- **MK-ULTRA** — the CIA's mid-20th-century mind-control research program (hallucinogens, hypnosis, subliminal messaging), referenced as a precedent for state-sponsored psychological experimentation, but constrained domestically by 1970s congressional hearings.
- **Depatterning** — Dr. Ewen Cameron's CIA-contracted "mind control" experiments conducted in Canada in the late 1950s–early 1960s, cited as precedent for extraterritorial psychological operations outside the CIA's domestic legal restrictions.
- **CIA/AFOSI nexus (speculative)** — the authors' hypothesis that the CIA, barred from domestic operations, could operationalize domestic scenarios through the Air Force's Office of Special Investigations (AFOSI), which has no such restriction, mirroring the alleged NSA/Army Cyber Command/CIA division of labor in the Stuxnet ("Operation Olympic Games") cyberattack; offered to explain figures like Richard Doty and the un-declassifiability of any resulting program (each agency could block the other's release).
- **SIGINT / ELINT / HUMINT** — espionage categories: SIGINT ("signals intelligence") and ELINT ("electronic intelligence") rely on technology (satellites, overflights, cyber espionage); HUMINT ("human intelligence") is traditional human-agent spying. The authors argue the Alien excels at SIGINT/ELINT but struggles at HUMINT.
- **Divine manipulation of the threads** — Sun Tzu's phrase (per the authors) for the art of running a spy network, evoking a spider at the center of its web; used to frame the total absence of human intelligence on the Alien's structure, numbers, or intentions.
- **Transmedium capability** — the observed ability of UAPs (e.g., over the Nimitz and both US coasts) to move from air directly into water without a wake or major surface disturbance; the authors connect this to abductee reports of passing through solid walls.
- **Cattle mutilation** — the phenomenon in which cattle (or other large animals) are found drained of blood with specific organs (often sexual organs, sometimes tongue or eyes) surgically removed, with no witnesses, and the animal's body left visibly displayed on the ground.
- **Secondary crime scene** — a criminological term the authors apply to cattle mutilation sites: the location where the body is found is not where the "crime" (draining/removal) actually took place, as deduced from the absence of blood at the site.
- **Fifth column (speculative)** — the authors' term for humans who insist the Alien is wholly benevolent and harmless, hypothesized as unwittingly (or wittingly) advancing a psychological-warfare campaign designed to lower humanity's guard toward an invader/colonizer.
- **Men in Black (MIB)** — figures reported by contactees as appearing nearly but not fully human, wearing subtly wrong clothing, driving old-model cars, and speaking in stilted or archaic language; the authors treat MIB encounters as the Alien's (imperfect) attempt at HUMINT-style infiltration.
- **High strangeness** — term the authors use (in describing Levenda's own encounter) for anomalous personal experiences that resist easy categorization or explanation.
- **Propaganda value** — the authors' proposed metric for why mutilations/abductions occur in stable, developed nations rather than war zones: such acts register as maximally shocking/newsworthy only where they contrast with an otherwise secure environment.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Chris Mellon's stated fear**: interviewed by the authors, Mellon (identified as a Department of Defense veteran) says his greatest concern is that decades of increased sightings suggest the craft are probes testing US "preparedness and defense posture," possibly preparatory to attack or invasion — offered as expert testimony, not the authors' own certainty.
- **Australian Ministry of Defense report, "Scientific Intelligence—General—Unidentified Flying Objects"** (endnote 202): a 1970s-era document on the National Archives of Australia website covering roughly 1958–1971. It gives a history of American military/intelligence UFO interest 1947–1969 (emphasizing covert CIA/USAF cooperation and Pentagon awareness the Phenomenon is real, likely extraterrestrial), and it itemizes weapon-like effects — sourced from Jacques Vallée and Allen Hynek's Northwestern University computerized UFO-sightings database — under categories including "Stalled Engines" (50+ instances of car engines failing near UFOs), "Radio/TV Interference," and "Pricklings," attributing these to devices for interfering with electrical circuits, inducing paralysis, or acting as a heat ray. The report notes circumstantial evidence these "weapons" are used deliberately but defensively, and flags "residual effects" as the most promising area for scientific study. Australia ultimately dropped serious investigation, which the authors attribute likely to American pressure and lack of resources.
- **"Science, Technology and Warfare" symposium** (US Air Force Academy, May 8–9, 1969): a DOD document on integrating scientific/technological advances into military operations; the authors use it as a window into Pentagon institutional culture — rigid hierarchy, distrust of outsiders and its own nonconformist members, comfort with ex-Nazi scientists over figures like Oppenheimer (regarded as "probably a Commie") — to explain why Project Sign's early consensus that the Phenomenon was non-terrestrial was shut down by generals rather than pursued.
- **The Nimitz incident**: referenced as the source of reported near-misses when chasing UFOs and as the case cited by skeptical scientists/"television physicists" who dismiss pilot observations as equipment malfunction; also cited as the location of observed "transmedium" flight (air-to-water transitions without a wake).
- **The Exorcist doctor scene**: the authors cite the film's line "The problem is not your bed, Mrs. McNeil" as an analogy for scientists dismissing physical anomaly reports (like Nimitz pilot observations) with confident but inadequate scientific explanations.
- **Stuxnet / Operation Olympic Games**: cited as a real-world precedent in which the NSA, barred by charter from conducting cyberattacks, relied on the US Army's Cyber Warfare Command and CIA (operating from NSA's Fort Meade) to carry out the operation — used as the model for the speculative CIA/AFOSI arrangement.
- **Richard Doty**: named as a UFO-community disinformation agent whose relatively unscathed activity the authors' speculative CIA/AFOSI arrangement is offered to explain.
- **Whitley Strieber's Randolph AFB speculation**: Strieber, described as a world-famous alien abductee, is noted as having once considered that his experiences may have been implanted by Air Force contract employees (some described as former Nazi scientists/medical officers) working out of Randolph AFB in San Antonio, Texas, where he grew up.
- **John Mack's abductee study**: cited (endnote 204) for the finding that patients reporting alien abduction experiences typically showed no other psychiatric pathology, and again later for revealing that abductees were often "middle-class Americans" far from any war zone.
- **Levenda's Rhode Island encounter (late 1990)**: Levenda, unloading shopping from his car, notices an old black Cadillac idling with a driver aiming a wide-angle lens at him; he gives chase but is blocked by a second old-model car (a wood-paneled station wagon) from which two women in cloth coats emerge simultaneously (unusual behavior — no driver normally exits to ask directions) and ask for directions to a place ("DeVilbiss," a real machinery-manufacturing company Levenda recalled from an unrelated work conversation) that did not exist anywhere in Rhode Island. He concludes it was a coordinated "stall" by a two-car team.
- **Levenda's Singapore encounter (years later)**: at Changi Airport, one of the same women from the Rhode Island incident taps Levenda on the shoulder, smiles, then vanishes into the crowd — cited as confirming the Rhode Island episode was not random.
- **Levenda's background check on possible motives**: he considers whether the encounter related to his past associations (Arab nationals in New York in the 1960s–70s, including attending a hafla fundraiser for the Red Crescent Society, and PLO observer-office contacts near the UN; time in China in the 1980s; presence in Chile in 1979) but finds none fit the ~20-25-year-later timing or the amateurish, overt nature of the operation (two cars, four agents for one writer).
- **Levenda's December 5, 2019 sighting**: his first-ever personal UFO sighting — a stationary, soundless triangle craft over Fort Lauderdale, Florida — noted as occurring nearly thirty years after the Rhode Island MIB encounter, with no UFO sighting in between.
- **The WWII "World Series" spy test**: a cited wartime trope in which suspected German infiltrators posing as American GIs were caught by asking who won the World Series; used as an analogy for how the Alien or MIB, however fluent, "will eventually screw up."
- **Cattle mutilation phenomenon**: described in detail — animal (usually a cow) found drained of blood, with organs (often sexual organs, sometimes tongue or eyes) surgically removed, no witnesses, no blood at the scene, body displayed on open land ("secondary crime scene"). Jacques Vallée's cited opinion (endnote 205) is that mutilations "may be just what they seem to be: acts of terrorism," with no further discoverable purpose than demonstrating total control and terrifying the population.
- **Parallel structure of cattle mutilation vs. human abduction**: both involve overnight abduction, extraction of fluids (often from sexual organs), and return to (near) the point of origin — cattle returned dead, humans returned alive, sometimes with implants; the authors treat the differing outcome as potentially a deliberate warning.
- **Sekret Machines: Gods cross-reference**: the authors recall claims from the first volume that nonhuman entities have visited Earth since the dawn of recorded history, that ritual specialists across cultures claimed to summon such entities using strict, literal, idiom-free language (to avoid deception), and that Aztec ritual human/animal sacrifice ("factory of murder," hearts removed atop temples) may relate to a hypothesized alien requirement for such practices. The authors ask, since animal sacrifice "still takes place on a grand scale" elsewhere in the world, whether cattle mutilation fills a gap left by the disappearance of ritual animal sacrifice specifically in the American West.
- **Twentieth-century atrocity catalogue**: the authors list WWI trench warfare/gas attacks, WWII firebombing/atomic bombs/the Holocaust, Vietnam, Korea, Indonesian and Philippine dictatorships, African/Middle Eastern/Latin American wars and revolutions — as evidence that if the Alien needed bodies for experimentation, an effectively limitless, low-attention supply already existed, making the choice to target middle-class Americans (or drop mutilated cattle on American farmland) implausible as a resource-driven decision. The authors add that human trafficking, or the abduction of Indigenous people from the Amazon basin or the Australian outback, or of refugees fleeing Syria or Iraq, "could have done all of this easily and without attracting any attention at all" — yet the Alien instead chose visible, attention-drawing targets.

## Thread
This chapter is the third of the volume's declared sequence of lenses on the UFO Phenomenon — after religion and science — now applying military/strategic theory, and it directly continues threads from earlier in the book: the recurring DOD-video/Nimitz/"Tic Tac" evidentiary base, Chris Mellon as a recurring named source, and the Nazi-to-Russian-to-Chinese-to-American government-response survey referenced at the chapter's close. It explicitly cross-references Sekret Machines: Gods (volume one) on ancient nonhuman visitation and ritual sacrifice, tying this volume's "war" framing back to the series' foundational claims. The chapter's speculative CIA/AFOSI/Stuxnet framework and its "fifth column" hypothesis about alien-benevolence believers set up further discussion (signaled but not resolved here) of disinformation, government psychological operations, and the shape of a coming "new alien paradigm" as official acknowledgment (Nimitz, FLIR, Tic Tac videos) proceeds — themes the book's later chapters and the Afterword ("The Alien in the Panopticon") likely develop further regarding secrecy, control, and disclosure.

- Within this book: builds on and cross-references *Sekret Machines: Gods* (volume one) for ancient visitation and ritual-sacrifice claims; continues the DOD-video/Nimitz/Tic Tac evidentiary thread and the Chris Mellon interviews used elsewhere in the volume; sets up the book's ongoing concern with government secrecy and disinformation that the Afterword ("The Alien in the Panopticon") is positioned to address.
