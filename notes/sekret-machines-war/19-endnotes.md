# Endnotes

## In one paragraph
This unit is the book's full citation and annotation apparatus, running from the Preface through the Afterword, and it is far more than a bibliography — many notes carry paragraphs of independent argument, biographical detail, and speculation that never made it into the main text. It documents the sourcing behind each chapter's claims (news stories, declassified CIA documents, memoirs, FBI files, prior Sekret Machines volumes, and a wide range of Ufology, occult, and Cold War literature), while also expanding on threads such as the Qian Xuesen biography, the Reich persecution, the Riconosciuto/Casolaro "Octopus" affair, the Puharich/Nine channeling circle, Soviet and Chinese psychic-research programs, and the numerological reading of the Washington Monument. The Afterword's notes are the most argumentative, laying out DeLonge and Levenda's case that alien abduction is definitionally a form of abuse because it is involuntary, and tying that claim to Foucault's panopticon and History of Sexuality. Read together, the endnotes function as the book's evidentiary backbone — showing where its claims rest on primary documents versus where they rest on contested Ufology lore, informant testimony, or the authors' own interpretive leaps.

## Core claims
- The authors suggest that the "leaked" UFO documents of recent decades, especially the MJ-12/Majestic 12 papers, may be a deliberate intelligence hoax or PSYOP crafted "to gauge public reaction or to follow a disinformation stream," though they concede its value as a PSYOP device "seems rather thin at this point" (note 90).
- The authors question whether Richard Doty actually fed disinformation to Ufologists on behalf of the Air Force, or invented that role for himself, and ask "why give him any attention at all?" (note 13).
- The authors argue that Dr. Garry Nolan's and Dr. Christopher "Kit" Green's research into biological, neurological, or genetic effects on close-encounter experiencers is a legitimate scientific basis for taking the phenomenon seriously (note 14).
- The authors treat the panspermia idea — that the genetic code arrived on Earth from Mars, per Francis Crick's theory and echoed by Neil deGrasse Tyson — as directly relevant to the book's argument about extraterrestrial origins of life (note 26).
- The authors present the Hameroff-Penrose "Orch OR" theory (microtubules capable of quantum states enabling nonlocal consciousness) as science the book will draw on later to support claims about consciousness (note 36).
- The authors imply a deliberate, symbolically loaded choice in commemorating a rocket test's 32nd anniversary on October 31, 1968 — rather than a rounder number like the 30th or 25th — reading Masonic or Kabbalistic significance into both the anniversary count and the October 31 date itself (note 49).
- The authors document that Ray Bradbury, Jack Parsons, and L. Ron Hubbard were all targets of FBI suspicion as Communist sympathizers or foreign agents, framing this as evidence of Cold War paranoia directed at visionaries and countercultural figures (note 51).
- The authors argue that Mildred Brady's 1947 attack on Wilhelm Reich was really an attack on Freud and psychoanalysis by proxy, since Reich's claims about sexual energy made him an easy target (note 150).
- The authors suggest that Reich, Ray Bradbury, and other figures targeted by intelligence agencies represent a pattern of state suppression of unconventional science and thought that recurs throughout the book's Cold War narrative.
- The authors read numerological significance into the number 555 (the Freemason-designed Washington Monument's height in feet, the Hebrew value for "obscurity," and the Greek value of "Necronomicon") as evidence of a hidden layer of meaning in Puharich's Nine material (note 119).
- The authors argue in the Afterword that all alien abduction, by virtue of being involuntary, constitutes "hideous experimentation and rape," drawing an equivalence between a surgeon and Dr. Mengele, and between consensual sex and rape, based solely on the presence or absence of consent (note 212).
- The authors' framing of institutional secrecy draws directly on Michel Foucault's Discipline and Punish (panopticon) and History of Sexuality to underwrite the Afterword's "Alien in the Panopticon" thesis about surveillance, confession, and control (notes 211, 218–219).
- The authors note that Qian Xuesen's forced defection to China after McCarthy-era detainment directly enabled the Chinese missile program and the Silkworm missile later used by Saddam Hussein's forces — implying that American paranoia had strategic blowback (note 46).

## Concepts
- **MJ-12 / Majestic 12 documents** — a set of purportedly leaked government UFO documents whose authenticity the authors treat as dubious; possibly created by Air Force intelligence officer Richard Doty and spread through Ufologist William Moore, conceivably as disinformation aimed at the Soviets.
- **Foo fighter** — WWII-era term for balls of colored light or fire seen by pilots, derived from the French word *feu* ("fire," but also "light" and "heat").
- **Panspermia** — Francis Crick's theory that the genetic code originated off-Earth (here linked to claims the code, or life itself, arrived from Mars).
- **Orch OR (Orchestrated Objective Reduction)** — Stuart Hameroff and Roger Penrose's theory that microtubules in the brain sustain quantum states, allegedly enabling consciousness to achieve nonlocality.
- **Operation HIGHJUMP** — the U.S. Navy Antarctic Developments Program of 1946–1947, officially a scientific/logistics mission but treated by the authors as a site of possible UFO/base encounters.
- **Operation Paperclip** — the postwar U.S. program (referenced via the White Sands Missile Range "Paperclip Crew" history) that imported German scientists, including rocket engineers, into American programs.
- **Flying saucer (term)** — coined in the wake of Kenneth Arnold's 1947 sighting, even though Arnold's was not the first UFO sighting that year.
- **Maury Island incident** — the 1947 Puget Sound case involving Fred Crisman and Harold Dahl, investigated by the FBI while Crisman was separately undergoing an Atomic Energy Commission background check.
- **"The Octopus" (Casolaro/Riconosciuto affair)** — the shorthand for journalist Danny Casolaro's investigation into a sprawling conspiracy (tied to Michael Riconosciuto), cut short by his death in a West Virginia motel room ruled a suicide despite suspicious circumstances, including missing files.
- **Orgone energy / Oranur experiment** — Wilhelm Reich's concept of a universal life energy, and his "Oranur Second Report" documenting an experiment involving it; the basis for his orgone accumulator devices and the later FDA fraud investigation.
- **Teslar watch** — a device invented by Andrija Puharich growing out of his research into Nikola Tesla's work.
- **Philadelphia Experiment** — the purported wartime naval invisibility/teleportation experiment associated with M. K. Jessup and the "Allende" letters, covered in William Moore and Charles Berlitz's book and disputed by other researchers.
- **Psychotronic** — a term that replaced "parapsychology" in Eastern Europe and Russia, favored for sounding more scientific and for reflecting increased use of electronic instruments to measure ESP and other psychic states.
- **The Nine** — the channeled entity/entities contacted through Andrija Puharich and later Phyllis Schlemmer and John Whitmore, with "Tom" reportedly identified as the Egyptian god Atum; the subject of Peter Levenda's *Sinister Forces: The Nine*.
- **Book of Babalon** — a text by Jack Parsons collected in *The Collected Writings of Jack Parsons*, tied to his occult and rocketry milieu.
- **Great Beast / Scarlet Woman / OTO / Golden Dawn** — Thelemic/occult terms from Aleister Crowley's system, which the purported L. Ron Hubbard Jr. memoir claims heavily influenced L. Ron Hubbard's belief system.
- **Necronomicon** — the fictional grimoire from H. P. Lovecraft's stories, used to summon "dark extraterrestrial forces," whose Greek numerical value (555) the authors link to Puharich material.
- **Acámbaro figurines** — clay figurines from the Mexican town of Acámbaro suggesting (per the claim the authors relay) that humans and dinosaurs coexisted; visited by both Andrija Puharich and Perry Mason creator Erle Stanley Gardner.
- **Hokkani boro** — a Roma/Romani term the authors say gave English the phrases "hanky panky" and "hocus pocus," raised in connection with Crisman's "Gypsy" testimony.
- **CIC (Counter Intelligence Corps)** — the U.S. Army investigative body whose involvement in the Kenneth Arnold case (via Frank Brown) the authors read as evidence the Air Force may have suspected Soviet aircraft or a Soviet disinformation campaign.
- **AFOSI (Air Force Office of Special Investigations)** — the agency whose interest in Ufologist William Moore stemmed from his contact with Russian agents seeking information on the American UFO community.
- **Psychosynthesis** — a method developed by Italian psychiatrist Roberto Assagioli, who worked for years with Alice Bailey.
- **Celestial Eye** — per Li Hongzhi's *China Falun Gong*, an ability ("remote sight") allowing a person to see things "a thousand Li (miles) away," raised in the context of China's psychic-research and Falun Gong material.
- **Spiritual pollution** — a phrase Levenda says a Chinese business contact used to describe American culture, distinct from the investment and technology China wanted, in a personal anecdote about unavoidable cultural transmission.
- **Panopticon** — Michel Foucault's concept (from *Discipline and Punish*) of a surveillance architecture that the Afterword invokes as the organizing metaphor for institutional secrecy and control ("the Alien in the Panopticon").
- **Checkist** — an old-fashioned term for Russia's secret police.
- **CinCLant** — naval-memo shorthand for "Commander in Chief, Atlantic Command."
- **When Prophecy Fails** — Festinger, Riecken, and Schachter's 1956 study of a group that predicted the world's destruction, cited as a lens on belief persistence after disconfirmation.
- **Jinn** — beings in Islamic cosmology described by Amira El-Zein as part of "the intelligent world," cited in the Afterword's comparative discussion of non-human intelligences.
- **Tesla's 1901 "message from Mars"**: a front-page newspaper headline the authors cite — "World Speaks to World with Mysterious Signals Through Vast Space, Tesla, the Electrician, Says He Received A MESSAGE FROM MARS," *The Examiner* LXXII (January 4, 1901) — reporting Nikola Tesla's claim to have received a signal from Mars (note 22).
- **Red Shambhala** — Andrei Znamenski's book, cited by the authors for more detail on what they call "this fascinating period where religion, mysticism, and politics combined in weird ways," in connection with the postwar Nazi-UFO discussion opened in Chapter Two (note 41).
- **ELF Magnetic Model document** — a 1987 paper titled "ELF Magnetic Model of Matter and Mind; the Origin of Life; and the Art of Healing" (Essentia Research Associates and ELF Cocoon Corporation), cited as the source behind a capability the authors say was "actually demonstrated by UFOs both in the US and in the Soviet Union" (notes 123–124).
- **"Flying pelicans" theory** — one of several skeptical explanations offered for the Kenneth Arnold sighting, described by the authors as "only one among many improbable solutions" and surveyed alongside other theories in Martin Shough's analysis "The Singular Adventure of Kenneth Arnold" (note 143).
- **Project "Sign"** — the U.S. Air Force's 1949 official study of unidentified aerial objects (Technical Report No. F-TR-2274-IA, Headquarters Air Materiel Command, Wright-Patterson Air Force Base), cited as a primary-document source in Chapter Four (note 88).
- **Willcutts Report** — the 1949 official report on the death of James V. Forrestal, held in the Forrestal Papers at Princeton University Library, cited in Chapter Four (note 100).
- **Tunguska meteorite analysis** — K. P. Stanyukovich and V. A. Bronshten's "Velocity and Energy of the Tungusk Meteorite" (NASA, 1962), part of the Soviet scientific-literature trail the authors cite around Stanyukovich's work in Chapter Three (note 68).
- **The Franklin Scandal** — Nick Bryant's book on powerbrokers and child abuse, cited as further reading in the Afterword's closing note (note 220).

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The Coast to Coast AM interview (2020)**: Lue Elizondo and Christopher Mellon interviewed together by George Knapp — cited as a preface-level marker of the modern disclosure conversation.
- **Congressional UAP disclosure legislation (2023)**: per Kayla Guo's *New York Times* reporting, Congress ordered UFO records released as part of the FY2024 NDAA but dropped a bid for broader disclosure — the authors use this as evidence of partial, contested transparency.
- **Qian Xuesen (H. S. Tsien)**: a Chinese-American rocket scientist arrested and detained by the U.S. government during the McCarthy era; he later defected to the People's Republic of China, "jump-started" its missile program, contributed to development of the Silkworm missile (used by Saddam Hussein's forces in Iraq), and became a vocal PSI/ESP research supporter noticed by the CIA (note 46).
- **Ray Bradbury's FBI file**: a forty-page FBI report characterized his fiction — *The Martian Chronicles* (1950) and *Fahrenheit 451* (1953) — as "definitely slanted against the United States and its capitalistic form of Government," objecting to his anti-McCarthyism and anti-Vietnam War views (note 51).
- **L. Ron Hubbard Jr.'s (Ron DeWolf's) memoir**: "The Telling of Me, by Me" (1981), surfaced through Jamie DeWolf, describes a December 1952 monologue in which Hubbard told his son "the secret to life," laced with Crowleyan Thelemic references; the authors note its authenticity is uncertain — it may be DeWolf's invention (note 55).
- **The 32nd-anniversary rocket-test ceremony (October 31, 1968)**: attended by surviving members of the original test group — Frank Malina, Ed Forman, William C. Rockefeller, and others — but not Jack Parsons, who died in 1952; the authors flag the choice of the 32nd (not 30th or 25th) anniversary, and the October 31 date, as possibly deliberate Masonic/Kabbalistic symbolism (note 49).
- **Kenneth Arnold sighting and the Arnold/Dahl/Crisman affair (1947)**: sourced largely from Arnold and Raymond Palmer's *The Coming of the Saucers*; includes the disputed Tacoma arrival date (Arnold claimed dusk July 29, hotel records show July 30) and the mystery of a hotel reservation Arnold says he never made (notes 139–141).
- **Maury Island incident and Fred Crisman's background**: Crisman became an Army Air Force lieutenant in WWII and later a Reserves captain during Korea; an August–September 1947 FBI background check (triggered by his Atomic Energy Commission job application) ran concurrently with an FBI investigation into his role in the Maury Island incident (note 138); Crisman and Harold Dahl signed an affidavit on August 7, 1947 for the FBI Seattle office (note 144); a witness told SAC Jack B. Wilcox he would deny "ever having seen anything" and claim to be "the biggest liar that ever lived" if contacted by authorities (note 145).
- **The Michael Riconosciuto / Danny Casolaro "Octopus" affair**: journalist Danny Casolaro was found dead in a West Virginia motel room, ruled a suicide despite missing files and suspicious circumstances, while investigating Riconosciuto's story. In February 2001, Riconosciuto (via attorney Louis Buffardi) sent Colin Powell a letter warning of an imminent terrorist attack, later identifying Osama bin Laden by name; the FBI interviewed him both before and two days after the September 11, 2001 attacks. Riconosciuto once referenced a UFO angle to the broader intelligence situation, then recanted (note 146).
- **Wilhelm Reich's persecution**: Mildred Brady's May 1947 *New Republic* article "The Strange Case of Wilhelm Reich" triggered an FDA fraud investigation into his orgone accumulators; the authors frame this as a proxy attack on Freud and psychoanalysis, since Reich had been part of Freud's Vienna circle (note 150).
- **The MJ-12 documents' creation and spread**: allegedly created by Air Force intelligence officer Richard Doty and disseminated through Ufologist William Moore, whose contact with Russian agents interested in the American UFO community drew AFOSI's attention (note 152).
- **The Philadelphia Experiment / M. K. Jessup / Allende affair**: covered in William Moore and Charles Berlitz's book, with corroborating access to information via Jessup's friends Gray Barker and Ivan T. Sanderson; the same William Moore later became entangled with Doty and the MJ-12 documents (note 154).
- **ONR-commissioned UFO study (1967)**: the Office of Naval Research commissioned Dr. James E. McDonald's UFO study; McDonald is noted as a later "fatality" (implying his death is treated as suspicious elsewhere in the book) (note 155).
- **The Puharich/Nine channeling circle**: Andrija Puharich's *Uri* documents séances and the emergence of "the Nine," later continued by Phyllis Schlemmer and John Whitmore after Puharich's death; John Whitmore's 2005 letter to Levenda disputed the book *Sinister Forces: The Nine*'s linkage of Arthur Young, Puharich, the Nine, and the JFK assassination (note 116).
- **Arthur Young / Marina Oswald connection**: Marina Oswald reportedly communicated with Ruth Paine while Paine was visiting Arthur Young in Paoli, per Thomas Mallon's *Mrs. Paine's Garage*, which interviewed both Ruth and Michael Paine (note 132); Young also wrote the introduction to Eric Schroeder's *Zodiac: An Analysis of Symbolic Degrees* (note 131).
- **Falun Gong crackdown and Chinese UFO wave (1999)**: the Chinese government's crackdown on Falun Gong began in July 1999 (per William Dowell's Time interview with Li Hongzhi), followed by a wave of Chinese UFO sightings in September and the remainder of 1999 (note 183).
- **China's AI-assisted UFO tracking**: per a 2021 South China Morning Post article by Stephen Chen, the Chinese military uses AI to track a rapidly increasing number of UFOs (note 186).
- **Mikhail Gorbachev's Esalen ties**: Gorbachev later became deeply involved with the Esalen Institute, the New Age center founded by Michael Murphy and Richard Price (note 188).
- **General Boris Ratnikov (KGB/FSO)**: an Afghanistan War veteran heavily involved in Soviet/Russian paranormal research, including telepathy and remote viewing, per Casey Michel's Daily Beast profile and a Russian obituary noting his comments on coronavirus and paranormalists before his death (note 197).
- **Levenda's 1980s China business trip**: a Chinese customer told Levenda that China wanted American investment and technology but not American culture ("spiritual pollution"); Levenda replied it was already too late, since his mere visible presence carried unavoidable cultural impact (note 207).
- **CIA admission on UFO sightings (1997)**: per William J. Broad's *New York Times* article, the CIA admitted the government had lied about UFO sightings (note 209).

## Thread
As the book's closing apparatus, this unit retroactively grounds (and sometimes visibly strains) the claims made across the Preface, Introduction, all eleven chapters, and the Afterword. It shows the authors' method in miniature: layering declassified CIA files, FBI reports, and mainstream news alongside Ufology books, occult histories, and personal correspondence, then drawing connective and often numerological or symbolic inferences between them (the 32nd anniversary, the number 555, the panopticon frame). The Afterword's notes are pivotal — they supply the philosophical scaffolding (Foucault's panopticon and History of Sexuality) for the book's title thesis, "The Alien in the Panopticon," and make explicit the authors' ethical argument that non-consent alone defines alien abduction as abuse. Read as a whole, the endnotes function as both a paper trail for readers who want to verify sources and a repository of tangents (Qian Xuesen's biography, the Riconosciuto/Casolaro affair, Reich's persecution, Soviet and Chinese psychic programs) that deepen the book's Cold War/national-security through-line without disrupting the main narrative.
