# The Brain-Computer Model

## In one paragraph
DeLonge and Levenda distinguish the computational intelligence of machines from subjective consciousness, contrasting strong AI's brain-as-computer model with the claim that computation can never become conscious and with panpsychism's universe-wide consciousness. They argue that definitions of mind, machine, experience, and knowledge matter to Ufology because an encounter filtered through shock and culture lacks a shared human vocabulary, while sufficiently advanced human-machine integration may erase the practical boundary among human, machine, and alien. GANs provide the chapter's concrete AI mechanism: a generator and discriminator iteratively produce convincing artificial outputs, enabling self-learning and useful simulations but also deepfakes, false environments, propaganda, and inherited racial or gender bias. Integrated information theory then becomes the authors' model for asking whether sensory deficits would make Gray consciousness radically unlike human consciousness, especially if Grays neither hear, speak, eat, taste, reproduce sexually, nor possess art and culture. The chapter's final movement is explicitly speculative: machine-like Grays and biologically destructive UFO maneuvers are interpreted as possible robots or cyborgs, and humanity's march toward the Singularity is portrayed as a convergence with those reported beings rather than proof of their nature.

## Core claims
- One major theory treats the brain as a computer and consciousness as an emergent property or neuronal "secretion." Its strong-AI conclusion is that a sufficiently advanced computer will become conscious.
- Strong AI predicts the Singularity, when conscious machines become human partners in a new form of evolution. Ray Kurzweil is presented as its leading proponent and *The Age of Spiritual Machines* as an emblematic statement.
- The authors identify a conceptual problem in strong AI: intelligence and consciousness are often conflated even though computation or intelligent performance may not entail subjective experience.
- A second school says machines may become intelligent but never conscious because consciousness and computation are fundamentally different.
- Panpsychism supplies a third position: consciousness permeates the universe and all things possess some degree of it.
- Artificial intelligence may therefore differ from artificial consciousness; the authors also ask whether any consciousness can meaningfully be called artificial.
- Continued pursuit of strong AI will experimentally pressure the competing theories. The result matters to alien contact because distinguishing intelligence from consciousness may help identify what humans encounter.
- Neither "consciousness" nor "machine" is adequately defined. Philosophers who reduce humans to machines intensify the legal and ethical problem because eventually turning off a machine could be treated as murder.
- Machines extend human reach: telescopes and microscopes extend sight; telephones and the Internet extend communication; domestic access to media, art, information, commerce, and audiences extends action across time and distance.
- Sensory or bodily extension is not automatically extension of consciousness. Hearing aids, glasses, artificial limbs, pacemakers, and insulin pumps make the human-machine boundary gradual rather than obvious.
- The authors ask whether reports of alien-human hybrids conceal a simpler organic-machine hybrid: the alleged aliens themselves may combine life and machinery.
- Questions about human, alien, and machine identity converge on the question of consciousness.
- Consciousness studies expose unstable epistemological standards: people are told not to trust witnesses' senses while scientific instruments are refined to gather only data already recognized by prevailing definitions of senses and evidence.
- Ufology is classified as belief rather than knowledge and religion rather than science. The authors call that boundary artificial and socially constructed, referring back to the first book.
- Alien-contact witnesses expect ridicule and vilification if they report what they regard as true.
- Critics use both consistency and inconsistency against experiencers: similarity is attributed to collusion or shared television and film imagery, while variation is said to show unrelated events. Either route invalidates testimony.
- Lack of formal credentials—scientist, engineer, military officer, or intelligence officer—is used to discount witnesses' descriptions.
- Ordinary cross-cultural categories such as chair, child, and death have shared human referents despite differences in language and value. UFO, UAP, abduction, and close encounter do not have an equally grounded common vocabulary.
- American media offers culturally specific options from peaceful, luminous, blond and blue-eyed Nordics to sinister short Grays who manipulate organs. The authors treat both as projections by a shocked nervous system confronting an ineffable event.
- The need to distinguish machine from human requires investigation of knowledge, memory, intelligence, language, human value, and whatever is irreducibly human.
- Physical laws should apply somehow to the Phenomenon, though not necessarily identically. Any alien consciousness should share at least some features with human consciousness.
- David Chalmers is quoted treating consciousness as fundamental and irreducible, like space, time, and mass.
- Chalmers's hard problem asks why sensory processing has a felt, subjective dimension and what purpose that dimension serves.
- Pure information-processing machines would not need entertainment, music, literature, art, Valentine cards, funeral services, or *Moby Dick*. The authors use these products to challenge a simple account of consciousness as neuronal emergence.
- Ray Kurzweil is said to discuss programs that write prose and poetry; Ian Goodfellow and the Google Brain Team are said to pursue machine "imagination" for art production.
- A generative adversarial network uses a generator and discriminator in opposition. Their iteration can make synthetic output indistinguishable from data treated as real.
- GANs accelerate tasks that otherwise require long calculations or simulations and are applied to games, Google Maps, medicine, particle physics, and other disciplines.
- Engineers are said to investigate GANs as a route to machine consciousness because a system could self-learn rather than depend upon continuous instruction by an AI programmer.
- Deep learning could compare simulations with large datasets, including mined Internet material, to learn languages and produce poetry, music, and other outputs. These achievements remain functions in the authors' account, not demonstrated subjective consciousness.
- The same capacity creates perfect false images, text, and news. The authors warn that virtual reality can become fake reality with consequential political and social decisions built upon it.
- Self-driving systems could receive plausible images of nonexistent roads or misread altered signs. Whether an AI is flawed or highly convincing, it can cause unprecedented social, political, and cultural disruption.
- Deepfake pornography demonstrated that accessible software could place a famous person's animated face onto a performer's body convincingly enough to damage trust and reputation.
- Digital media manipulation is described as increasingly pervasive and undetectable. The authors call trust the first casualty of AI, adapting the saying that truth is war's first casualty.
- AI can be deliberately used for politics, conspiracy creation, commerce, or defamation, but it can also reproduce unrecognized racial and gender bias in data and engineering assumptions.
- Bryor Snefjella warned on March 22, 2018 that natural-language understanding and generation could be weaponized for authoritarian regimes and that AI can inherit racial and gender biases from training data.
- The authors turn the bias critique on their own subtitle, *Man*, asking whether it encodes gender bias or a deliberately gendered encounter and how a woman, person of color, or differently titled project might organize the material.
- They then speculate that putative alien AI could be hardwired with unfamiliar biases against organic life, sexual reproduction, height, humor, or other characteristics; no evidence for such programming is offered.
- Reported alien environments contain no art, literature, music, or nonutilitarian cultural artifact. Some therefore interpret the archetypal Gray as a human projection of ultimate intelligence rather than an entity.
- Mr. Spock is used as a cultural comparison: logical, emotionless, humorless, anatomically near-human, naturally rather than artificially intelligent, and affectively closer to reported Grays than his anatomy is.
- The chapter asks rather than establishes whether Grays or other reported aliens are artificially intelligent or artificially conscious.
- Giulio Tononi's integrated-information approach is summarized as the brain combining sensory inputs into one picture of reality. Functioning organs and neural pathways determine how much information can be integrated.
- Impaired or absent senses imply less integrated information and, in the chapter's rendering, a corresponding impairment or difference in full consciousness.
- A plant may count as conscious because it integrates light through photosynthesis along with water, temperature, location, soil nutrients, pollutants, and airborne chemicals, though it lacks many human senses and cannot speak.
- Claims that music improves plants' health and growth are mentioned as theorists' claims, not as a result validated in this chapter.
- Grays are reported as mute and earless, like the Prague golem. If so, sound, music, and spoken language could not enter their integrated experience, reducing their understanding of human consciousness by at least one sense.
- Their apparently nonfunctional, vestigial mouths suggest they may not eat. The authors infer that cuisine, flavor, taste, aroma, and associated culture would likewise be inaccessible to them.
- Future machines may have vast computing capacity but no appetites. The authors ask whether taste, preference, culinary discrimination, and a Proustian memory response are necessary to consciousness like ours.
- A machine consciousness critically different from human consciousness could become dangerous, though the chapter frames this as a question.
- Genesis is invoked as a warning that a creator—God—once sought to destroy creations made in the creator's image and likeness.
- The authors assert that technological development is moving toward Singularity and full human-machine symbiosis. They ask whether that endpoint would make humans indistinguishable from Grays.
- Devices that eliminate eating and drinking could create convergence between human and alien as described, while eliminating sexual reproduction and therefore much of the source material of human art, spirituality, emotion, and culture.
- In the authors' cautionary scenario, sexuality becomes obsolete software in "Human Being Rev. 2.0"; humans lose identity while machines gain capacities, efficiency, and access to space.
- Strong-AI arguments can define unwanted human qualities as inefficiencies, but the definition of the greater good depends on whoever controls technology.
- Machine-like bodies would reduce time, cost, and energy barriers to interstellar travel and planetary colonization, while raising the question of whether the colonizers remain human.
- UFO maneuvers involving extreme speed, sudden stops, and right-angle turns would impose G forces destructive to ordinary humans. This motivates, but does not prove, the robot/cyborg/machine-pilot hypothesis.
- The machine-like and apparently sexless character attributed to Grays is presented as consistent with such pilots.
- Human-machine convergence might reach the stars only by leaving both Earth and Earthly human identity behind.
- The authors ask readers to track alien implications alongside every advance in mind research, because technical and neurobiological strengths and vulnerabilities affect control of future outcomes.
- Approaching perfect AI creates what they call an existential crisis for both human and alien consciousness.
- The chapter is expressly not an attack on AI; the authors expect major benefits but say the risks and values have not been thought through adequately.
- Facebook and Google are named as sites accused of collecting personal information, renting it to commercial firms and political lobbyists, violating privacy, and making privacy seem obsolete.
- Alien narratives likewise center on privacy violation: omniscient craft hover as all-seeing eyes and abductors intrude into bedrooms.
- Suspected governmental secrecy about UFO knowledge is contrasted with individual privacy, raising the question of whose information may legitimately be withheld.
- The surveillance state could be an institutional adoption of an alien observation mechanism, or the alien narrative could camouflage government activity. The authors ask whether strong AI will make those alternatives distinguishable.
- Stopping technological development toward Singularity is compared with burning the Library of Alexandria and denying knowledge.
- The proposed response is self-knowledge: humanity must decide what it is, what it wants, and whether it wants to remain human before machines determine those answers.

## Concepts
- **Brain-as-computer model** — consciousness is an emergent output of neuronal computation; advanced computers should therefore eventually develop consciousness.
- **Strong AI** — the prediction that machine intelligence can become machine consciousness, culminating in Singularity and a new human-machine evolutionary partnership.
- **Intelligence-consciousness distinction** — intelligent computation, problem-solving, or creative-looking output need not include subjective awareness. The chapter makes this distinction its central diagnostic tool.
- **Artificial consciousness** — consciousness attributed to a manufactured system. The authors question whether consciousness itself can be artificial even if its substrate is made.
- **Singularity** — the anticipated point at which machines become conscious and human-machine integration alters evolution and social identity.
- **Panpsychism** — the theory that consciousness is a fundamental and distributed feature of the universe rather than an output restricted to brains.
- **Machine as extension** — technology enlarges sight, hearing, touch, communication, mobility, biological regulation, memory access, and social reach without necessarily enlarging consciousness.
- **Organic-machine alien hybrid** — the alternative interpretation that reported aliens, rather than alien-human offspring, combine living material with mechanisms.
- **Experience as knowledge** — the contested proposition that witnesses' direct UFO or contact experiences carry knowledge even when they do not fit credentialed scientific categories.
- **Ineffable encounter / cultural projection** — an event outside ordinary human experience is translated into available images, such as Nordics or Grays, by a nervous system in shock.
- **Fundamental consciousness** — Chalmers's view of consciousness as irreducible, on the conceptual level of space, time, or mass.
- **Hard problem of consciousness** — why sensory information is accompanied by feeling and subjective experience rather than processed without awareness.
- **Generative adversarial network (GAN)** — two neural networks improve synthetic outputs through competition: the generator creates candidates and the discriminator tests them against real data until the distinction can fail.
- **Generator / discriminator** — the GAN's creative and evaluative components. Feedback from the discriminator trains the generator to produce increasingly convincing false output.
- **Deep learning / self-learning** — machine learning from repeated simulations and large datasets without each behavior being directly taught by a human programmer.
- **Simulacrum** — an artificial image, text, face, environment, or other output convincing enough to be taken for its real counterpart.
- **Fake reality** — synthetic input that does not merely represent a virtual environment but deceives human or machine observers and becomes a basis for consequential action.
- **Deepfake** — animated synthetic media that overlays a recognizable person's facial appearance and gestures onto another body or performance.
- **Adversarial input** — credible but false or altered visual information that can cause an autonomous system to perceive a road or sign incorrectly.
- **Algorithmic bias** — racial, gender, or other assumptions inherited from training data or designers, including biases the engineers do not consciously recognize.
- **Integrated information theory** — the cited model associated with Tononi in which consciousness arises through integration of information from senses and internal pathways into a composite reality.
- **Sensory consciousness** — the chapter's inference that absent sensory organs exclude whole classes of information and produce a consciousness unlike or less complete than human consciousness.
- **Plant consciousness** — the implication that plants may possess some consciousness because they integrate environmental data despite lacking many animal senses.
- **Machine appetite** — the proposed test of whether a machine can taste, prefer, remember through taste, or participate in cuisine rather than merely identify ingredients.
- **Human-machine symbiosis / Convergence** — full integration in which humans gain machine efficiencies and extraterrestrial mobility but may lose appetite, sexuality, vulnerability, culture, and recognizable human identity.
- **Privacy versus secrecy** — the individual's claim against surveillance contrasted with an organization's withholding of information, particularly alleged government knowledge of UFOs.
- **Alien Nation** — the authors' phrase for the possibility that state surveillance extends or imitates the alien's reported observation and bodily intrusion.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Loren Eiseley, *The Invisible Pyramid*** — the epigraph imagines programming the best aspects of human personality into a deathless machine to nearly escape bodily mortality. It frames AI as both preservation and replacement.
- **Strong-AI and rival models** — Ray Kurzweil and *The Age of Spiritual Machines* represent the prediction of conscious computers; an unnamed rival school separates consciousness from computation; panpsychism distributes consciousness across all things. The chapter reports the alternatives without a resolving experiment.
- **Living-room extension inventory** — from one chair a person can see stars and planets in real time, converse across the world, watch hundreds of channels or streamed film, hear music old or new, read literature, distribute a photograph to millions, and order pizza or books. This demonstrates extension of reach, not consciousness.
- **Assistive and implanted devices** — hearing aids, glasses, artificial limbs, pacemakers, and insulin pumps show existing human-machine combinations and make any single boundary point arbitrary.
- **Credential and testimony dilemma** — similarity among alien-contact reports is dismissed as copied media imagery; differences are dismissed as evidence of unrelated events. The experiencer loses under either standard, especially without scientific, military, engineering, or intelligence credentials.
- **Shared-word comparison** — chair, child, and death translate across languages because humans share the referents and experiences, even when values differ. UFO and abduction experiences lack that ordinary common base.
- **American alien-image spectrum** — tall blond, blue-eyed Nordics radiating peace and light and short sinister Grays manipulating organs are described as US cultural options for interpreting shock, not as validated species descriptions.
- **David Chalmers, *The Conscious Mind*, 1996** — the Oxford University Press work (New York) is cited for treating consciousness as a fundamental, irreducible feature of reality and for the hard problem of subjective experience.
- **Chalmers's hard-problem examples** — *Moby Dick*, Valentine's Day cards, funerals, music, literature, and art seem unnecessary to a pure processing machine. The examples ask what subjective life adds without establishing a mechanism.
- **Ian Goodfellow's GAN, June 10, 2014** — *Generative Adversarial Nets* (arXiv:1406.2661) describes generator and discriminator networks. The generator sends artificial images; the discriminator compares them with originals and returns judgments; iteration ends when synthetic and real cannot be distinguished.
- **GAN applications** — video and computer games and Google Maps are named as existing applications; medicine and particle physics are proposed as major beneficiaries because adversarial simulation can compress months or years of tests.
- **GAN consciousness proposal** — engineers are said to explore GANs as a route to consciousness and self-learning. Mining the Internet and comparing simulations could produce language, poetry, music, and other functions, but the source offers no test showing felt awareness.
- **Autonomous-car false environment** — a self-driving vehicle could receive a realistic image of a road that does not exist or misread a defaced sign. This illustrates machine vulnerability to convincing visual input.
- **Deepfake pornography, late 2017** — readily available software placed famous faces on porn performers with moving eyes and gestures. Emma Watson was depicted; the material surfaced on Reddit, was later banned even by porn sites, and moved into the Dark Web.
- **Peter Cushing in *Rogue One*** — a similar simulation technology made the deceased actor appear in the film. It demonstrates benign cinematic use of a technique that can also falsely attribute acts to living people.
- **Groucho Marx reversal** — "Who are you going to believe? Me, or your lying eyes?" becomes a warning that manipulated digital evidence makes visual trust unsafe.
- **Bryor Snefjella, March 22, 2018** — the cognitive-science-of-language PhD candidate's BuzzFeed article, "This is the A-Bomb Moment for Computer Science," warns that language technology could serve authoritarianism and that meaning-measurement systems inherit racial and gender bias from training data.
- **The subtitle *Man*** — the authors audit their own framing by asking how *Woman* or *Human*, a female author, or a person of color would change organization and meaning. They do not answer the questions.
- **Alien-AI bias thought experiment** — hypothetical programming preferences could target organic beings, sexual reproducers, tall beings, or humor. The example marks the authors' transition from documented terrestrial bias to unsupported alien speculation.
- **Mr. Spock comparison** — the *Star Trek* character is logical, emotionless, humorless, genetically and environmentally intelligent, and almost human anatomically. His reported affect resembles a Gray more than his body does, illustrating how culture models nonhuman intelligence.
- **Integrated information theory sources** — Giulio Tononi, Melanie Boly, Marcello Massimini, and Christof Koch's July 2016 *Nature Reviews Neuroscience* article (vol. 17, pp. 450–461), plus Masafumi Oizumi, Larissa Albantakis, and Tononi's May 2014 *PLOS Computational Biology* article (vol. 10, issue 5), are cited for consciousness and information integration.
- **Plant data integration** — photosynthesis, water, temperature, location, soil nutrients, air pollutants, and chemicals are inputs a plant integrates. Claims that music improves plant growth are attributed only to unnamed theorists.
- **Mute and earless Gray inference** — reported Grays lack ears and speech, and their vestigial mouths appear unused. The authors infer no music, spoken language, eating, flavor, aroma, or cuisine, then ask how such beings could comprehend human culture.
- **Proustian madeleine test** — a future machine might classify coffee, cognac, and Chinese, Japanese, Spanish, or Italian food but still lack preference and taste-triggered autobiographical feeling. The madeleine asks whether semantic knowledge equals lived consciousness.
- **Human Being Rev. 2.0** — the cautionary scenario removes appetite and sexual reproduction as inefficient software, permitting cheaper interstellar travel while erasing emotional vulnerability and cultural identity.
- **November 2004 southern California UFO footage** — gun-camera footage of an object off the coast was released with Helene Cooper, Ralph Blumenthal, and Leslie Kean's *New York Times* article “Glowing Auras and ‘Black Money’: The Pentagon's Mysterious U.F.O. Program” on December 16, 2017. The chapter uses extreme reported maneuvers to motivate the nonorganic-pilot hypothesis.
- **High-speed pilot constraint** — tremendous speed followed by an immediate stop or ninety-degree turn would generate G forces destructive to a normal human. Robots, cyborgs, or machines are offered as possible pilots; no calculation or biological measurement is included.
- **Facebook and Google privacy allegations** — the sites are said to have compiled personal data and rented it to businesses and political lobbyists, provoking questions about whether privacy survives.
- **Alien surveillance analogy** — a craft appearing without warning and hovering like an all-seeing eye, or an abducting presence entering a bedroom, mirrors technological and state intrusion. The authors leave open whether government copied alien methods or generated alien stories as camouflage.
- **Library of Alexandria analogy** — halting technological progress is equated with destroying knowledge. The authors therefore argue for ethical self-definition rather than prohibition.

## Key quotes
- "a kind of secretion of the neurons" — the brain-computer account of consciousness.
- "machines can be intelligent, but that is not the same thing as conscious" — the chapter's governing distinction.
- "There is a missing vocabulary here" — the epistemological problem in contact testimony.
- "a fundamental feature, irreducible to anything more basic" — Chalmers on consciousness.
- "Eventually, the discriminator can't tell the difference" — the GAN's threshold of successful simulation.
- "virtual reality" could become "fake reality" — the social danger of convincing synthesis.
- "the first casualty of AI is trust" — the chapter's deepfake warning.
- "inherit the racial and gender biases of their training data" — Snefjella on machine meaning systems.
- "Not a bug, but a feature." — the speculative possibility of programmed alien bias.
- "Will a machine be able to taste a madeleine and have a Proustian moment?" — the test of lived rather than computed experience.
- "will we be able to distinguish ourselves from the Grays?" — convergence turned into alien identification.
- "before the machines make up our minds for us" — the closing reason for human self-knowledge.

## Thread
Chapter 11 gives Section Three its conceptual diagnostic: do not confuse high-performing intelligence, convincing simulation, information integration, and subjective consciousness. The authors begin with established or actively researched technologies—assistive devices, GANs, deep learning, synthetic media, autonomous perception, and data-driven bias—and use them to show that the human-machine boundary and trust in sensory evidence are already unstable. They then apply integrated-information reasoning to reported Gray anatomy, inferring a consciousness without sound, speech, food, taste, sexuality, or culture. The final alien identification remains conjectural: extreme UFO maneuvers and utilitarian Gray reports fit a robot or cyborg model, but the chapter supplies no direct test of a Gray, so terrestrial technological convergence functions as analogy and warning rather than confirmation.

## Connections
- **Explicit connection to the Section Three introduction:** The chapter develops the announced brain-computer route to consciousness and the alien while repeating the problem of language, projection, culture, and the ineffable.
- **Explicit connection to Chapters 7–10:** The two schools of consciousness, panpsychism, Chalmers's hard problem, sensory anatomy, and brain information processing resume Section Two's theoretical and neurobiological groundwork.
- **Explicit connection to Chapters 1 and 3:** The artificial Visitor hypothesis, reported Gray anatomy, lack of empathy and culture, hybrid language, and golem-like muteness are reconsidered through AI and integrated information.
- **Explicit connection to Chapter 4:** The possibility that humans are reverse engineering not only craft but pilots reappears as convergence toward robotic or cyborg space travelers.
- **Explicit forward connection:** The claim that implanted and assistive technologies already make humans partial machines leads directly into *I, Cyborg* and the historical design of biological systems for space.
- **Explicit forward connection:** The chapter's concern with language-free information and machine minds prepares *Can You Hear Me Now?*, where telepathy, remote viewing, brain interfaces, and information theory become the communications mechanism.
- **Explicit trilogy connection:** The authors refer back to the first book's argument that science and religion are socially constructed categories and defer the government UFO program and its historical setting to the project's broader investigation.
- **Source-bounded critical connection:** GANs, deepfakes, integrated-information publications, assistive devices, privacy disputes, and released footage are the chapter's terrestrial evidentiary base; artificial Grays, alien programming bias, convergence with Visitors, and an "Alien Nation" remain extrapolations signaled by questions and conditional language.
