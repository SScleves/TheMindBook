# The Chinese Configuration

## In one paragraph
DeLonge and Levenda compare the sixty-four hexagrams of the Chinese *I Jing* with the sixty-four genetic codons, then extend the numerical comparison through Yoruba Ifa, Arab and European geomancy, and John Dee's Angelic Tablets. They openly call the proposed twenty-cell Tablet of Union/twenty-amino-acid correspondence outrageous and unproven, but interpret the recurring powers of four, binary forms, serpent imagery, and matrix patterns as possible cultural intimations of a deliberately seeded genetic code. The chessboard then shifts the chapter from symbolic correspondences to conflict: the authors portray pregnancy as a biological contest between fetus and mother, genetic inheritance as a contest between paternal and maternal interests, and individual consciousness as resisting DNA's reproductive program and indifference to personal immortality. From there they construct a speculative alien-hybrid scenario, using contemporary CRISPR embryo editing only to argue technological plausibility, not as evidence that nonhuman beings are carrying out such a program. They end by separating documented scientific statements and published figures from their own interpretations, arguing that materialist science, political funding, and threat-focused UFO research may miss the central question of whether consciousness can exist apart from biology and whether the Visitors are conscious beings or intelligent machines.

## Core claims
- The chapter begins from the precise mathematical correspondence that four genetic bases in ordered groups of three produce sixty-four codons, while the *I Jing* combines binary lines into sixty-four six-line hexagrams.
- The authors do not claim that any particular assignment of G, A, C, and T to Chinese line-pairs carries demonstrated meaning. They explicitly call those assignments arbitrary and restrict their initial claim to the numerical fit.
- They nevertheless connect that fit conditionally to earlier material: if Crick and Orgel were right about directed panspermia, and if shCherbak and Makukov were right about deliberate mathematical design, cultural and spiritual systems might preserve an awareness of the code.
- The *I Jing* is presented not merely as fortune-telling but as a philosophy of process, relation, and constant movement between states; the authors treat this as a poetic analogue to the dynamic genetic code.
- Yoruba Ifa supplies a larger binary structure: sixteen base figures combine with sixteen to make 256 odu. Its oral corpus preserves cultural, religious, and historical knowledge, making it metaphorically a clan's genetic code.
- Geomancy, the *I Jing*, and Ifa are organized as successive powers of four: $4 \times 4 = 16$, $4 \times 4 \times 4 = 64$, and $4 \times 4 \times 4 \times 4 = 256$.
- The special role of four may reflect the Platonic elements or a more general image of completion. The chapter reports C. G. Jung's view that the quaternary expresses wholeness and points to mandalas, crosses, and swastikas as four-direction ideographs.
- The authors characterize John Dee's occult work as a possible "meta-science" joining mathematics, physics, biology, and consciousness, while acknowledging that his Angelic Tablet has resisted both skeptical and believing explanations.
- Dee's large $25 \times 27$ grid yields four elemental subtablets, sixty-four remaining lettered elemental cells in each, 256 such cells in all, and a twenty-cell Tablet of Union.
- The sixty-four-cell elemental structure can be overlaid with either *I Jing* hexagrams or genetic codons, and its fourfold total can be overlaid with Ifa's 256 odu; these are structural correspondences asserted by the authors, not experimental evidence of common origin.
- DeLonge and Levenda propose that the twenty cells of the Tablet of Union unconsciously anticipate the twenty amino acids produced through the sixty-four-codon system. They expressly label this claim "outrageous" and "unproven."
- Their broader interpretation is that genetic knowledge may have emerged progressively in Chinese, African, Middle Eastern, and European symbolic systems before Watson and Crick's twentieth-century work, which they link speculatively to directed panspermia, alien intelligence, and consciousness.
- The caduceus, yogic twin channels, Ningishzida, and intertwined Nu Wa and Fu Xi are presented as old double-serpent or double-helix ideographs. The resemblance is interpretive; the chapter offers no scientific dating or transmission study establishing knowledge of DNA.
- The Staff of Asclepius is treated as a single-helix anticipation of RNA, again as symbolic resemblance rather than biological evidence.
- The chapter claims a double helix occurs naturally only in DNA, unlike isolated single helices in shells and climbing plants, and compares that uniqueness with the earlier chapter's treatment of zero. This is the authors' stated premise, not independently evaluated here.
- A standard chessboard supplies a widely distributed $8 \times 8 = 64$ matrix onto which codons or hexagrams can be placed. Its opposing armies also introduce the chapter's conflict motif.
- The authors use *shah mat*, "the king is dead," to ask why a life-associated number became the field for lethal opposition and to foreshadow *Sekret Machines: War*.
- They say humanity repeatedly reenacts an ancient trauma through religion and war; that is a trilogy-level interpretation, not a biological finding.
- Pregnancy is framed as maternal-fetal conflict: the placenta seeks blood and nutrients while the endometrium restricts access to protect the mother, producing a negotiated outcome rather than simple biological harmony.
- The chapter reports that more than 500,000 women die annually in childbirth or pregnancy, millions more are injured or disabled, and 15 percent suffer life-threatening complications. These are figures stated by the authors without a source citation in the unit.
- Eclampsia, menstruation, fetal-cell transfer, blood-pressure and blood-sugar changes, and placental control of maternal resources are used to support the conflict model. The chapter's broad contrast with other mammals is its own presentation.
- Genetic imprinting extends the conflict into the fetus: paternal genes may turn fetal functions on or off, and the authors describe paternal reproductive interests as diverging from maternal health interests.
- The claim that fetuses from short-term relationships may be weaker than those from long-term relationships is reported as what the authors call a prevailing biological belief; the chapter gives no study, researcher, or data for it.
- "Matrix" links womb, mother, and matter. The authors treat the placenta/endometrium interface, chessboard, *I Jing*, codon table, and double helix as two- and three-dimensional forms of a world-forming pattern.
- Pregnancy produces not just matter but a new consciousness; the authors speculate that consciousness may depend on the maternal-paternal genetic combination plus its position in space, time, and environment.
- Following Dawkins's "selfish gene" image, the chapter treats organisms as carriers through which DNA survives and reproduces. Genetic continuity is called a practical kind of immortality.
- The authors distinguish the absence of a programmed death command from the absence of a program for individual immortality: after reproduction, continued repair brings little genetic advantage, while accidents, violence, and environmental threats make very long-lived bodies inefficient.
- Individual consciousness wants survival and immortality while the genetic program favors turnover and diversity. The authors interpret this disagreement as evidence that consciousness may have arrived relatively late and may not be reducible to the genetic imperative.
- Reported abductors are said to bypass or suppress human consciousness and work directly on bodies, especially reproductive systems. This is a characterization of abduction accounts, not a verified account of nonhuman practice.
- The authors explore, without asserting, whether seeded RNA mutated into terrestrial DNA, whether the Others' lineage reached a genetic dead end, and whether human material is being used to restore diversity while preserving selected nonhuman traits.
- The scenario assumes shared ancestry, different environments, inbreeding or over-editing, embryo farming, and hybrid testing. Every stage is introduced as a "what if" or "for the sake of argument," not as an established program.
- Contemporary CRISPR embryo editing is offered as a technological analogy that makes deliberate gene replacement imaginable. It is not presented as evidence of extraterrestrial or ultraterrestrial involvement.
- The chapter says nonconsensual genetic experimentation would be a *casus belli* between nations and compares an inaccessible, intermittently appearing actor with an accidental terrorist whose effects cause terror even if terror is not its tactic.
- Columbus's arrival and its consequences are used as a cautionary analogy for technologically unequal contact, disease transfer, slavery, and catastrophic unintended effects.
- Cancer cells and DNA are both called theoretically immortal while reproducing; cancer differs by killing the host on which its own persistence depends. The authors then ask speculatively whether a balanced tumor could develop a consciousness separate from its host.
- Myths and practices of resurrection, afterlife, shamanic death and rebirth, Asclepius, Osiris, and star travel are interpreted as consciousness resisting genetic mortality and as possible responses to a stellar origin of the code.
- The authors propose that contact occurred after humanity reached a programmed level of sophistication and that humans interpreted genetically related, flight-capable, anomalous beings as gods from the stars. This is their synthesis of prior textual material, not a published scientific result.
- Materialist use of "ghost in the machine" is criticized for assuming mind dies with body. Religious and spiritual claims that soul or spirit survives are reported as contrary positions, not proved by the chapter.
- Heisenberg's published statement that science describes nature as exposed to human questioning supports the narrower claim that observer and method cannot be cleanly removed from what is known.
- The authors argue that materialist selection, grant politics, and defense rationales narrow scientific inquiry and encourage UFO research to be treated chiefly as a military or intelligence threat.
- The final open alternatives are whether Visitors solved the mind-body problem, possess nonhuman consciousness, or are intelligent machines without consciousness. The chapter says either answer would alter understanding of human mind and will.
- Instinct is described as genetically encoded machine programming, while human will is the capacity to inhibit or transcend instinct. The independent will is the chapter's "ghost in the sekret machine."

## Concepts
- **I Jing / Classic of Changes** — an ancient Chinese binary divination and philosophical system using changing broken and unbroken lines to represent processes and relationships.
- **Yin and yang** — passive and active principles represented respectively by broken and solid lines; the chapter also associates them with even and odd numbers.
- **Trigram / hexagram** — three-line binary figures form eight trigrams; paired trigrams form sixty-four six-line hexagrams.
- **Codon correspondence** — the numerical equivalence between four bases grouped by threes and the sixty-four Chinese combinations. The chapter separates this exact count from arbitrary base-to-line assignments and symbolic meanings.
- **Ifa / odu** — Yoruba divination and oral scripture built from 256 eight-digit binary forms; practitioners must learn each figure's meanings, rituals, associations, and correspondences.
- **Geomancy** — an Arab and European sixteen-figure system that may, the authors suggest, underlie or precede Ifa; it is also called *sikidy* in Madagascar in the footnote.
- **Quaternary** — organization by four, associated with the four Platonic elements, cardinal directions, mandalas, crosses, and completion.
- **Angelic Tablet** — John Dee and Edward Kelley's $25 \times 27$ letter grid for angelic communication, later reorganized through Golden Dawn elemental correspondences.
- **Calvary Cross** — a Golden Dawn name for ten-cell crosses within each elemental subtablet; four crosses account for forty cells per subtablet.
- **Tablet of Union** — the twenty unique lettered cells derived from the grand cross joining the four elemental tablets; the authors compare its count with twenty amino acids.
- **Directed panspermia** — deliberate seeding of terrestrial genetic material by intelligence; here it is the proposed bridge between mathematical correspondences and the Phenomenon.
- **Caduceus / double-helix ideograph** — twin serpents around an axis, associated with Hermes and interpreted by the authors as prefiguring DNA.
- **Ida, Pingala, and Sushumna** — two yogic energy channels entwined around a central bodily channel; balancing them is said to integrate autonomic, hormonal, biological, and psychological systems.
- **Chessboard matrix** — sixty-four alternating cells that fit codons and hexagrams while staging conflict between opposed forces.
- **Maternal-fetal conflict** — competition between fetal demand through the placenta and maternal protection through the endometrium, with healthy birth as a draw.
- **Genetic imprinting** — parental-origin effects that turn fetal DNA functions on or off; used here to describe competing paternal and maternal evolutionary interests.
- **Matrix** — a pattern or womb in which material form develops; its Latin root is linked in the chapter to mother, matter, and womb.
- **Selfish gene / genetic imperative** — DNA's tendency to persist through reproduction and variation rather than preserve any individual carrier indefinitely.
- **Genetic immortality** — continuity of ancestral genetic material across reproducing organisms, contrasted with mortality of each host.
- **CRISPR** — "clusters of regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats," described as an editing tool that removes and replaces gene sections.
- **Ultraterrestrial** — a hypothetical nonhuman species hidden somewhere on Earth rather than arriving from another planet.
- **Accidental terrorism** — terror produced as a side effect of intermittent, invasive action rather than a demonstrable deliberate tactic.
- **Cancer-cell immortality** — indefinite cell reproduction while a host remains alive; it becomes self-defeating when tumor growth kills that host.
- **Ghost in the machine** — the mind-body problem framed as a possibly separable mind inhabiting a biological mechanism; the authors turn it into the "ghost in the sekret machine."
- **Hard problem of consciousness** — unexplained subjective phenomena that do not fit a strictly material account and are therefore too often discarded, according to the authors.
- **Instinct versus will** — inherited unconscious programs promote survival and reproduction, while conscious will can resist those programs.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Oppenheimer's 1954 methodological epigraph** — *Science and the Common Understanding* (Simon & Schuster, New York, 1954) says new truth can arise when techniques and ideas developed separately are brought together. It frames the chapter's cross-cultural comparisons.
- **Chinese binary construction** — more than three thousand years ago, according to the chapter, philosophers grouped solid yang and broken yin lines into eight trigrams and sixty-four hexagrams. Even 6 and 8 mark broken lines; odd 7 and 9 mark solid lines.
- **G-A-C-T line assignment** — for illustration only, the authors pair guanine, adenine, cytosine, and thymine with four two-line figures. Any such digram can be assigned to any base, and replacing RNA's U with DNA's T still leaves all sixty-four combinations.
- **Tempting hexagram meanings** — a stop codon can be paired with titles such as "Breaking Apart" or "After Completion," but the authors explicitly decline to treat this as their argument because the assignments are arbitrary.
- **Dynamic *I Jing*** — solid lines become broken and broken lines solid, making each hexagram part of a changing process. The authors call this a poetic analogue rather than a biochemical mechanism.
- **Yoruba Table of Ifa** — $4 \times 4 \times 4 \times 4 = 256$ odu, or sixteen combinations of sixteen fundamental figures, use single and double lines. Consultants train for years to memorize the unwritten scripture's meanings, rituals, associations, and history.
- **Eji-Ogbe and Oyeku Meji** — among the sixteen fundamental odu, two columns of single lines signify light in Eji-Ogbe and two columns of double lines signify darkness in Oyeku Meji; the authors compare them with yang and yin.
- **Geomantic precursor** — Arab and European geomancy has sixteen simpler figures and is presented as a possible older source for Ifa, metaphorically "divinatory RNA" beside Ifa's fuller DNA. Footnote 44 names Madagascar's *sikidy* and cites Philip M. Peek's 1991 Indiana University Press volume, pp. 52–68.
- **Powers-of-four sequence** — geomancy has 16 forms, the *I Jing* 64, and Ifa 256. Earth, Air, Fire, and Water supply one possible later association with the four genetic bases.
- **Jung's quaternary examples** — Asian mandalas organize four directions, gates, paths, and gods; crosses and swastikas represent four directions or a rotating sun/star. These examples support symbolic wholeness, not genetic transmission.
- **Jack Parsons and Dee/Kelley** — rocket scientist and Jet Propulsion Laboratory cofounder Parsons is said to have used the Elizabethan magicians' angelic system in his occult work; fuller discussion is deferred to *Sekret Machines: War*.
- **John Dee's scientific reputation** — navigation, astronomy, mathematics, and his 1570 introduction of Euclid's *Elements* to English readers complicate his popular image as merely a sorcerer.
- **Leibniz's binary bridge** — Gottfried Leibniz (1646–1716), co-inventor of calculus with Newton and creator of the first mechanical calculator in the chapter's account, recognized modern binary notation as the basis of the *I Jing*. Figure 5 reproduces his sixty-four-hexagram square and circle.
- **Angelic Tablet arithmetic** — the main grid has 675 cells ($25 \times 27$). Four elemental grids have 156 each ($12 \times 13$), leaving 51 cells as a grand cross; 40 contain repeated letters, which reduce to 20 unique cells in a $4 \times 5$ Tablet of Union.
- **Calvary Cross subtraction** — each 156-cell tablet contains four ten-cell crosses, plus sixteen cells across their horizontal arms. The authors say sixty-four individually lettered, elementally associated cells remain.
- **Elemental overlay** — an Air rank intersecting an Air column on the Air tablet is "purely air." This nested association lets the authors overlay sixty-four hexagrams or codons on each tablet and 256 Ifa odu on all four.
- **Twenty-amino-acid proposal** — the authors compare the twenty-cell Tablet of Union with twenty amino acids produced by the codon system. They place it alongside Gamow's, shCherbak's, and Makukov's number patterns but supply no test establishing intentional encoding.
- **Hermes's caduceus** — two winged serpents entwine around an axis on the messenger god's emblem; wings become an additional alleged sign of divine or nonterrestrial origin.
- **Yogic body map** — Ida and Pingala entwine Sushumna like twin serpents around a spine. Yoga and Tantra are said to balance this binary energy system and integrate autonomic, hormonal, bodily, and psychological activity.
- **Ningishzida image** — Figure 7 shows the four-thousand-year-old Sumerian "Serpent Lord" as two serpents around a central axis.
- **Nu Wa and Fu Xi image** — an undated Xinjiang Province painting shows the Chinese creator goddess and her brother, the "first human," as intertwined serpents amid constellations, holding instruments the authors say could be read as Masonic. The caption interprets humanity as a double helix "coming from the stars."
- **Natural-form comparison** — shells and climbing plants can display a single helix, while the authors say a natural double helix is unique to DNA. They infer special significance from its use in Greece, China, Sumer, and India.
- **Staff of Asclepius** — a single serpent around a staff is treated as an RNA ideograph. Asclepius is also a divine-human hybrid, cut from his unfaithful human mother's womb after Apollo had her killed.
- **Common chessboard** — 64 squares divide into 32 black and 32 white or red cells. The board also fits the authors' genetic table, *I Jing* arrangement, Mercury's magic square, and other associations shown in Figure 10.
- **Checkmate etymology and battlefield** — Persian *shah mat*, "the king is dead," supplies the lethal meaning of the board. The authors use this to pivot from life's code to conflict among kings, gods, humans, hybrids, and weapons in *War*.
- **Pregnancy-risk figures** — the text estimates more than 500,000 maternal deaths yearly, millions of pregnancy injuries or disabilities, and life-threatening complications in 15 percent of women. No underlying report is identified in the unit.
- **Placenta/endometrium contest** — the fetus attaches to a blood-rich surface, changes maternal sugar and pressure, sends fetal cells into her blood, uses her waste system, and competes for oxygen and nutrients. Eclampsia is given as high maternal blood pressure arising from excessive fetal demand.
- **Human menstruation comparison** — primates and a kind of bat also menstruate, but the chapter says human shedding carries unusually heavy bleeding, headaches, cramps, mood changes, and vulnerability. Flushing removes unfertilized ova and nonviable embryos while some blood is reabsorbed.
- **Pregnancy as chess** — placenta and endometrium are opposing players; mother and fetus both survive only in a draw. The child contains two "armies," maternal and paternal DNA, and the queen is the board's most powerful piece.
- **Genetic-imprinting scenario** — paternal genes switch fetal functions on or off. The chapter claims a father favors his fetus even at maternal cost and reports the unsupported belief that parental closeness strengthens certain fetal traits relative to conception in a one-night stand.
- **Matrix and chaturanga** — placenta/endometrium, the $8 \times 8$ chessboard, the $8 \times 8$ hexagram layout, and the $4 \times 4 \times 4$ code are called womb-like matrices. Indian chaturanga is another $8 \times 8$ game; the authors turn a two-dimensional combination table into a three-dimensional helix.
- **Walking genetic libraries** — inherited DNA millions of years old makes people carriers of ancestral history. The body's skeleton, organs, brain, and nervous system are described as serving transmission to later generations.
- **Thousand-year person and Methuselah** — a person living for a millennium would keep redundant genetic material in circulation, while Biblical longevity represents consciousness's value rather than DNA's. These are thought experiments about turnover, not studies.
- **Abduction/reproduction puzzle** — alleged Visitors ignore consciousness, focus on genitals and animal or human reproductive organs, and perform apparently crude procedures despite presumed advanced travel. The chapter challenges that presumption and asks whether the biological task is deeper.
- **RNA-seed hypothesis** — the authors speculate that RNA rather than DNA was seeded, that terrestrial DNA is a mutation, and that RNA's present service to DNA might puzzle the originating beings.
- **Genetic-dead-end scenario** — prolonged inbreeding or excessive removal of unwanted genes could leave another species nonviable and unable to adapt. The authors compare that hypothetical extinction risk with Neanderthals and Denisovans and project similar long-term risks onto a shrinking human population.
- **Human vulnerability inventory** — climate change, war, famine, drought, epidemic, super-bugs, antibiotic resistance, and unintended effects of gene editing are used as pressures that could shrink diversity or release an incurable virus or bacterium. The statement that most human DNA is bacterial is made parenthetically by the authors.
- **Alien-human embryo farm** — a related off-world or hidden terrestrial species takes robust human material, edits it, preserves valued nonhuman traits, avoids diseases, and grows hybrid embryos on Earth because its home environment is unstable or nutrient-poor. The entire chain is hypothetical.
- **Chinese CRISPR reports** — while the book was being prepared, the authors report that Chinese scientists edited nonviable embryos to neutralize a gene considered an HIV marker; the previous year another Chinese team modified a blood-disease gene. These reports demonstrate a human editing capability only.
- **Dictatorship and Nazi analogies** — accessible CRISPR could enable unethical work on viable embryos or unwilling women under a dictatorship; the authors say Nazi eugenic scientists would have used it if available. No actual modern abduction program is identified.
- **Invisible enemy analogy** — nonconsensual alien or ultraterrestrial experimentation would be a reason for war, yet there is no reachable territory or visible army. Intermittent appearances, mixed signals, abduction, release, and hybrid babies produce "accidental" terror.
- **Columbus, October 11, 1492** — the authors say Columbus saw a UFO the night before landing in the Bahamas, then speculate playfully about Antarctica or Sirius. They contrast his unfamiliar ships, clothes, language, and technology with Native Americans' later deaths from imported illness and Spanish slavery.
- **Cancer-host thought experiment** — cancer reproduces indefinitely while a host lives but dies when it kills that host. The authors ask whether a tumor that learned restraint might eventually form a separate consciousness.
- **Aging inventory** — prolonged human life brings later illness, reduced mobility, withdrawal from world to home, room, and bed, and breakdown of bodily and psychological support. The authors personify genes as eventually wanting the host dead.
- **Immortality traditions** — heaven, Olympus, Kailash, Christian resurrection, Asclepius restoring the dead, shamanic death/rebirth and star travel, and Egyptian Osiris and mummification all exemplify consciousness's refusal of mortality.
- **Contact-as-gods scenario** — after genetically programmed development, contact occurs with the code's makers or a related species. Differences in size, animal features, paranormal abilities, and flight lead humans to call them gods from the stars who created humanity; the authors then say "something went wrong."
- **Heisenberg, 1958** — *Physics and Philosophy* (Harper & Row, New York, p. 81) says science participates in the interplay between nature and questioner. The authors apply this to observations rejected because they do not fit materialist theory.
- **Grant and defense system** — expensive work at CERN or in genetic laboratories requires institutional money; poets-in-garrets cannot substitute for equipment. The authors say national-security utility improves funding odds and channels the Phenomenon into military/intelligence categories.
- **Visitor-consciousness alternatives** — conscious nonhumans would separate consciousness from human neurobiology; super-intelligent but unconscious machines would support a different answer. The chapter presents both as questions pending evidence.

- "Consciousness is no friend of the genetic imperative." — the chapter's central opposition.

- "...nature as exposed to our method of questioning." — Heisenberg on scientific observation.
- "It’s the ghost in the sekret machine." — will as the unplanned element in a programmed organism.

## Thread
Chapter 5 answers the previous chapter's sixty-four-fold riddle with the *I Jing*, then widens the pattern through Ifa, geomancy, Dee's Angelic Tablets, serpent imagery, and the chessboard. Its first movement is comparative and explicitly speculative: exact arithmetic is repeatedly distinguished from unproved meaning. The chessboard's opposing sides then become a biological model for pregnancy, inheritance, aging, and the conflict between DNA's continuity and consciousness's wish to preserve the individual. CRISPR makes genetic intervention technologically imaginable, but the alien rescue or hybrid-farming scenario remains a chain of hypotheticals. The close converts that conflict into Section Two's question: whether consciousness is generated by, received through, or independent of the biological machine.
