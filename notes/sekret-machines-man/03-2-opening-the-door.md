# Opening the Door

## In one paragraph
DeLonge and Levenda argue that twentieth-century culture split the Phenomenon into comforting extraterrestrials, Christian demons, physical craft, and psychologically or religiously explained abductions, even though these may be culturally different representations of one source. They say human consciousness manufactures meaning through what Jung called an "acausal connecting principle," so witnesses may anthropomorphize a sekret machine as piloted transport when it could be empty, autonomous, immaterial, drone-like, or projected. The authors trace how church, state, science, and psychology have successively determined sanctioned reality: medieval authorities treated contact with alien spiritual powers as witchcraft and treason, while modern institutions deny the paranormal and pathologize witnesses, leaving no recognized defense or interpretive authority. They compare abduction reports with succubi, incubi, jinn, and the Witches' Sabbat, emphasizing a recurring experience under changing cultural vocabularies rather than claiming those categories are literally identical. Finally, they contrast official denial with nuclear-site incidents, Soviet records, AATIP, a NASA study of interstellar communication, and Hynek's close-encounter scale to argue that both machines and human testimony must be studied without assuming that observed entities are necessarily pilots.

## Core claims
- The authors describe 1970s popular culture as offering two opposing nonhuman futures: largely benign aliens in *E.T.* and *Close Encounters of the Third Kind*, and explicitly Christian demonic possession in *The Exorcist* and *The Omen*.
- They interpret Pazuzu's history as showing how pre-Christian divine or paranormal beings were subordinated to Christian power and recast as demons, rather than being invented by Christianity.
- The end of the Vietnam War, Watergate, the fading of 1960s optimism, and the transition from Woodstock to Altamont are presented as a cultural setting in which political leaders and younger generations could both appear demonic or untrustworthy.
- By the 1980s, cattle mutilations and alien-abduction reports allegedly shifted the popular alien from benign elder sibling to amoral experimenter manipulating DNA, implanting devices, probing bodies, and reproducing the older sexual functions attributed to succubi and incubi.
- The authors acknowledge that physical UFO evidence is easier to accept than beings who land, pass through walls, repeatedly abduct people, conduct sexual experiments, and implant devices.
- Their central interpretive claim is that reported gods, angels, demons, jinn, alien abductions, and contact with demonic forces may be cognate expressions of a singular Phenomenon described through different eras and cultures. They say UFOs and abductions are related, though the mechanism may place them "a step or two" apart.
- Human consciousness sets up relationships and manufactures meaning outside a Newtonian causal context; Carl Jung's term "synchronicity" names coincidence as an "acausal connecting principle."
- The authors define a UFO minimally as a machine appearing and disappearing according to unknown laws and motives—a "sekret machine"—and warn against assuming every object is material transport with an internal operator.
- Possible craft explanations posed in the chapter include occupied machines, empty devices, drones/UAVs, autonomous or nonconsciously controlled objects, and holographic projections; these are possibilities, not reported findings.
- Because human flying machines have operators, witnesses project the same relation onto UFOs. The authors argue that this inference is logical but exceeds the publicly available evidence.
- When academic, scientific, governmental, and military authorities withhold evidence or refuse interpretation, experiencers must interpret events themselves; the authors summarize the result as "interpretation becomes representation; speculation becomes revelation."
- Institutional withdrawal may postpone the issue but, the authors argue, erodes public trust in the state's ability to inform, protect, and defend.
- The December 2017 public revelation of the Pentagon's Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program is presented as a tipping point after which official ridicule and blanket denial are no longer viable, especially because knowledgeable government and industry figures had come forward since 2015.
- Medieval church and state possessed a coherent, if misguided, paranormal framework: unexplained phenomena belonged to the Devil, witnesses or intermediaries could be arrested and killed, and confiscation of accused persons' property introduced an economic motive into the Inquisition.
- The chapter insists the Phenomenon cannot be studied solely as technology, spirituality/consciousness, or military conflict because it also interacts with human institutions and politics.
- The authors contrast US Air Force claims that UFOs pose no security threat with reports of nuclear-site interference in the Soviet Union and United States; they say such incidents logically merit security study.
- In medieval Europe, witchcraft was both spiritual rebellion and treason because secular authority derived legitimacy from the church, which also adjudicated paranormal and scientific reality.
- The authors characterize reality as socially and politically sanctioned, noting the shared root behind Romance-language terms for "real" and "royal" and arguing that specialists funded by the state define what counts as real.
- Psychology is presented as the modern successor to religious authority: it explains conflicting personal experience through unconscious drives, inherited instincts, delusion, or pathology, converting formerly cosmic forces such as love into measurable hormones, pheromones, conflicts, and neuroses.
- The authors claim the first *Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders* followed a US Department of Defense mandate after World War II to distinguish genuine mental trauma from soldiers' attempts to escape active duty; state acceptance thereby lent psychology respectability.
- Their paradoxical summary of modern policy is that because no recognized defense exists against paranormal phenomena, institutions declare the phenomena nonexistent.
- Islamic jinn offer a non-Christian category of corporeal, reproductive, free-willed nonhumans made of "smokeless fire"; the authors compare evil jinn and Iblis with both Christian demons and some alien-abduction interpretations.
- They distinguish "alien" from necessarily extraterrestrial and agree that a truly off-planet intelligence might have wholly different senses, bodies, concepts, and empathy, making communication or even mutual recognition extremely difficult.
- A 2014 NASA-sponsored interdisciplinary volume is reported to reach a broad consensus that human–extraterrestrial communication would be nearly impossible, but the authors criticize it for ignoring people who claim communication has already occurred and ancient contact literature.
- Colonization requires some shared needs and communication; the authors say a more likely model for contact is Europeans' treatment of Indigenous Americans as nonhuman or non-Christian, with extermination, enslavement, conversion, and suppression of traditional contact with the Other.
- They reverse demonization as a thought experiment: extraterrestrials observing human slaughter and environmental destruction might regard humans as demons or destructive termites.
- Witchcraft manuals treated witches as human intermediaries behind unexplained physical and social harm. The authors allow that perhaps 80–90 percent of historical cases had ordinary causes but retain a remainder of 10 percent or less as unexplained.
- They compare the Witches' Sabbat with twentieth-century abduction reports through sleep, travel or flight to a remote place, groups of nonhumans, sexual penetration, helplessness, and return to bed; this is a structural comparison, not proof of literal identity.
- The Salem trials are presented as an eventual recognition that human agency did not explain alleged events: authorities stopped the proceedings, while Tituba was released and lived normally despite her stories.
- The alliance of science and state displaced the church by delivering navigation, manufacturing, metallurgy, medicine, chemistry, and weapons, but also placed religion, dreams, and invisible forces in separate boxes.
- Soviet and Maoist communism took state-science alignment to an atheistic extreme, suppressing religious interpretive mechanisms while still facing UAP reports over sensitive sites.
- Soviet censorship meant reported cases could not readily be blamed on officially eradicated superstition. Soviet experts explained over 90 percent as ordinary phenomena, secret aircraft, or rocket launches, leaving 10 percent or less unresolved—ratios the authors say resemble US results.
- Although Soviet authorities avoided the American term UFO in favor of "paranormal phenomena" and publicly minimized Russian sightings and abductions, post-Soviet accounts allegedly describe abductions, close encounters, base overflights, and military attempts at communication.
- Radar traces and coincident technological failures cannot be dismissed as witness culture because, as the authors put it, "machines do not hallucinate."
- Hynek's scale progressively links a machine, its effects, and an entity, but the authors question whether the third category unjustifiably assumes humanoids are operators of the observed craft.
- They end one step short of denying the alien figure altogether: it may be a liminal, anthropomorphic face imposed on an otherwise incomprehensible machine, but the final "Almost" leaves room for a real relation.

## Concepts
- **Cognate experiences** — the authors' description of alien abduction and demonic, angelic, divine, or jinn contact as culturally transformed reports of a possibly singular underlying source.
- **Synchronicity / acausal connecting principle** — Jung's term for meaningful coincidence without Newtonian causation; used here to describe consciousness creating relationships among phenomena.
- **Sekret machine** — a machine or machine-like apparition whose laws, motives, material status, and relation to any occupant are unknown.
- **Anthropomorphism of the craft** — projecting the familiar human operator/vehicle model onto UFOs and assuming a quasi-human pilot because human airplanes and helicopters have one.
- **Interpretation becomes representation** — without trusted authorities or evidence, a witness's attempt to explain an event solidifies into its public image.
- **Speculation becomes revelation** — the companion process by which an unsupported explanatory guess acquires the authority of disclosed truth.
- **AATIP** — the Pentagon's Advanced Aerospace Threat Identification Program, publicly reported in December 2017 as a government effort studying UFOs; its disclosure is treated as ending the viability of complete official denial.
- **Witchcraft as national security** — in the medieval church-state order, contact with the Devil was both religious rebellion and political treason, authorizing state action.
- **Reality as social construct** — the chapter's view that accepted reality is defined by changing scientific, technological, cultural, and state authorities, while personal experience can conflict with it.
- **Psychology as secular authority** — the discipline that inherits religion's task of explaining anomalous experience, but relocates it inside faulty minds rather than spiritual forces.
- **Paranormal-defense paradox** — because modern institutions recognize no defense against demons, witches, or UFO-related effects, they solve the policy problem by declaring those phenomena unreal.
- **Jinn** — Islamic corporeal, reproductive, free-willed beings made from smokeless fire; some convert to Islam and some, including Iblis in the chapter's account, are evil.
- **Extraterrestrial hypothesis (ETH)** — the proposal that aliens are biological beings from another planet; the chapter stresses the radical sensory, bodily, conceptual, and empathetic difference such beings might have.
- **Witches' Sabbat** — an alleged nocturnal encounter involving sleep, supernatural transport, remote gathering, demons, coercive sex, and return home; used as a premodern structural parallel to abduction.
- **Censorship by sarcasm** — Western skeptics' use of ridicule to marginalize reports without formal state press censorship.
- **Rejected knowledge** — the broader paranormal category within which Soviet language located UFOs instead of using the American acronym.
- **Close encounter of the first kind** — Hynek's sighting of a UFO at close range, defined here as up to 500 feet, focused on the machine.
- **Close encounter of the second kind** — a craft's electrical, chemical, physical, or other trace effects, again focused on the machine.
- **Close encounter of the third kind** — observation of a robot, humanoid, or other entity associated with the UFO; the authors question whether the category improperly fuses entity and craft.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Arthur Machen's *The Great God Pan*** — the epigraph's speaker opens "the house of life" and releases poorly understood energies, warning that inquiry can admit unknown forces as well as reveal them.
- **Benign 1970s screen aliens** — *E.T.*, *Close Encounters of the Third Kind*, and the first *Star Wars* installment offered nonthreatening or only mildly manipulative aliens; French astrophysicist and Ufologist Jacques Vallée consulted on *Close Encounters*.
- **Christian demon films** — *The Exorcist*, based on William Peter Blatty's bestseller and advertised as based on a true story, and *The Omen* portrayed possessed or diabolical children. The Roman Catholic *Rituale Romanum* supplied the exorcistic authority regardless of a victim's religion.
- **Pazuzu and lamaštu** — *The Exorcist* identifies its demon with the Babylonian Pazuzu, blamed for famine and locust plagues but also used to protect homes from female lamaštu preying on pregnant women and newborns. First-millennium-BCE images reportedly show wings, talons, a scorpion tail, hideous face, and serpent penis.
- **Vietnam and Watergate context** — the authors place alien/demon anxiety amid the end of the Vietnam War, Watergate's beginning, Woodstock idealism turning into Altamont violence, and distrust of Nixon and Kissinger. Images of people clinging to US Embassy helicopters in Saigon, the fall of Saigon, and Cambodia's killing fields contradicted promises of "peace with honor"; Iraq is named as a repetition more than twenty-five years later.
- **Succubus/incubus reproductive cycle** — older accounts said succubi visited sleeping men and stole semen, after which incubi used it to impregnate women and produce hybrids. The authors compare this sequence with modern reports of DNA work, probing, and hybridization by Strieber's "Visitors."
- **Potentially empty craft** — the authors list occupied physical machines, empty UAV-like drones, nonmaterial objects, and holographic projections as alternative possibilities that weaken automatic pilot assumptions.
- **Post-2015 testimony and December 2017 AATIP disclosure** — increasing government/industry witnesses and internet-connected experiencers are said to have made the older denial strategy untenable; no individual testimony is detailed here.
- **Medieval Inquisition** — unexplained events were judged within God-versus-Devil categories; alleged intermediaries were arrested, tried, tortured, executed, and stripped of possessions. The authors use confiscation to expose an economic as well as spiritual and political conflict.
- **Byelokoroviche, October 4, 1982** — at a Soviet intermediate-range ballistic-missile base, a reported incident allegedly compromised a missile installation severely enough to risk World War III. The chapter cites it as a national-security contradiction to official disinterest.
- **Minot Air Force Base, late 1960s** — nuclear missiles at a US base reportedly went offline during a UFO overflight. Robert Hastings's August 2012 MUFON article and book *UFOs and Nukes* are cited for both nuclear cases.
- **Galileo and Giordano Bruno** — their trials exemplify the church's authority to determine not only spiritual legitimacy but acceptable science.
- **Marsilio Ficino, Bruno, and Renaissance love** — love had been treated as a natural or magical force enabling influence across time and space, even "spooky action at a distance"; modern psychology reduced it to hormones, pheromones, conflicts, and Freud's Oedipal/Electra complexes.
- **DSM-1 after World War II** — the authors state that a DOD mandate sought criteria for distinguishing shell shock or trauma from soldiers feigning disorders to avoid duty, helping create the first DSM and giving psychology state legitimacy.
- **Islamic jinn** — still believed in among Arab populations in the Middle East and North Africa, jinn are described as bodily, reproductive, free-willed, fire-composed beings living much like humans. Some convert to Islam; evil jinn include Satan/Shaytan or Iblis, linking Islamic and Christian categories and some Evangelical claims that aliens are demons.
- **Stephen Hawking's warning, April 2010** — in Discovery Channel's *Into the Universe with Stephen Hawking*, he warned against contact or commerce with extraterrestrials because their intentions and relation to humans are unknowable.
- **NASA's 2014 *Archaeology, Anthropology, and Interstellar Communication*** — edited by Douglas A. Vakoch, SETI Institute director of interstellar message composition and California Institute of Integral Studies clinical-psychology professor, the NASA-sponsored volume assembled sixteen contributors across SETI, Greek and Maya archaeology, ethnology, evolution, and culture. The chapter reports a consensus that radically different evolution would make communication nearly impossible.
- **Dutch colonization of Java** — used as the communicative model: despite not initially speaking Indonesian languages, Dutch colonists encountered humans with recognizable needs and desires, enabling language learning and colonization.
- **European colonization of the Americas** — English and other commentators allegedly treated Native Americans as nonhuman or demonic because the Bible did not mention them, helping rationalize genocide. Spanish conquistadors treated Indigenous peoples as human but non-Christian and imposed conversion, new names, and priestly control of contact with the Other under threat of death. Reversing the judgment, the authors ask whether an outside observer seeing human violence would view the planet as populated by demons, like three-dimensional figures from a Hieronymus Bosch painting, or as destructive termites.
- **NASA study's omitted demographic** — the authors say its anthropologists ignored contemporary people claiming repeated alien communication and thousands of years of contact literature; they call for scientific comparison of message content and transmission methods without assuming primitive delusion.
- **The *Malleus Maleficarum*, circa 1484** — Dominican Inquisitors Heinrich Kramer and James Sprenger produced the "Hammer of the Witches" for Catholic and Protestant use. Its framework attributed dead or mutilated cattle, unexplained disease, succubi/incubi, and Sabbats to human witches acting between society and spiritual powers.
- **The unexplained remainder** — the authors estimate that perhaps 80–90 percent of witchcraft accusations could now be explained by ordinary causes, leaving 10 percent or less without rational explanation; they do not provide a dataset for the percentages.
- **Witches' Sabbat pattern** — an often-female sleeper awakens far away among humans and demons in a remote place, sometimes after aerial flight; the Devil may demand sex, penetration is described as icy, obedience may require kissing his buttocks, and the person flies home or wakes in bed. The alleged rewards are minor harms such as poisoned cattle, spoiled milk, stillbirth, or infant death despite risking torture and execution.
- **Salem trials, 1692** — dozens, mostly women, were accused and nineteen executed before authorities stopped proceedings. Tituba, the West Indian enslaved woman blamed for initiating the hysteria and associated with spells, aerial Sabbats, and a Man in Black, was released and reportedly lived normally; Elaine Breslaw (1996) and Chadwick Hansen (1992) are cited.
- **Modern cattle mutilation and abduction comparison** — current mutilations are said to suggest nonhuman involvement, according to evidence and unnamed informants; abductions resemble Sabbats through flight and penetration by a cold instrument. The political reversal is that modern humans are victims rather than accused agents.
- **Science-state bargain** — secular rulers gained weapons, navigation, manufacturing, metallurgy, medicine, and chemistry from science; observable evidence displaced invisible spiritual causes and weakened church authority.
- **Darwin, archaeology, and Eastern spirituality** — Darwin challenged Biblical creation, archaeology in Egypt and the Middle East challenged Biblical chronology, and Helena Blavatsky's Central Asian/Indian work plus Alexandra David-Neel's Tibetan journey exemplified European interest in non-Christian spiritual alternatives.
- **Soviet Union and Maoist China** — Communist Party membership required atheism, churches were seized for offices or museums, clergy were killed, sent to labor, or used by the state, and technology outranked religion. The authors argue this removed an interpretive mechanism for anomalous phenomena.
- **US/USSR explained-to-unexplained ratio** — both systems allegedly explained more than 90 percent of reports through weather, balloons, astronomy, aircraft, secret tests, or launches and retained 10 percent or less as unidentified, while rejecting extraterrestrial conclusions.
- **Soviet terminology and reported military communication methods** — authorities preferred "paranormal phenomena," publicly suggested aliens did not abduct Russians, and treated Ufology as Western decadence; later reports allegedly included abductions and predictable military-base appearances that led the military to develop possible communication methods.
- **Hynek's three close encounters** — first kind: machine within 500 feet; second: electrical, chemical, or physical effects; third: robot or humanoid. The authors argue the third inserts conscious entity control into a scale otherwise based on craft and traces.

- "The power of Christ compels you!" — the popular Catholic formula marking Christian authority over demons.

- "Almost." — the ending that qualifies a complete reduction of entities to projection.

## Thread
Chapter 2 expands Chapter 1's distinction between event and interpretation into a history of authority. It argues that the same anomalous core can become angel, demon, jinn, witchcraft, pathology, extraterrestrial, or machine depending on which institution defines reality. The chapter then uses nuclear cases, Soviet reports, AATIP, and instrumental traces to prevent that cultural thesis from collapsing into "nothing happened," while using drones, holograms, and Hynek's categories to prevent physical evidence from becoming automatic proof of humanoid pilots. This prepares the next chapter's intermediate possibility: the apparent alien may be neither ordinary biological pilot nor pure projection, but an artificial being entangled with human ideas of angels, golems, implants, and control.

## Connections
- **Explicit trilogy link:** The authors say this chapter repeats the project-wide claim developed in *Sekret Machines: Gods*: gods, angels, demons, jinn, and Visitors may be cultural representations of a common source.
- **Explicit connection to Chapter 1:** Succubi/incubi and the Witches' Sabbat are offered as older structural counterparts to the sexual and invasive experiences described by abductees.
- **Explicit forward link:** The final question about anthropomorphized entities leads directly into *Of Golems and Grays*, where angels, Grays, golems, implants, and artificial beings are compared.
- **Reader-side speculative connection:** The social construction of reality and institutional control of anomalous knowledge can be compared thematically with works on paradigms or simulation, but the chapter makes no explicit link to another book in this knowledge base.
