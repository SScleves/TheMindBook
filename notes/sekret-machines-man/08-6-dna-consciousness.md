# DNA Consciousness

## In one paragraph
DeLonge and Levenda ask whether consciousness is encoded in DNA, produced by the brain, received from elsewhere, or formed by some combination, beginning from the conflict between hereditary turnover and the human desire for immortality. They use the reported consistency, telepathy, morphology, and reproductive focus of alien-abduction accounts to challenge assumptions that the Visitors are organic space travelers with humanlike senses, motives, or consciousness, while preserving those accounts as reports rather than confirmed anatomy. The chapter's published scientific center is Francis Crick and Christof Koch's 1990 neurobiological program: visual processing, neural correlates, memory, sensory integration or "binding," awareness, and attention are proposed as tractable parts of consciousness, though the authors criticize their materialism and their exclusion of self-consciousness, intention, hypnotic states, lucid dreams, and sleepwalking. Eyewitness discrepancies, Kenneth Arnold's 1947 sighting, ELIZA, amnesic patient H.M., sensory disability, and machine-memory comparisons let the authors contrast unfamiliar perception with programmed intelligence and argue that a conscious machine would require personal development, emotion, subconscious conflict, and dreams, not only calculation and language. Finally, they present John Grandy's published neurogenetic correlates and "DNA consciousness" proposal, including claimed quantum effects at nucleotide scale and links between genetic deterioration and Alzheimer's disease, while using a radio-receiver analogy to keep open the authors' own speculation that brain damage may impair access to consciousness without destroying consciousness itself.

## Core claims
- The chapter restates earlier premises rather than independently re-proving them: ancient divination systems may anticipate genetic structure, Crick supported directed panspermia, and two Russian mathematicians found design-like patterns in the code.
- DNA carries no known individual immortality program in the authors' account; generational death permits new combinations and capacities, much as obsolete machines are replaced while their design lineage continues.
- Human desire for indefinite life conflicts with this genetic turnover. The authors ask, but do not establish, whether the desire itself signals an immortal component.
- Chinese alchemy, Indian yoga, European *elixir vitae*, longevity culture, and cryogenics are presented as recurring human resistance to mortality.
- Two main possibilities organize the chapter: consciousness is built into DNA, or it enters from outside the biological system. The brain might generate signals, receive them, or do both.
- Alien-abduction reports are treated as a test case because experiencers commonly describe telepathic words or images received internally while they lack bodily control.
- The authors infer reality from consistency among accounts across places, times, and decades and reject sleep paralysis or hallucination as complete explanations. This is their interpretation of testimony, not an experimental finding.
- Reported abductors are nonhuman, associated with UFOs/UAPs, telepathic, and often focused on reproduction or genetics; these are recurring report features, not verified facts about a species.
- Their apparent lack of ears, nostrils, functional mouths, and visible feet, together with a roughly human four-limb form, is used to ask whether they share ancestry, manipulate perception, or are machines.
- Greater regional variation outside the United States could reflect experiencer projection, sensory manipulation, or deliberate psychological warfare. The chapter marks the agenda and "divide and conquer" reading as speculation.
- The authors identify four unsupported assumptions behind ridicule of reproductive-abduction claims: long-distance space travel, humanlike reproduction, organic bodies, and humanly intelligible goals.
- Understanding human consciousness and scientifically studying telepathy or paranormal ability are therefore prerequisites to comparing humans with alleged Visitors.
- Crick and Koch's published neurobiological project seeks neural correlates of consciousness in physical brain processes, beginning with the visual system because it offers a bounded experimental field.
- A black box accepts information and produces results while its internal transformation is unknown; consciousness remains opaque in a way engineered devices are not opaque to their technicians.
- The brain integrates many discrete visual and other sensory inputs into a rapidly changing coherent scene, while most underlying processing remains outside awareness.
- Yogic breath control shows that at least one normally automatic system can enter conscious control; the authors present it as an ancient practical observation, not proof that all autonomic processes can be mastered.
- Crick and Koch's statement that consciousness probably correlates with nervous-system complexity leads the authors to question whether apparently sense-poor Grays have less consciousness or a different consciousness not based on five human senses.
- Language is not necessary for consciousness in Crick and Koch's view because higher mammals may possess essential conscious features. The chapter distinguishes this from the inverse question of whether language performance proves consciousness.
- Descartes treated animal and machine behavior as organ-driven rather than minded and used a clock's superior timekeeping as an example of skill without mind. The authors apply his reasoning to apparently programmed Visitors.
- Crick and Koch bracket self-consciousness, volition, intentionality, hypnosis, lucid dreaming, and sleepwalking. DeLonge and Levenda object that these are among the very phenomena consciousness research must explain.
- Abduction literature's reported lack of individual facial, clothing, speech, and gesture differences among Visitors is interpreted as minimal self-awareness or machine-like group identity. This is an inference from the cited reports.
- ELIZA demonstrates that appropriate language-like responses can be simulated without establishing consciousness; the authors ask whether alien image communication could be a more advanced programmed analogue.
- Large reported eyes, image exchange, and limited reciprocal conversation are used to speculate that the Visitors privilege vision or merely execute a communication program.
- Visual consciousness selects and values elements in an immense scene; conscious reading is sequential even when a whole shelf enters the visual field at once.
- Memory registration and conscious access are distinct. Machines can retain all captured data, but a program still determines retrieval.
- Short-term memory appears tightly related to consciousness in Crick and Koch's cited discussion: no conscious person wholly lacking every form of short-term memory had been reported.
- Patient H.M. remained conscious and retained short-term memory after brain surgery for epilepsy but had great difficulty forming long-term memories, demonstrating that long-term memory formation is not identical with consciousness.
- Blind, deaf, or nonspeaking people, including Helen Keller, refute any claim that a particular classical sense or spoken language is required for full consciousness; communication makes their consciousness evident.
- "Binding" joins simultaneous sensory features into one experience. Crick and Koch distinguish genetically/developmentally shaped binding, learned binding, and temporary binding of novel combinations.
- The first binding type is linked to genes and ancestral development; the second to repetitive learning and memory; the third uses attention when no established neural pattern fits a novel object.
- Awareness and attention are "intimately bound" in Crick and Koch's formulation, but the authors add an interpretive component: consciousness tries to map unfamiliar events onto stored categories.
- Eyewitnesses can agree that an event occurred while disagreeing about height, color, or sequence. The authors argue that UFO criticism often treats detail conflicts as proof no event happened because there is no physical "corpse."
- Familiar human beings and aircraft generate more consistent descriptions than unprecedented beings and craft because existing neural and memory categories support recognition.
- Kenneth Arnold's 1947 report is used to show cultural homogenization: his description concerned skipping motion and flying-wing-like forms, but "flying saucer" became a circular visual template in media and later testimony.
- Media can shape how an anomalous event is remembered without necessarily creating the event; the authors say myths from separate witnesses can bind into a homogenized memory.
- Crick and Koch reach no definitive theory, only experimental directions. The authors nevertheless retain their distinctions between awareness and attention and their recognition that inherited neural networks may fail with novel stimuli.
- Crick's later materialism treated consciousness as an emergent biochemical property and rejected quantum mechanisms and machine consciousness, but the chapter says this position lacked a workable, testable account and rested on an undefined term.
- The conscious-machine debate cannot be settled while consciousness itself lacks a generally accepted definition. Calculation, intelligence, mimicry, and communication may not be equivalent to human subjectivity.
- Human memory is not stored in a single locatable cell corresponding to a house or smell, unlike deliberately addressed computer data; the authors use this difference against a simple brain/computer equivalence.
- Human consciousness includes history, development, emotion, inconsistency, attachment, fear, desire, and action against self-interest. A purpose-built machine arrives with specifications and input data rather than gestation and biography.
- The authors argue that a genuinely conscious machine would need a subconscious capable of apparently capricious behavior, stress-triggered recovery, sleep, and dreaming. This is their philosophical requirement, not a demonstrated engineering result.
- Intelligent autonomous machines could nevertheless conduct century-long space missions, land, collect chemical and biological samples, and report home; this supplies a human analogue for alleged alien activity without proving that Visitors are probes.
- John Grandy's neurogenetic correlates of consciousness extend neural correlates downward to genes, gene products, and transcription factors involved in conscious experience.
- Grandy's continuum proposes quantum-scale genomic effects, then DNA consciousness, cellular consciousness, neurogenetic consciousness, and higher human consciousness.
- Genetic activity continues after development and in some cases for days or weeks after death; the chapter uses this claim to argue genes may influence ongoing consciousness.
- Grandy associates neurodegeneration and psychiatric disorders with genetic deterioration or mutation, which the authors regard as support for a genetic basis of consciousness but not definitive proof.
- Grandy's hydrogen-bonding example introduces quantum effects between DNA nucleotides and proteins and leads him to call DNA a possible source of higher degrees of consciousness.
- The authors compare this with a quantum-computer model of the brain, but the published proposal as summarized does not settle whether DNA produces subjective experience.
- Alzheimer's disease separates consciousness, awareness, attention, memory, and their biological medium. Wakefulness, emotion, agitation, and distress may persist while recognition and retrieval fail.
- Identifying genes associated with Alzheimer's is not enough to prove consciousness genetic, because not everyone carrying them develops the disease and unidentified factors remain.
- The radio analogy preserves an external-source hypothesis: a damaged receiver can lose or distort a station without ending the broadcast. This is the authors' metaphor, not Grandy's demonstrated conclusion.
- The chapter ends with an open question: if humans and Visitors do not share compatible DNA, their reported communication may require a deeper common framework than genetics.

## Concepts
- **Genetic imperative** — DNA's persistence through reproduction, variation, and replacement of individual generations rather than preservation of each host.
- **DNA consciousness** — John Grandy's term, as summarized, for nucleotide/protein interactions that may give rise to cellular and higher consciousness.
- **Brain as generator or receiver** — competing models in which neural tissue creates conscious signals, receives consciousness from elsewhere, or performs both roles.
- **Alien telepathy** — reported receipt of words or images in the head without audible speech; the chapter treats it as a consistent testimony feature requiring study.
- **Gray alien** — the commonly reported US form with large head and eyes, tapering fingers, limited facial anatomy, and a roughly humanoid body; all anatomy remains witness description.
- **Psychological warfare hypothesis** — speculation that varied regional forms prevent a coherent multinational threat assessment and could be used to divide human responses.
- **Neural correlates of consciousness (NCC)** — Crick and Koch's search for physical brain activity associated with conscious experience.
- **Neurobiological theory** — a material research program that decomposes brain systems and begins with experimentally manageable sensory processing.
- **Black box** — a system whose inputs and outputs are observable while its internal operation is unknown; consciousness is the chapter's primary example.
- **Visual consciousness** — selection, organization, valuation, and rapid replacement of visual information as a proposed route into conscious mechanisms.
- **Pranayama** — yogic breath regulation used to extend conscious control into an ordinarily automatic bodily process.
- **Nervous-system complexity** — Crick and Koch's proposed rough correlation between richer sensory discrimination and greater consciousness.
- **Language and consciousness** — language is not required for key conscious features, and language-like output by itself may not demonstrate consciousness.
- **Self-consciousness** — consciousness referring to an individual self distinct from a group; explicitly set aside by Crick and Koch and central to the authors' machine/Visitor comparison.
- **ELIZA / chatterbot** — a 1960s program that simulated relevant conversation, demonstrating communicative appearance without proving awareness.
- **Binding** — integration of color, shape, sound, speech, sensation, and context into a coherent experience.
- **Genetic/developmental binding** — inherited or developmentally evolved connections that organize familiar sensory relationships.
- **Learned binding** — neural association built through repetition, such as connecting alphabet shapes with sounds and concepts.
- **Novel-feature binding** — temporary attention to an unprecedented combination whose neurons are not already strongly connected.
- **Serial attentional mechanism** — Crick and Koch's mechanism for recording a novel event when its details cannot yet form a stable intelligible memory.
- **Awareness and attention** — related but distinct conditions; an event can enter awareness without effective focus or established interpretation.
- **Perceptual projection** — applying familiar memory and media categories to a stimulus for which no adequate prior neural pattern exists.
- **Homogenized memory** — individual anomalous recollections becoming aligned with shared cultural imagery and other people's reports.
- **Emergent property** — Crick and Koch's view that consciousness arises from sufficiently complex biochemical brain activity.
- **Conscious machine** — a machine with subjective awareness rather than merely intelligent behavior; the chapter argues that the category remains undefined and untested.
- **Subconscious and dream requirement** — the authors' claim that humanlike machine consciousness would need illogical, developmental, emotionally recoverable inner processes and the capacity to dream.
- **Neurogenetic correlates of consciousness (NgCC)** — Grandy's study of genes and gene products affecting conscious experience beneath the neural level.
- **Continuum of neuron-based consciousness** — Grandy's proposed hierarchy from genomic and cellular processes through neurons to human consciousness.
- **Quantum DNA effect** — proposed small-scale effects involving nucleotide hydrogen bonds and DNA-protein interaction, used by Grandy to connect genetics with consciousness.
- **Alzheimer's disease as consciousness case** — neurodegeneration that damages memory, recognition, orientation, and independence while leaving wakefulness, emotion, and some awareness, complicating definitions.
- **Radio-receiver model** — brain damage compared with a broken tuning mechanism: reception fails even if the source continues elsewhere.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Loren Eiseley epigraph** — *The Night Country* asks whether genius comes from genes, unknown brain chemistry, or capacities locked in ordinary people's subconscious minds. It supplies the chapter's three candidate levels.
- **Obsolete-computer analogy** — Model-T Fords, Radio Shack TRS-80 computers, and Commodore 64s are replaced rather than made immortal, but their technological "DNA" persists in later machines. The analogy explains genetic continuity without individual continuity.
- **Immortality practices** — Chinese alchemy, Indian yoga, European savants' *elixir vitae*, modern longevity work, and cryogenics are listed as attempts to resist the selfish gene's generational program.
- **Abduction-state reports** — people are reportedly removed from homes or moving vehicles, lose control, and receive words or images directly in their heads. Similarity across locations and decades is the authors' reason for treating the reports seriously.
- **Reported Visitor anatomy** — no discernible ears, little or no nostril, slit mouth and sometimes nasal slits, unclear feet, four limbs, torso, and head. The authors say this could indicate shared genetic/environmental heritage while emphasizing insufficient similarity for common humanity.
- **Regional forms** — US accounts often feature Grays with large heads, black eyes, and tapering fingers; Europe, Africa, and Asia show greater variety although Grays also appear. Telepathy and nonhuman appearance are said to persist across regions.
- **Four-assumption audit** — critics' sexual/reproductive objection assumes cosmic travel, familiar reproduction, organic bodies, and humanly legible motives. The authors say none follows from the abductees' reports.
- **Crick and Koch, 1990** — "Towards a neurobiological theory of consciousness" appeared in *Seminars in the Neurosciences*, volume 2, pp. 263–275. The chapter cites p. 263 for unconscious processing, p. 264 for nervous-system complexity, higher mammals, language, and bracketed states, p. 269 for the three binding forms and awareness/attention, and p. 270 for the short-term-memory observation.
- **Brain-component program** — hippocampus, thalamus, and neocortex are examples of components Crick hoped to isolate and study after treating the brain as a machine.
- **Everyday black boxes** — computers, tablets, and smartphones are opaque to ordinary users but transparent to engineers; no comparable technician can yet explain consciousness.
- **Visual-scene problem** — a leafy tree and crowded street contain hundreds or thousands of images; shifting from sky outside a window to a dinner table indoors requires selection, construction, and immediate replacement of a complete scene.
- **Pranayama and autonomic control** — yoga recognizes peristalsis, respiration, and other systems below awareness. Breath is the accessible case: even a child can hold it, while breathing practice is described as a path toward influencing heart rate.
- **Multisensory binding example** — a face, spoken words, heat or cold, and cookies' smell arrive simultaneously and become one coherent moment rather than disconnected streams.
- **Gray-consciousness inference** — absent ears, apparently unused mouth, and vestigial nose imply limited classical senses. Under Crick and Koch's complexity claim, this could mean reduced consciousness or a different consciousness.
- **Higher mammals** — Crick and Koch say higher mammals may possess essential conscious features without human language, supporting language-independent consciousness.
- **Descartes's machine and clock** — Part 6 of *The Discourse on Method* argues that animal passions can be mechanically imitated, specialized animal skill does not prove mind, and a wheel-and-spring clock measures time better than humans without consciousness.
- **Excluded states** — Crick and Koch set aside self-reference, volition, intentionality, hypnosis, lucid dreams, and sleepwalking unless experimentally useful. The authors identify this exclusion as a major limitation.
- **Abductor individuality reports** — accounts associated with David Jacobs, Mack, Bryan, and others are said to lack distinguishing faces, clothes, speech, and gestures, suggesting to the authors a minimal individual self or machine uniformity.
- **ELIZA, 1960s** — one of the earliest chatterbots returned apparently appropriate answers to typed questions. Its once-cutting-edge but later-clunky performance illustrates the gap between simulated communication and consciousness.
- **Image-based ELIZA thought experiment** — large alien eyes and reported mental pictures could indicate a refined visual communication program. Limited reciprocal exchange suggests preprogramming to the authors, though they also leave open a genuinely nonverbal consciousness.
- **Bookshelf attention** — a person sees a row and may distinguish hardcover from paperback immediately but must read spine titles sequentially. Footnote 54 allows that eidetic memory might register all titles while still requiring later access through memory.
- **Machine visual storage** — a machine can record every detail but needs a program to choose which stored data to retrieve; total capture therefore does not by itself solve attention or consciousness.
- **Short-term-memory proposition** — Crick and Koch report no known conscious person who had lost all forms of short-term memory, implying a close connection without proving identity.
- **Patient H.M.** — surgery removed brain sections to relieve epilepsy. He remained fully conscious and could form short-term memories but had severe trouble making long-term ones. Footnote 56 cites Suzanne Corkin, *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, volume 3, February 2002, pp. 153–159.
- **Blindness, deafness, and Helen Keller** — people born without sight, unable to hear, or unable to speak remain fully conscious and communicate; Keller is offered as an especially strong example against tying consciousness to any one sense.
- **First binding type** — genes and developmental processes inherited from distant ancestors create established sensory combinations; this is Crick and Koch's first explicit genetic component of consciousness in the chapter's reading.
- **Second binding type** — learning links alphabet shapes to sounds and then letter combinations to nouns, verbs, and concepts, using memory and repetition.
- **Third binding type** — an unprecedented feature combination lacks strongly connected neurons. A serial attentional mechanism records that something occurred even when detailed binding is too weak for intelligible familiar memory.
- **Ten eyewitnesses** — the law-enforcement analogy has ten observers produce ten contradictory descriptions of a robbery or murder while agreeing it occurred. A corpse anchors the event; UFO testimony lacks that physical anchor.
- **Familiar aircraft landing** — if human-looking occupants emerged from a recognizable aircraft, existing categories would make independent descriptions converge more closely than reports of unknown forms.
- **Kenneth Arnold, 1947** — in the Cascades he drew forms more like flying-wing aircraft developed during the prior decade and compared their motion with saucers skipping on water. "Flying saucer" was then converted into a circular object and blamed for later witness imagery.
- **Media-projection account** — tabloids, pulp magazines, and Hollywood provide categories that may shape a report; less-prepared witnesses may describe other forms and behavior. The authors argue shaping does not prove the initiating event unreal.
- **Superman identification sequence** — "It's a bird! It's a plane! It's Superman!" illustrates progressive remapping of an impossible unaided flying human from familiar bird to familiar aircraft to an unprecedented category.
- **Crick's 1994 book** — *The Astonishing Hypothesis: The Scientific Search for the Soul* (Scribner, New York) urged scientific focus on consciousness while maintaining a classical neurobiological and materialist approach.
- **House and steamed-rice memories** — no single identified brain cell stores the image of a house or smell of steamed rice, whereas computer memory has known storage addresses. The authors use these examples against literal equivalence.
- **Human developmental history** — fetus-to-adult growth, accumulated experience, thought, emotion, memory, and culture make each consciousness unique; machines are contrasted as purpose-built artifacts delivered with capabilities already specified.
- **Emotion inventory** — humans lust, love, gestate, hunger, thirst, fear death, bond with children, fear each other, and act illogically. Ordinary machines do none of these and have no will or awareness of demise.
- **Dreaming-machine criterion** — an AI would need a subconscious algorithm permitting caprice and storing random experience for recovery under stress or during sleep. The authors compress this as the requirement that a conscious machine dream.
- **Hundred-year space probe** — autonomous machines could replace humans unable to survive a century or more in suspended animation, fly a starship, land, gather chemical and biological samples, and send results to Earth. The resemblance to alleged alien behavior is analogical.
- **John Grandy's 2011 presentation** — he first presented the neurogenetic ideas at a Swedish conference and subsequently expanded them.
- **Grandy's 2013 paper** — "The Three Neurogenetic Phases of Human Consciousness," *Journal of Conscious Evolution*, issue 9, p. 2, defines NgCC around genes and gene products such as transcription factors and calls the model a continuum of neuron-based consciousness.
- **Genes after development and death** — genes remain differentially active through life, and some are said to remain active days or weeks after death. The chapter infers they may continue affecting consciousness after building neurons.
- **Hydrogen-bond example** — Grandy cites quantum effects in forces between DNA nucleotides. DNA-protein interactions then produce "DNA consciousness," which he says can give rise to cellular and human degrees of consciousness.
- **Alzheimer's neurodegeneration** — genetic deterioration accompanies or causes loss of conscious faculties in Grandy's model. The authors ask whether the damage affects consciousness itself or only the instrument through which it is expressed.
- **Alzheimer's lived symptoms** — a patient may not recognize a child or spouse, believe a dead relative lives, lose names and locations, become agitated or depressed, and gradually lose hygiene, balance, and self-care as the brain shrinks and neural cells die.
- **Awareness/attention reinterpretation** — an Alzheimer's patient is awake and aware of something even when it is not the shared surroundings; binding may have failed, or attention and awareness may have joined a different stimulus set.
- **Distress as evidence** — emotional pain over declining memory and vocabulary implies continuing self-awareness to the authors, precisely the topic Crick and Koch set aside.
- **Genetic-carrier limit** — recent identification of Alzheimer's-associated genes cannot establish a genetic ground for consciousness because some carriers do not develop the disease and other factors remain unknown.
- **Card-hand thought experiment** — an Alzheimer's-associated gene is one card among several possible illnesses; the authors speculate whether an unconscious factor affects which card is "played."
- **Broken-radio analogy** — impaired tuning does not stop a station from broadcasting. Applied to brain disease, it leaves open an external consciousness that a damaged nervous system can no longer receive reliably.

## Key quotes
- "Is DNA the source of this consciousness?" — the chapter's genetic question.
- "Does our brain... create its own signals, or does it receive signals from elsewhere?" — the generator/receiver alternatives.
- "There is no evidence to support that view." — the authors on assumed interstellar travel.
- "...one can have the key features of consciousness without language." — Crick and Koch's stated conclusion.
- "One may as well be dealing with machines." — the authors' reading of uniform abductors.
- "...awareness and attention are intimately bound together." — Crick and Koch on novel experience.
- "In other words, there is no corpse." — why UFO eyewitness disputes are treated differently.
- "A machine just is." — the authors' contrast with human developmental history.
- "...the machine... must be able to dream." — their proposed threshold for humanlike AI consciousness.
- "...give rise to higher degrees of consciousness..." — Grandy's DNA-to-cellular-to-human continuum.
- "If our radio is damaged, does that mean the station has stopped broadcasting?" — the external-source analogy.

## Thread
Chapter 6 begins Section Two by carrying forward the genetic machine's unresolved "ghost." Reported abductors first function as a pressure test: if their communication and bodies are unlike ours, neither speech, appearance, nor technological sophistication can safely stand in for consciousness. Crick and Koch then provide the chapter's strongest published framework, breaking vision, memory, binding, awareness, and attention into researchable neural questions, while the authors use the framework's omissions to reopen selfhood, dreams, interpretation, and machine minds. Grandy moves the physical search beneath neurons to genes and quantum-scale interactions, but Alzheimer's disease shows why genetic correlation still cannot define consciousness. The chapter therefore ends where it began, with DNA as a possible substrate but not a demonstrated source and with communication across incompatible biology pointing toward a deeper level.
