# Introduction to Section Two

## In one paragraph
DeLonge and Levenda introduce consciousness as science's remaining "black box" and frame the section around competing possibilities: it may emerge mechanically from the brain, pervade the universe, belong to animals or machines, or take a form in nonhuman beings that humans do not yet understand. They say consciousness is foundational to study of the Phenomenon because even a distant UFO witness undergoes perceptual effects, while close-contact experiencers may be unable to describe what happened and some abductees studied by John Mack reportedly show post-traumatic stress. The encounter with the Other therefore raises questions about both the Visitors and the human observer. The section will compare the brain's physical structure with hypotheses locating consciousness in neurons, microtubules, DNA, or quantum events at the Planck scale. It will also ask when consciousness evolved on Earth so that human development can serve, cautiously, as a basis for thinking about consciousness on other worlds.

## Core claims
- Consciousness is presented as a major unresolved scientific problem, not as a phenomenon for which the authors claim a settled explanation.
- Theories span reductionist and mechanical accounts, in which consciousness emerges from the brain, and expansive accounts in which it exists throughout the universe.
- Animal, machine, and alien consciousness are posed as open questions whose answers depend partly on what consciousness means.
- The authors will select relevant theories rather than survey every theory exhaustively.
- They attribute to Jacques Vallee and Hal Puthoff the view that consciousness studies are foundational to understanding the Phenomenon.
- Even a simple UFO overflight is said to produce immediate consciousness effects; proximity to Visitors intensifies the perceptual and psychological problem.
- Experiencers may lack ordinary language for events that alter their psyches, and abductees studied by John Mack are said to have shown post-traumatic stress disorder.
- Encountering the Other destabilizes assumptions about both the Visitors' identity and the meaning of being human.
- Investigation must include the physical brain without assuming in advance that consciousness originates there.
- Candidate levels named by the authors are neurons, microtubules, DNA, and quantum effects at the Planck scale of particles and waves.
- Reconstructing the evolution and first terrestrial appearance of consciousness may help formulate, but cannot by itself answer, how it evolved on other planets.
- The task is deliberately circular and difficult: humanity is trying to understand a nonhuman Phenomenon before it understands its own consciousness.

## Concepts
- **Consciousness** — the unresolved capacity at the center of experience, variously treated as a brain product, a universal property, or something located at biological or quantum levels.
- **Black box** — a system central to human experience whose internal mechanism remains unknown despite observable inputs and outputs.
- **Reductionist / mechanical theory** — an account that explains consciousness as an emergent property of physical brain processes.
- **Universal consciousness** — the alternative possibility that consciousness exists throughout the universe rather than being generated only by individual brains.
- **Consciousness effects** — changes in perception and psyche reported from witnessing a UFO or encountering Visitors, before any explanation of cause is established.
- **Experiencer** — a person reporting contact with the Phenomenon whose event may be difficult to translate into everyday terms.
- **Other** — the nonhuman or radically unfamiliar presence that forces reconsideration of what the Visitors and humans are.
- **Emergent property** — a property arising from interaction among brain components rather than residing in any one component; presented here as one candidate model.
- **Microtubule hypothesis** — the possibility, named but not developed in this introduction, that cellular microtubules participate in consciousness.
- **DNA consciousness** — the possibility that the genetic molecule contributes to or produces consciousness, reserved for the following chapter.
- **Planck-scale consciousness** — a proposed connection to quantum particles and waves at the smallest stated material scale; no mechanism or evidence is supplied in this unit.
- **Evolution of consciousness** — inquiry into when awareness first appeared and changed on Earth, used as a possible comparative framework for other planets.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Dean Koontz mosaic epigraph** — *One Door Away from Heaven* (Bantam, New York, 2001, p. 472) compares apparent coincidences with carefully placed mosaic tiles whose full pattern cannot be perceived. It frames the section's effort to assemble partial theories and data.
- **Range of consciousness theories** — "emerging property of the brain" and "everywhere in the universe" define the section's endpoints; animals, machines, and aliens are the test cases named between them.
- **Ordinary UFO overflight** — a person merely watching an object cross a dark summer sky is the lowest-intensity example of the Phenomenon's consciousness effects.
- **Presence of the Visitors** — close-contact experiencers occupy the other end of that range and reportedly struggle to describe the event in ordinary language.
- **John Mack's abductees** — the authors repeat the earlier claim that some people Mack studied suffered PTSD. This is evidence of reported psychological trauma in their argument, not proof of a literal extraterrestrial cause.
- **Visitor questions** — "Who are they? What are they? What do they want?" illustrate how contact with the Other redirects inquiry back toward human identity and cognition.
- **Candidate physical levels** — neurons, microtubules, DNA, and Planck-scale quantum events form the announced research sequence; the introduction reports no experiment choosing among them.
- **Earth-to-other-world comparison** — a timeline for terrestrial consciousness is proposed as a way to imagine other planetary psyches, with the limitation that the section has not yet established such a timeline.

## Key quotes
- "Consciousness is the one great remaining mystery for science." — the section's starting problem.
- "It’s the “black box” at the heart of the human experience..." — consciousness as an unknown mechanism.
- "Are aliens conscious?" — the Phenomenon-specific question.
- "The study of consciousness is foundational..." — the authors' methodological claim.
- "Who are they? What are they? What do they want?" — questions produced by the encounter with the Other.
- "...consciousness arises from the neurons..." — one candidate level among several.
- "...before we even understand ourselves." — the task's central difficulty.

## Thread
This introduction changes the book's scale from the genetic machine to the experience that may inhabit, emerge from, or exceed it. It defines the section's problem without choosing a theory and makes the Phenomenon a comparative test: reported contact affects consciousness, while the uncertain consciousness of the Visitors may reveal the limits of human models. The announced path moves from biological substrates through evolution and quantum-scale proposals toward the question of nonhuman minds.
