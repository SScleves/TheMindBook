# I, Cyborg

## In one paragraph
DeLonge and Levenda recover “cyborg” from its original space-program setting: not a robot, but a human whose artificial components, drugs, and autonomic controls form an integrated cybernetic organism capable of surviving beyond Earth. J. C. R. Licklider’s 1960 man-computer symbiosis and intelligence amplification preserve human initiative, while Manfred Clynes and Nathan Kline’s work proposes a machine-derived unconscious that regulates respiration, blood pressure, medicine, stress, and other homeostatic functions without occupying astronaut attention. Their studies also reach into hypnosis, dissociation, vestibular response, sensory invariance, hallucination, pleasure, and psychosis, producing a mixed result: hypnosis could simulate nystagmoid sensations in a stationary subject but could not erase actual vestibular reactions during movement. The chapter places this research within ARPA, NASA, Cold War mind-control interest, Rockland State Hospital, pharmacology, and Clynes’s work on music, brain signals, nonverbal communication, and the eye lens as an existing “implant.” Only at the end do the authors cross from documented human programs to alien speculation, asking whether scientists’ pursuit of implants, voiceless communication, and telekinesis arose independently of contemporaneous stories about telepathic spacecraft occupants.

## Core claims
- J. C. R. Licklider’s 1960 description of man-machine systems marks a reversal: machines had been extensions of human arm and eye, but automation increasingly replaced people and left the remaining humans helping machines rather than being helped.
- Licklider, working for the Pentagon, developed an early networking concept called the “Intergalactic Computer Network.” It led toward ARPANET and eventually the Internet.
- His April 23, 1963 memorandum addressed “Members and affiliates of the Intergalactic Computer Network” under the Defense Department’s Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA).
- The chapter uses one author’s experience with punched paper tape and keypunch cards at a multinational manufacturing firm in the 1970s to illustrate rapid change from a room-sized computer in 1975 to a far more powerful desktop computer with keyboard and mouse.
- Licklider combined psychology, physics, and mathematics; he moved to MIT in 1950 after Washington University in St. Louis and Harvard, then to ARPA by 1962.
- ARPA’s defense mission required quick decisions about national security and exemplified the value of combining disciplines.
- President Dwight D. Eisenhower created ARPA in 1958 after the Soviet Union launched Sputnik 1, recognizing that a broad scientific program had beaten the United States into space.
- A comparable integrated approach had been proposed after World War II, when General Hap Arnold sent Theodor von Karman to study German rocket work at Peenemünde and Nordhausen and interrogate Nazi scientists and engineers.
- Eisenhower also created NASA in 1958. NASA assumed many ARPA duties, leaving ARPA to pursue high-risk, unconventional basic research without requiring immediate results.
- Licklider helped develop MIT’s Semi-Automatic Ground Environment (SAGE), in which computers gathered data but a human operator chose the response.
- Licklider’s emphasis was intelligence amplification (IA), not artificial intelligence (AI): computers, sensors, and process-control devices should extend human judgment rather than replace it.
- The authors say computer science later moved beyond this limit as researchers recognized that AI could do more than sense and calculate.
- What survived was Licklider’s proposal that humans and computers could enter a desirable symbiosis, perhaps on the assumption that machines would not become conscious and humans would retain authority.
- By the early twenty-first century, deeper integration appeared likely to blur human and machine rather than simply support a human decision maker.
- In the same year as Licklider’s paper, Manfred Clynes and Nathan Kline’s “Cyborgs and Space” proposed that space travel invites humanity to participate consciously in its own biological evolution.
- The authors note that science and spirituality were less sharply separated in 1960; a July 1952 US Air Force press conference about UFO overflights of Washington, D.C., linked UFOs with Biblical events, spiritualism, and mental telepathy.
- Clynes and Kline used respiration to show that mammals, fish, insects, and plants are adapted to specific environments and face apparently insurmountable limits outside them.
- Humans can consciously design mechanisms that defeat such limits, as underwater breathing systems and high-altitude pilot equipment demonstrate.
- This is biological rather than merely computational symbiosis: machinery becomes part of the organism’s adaptation.
- Their key requirement was a self-regulating integrated system that functions without consciousness and cooperates with autonomous homeostatic controls.
- Human breathing and heartbeat ordinarily proceed outside awareness. In space, machines must reproduce environmental support without demanding constant conscious intervention, creating what DeLonge and Levenda call a machine-derived “unconscious.”
- Designing that artificial unconscious may illuminate consciousness and help explain how a nonhuman system could behave autonomously and human-like while remaining intuitively alien.
- Clynes and Kline named the “exogenously extended organizational complex” functioning as an unconscious homeostatic system a “Cyborg.”
- Manfred Clynes coined the word. A cyborg is neither robot nor android, but a cybernetic organism combining an organic human with artificial components.
- An artificial limb can make a person a cyborg under the broad definition. Psychoactive and other drugs also qualify when they enhance performance, extend survival in space, or protect against psychological stress.
- Norbert Wiener coined “cybernetics” in 1948 as “control and communication in the animal and the machine.”
- Cybernetics arose in the same postwar setting as modern UFO reports and, from 1950, government attempts to counter brainwashing by directly manipulating consciousness.
- Psychological warfare and wartime propaganda helped create the premise that minds could be controlled. The authors connect this milieu with postwar advertising and C. D. Jackson’s transition from Eisenhower propaganda specialist to head of Time-Life.
- Some scientists reconceived the brain from human soul to central processor, susceptible to adjustment and perfection; critics saw reduction of people to wet computers or components in a “clockwork orange.”
- Clynes and Kline adopted cybernetic control while insisting that enhancement for space should not erase spiritual or human qualities.
- A 1963 NASA-commissioned United Aircraft report declared that “The Cyborg study is the study of man.” It covered similar ground without naming Clynes or Kline.
- Clynes and Kline’s expanded 1961 paper challenged declarations of “unknowable mystery,” associating such resistance with Epimethean afterthought rather than Promethean foresight.
- Their practical cyborg design included implantable osmotic pumps that deliver biochemically active substances at biological rates.
- A pump could continuously monitor blood pressure and automatically release a correcting compound when values rose above or fell below a norm.
- Yoga and hypnosis were proposed as minimum demonstrations of the upper range of natural physiological and psychological performance.
- The paper describes work on a preparation intended to increase hypnotizability by combining drugs and hypnosis symbiotically.
- Hypnosis might assist space travel, but the authors called for better knowledge of dissociation, generalization of instructions, and abdication of executive control.
- DeLonge and Levenda compare that language with CIA mind- and behavior-control work, especially MKULTRA, and note that dissociative identity disorder, formerly multiple personality disorder, was studied by psychiatric subcontractors.
- Vestibular experiments reportedly began fifteen years before the 1961 paper, in 1945, using a Barany-type swivel chair and nystagmus as a response measure.
- Under hypnosis, investigators induced nystagmoid reactions associated with acceleration and deceleration while the subject remained stationary.
- Even in a trance reaching “negative hallucinations,” they could not eliminate nystagmus during real acceleration or deceleration.
- The result suggested that hypnosis could simulate a physiological experience in its absence but could not suppress the actual vestibular response, at least in that protocol.
- The source does not define “depth of negative hallucinations,” and DeLonge and Levenda explicitly leave the phrase unresolved.
- Clynes and Kline argued that sensory deprivation was less important than sensory or action invariance: the inability to vary one's permissible actions and environment.
- In an incompletely described simulated space-flight study, two subjects developed marked visual hallucinations despite movement, radio contact, and closed-circuit visual contact because the confined environment and its possible actions did not change.
- The report raised erotic and emotional satisfaction, a possible brain pleasure center, and psychosis during long missions without supplying a developed solution for the first two.
- If a psychotic astronaut refused medicine, the paper proposed a release mechanism activatable from Earth or by another crew member. The chapter calls the recommendation ambiguous but regards its coercive implications as apparent.
- The cyborg program's final preference was to adapt the human organism to space through cybernetic homeostatic control rather than recreate as much of Earth as possible inside a vehicle.
- Clynes and Kline also predicted that such existence could enlarge the human spirit, preserving their scientific-spiritual combination.
- Manfred Clynes was both scientist and concert pianist, and his life joined war technology, brain research, music, communication, and human-machine theory.
- Clynes’s later work explored nonverbal brain-to-brain communication without words, gestures, phonemes, or their associated misunderstandings.
- He regarded vision, especially the eye lens, as especially promising for understanding brain and mind.
- The lens is described as already implant-like because it lacks a blood supply, sits separately in fluid, is not consciously controlled, and changes curvature as required for near or far focus without an obvious feedback loop.
- Clynes proposed that tapping the lens-control system to operate something else would be the nearest thing to telekinesis.
- The authors interpret the historical program as a scientifically mediated version of mystical spiritual regeneration: implants, prostheses, nonverbal communication, and telekinesis become engineering objectives.
- The closing alien connection is a chronology and a question, not evidence of transfer: as public stories described telepathic spacecraft occupants, scientists pursued strikingly similar capacities, leaving the authors to ask “Chicken... or egg?”

## Concepts
- **Man-computer symbiosis** — Licklider’s cooperative system in which human judgment and machine speed or sensing complement one another; later work deepens cooperation into biological integration.
- **Mechanical extension** — a machine that enlarges a human arm, eye, calculation, or reach while preserving human initiative and criteria.
- **Automation** — replacement of human labor or decision functions by machinery, reversing the older arrangement so remaining people increasingly support the automated system.
- **Intergalactic Computer Network** — Licklider’s early-1960s Pentagon/ARPA networking concept, an ancestor in the chapter's account of ARPANET and the Internet.
- **Intelligence amplification (IA)** — using computers as tools and sensors to increase human intelligence while retaining a human final decision maker, distinguished from AI replacement.
- **SAGE** — the Semi-Automatic Ground Environment, where computers collected defense data and displayed them to an operator who selected a response.
- **Cyborg / cybernetic organism** — Clynes’s term for an organic human whose exogenous mechanisms or chemicals are integrated with unconscious homeostatic regulation; not a wholly artificial robot or android.
- **Cybernetics** — Norbert Wiener’s framework for control and communication across animals and machines.
- **Biological symbiosis** — machine support that changes the organism’s effective environmental range, as breathing systems let humans enter ocean, upper atmosphere, or space.
- **Homeostasis** — autonomous bodily regulation, such as respiration, heartbeat, and blood pressure, which cyborg mechanisms must support without demanding conscious attention.
- **Machine-derived unconscious** — DeLonge and Levenda’s phrase for artificial systems that imitate or extend unconscious body regulation in an alien environment.
- **Exogenous extension** — an externally introduced device or substance incorporated into an organism’s functional control system.
- **Osmotic pump** — an implanted mechanism dispensing biochemical substances at biologically appropriate rates in response to monitored conditions.
- **Pharmacological-hypnotic symbiosis** — the use of a drug to increase hypnotizability and combine chemical intervention with trance for control or performance.
- **Dissociation / abdication of executive control** — separation of mental functions and surrender of conscious direction, both named as unresolved concerns for hypnotic space-flight techniques.
- **Vestibular function** — balance and spatial-orientation processes linking vision and movement, tested through rotation and nystagmus.
- **Barany chair / nystagmus** — a rotating pilot-training chair and the rapid eye movement induced by acceleration, deceleration, and stopping; together they supplied an objective response for hypnosis experiments.
- **Negative hallucination** — an undefined deep-trance condition named in the source; the chapter explicitly says Clynes and Kline do not clarify it.
- **Sensory invariance / action deprivation** — hallucination-producing monotony caused not simply by low stimulation but by an unchanging environment and restricted range of possible action.
- **Remote medication release** — the proposed ability of Earth control or another crew member to administer medicine when a psychotic astronaut refuses it.
- **Nonverbal communication** — direct exchange between human brains without sounds, words, gestures, syllables, or phonemes, a later focus of Clynes’s work.
- **Lens as implant** — Clynes’s interpretation of the isolated, bloodless, unconsciously controlled eye lens as a naturally present cyborg-like component.
- **Telekinesis through control signals** — repurposing the neural mechanism that changes lens curvature to operate an external object, described as the closest technological analogue to telekinesis.
- **Spiritual regeneration through technology** — the chapter's synthesis of space adaptation, implants, prosthetics, voiceless communication, and expanded human possibility with older mystical goals.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **J. C. R. Licklider, “Man-Computer Symbiosis,” March 1960** — published in *IRE Transactions on Human Factors in Electronics*, the epigraph says human initiative and integration once directed passive mechanical extensions but automation increasingly replaces people and makes the survivors helpers to machines.
- **Intergalactic Computer Network memorandum, April 23, 1963** — Licklider addressed “Members and affiliates” of his proposed network under ARPA. The chapter traces the concept through late-1960s ARPANET to the Internet.
- **One author’s computer experience, 1970s/1975** — at a multinational manufacturing company, the author used punched tape and cards to communicate with a room-sized computer, then within one lifetime wrote on a desktop many times more powerful. The anecdote illustrates scale and interface change.
- **Licklider’s institutional path** — trained across psychology, physics, and mathematics, he moved from Washington University in St. Louis and Harvard to MIT in 1950 and ARPA by 1962.
- **ARPA and NASA, 1958** — Eisenhower created ARPA in response to Sputnik 1 and NASA in the same year. NASA absorbed many space duties; ARPA’s revised role favored unconventional, high-risk basic research tied to national security.
- **Hap Arnold and Theodor von Karman after World War II** — Arnold commissioned von Karman to investigate multidisciplinary German rocket success at Peenemünde and Nordhausen. After interrogating Nazi scientists and engineers in Europe, von Karman proposed a broad integrated program resembling later ARPA.
- **SAGE** — MIT’s defense system let computers aggregate data while a human chose action. Licklider’s work on the operator side produced his IA model.
- **Clynes and Kline, “Cyborgs and Space,” September 1960** — the *Astronautics* paper (pp. 26–27) states that space travel calls humans to participate actively in biological evolution. Its respiration comparison covers mammals, fish, insects, and plants, each adapted to a limited operating field.
- **Underwater and high-altitude breathing** — human-designed apparatus allows ocean diving and high-altitude flight where unaided respiration fails; birds and fish cannot make the reciprocal transition. These are established models for machine-supported adaptation.
- **Washington, D.C. UFO press conference, July 1952** — the US Air Force reportedly associated that year’s UFO overflights with Biblical events, spiritualism, and mental telepathy. Details are deferred to *Sekret Machines: War*.
- **Cyborg definition** — Clynes and Kline called the unconscious integrated homeostatic extension a “Cyborg”; a later expanded version identifies Manfred Clynes as the inventor of the term.
- **Norbert Wiener, *Cybernetics*, 1948** — the MIT Press work published in Cambridge, Massachusetts, defined control and communication across animal and machine, providing the vocabulary Clynes and Kline adapted.
- **Postwar brainwashing and advertising** — from 1950, government agencies and contractors investigated consciousness manipulation in response to brainwashing fears. C. D. Jackson’s move from Eisenhower-era propaganda to leading Time-Life represents a parallel transfer into advertising culture.
- **Robert W. Driscoll, “Engineering Man for Space: The Cyborg Study,” May 15, 1963** — commissioned by NASA’s Biotechnology and Human Research division and submitted by the United Aircraft Corporate Systems Center in Farmingdale, Connecticut (final report NASw-512), it called cyborg study the study of man but did not credit Clynes and Kline.
- **“Drugs, Space, and Cybernetics: Evolution to Cyborgs,” 1961** — Nathan S. Kline and Manfred Clynes published the paper in Columbia University Press’s New York volume *Psychophysiological Aspects of Space Flight*. It criticized declaring poorly understood problems unknowable or ultimate.
- **Prometheus and Epimetheus** — the authors gloss Epimetheus as “afterthought” and Prometheus as “forethought”; “Epimetheans” represent those crying sacrilege before difficult creation problems.
- **Implantable osmotic pump** — the proposed pump monitors a measure such as blood pressure continuously and releases a compound whenever it moves outside the accepted range, integrating chemical control with the body’s rate.
- **Yoga and hypnosis performance limits** — capabilities demonstrated under controlled yoga or hypnosis were proposed as a minimum baseline for defining natural human physiological and psychological potential.
- **Hypnotizability preparation** — Clynes and Kline said they were working with a new drug preparation that might greatly enhance hypnosis, permitting pharmacology and trance to operate symbiotically. The chapter gives no compound, dose, sample size, or outcome.
- **CIA-program comparison** — the chapter situates that proposal during Bluebird/Artichoke/MKULTRA-era interest in mind and behavior control, noting hypnosis, dissociation, DID/MPD, and executive surrender as overlapping subjects. It does not document that the cyborg project itself was an MKULTRA subproject.
- **Vestibular hypnosis experiments, circa 1945** — using a Barany-type rotating chair, investigators could induce nystagmoid acceleration/deceleration reactions while a hypnotized subject was stationary. They could not eliminate nystagmus during actual acceleration or deceleration, even in deep trance involving “negative hallucinations.”
- **Interpretation of the vestibular result** — simulated bodily feeling could be produced without the physical event, but an occurring physiological response resisted hypnotic suppression. “Negative hallucinations” remains undefined in the cited report.
- **Two-subject simulated space-flight study** — two people developed marked visual hallucinations inside a constrained, unchanging vehicle despite being able to move, speak to controllers by radio, and see them through closed-circuit television. With details withheld, the report attributes the effect to action invariance rather than total sensory absence.
- **Erotic and emotional satisfaction** — the report mentions deprivation and a possible brain pleasure center but gives no concrete recommendation, leaving the chapter to wonder whether an osmotic pump and psychoactive chemicals were contemplated.
- **Psychosis and medication** — for a long-flight psychotic break in which the affected person refuses medicine, Clynes and Kline recommend a release device controlled from Earth or by a crewmate. Its operation and safeguards are not described.
- **Allen Ginsberg, “Howl,” 1955–56** — lines about Rockland and “the worms of the senses” introduce the institutional setting shared symbolically with Clynes and Kline’s work.
- **Manfred Clynes biography** — born in Vienna in 1925, he fled Nazi Austria with his family in 1938 and settled in Australia. At fifteen he designed an inertial-guidance system that aided the World War II effort; he later visited Albert Einstein, played piano in Einstein’s home, researched music and brain signals, won scientific awards, and studied music with Pablo Casals.
- **Nathan Kline and Rockland State Hospital, 1956** — Kline met Clynes by chance and hired him as chief research scientist at the Research Center of the sprawling upstate New York mental hospital. Kline developed tranquilizer and antidepressant use for depression and schizophrenia; electroconvulsive therapy and other severe measures were also used there.
- **Rockland and the solitary astronaut** — the chapter speculates, without evidence, that proximity to thousands of psychiatric patients and Ginsberg’s Rockland imagery may have shaped Clynes’s conception of a lone space traveler’s inner world.
- **Nonverbal brain communication** — Clynes later pursued the possibility of direct brain-to-brain exchange without words, sound, gesture, syllables, or phonemes, reducing ambiguity and misunderstanding.
- **Eye-lens control** — following vision-centered consciousness inquiry associated earlier with Descartes, Clynes emphasized the lens: no blood supply, isolated in liquid, unconsciously curved by the mind for distance, and lacking an identified ordinary feedback loop in the chapter's description.
- **Alexis C. Madrigal interview, September 30, 2010** — in *The Atlantic* article “The Man Who First Said ‘Cyborg,’ 50 Years Later,” Clynes says redirecting the lens-control system would be “the nearest thing to telekinesis.”
- **Alien chronology comparison** — stories of landed spacecraft and telepathic occupants spread while scientists engineered implants, prostheses, silent communication, altered consciousness, and telekinetic analogues. The authors pose influence or convergence as an unresolved chicken-and-egg question.

## Thread
Chapter 12 grounds human-machine symbiosis in Cold War institutions and concrete physiological requirements. Licklider’s IA preserves human decision-making, but Clynes and Kline move machinery beneath awareness, where it becomes an artificial homeostatic partner governing an adapted body. Their positive and negative experimental results complicate fantasies of total mind control: hypnosis can generate a false vestibular experience but cannot simply erase a real one, while environmental invariance can generate hallucination despite ordinary channels of contact. The chapter then reconnects these mechanisms to consciousness, spirituality, and direct mental communication through Clynes’s unusual career. Its alien claim is deliberately indirect: human research produced capacities resembling contact reports, but the source does not establish whether the reports inspired the science, the science shaped the reports, or both developed independently.
