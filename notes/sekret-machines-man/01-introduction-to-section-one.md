# Introduction to Section One

## In one paragraph
DeLonge and Levenda open Section One by asking whether the Visitors reported in fantasies, experiences, and radar traces are sentient machines rather than "extraterrestrial biological entities." They use humanity's movement toward lifelike robots and artificial intelligence to argue that the boundary between biological life and manufactured simulation may soon become difficult for humans—and perhaps for the Visitors—to recognize. The authors speculate that the frightening detachment reported by abductees could result from machines inspecting humans as machines, just as people open and repair computers, phones, vehicles, and other manufactured devices without remorse. They point to reported absences—no alien children, visible aging, or ordinary empathy—as questions consistent with created rather than born beings, while acknowledging that present robots are not yet genuinely indistinguishable from humans. Framed by Garet Garrett, Richard Dawkins, and Loren Eiseley, the introduction casts humanity itself as a made or programmed mechanism that may be creating a more invulnerable and heartless successor.

## Core claims
- The authors say contemporary public interest in robotics, AI, and machine consciousness is bringing humanity toward serious questions about coexistence with machines that may turn on or turn away from their creators.
- They pose, rather than establish, the hypothesis that the Phenomenon may involve sentient machines instead of biological extraterrestrials.
- The reported nighttime kidnapping, invasive examination, and instrumentation of abductees could, in the authors' analogy, be machines inspecting what they understand as other machines: human beings.
- Humans feel no remorse when opening or repairing a personal computer, smartphone, television, car, boat, or plane because those are designed and manufactured objects; the authors use this attitude to model the Visitors' reported lack of empathy.
- As humans build faster, smarter, more human-looking machines, the authors predict that distinguishing a machine from a human will become virtually impossible.
- They ask whether a being found on another planet could be confidently identified as organic rather than an artificial creation left by another civilization or produced by that planet itself decades or centuries earlier.
- The authors treat the apparent absence of alien children and visible aging in seventy years of close-encounter and abduction reports as suggestive questions: perhaps Visitors are created in their observed forms rather than born, aging, and dying.
- If the Visitors are manufactured, the authors ask whether organic beings created them or whether other machines did.
- Questions about the Visitors therefore become questions about life and consciousness; the authors argue that insufficient self-understanding has obstructed identification and explanation of the Phenomenon.
- They read the Biblical creation account as already making human beings artificial creatures—made by God in divine "image and likeness"—and compare that act to humans making robots in their own image.
- Contactees and abductees reportedly encounter detached, "machine-like" beings who treat humans as objects rather than showing the "humanity" people naively expect from them; the authors stress that empathy from a nonhuman intelligence is neither assured nor indicated.
- The introduction asks whether this machine-like endpoint is what humanity is itself creating through the widely publicized "Convergence."
- Using Loren Eiseley's formulation, the authors speculate that *Homo sapiens* may be a mechanism in a parasitic cycle that transfers itself into a more invulnerable, heartless form.

## Concepts
- **Survival machines** — Richard Dawkins's epigraphic description of organisms as "robot vehicles" programmed to preserve selfish genes; it supplies a biological version of the chapter's human-as-machine premise.
- **Sentient machines** — manufactured systems possessing or convincingly displaying consciousness; the authors say humanity is approaching the point where their coexistence and possible rejection of us must be addressed seriously.
- **Extraterrestrial biological entities (EBEs)** — the conventional biological interpretation of the Visitors, set against the authors' alternative possibility that they are machines.
- **Visitors as machines** — the section's hypothesis that reported aliens may be artificial, created beings that inspect humans as machinery and do not appear as children, visibly age, or empathize as biological humans do.
- **Simulacrum of life** — an artificial representation so lifelike that the boundary between living being and manufactured imitation becomes uncertain.
- **Life/nonlife boundary** — the increasingly unstable division between organic creatures and advanced machines; the authors ask whether humans and Visitors alike may be unsure where it lies.
- **Artificial consciousness** — the possibility that consciousness is not restricted to biological structures but may characterize sufficiently advanced machines.
- **Image and likeness** — the Biblical description of God's creation of humanity, reinterpreted here as a creator manufacturing an artificial being in its own form, just as humans make humanoid robots.
- **Convergence** — the publicized technological movement toward a machine-like endpoint; the excerpt does not formally define its component technologies, but associates it with humanlike robots, AI, and the transfer of humanity into a less vulnerable form.
- **Machine-like affect** — the emotional detachment and apparent absence of empathy that contactees and abductees report in Visitors treating human subjects as examinable things.
- **Parasitic cycle** — Loren Eiseley's proposed image of humanity as a mechanism whose function may be to transfer itself into a more invulnerable and heartless successor.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Garet Garrett's *Ouroboros: Or the Mechanical Extension of Mankind*** — the opening epigraph offers two alternatives: either the machine has a meaning to life not yet rationally interpreted, or it is itself a mysterious manifestation of life. The authors use the dilemma to erase a simple machine/life opposition.
- **Richard Dawkins's *The Selfish Gene*** — the second epigraph calls humans "survival machines—robot vehicles blindly programmed" to preserve genes. It frames biological humans themselves as mechanisms before the text asks whether Visitors are mechanical.
- **Abduction-procedure analogy** — the authors summarize reported bedroom kidnappings, experiments, and physical intrusion by tools and equipment, then compare them with humans tinkering inside computers, smartphones, televisions, cars, boats, and planes. The comparison is interpretive, not an experiment or proof.
- **Contemporary humanoid robots** — robots are said to have convincingly human appearance, warm synthetic skin, AI-enabled conversation and singing, and smoother motion than stereotypical Hollywood machines. The authors concede that they cannot currently be mistaken for people but predict the distinction will become difficult "in a few years."
- **Alien-planet identification thought experiment** — a human expedition lands on another planet and meets a being; the authors ask whether the explorers could know it was an organic native rather than an artificial being left by another civilization or made locally years or centuries before.
- **Seventy years of reported encounter evidence** — the authors ask why accumulated reports of close encounters and abductions contain no observed alien children and why Visitors do not seem to age. They offer machine manufacture as a possibility, not a demonstrated conclusion.
- **Biblical human manufacture** — the creation of human beings by God "in the image and likeness" of the creator is treated as an ancient precedent for artificial creatures and mirrored in humans' creation of humanoid robots.
- **Loren Eiseley in *The Lost Notebooks*** — in the passage edited by Kenneth Heuer and published by Little, Brown in Boston in 1987 (p. 199), Eiseley says humanity seized the machine and thereby the future, then tried to escape the future's embrace. The authors use it to ask whether technological destiny is necessarily harmful.
- **Competing film taglines** — *Dr. Strangelove* supplies "stop worrying and love the Bomb," while *The X-Files* movie asks audiences to "Fight the Future." The pair stages acceptance versus resistance toward technological fate.
- **Reported contactee/abductee affect** — experiencers are said to find the Visitors terrifying because they display no empathy and treat people as objects to dismantle and study. The authors describe the beings' affect as uniformly detached and machine-like, then connect it to the proposed endpoint of Convergence.
- **Loren Eiseley in *The Invisible Pyramid*** — the 1970 University of Nebraska Press passage (pp. 54–55) imagines *Homo sapiens* as a mechanism in a parasitic cycle transferring a more invulnerable and heartless version of itself. It serves as the section's closing warning.

## Key quotes
- "Either the machine has a meaning to life... or it is itself a manifestation of life and therefore mysterious." — Garet Garrett's framing dilemma.
- "We are survival machines—robot vehicles blindly programmed to preserve the selfish molecules known as genes." — Richard Dawkins's biological mechanism metaphor.
- "...what we have experienced in terms of the Phenomenon may be just that: sentient machines..." — the section's central hypothesis.
- "...machines inspecting what they believe to be other machines: us." — the authors' analogy for reported abduction procedures.
- "...it will become virtually impossible to distinguish a machine from a human being." — the forecast driving the identification problem.
- "Are the Visitors... actually machines?" — the direct question organizing the section.
- "Indeed, have the Visitors?" — the suggestion that nonhuman agents may also have lost the life/nonlife distinction.
- "Thus we are already a kind of machine..." — the authors' reading of Biblical creation.
- "...their affect seems uniformly detached; one might say 'machine-like.'" — the reported emotional quality used to support the hypothesis.
- "Perhaps Homo sapiens, the wise, is himself only a mechanism in a parasitic cycle..." — Eiseley's closing image of technological succession.

## Thread
This short introduction turns the Prologue's announced genetics and brain-computer agenda into Section One's governing reversal: rather than beginning with biological extraterrestrials piloting advanced machines, it asks whether the apparent pilots are themselves machines and whether humans are already gene-driven or divinely manufactured mechanisms. That reversal makes alien abduction relevant to genetics, artificial consciousness, and human technological convergence. It also establishes the section's ethical puzzle: behavior experienced as cruel may be the value-neutral maintenance behavior of a machine toward another object, while humanity's own construction of detached successors may reproduce exactly the beings it fears.

## Connections
- **Explicit connection to the Prologue:** The discussion develops the Prologue's announced focus on AI, robotics, cyborgs, and the brain-computer interface by asking whether UFO operators are versions of technologies humanity is beginning to build.
- **Explicit forward connection:** The machine/genetic framing prepares the following chapters' examination of abductee procedures, Grays, golems, implants, hybrids, and the possible design of the genetic code.
- **Reader-side speculative connection:** Dawkins's organisms-as-vehicles and Eiseley's parasitic-cycle image can be compared thematically with other works on evolutionary or machine consciousness, but the source makes no cross-book reference beyond the named works and films.
