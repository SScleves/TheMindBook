# Utter Chaos

## In one paragraph
DeLonge and Levenda add chaos theory and holonomic psychology to the neurobiological and quantum accounts surveyed in the preceding chapter, defining chaos as patterned nonlinear development governed by initial conditions, feedback, repetition, and self-organization rather than pure randomness. They report Larry Vandervert's use of Karl Pribram's holonomic brain model, in which dendritic microprocessors embed space and time in "holoscapes," and Melzack's phantom-limb work, in which the brain continuously generates a space-time body template even for some people born without a limb. They then move beyond those reports: if partial holographic information can restore a whole experience and quantum processes support consciousness, the authors speculate that an external actor might insert or stimulate a small pattern below awareness until a full encounter experience develops through a chaos-like cascade. Stanislas Dehaene's work on subliminal perception and transcranial magnetic stimulation supplies evidence that unconscious inputs and targeted brain activation can influence later conscious experience, but Dehaene explicitly warns that TMS correlations do not identify consciousness's causal mechanism. The authors nevertheless propose that alien abduction may be the delayed conscious "ghost" of a real externally induced event, use telepathic imagery and parietal-lobe levitation effects as clues, and end with the Gray as a Joker hidden in an ordinary fifty-two-card deck; those contact conclusions are DeLonge and Levenda's speculative synthesis, not findings reported by Vandervert, Pribram, Melzack, Dehaene, or Mack.

## Core claims

### Reported chaos and holonomic theories
- The chapter restates the book's methodological claim that UFO/UAP eyewitness reports and alien-abduction experiences require a multidisciplinary account of consciousness.
- Penrose and Hameroff's Orch OR theory is characterized as primarily physical and neurobiological, despite its use of physics and mathematics. The chapter adds a psychological model that also uses physics and mathematics but seeks a more holistic explanation.
- Chaos theory, as defined here, finds underlying order beneath apparently random or chaotic systems. Its features include feedback loops, self-organization, repetition, and dependence on an initial condition.
- The "butterfly effect" is identified as an overused chaos metaphor: a butterfly's wing movement in China can alter weather in Texas because a small change in a deterministic nonlinear system compounds into a major downstream difference.
- The authors contrast two accounts from the prior chapter: consciousness measured through nervous-system evolution beginning in the Cambrian roughly 500 million years ago, and Orch OR's proposal that consciousness existed all along and may predate the universe.
- During the 1990s, psychologist and neuroscientist Larry R. Vandervert published papers applying chaos theory to mathematics, language, and the mind-body problem.
- Vandervert argued that a complete consciousness study must include Karl H. Pribram's holonomic brain theory.
- In the account quoted here from Vandervert (1995), p. 114, dendrites function as microprocessors that embed space and time in "holoscapes." Those holoscapes create hologram-like perception and cognition, including dreams, within a ground of space-time consciousness inside the skull.
- External visible objects are represented as images in the brain. The chapter describes the outer world as creating a hologram within the skull, upon which humans act when they manipulate external objects.
- Motives, desires, fears, expectations, and dreams are neither visible nor tangible outside the subject, yet dreams present visible and felt events that do not occur in the shared external world.
- Vandervert distinguishes consciousness from mind. Animals are conscious, in his view, but lack appreciable minds because they lack culture; the chapter cites p. 116.
- Under Vandervert's neurological positivism (NP), mind has two forms: brain-and-nervous-system circuitry and culturally shareable mental models.
- Mind began with culture and evolves with humanity. The object world remains, but minds create and share models such as mathematics and music.
- These changing models develop against the same space-time geometry invoked by Orch OR. Humans also alter nervous systems through machinery, meditation, psychoactive chemicals, and other means, producing new models of the world. The quoted formulation of relationships among world, brain, and mind is also cited to Vandervert, p. 116.
- The models represent actual relationships among world, brain, and mind in the holonomic accounts associated with Pribram, David Bohm, and unnamed others.

### Reported phantom-limb evidence and interpretation
- People who lose limbs through accident or disease may still feel the absent limb and sensations in it. More surprisingly for the chapter's argument, some people born without a limb reportedly experience that limb.
- A researcher identified as Melzack proposes that the brain continuously emits an impulse pattern representing a "pure space-time template for the body"; the chapter cites Vandervert, p. 113.
- Vandervert calls the phantom-limb phenomenon a, or possibly the, vital clue to the origin and nature of experienced consciousness, also on p. 113.
- The brain or mind therefore maintains a coherent body model with dimension and movement in space and time, allowing navigation through sensation.
- A hologram's part contains the full hologram. Vandervert's model analogously says the brain creates a hologram of experience and that encountering any part can recall the whole experience.
- Proust's madeleine supplies the memory analogy: one sensory fragment restores a much larger experiential whole.
- Vandervert extends the phantom concept to "phantom seeing and phantom hearing experiences," not only phantom limbs; the chapter cites p. 115.

### Reported Dehaene research and cautions
- Stanislas Dehaene rejects Orch OR because, in his words, it rests on "no solid neurobiology or cognitive science." The source is *Consciousness and the Brain* (Penguin, New York, 2014), p. 263. DeLonge and Levenda dispute that assessment by pointing out that microtubules are essential parts of neurons, but that reply is theirs.
- Dehaene treats consciousness as an emergent property of the evolved brain and assigns no role to quantum mechanics in its production; the chapter cites p. 88.
- His work emphasizes subliminal images as evidence of hidden cognitive function.
- Subliminal inputs can provoke reactions without conscious awareness. The chapter says decades of advertising and media research support the basic effect.
- A subliminal image appears too briefly to cross the conscious threshold but is recorded by the eye and transmitted through the optical pathway to the brain.
- A strip of 16mm film consists of still frames. At twenty-four frames per second, the sequence produces a flickerless impression of smooth motion. A different image or written message inserted for one frame may go unnoticed while still registering in the brain and potentially influencing later action.
- Dehaene treats most processing as unconscious. The conscious mind, associated here with the prefrontal cortex, acts as overseer or watchdog that organizes and prioritizes incoming information from other brain regions.
- Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS) targets particular brain areas with a skull-mounted or surgically placed magnet carrying electrical current.
- Depending on placement, TMS can reportedly induce emotional responses, auditory or visual hallucinations, orgasms, memory retrieval, newly created experiences, and an out-of-body/levitation sensation when part of the parietal lobe is stimulated.
- Dehaene explicitly says these effects alone do not settle the causal mechanism of consciousness. They demonstrate correlation between targeted activation and reported experience, not causation explaining the hard problem; the source is p. 153.
- The chapter quotes Dehaene's further result from p. 153: an initial induced bit of activity is unconscious; conscious experience occurs only if activation spreads to distant parietal and prefrontal regions.

### Reported depth-psychology and Mack material
- Sigmund Freud, Carl G. Jung, and other depth psychologists hold that unconsciously retained childhood trauma can shape adult behavior repeatedly until therapy identifies and neutralizes it.
- The authors compare such trauma to a ghost: an invisible trace demonstrates that something real was once wrong even when no current organic cause is visible.
- John Mack reportedly found post-traumatic stress disorder among self-identified abductees without a known conventional trigger. They were not war veterans, childhood-trauma survivors, or violent-crime victims and otherwise appeared normal and adjusted.
- Their recurring report included nonhuman beings, removal from home or vehicle, missing time, and a mysterious traumatic surgical procedure.
- Mack's finding, as used here, establishes unexplained trauma associated with a class of remembered experience; it does not independently identify extraterrestrials, telepathy, holographic insertion, or quantum entanglement as the cause.

### DeLonge and Levenda's inferences from reported theories
- The modern Western separation of real and imaginary often defines an imaginary event as one experienced by one person but not another. The authors say some cultures do not draw the line so sharply, especially where socialization creates and maintains identity.
- They ask whether reported abduction might occur outside the materialist "real world" yet retain consistent details. They note psychologists' sleep-paralysis and neurobiological explanations but regard witness consistency as requiring further attention.
- Vandervert's culture/mind distinction is applied to Visitors: if an alien is conscious, determining whether it has culture becomes necessary to determine whether it also has "mind." The same question is applied to allegedly conscious AI.
- The authors infer that phantom experience, under holonomic theory, must reflect some real experience occurring somewhere in the mind's development. That is their extension of Pribram and Vandervert, not a cited conclusion that alien abduction has occurred.
- They claim a classical-computer brain would exclude paranormal experience, afterlife, and telepathy because such phenomena do not compute, while a quantum-computer brain would make them possible or probable. The move from quantum computation to paranormal probability is the authors' argument.
- Applying chaos to a quantum event—perhaps one neuron or a series firing—is proposed as a way to explain creativity and imagination through rapidly expanding connections among previously unrelated ideas.
- The authors ask whether phantom limbs, creativity, and imagination occupy a boundary between classical and quantum worlds and whether the brain can reach beyond the skull.
- They distinguish ordinary creativity, which begins internally and may be externalized as art, music, or mathematics, from experiences that seem to enter from outside.
- **Authors' proposed mechanism:** UFO and abduction experiences may arise when an external force switches on the same neural mechanism that produces creative imagery, forcing waking perception to "dream" material it would not otherwise see.
- The authors invoke microtubules, non-locality, entanglement, and wave-function collapse as a physical vocabulary for such intervention, but no experiment in the chapter demonstrates an external alien collapse of a human neural wave function.
- Drugs, anesthesia, and certain EEG-type equipment are said to alter neural activity and brain waves, producing laughter or tears. The authors call these clumsy examples showing that brain activity can be externally manipulated.
- They contrast local manipulation of an individual skull with a nonlocal hypothesis: if consciousness is not contained like wine in a goblet, a remote actor might communicate directly with quantum processes in a brain—"microtubule to microtubule"—without ordinary radio or extremely low frequency (ELF) waves, though those are also called possible.
- **Chaos/contact speculation:** A tiny quantum-level external nudge could grow into a "consciousness hurricane," just as the butterfly metaphor turns a small initial change into large weather effects.
- **Holonomic/contact speculation:** If a tiny hologram fragment restores the whole pattern, an inserted slice could initially remain unconscious and later expand into a complete hallucination-like encounter.
- The authors interpret Dehaene's spreading induced activation in terms Hameroff might use: the moment activation becomes conscious could be compared with wave-function collapse. This is a cross-theory gloss, not Dehaene's stated endorsement of Orch OR.
- They suggest that TMS-like activity could have a source other than laboratory stimulation and remain unconscious until activation passes a threshold and cascades through neural networks.
- Context matters for memory. Proust's madeleine recalls a childhood network because the protagonist had eaten the pastry before; TMS can provoke a response without that ordinary context. The authors ask whether lifelong dormant memories lacking conscious context can still influence action.

### Explicitly speculative contact model
- The authors begin this portion conditionally: **if** the Phenomenon exists, it should leave traces in human psyches, and those traces may be difficult to recognize because dream memory can outlast ordinary memory and extraordinary events can be classified as dreams merely for lacking context.
- They propose treating the full reported abduction complex as one coherent phenomenon: abduction, Gray imagery, procedures, telepathic contact, fear, and missing time, rather than reducing one component in isolation.
- Telepathic image transmission from a Gray to an abductee is described as the gateway clue. It would imply mind-to-mind contact and some similarity between Gray and human brains.
- **Authors' quantum speculation:** If telepathy is entanglement, floating, moving through walls, and other abduction sensations may be the conscious mind's approximation of that event. TMS-induced levitation from parietal stimulation is offered as an analogy, not an alien-contact demonstration.
- They explicitly deny that their proposal reduces abduction to psychological illness or random neural malfunction. Instead, they posit a real event involving external actors with unparalleled ability to manipulate consciousness—an ability humans may soon acquire.
- The Visitors' reported resemblance to invisible genies, sprites, ghosts, or devils is used to demand deeper investigation rather than dismissal as imagination, hysteria, or disorder.
- First abductions are often said not to be remembered immediately. After a second or third event, experiencers recognize recurrence and memories become more conventionally real.
- The authors explain delayed recall through context and neural repetition: an unprecedented event has no existing pattern into which it can imprint, but repeated experiences build a recognizable firing pattern.
- They call first experiences subliminal in the broad sense that circumstances make them initially invisible, imperceptible, or unconscious.
- "Abduction" is retained only because no better term captures powerlessness, stranger control, lack of agency, inability to refuse, and absence of safeguards against repetition.
- The experience differs from ordinary kidnapping: no intruder drags a victim at gunpoint; there is no escape plot, weapon stockpile, bargain with a corrections officer, or negotiation with a terrorist. The chapter classifies the reported circumstances as paranormal.
- The authors ask who the abductors are, why they remain invisible most of the time, and how an invisible cause can change a person so deeply. No answer is demonstrated.
- Their final film and card analogies make the Visitor an anomalous element below ordinary temporal resolution. Slowing the sequence would reveal what normal-speed awareness misses.
- A standard deck contains fifty-two cards. An added Joker can pass unnoticed during fast shuffling but prevents a game of Solitaire from resolving because the system contains an extra element.
- If the Joker replaces one card so the count remains fifty-two, the deck looks numerically correct but one of four suits is incomplete. The authors compare that imbalance with mental illness, confusion, and depression.
- Most people continue playing with the compromised deck; abductees have noticed the Joker—or, in the chapter's final reversal, the Joker has noticed them.
- The Joker model is a metaphor for the authors' interpretation. It is not a diagnosis reported by Mack, a result of Dehaene's experiments, or evidence that a Gray literally exists outside awareness.

## Concepts
- **Chaos theory** — the study, as presented here, of deterministic nonlinear systems whose apparent randomness conceals patterns governed by feedback, self-organization, repetition, and sensitivity to initial conditions.
- **Butterfly effect** — the metaphor in which a tiny wing movement in China eventually changes Texas weather because its influence compounds through a nonlinear system.
- **Initial condition** — a starting state or small deviation upon which a system's later, apparently chaotic features depend.
- **Holonomic brain theory** — Pribram's model of distributed, hologram-like perception and cognition in which dendritic processing embeds space and time.
- **Holoscape** — Vandervert/Pribram's space-time-embedded pattern generated by dendritic microprocessors, composing waking and dream experience within the skull.
- **Holographic perception** — the chapter's claim that external objects are represented through internal images and that a fragment of a stored experiential pattern can restore its whole.
- **Consciousness versus mind** — Vandervert's distinction: animals can be conscious, while mind requires culture and culturally shareable models.
- **Neurological positivism (NP)** — Vandervert's theory in which mind is both biological circuitry and shared cultural modeling.
- **Culturally shareable mental model** — a model such as mathematics or music through which a culture represents relationships among world, brain, and mind.
- **Phantom limb** — awareness or sensation of an absent limb, including reports from some people born without it; used as evidence for an internally generated body model.
- **Pure space-time body template** — Melzack's proposed continuously generated neural pattern representing an intact body in space and time.
- **Holographic memory** — the proposal that any part of an experiential hologram can evoke its full pattern, exemplified by Proust's madeleine.
- **Phantom seeing / phantom hearing** — Vandervert's extension of the phantom model to visual and auditory experience without the ordinarily corresponding external input.
- **Classical versus quantum brain** — the authors' contrast between an algorithmic machine that excludes paranormal events and a quantum processor in which nonlocal experience is considered possible.
- **External consciousness manipulation** — DeLonge and Levenda's proposal that an actor outside the subject can activate creative/perceptual neural mechanisms and induce waking dreamlike experience.
- **Subliminal image** — a visual input presented below the threshold (*liminis* in the chapter's wording) of conscious notice but still processed by the optical and neural system.
- **Conscious threshold** — the duration or activation level beyond which an input enters awareness; the chapter notes debate over its precise location.
- **Unconscious processing** — neural analysis occurring without awareness, with the prefrontal conscious system acting as organizer or overseer.
- **Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS)** — magnetic/electrical targeting of brain regions that can provoke emotion, sensation, hallucination, memory, orgasm, or body-location effects.
- **Correlation versus causation** — Dehaene's warning that inducing a response by stimulating a site does not reveal the causal mechanism by which consciousness itself exists.
- **Depth-psychology ghost** — a symptom or recurring behavior treated as the surviving trace of unremembered trauma rather than a literal apparition.
- **Phenomenon trace** — the authors' conditional expectation that a real UFO/abduction event should leave psychological effects even if the initiating event remains inaccessible.
- **Telepathic gateway** — the authors' use of reported image transfer from Gray to abductee as the key to interpreting the remaining encounter sensations.
- **Contextual memory** — memory's dependence on an existing experiential network; unprecedented events may fail to register as ordinary memories until repetition supplies a pattern.
- **Paranormal abduction** — the authors' category for a total-loss-of-agency encounter unlike conventional kidnapping and without an ordinary physical or social context.
- **Joker in the deck** — the closing metaphor for a hidden Visitor whose presence disrupts an otherwise coherent perceptual and psychological system.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Larry R. Vandervert epigraph and 1995 paper** — "Chaos Theory and the Evolution of Consciousness and Mind: A Thermodynamic–Holographic Resolution to the Mind-Body Problem" appeared in *New Ideas in Psychology* in 1995, volume 13, number 2, pp. 107–127. Its epigraph asks why humans do physics, mathematics, and art and proposes psychology as a likely provider of any complete theory of everything.
- **Butterfly in China/Texas weather** — the standard small-cause/large-effect example illustrates sensitivity, accumulation, and nonlinearity rather than claiming a particular documented butterfly caused a particular storm.
- **Pribram source** — *Brain and Perception: Holonomy and Structure in Figural Processing* was published by Lawrence Erlbaum Associates in Mahwah, New Jersey, in 1991.
- **Outer-world/internal-image model** — a person acts on real objects through neural images of them. The example establishes that ordinary action already joins an external object with an internal representation.
- **Dream contrast** — dreams contain seen and felt events without external occurrence, while motivations, desires, fears, and expectations remain internally real despite lacking visible objects.
- **Cross-cultural real/imaginary distinction** — Western materialism separates private experience from shared reality; societies grounding identity in socialization may draw the boundary differently. No specific culture is named, so no further example is inferred.
- **Alien culture question** — Vandervert's animal/culture distinction is turned into a test for Visitors: consciousness alone would not demonstrate an alien "mind" unless it also forms culture and shared models.
- **Mathematics and music as mind-products** — the external object world remains stable while cultural minds create increasingly complex models; machines, meditation, and psychoactive substances further alter model-making nervous systems.
- **Congenital phantom limb** — a person born with one leg may still intermittently experience the missing second leg. Because no ordinary lost-limb memory exists, Melzack's continuous intact-body template is proposed as the cause.
- **Acquired phantom limb** — accident or illness removes a limb but sensation persists, demonstrating that the internal body representation can outlast the external structure.
- **Proust's madeleine** — tasting the small pastry restores a flood of childhood memories because the fragment belongs to an earlier contextual whole. The chapter later contrasts this with induced experience lacking such context.
- **Phantom sight and hearing extension** — Vandervert suggests that the same model can cover visual and auditory experience, allowing the authors to ask about privately seen abductors.
- **Classical-computer limit** — an algorithmic machine cannot, in the authors' framing, support paranormal experience, afterlife, or telepathy. A quantum computer is proposed as able to do so, though no paranormal computer experiment is cited.
- **Creative concretization** — interior imagination becomes externally sharable only through art, music, or mathematical proof. This normal inside-to-outside sequence is contrasted with alleged contact moving from outside inward.
- **Waking dream proposal** — an outside force activates the imagery mechanism and makes a witness see what ordinary perception would not. It is the authors' hypothesis, without an identified force or controlled trial.
- **Known brain interventions** — drugs, anesthesia, and EEG-type equipment can alter neural activity; equipment is said to make subjects laugh or cry. These show manipulability at a broad level, not quantum alien intervention.
- **Goblet analogy** — local cranial targeting follows if consciousness is contained in the skull like wine. If it is nonlocal, remote quantum access becomes conceivable in the authors' model.
- **Quantum nudge/consciousness hurricane** — a tiny intervention below the neuron level grows through chaos until it dominates conscious experience. The butterfly effect supplies the analogy, not proof.
- **Inserted hologram slice** — because a fragment can reproduce a whole hologram, a small implanted pattern might later expand into a complete hallucination. The chapter poses this as a question.
- ***The Exorcist* rumor, 1973** — an urban legend claimed the film used subliminal images to intensify fear. The chapter says the claim was false but that it increased awareness of subliminal influence during a turbulent US period marked by rumors of intelligence-agency media manipulation.
- **16mm film at twenty-four frames per second** — individual stills appear as continuous flickerless motion. One substituted frame can escape notice yet be neurologically registered and possibly influence behavior.
- **TMS method** — a magnet placed on the skull or surgically on the brain carries current to a chosen location. Placement can trigger emotion, hallucination, orgasm, memory, and body-displacement sensations.
- **Parietal-lobe levitation effect** — stimulation of part of the parietal lobe can create an experience of floating outside the body and looking down at it. The authors later compare this with abduction floating reports.
- **TMS limitation** — retrieving a response resembles opening a computer file: access does not prove the machine knows, integrates, or is conscious of its contents. Induced feelings are described as free-floating and contextless.
- **Spread into awareness** — Dehaene reports that initial induced activity remains unconscious and becomes conscious only after spreading to distant parietal and prefrontal cortex. The authors compare that threshold with Hameroff's collapse language.
- **Childhood-trauma repetition** — an unrecognized past event repeats through adult dysfunction until analysis identifies it. The symptom is a ghost-like trace of a real causal history.
- **Dream-memory ambiguity** — some dreams remain more vivid than actual events, while some anomalous events are classified as dreams because their lack of context makes ordinary reality seem impossible.
- **Mack abductee pattern** — otherwise adjusted people show PTSD without war, childhood abuse, or violent crime and report alien removal, missing time, and surgery. The chapter uses the pattern as a psychological trace, not proof of its proposed mechanism.
- **Whole-experience method** — Gray appearance, surgical procedure, removal, telepathy, fear, and time loss should be tested as a coherent configuration rather than isolated motifs.
- **Telepathic-image hypothesis** — direct imagery suggests enough structural similarity for Gray and human minds to interact. Entanglement is proposed as a mechanism; no image-transfer measurement appears here.
- **TMS/abduction analogy** — laboratory parietal stimulation can produce levitation sensations, so the authors ask whether externally induced contact could produce floating and wall-passing approximations. Similar sensation does not establish identical cause.
- **Repeated-abduction memory** — second or third experiences allegedly make the first recognizable. Neural repetition supplies context and a stable firing pattern absent from the initial event.
- **Ordinary kidnapping contrast** — gunpoint intrusion, escape planning, hidden weapons, corrections-officer bargains, and terrorist negotiation are all possible within ordinary captivity; none fits the reported total agency loss.
- **Slowed film question** — an input invisible at twenty-four-frame speed becomes visible when the sequence is slowed, modeling how a Visitor might exist below normal conscious resolution.
- **Fifty-two-card Joker addition** — a hidden fifty-third card disrupts Solitaire and prevents a win even though the player did not notice it during shuffling.
- **Fifty-two-card replacement** — replacing a standard card with the Joker preserves the total count but breaks one of four suits. The authors associate the resulting imbalance metaphorically with confusion, depression, and mental illness.
- **Abductee/Joker reversal** — abductees are people who have noticed the anomalous card; the final sentence reverses agency and suggests the anomaly noticed them first.

## Thread
Chapter 10 takes the quantum substrate of Chapter 9 and asks how a tiny event could become a large, structured experience. Chaos theory supplies amplification, holonomics supplies distributed reconstruction from fragments, phantom limbs supply a body-model example, subliminal perception supplies below-threshold processing, and TMS supplies proof that targeted activation can generate striking sensations. None of those cited elements identifies an alien actor. DeLonge and Levenda assemble them into a conditional contact model: an external nudge enters below awareness, spreads through quantum and neural systems, reconstructs a coherent encounter, and becomes consciously available only after repetition or activation crosses a threshold. Mack's unexplained trauma gives the proposed event a psychological trace, while the Joker metaphor expresses the model's core claim that an anomalous input can remain unseen yet destabilize the entire system. The chapter's evidentiary boundary is therefore essential: manipulable and unconscious neural processing is reported; quantum alien insertion remains the authors' speculative explanation.
