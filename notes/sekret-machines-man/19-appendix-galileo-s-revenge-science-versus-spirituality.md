# Galileo's Revenge: Science versus Spirituality

## In one paragraph
In this appendix, DeLonge and Levenda argue that the scientific victory over religious authority has hardened into a new orthodoxy that can dismiss UFO and paranormal testimony before examining it, while organized religion remains equally inadequate because the reported phenomena cross denominational boundaries. They trace this reversal from Galileo's punishment for heliocentrism through the cosmic displacement of humanity, modern atheism, Sam Harris's critique of religion, and Carl Sagan's skeptical treatment of UFOs, repeatedly conceding that religion has caused harm and that science possesses unique predictive power while accusing both sides of ideological simplification. Their institutional explanation is that government, military, intelligence, and corporate funding structures shape which evidence civilian science can see, so a deliberate centralized cover-up is not required. A September 2015 military-source conversation and the fragmented history of Psi programs lead into the Cold War origin of modern UFO investigation and the practical impossibility, in the authors' view, of one comprehensive declassification order. The Soviet Byelokoroviche and American Minot missile cases then serve as the appendix's strongest reported anomalies: not accepted at face value as alien intervention, but presented as serious, instrument-linked events that should prevent prior scientific assumptions from ending inquiry.

## Core claims
- The appendix describes the main volume as an investigation of physical and scientific aspects of the Phenomenon and as a response to objections from atheists and some scientists who allegedly dominate mainstream interpretation.
- Governments and militaries are said to possess the economic resources required for large scientific research and development, while corporations survive partly through government grants and contracts.
- The resulting government-military-corporate relationship is identified with Eisenhower's "military-industrial complex." The authors say it is *possible*, not established here, that this complex limits governmental power and monitors information about the Phenomenon.
- Serious US paranormal research is described as having occurred under military and intelligence sponsorship because agencies were interested in weaponization; CIA, FBI, military, paranormal, and UFO involvement therefore belong under the authors' broad category of the Phenomenon.
- *Gods* and *Man* are presented as groundwork for *Sekret Machines: War*, which will examine post-World War II expansion of research, civilizational interpretations, politics, science, and recommendations for an enlightened response.
- The authors want hard science, soft science, humanities, engineering, philosophy, psychology, astronomy, genetics, and art to integrate supposedly irrational experience with rational knowledge rather than enforce a rigid split.
- Their immediate obstacle is framed as an ideological conflict between scientists and experiencers.
- Alien-astronaut theory might appear congenial to atheism because it can translate divine beings, heavenly power, weapons, clouds, miracles, and ancient revelation into scientifically possible contact misunderstood by prescientific people.
- Under that translation, religion becomes a kind of ancient cargo cult rather than pure delusion, preserving an actual event while replacing supernatural God with natural alien.
- The authors say opponents of religion often reject all paranormal claims because admitting them might reopen the door to religion.
- Although atheists have some justification for blaming religion for violence and division, the authors distinguish religion from astrology, alchemy, mediumship, and psychism and note that the latter disciplines are not commonly associated with holy wars.
- UFO witnesses are portrayed as ordinary people, including military observers, pilots, and radar operators, unexpectedly confronting something unexplained and undergoing awe, fear, paranoia, confusion, or other lasting changes in consciousness.
- The appendix characterizes scientific dismissal as heresy-making: witnesses are told that Venus, swamp gas, radar anomalies, night terrors, deception, or hallucination explain what they experienced.
- The authors accuse some skeptics of reversing the scientific method by beginning with nonexistence and invalidating both event and witness before developing hypotheses from observation.
- They concede that their characterization of skeptical readers as self-congratulatory cynics is unfair to a serious issue requiring patient analysis.
- "Scientific inquisition" names an alleged culture in which paranormal researchers, psychics, experiencers, mediums, astrologers, and ancient-alien theorists are treated as heretics against truth.
- Richard Dawkins, Christopher Hitchins, Michel Onfray, and Sam Harris are placed in a secular tradition the authors trace to Galileo's victory over church authority.
- The Roman Inquisition's 1616 judgment against Galileo is presented as religious authority suppressing a heliocentric model supported by Copernicus because it conflicted with Genesis and Biblical descriptions of the Sun.
- Galileo's house arrest, publication ban, and delayed circulation of his writings follow Giordano Bruno's execution sixteen years earlier in the book's account.
- Because heliocentrism prevailed, the Galileo case symbolizes truth and knowledge defeating ignorance and imposed doctrine.
- The authors argue that heliocentrism did more than relocate the center of the planetary system: it displaced Earth, humanity, and the Biblical "image and likeness" from the center of creation.
- Humanity becomes an artifact on an insignificant planet around an insignificant star in one galaxy among a stated 170 billion, producing the appendix's account of modern existential anxiety.
- Hegel, Schopenhauer, Darwin, Nietzsche, Kafka, and Freud are grouped as contributors to a picture of humanity without cosmic purpose beyond survival and genetic continuation.
- Nietzsche's "God is dead" becomes the endpoint of "Galileo's Revenge": Galileo and scientific successors killed God conceptually, and Nietzsche discovered the result.
- Galileo's telescope allowed clergy as well as scientists to inspect the Moon, phases of Venus, and moons of Jupiter, replacing revealed authority with logic and rational observation.
- The appendix portrays modern culture as demanding a choice between science and religion; Sam Harris is said to treat holding both perspectives as equivalent to madness.
- Science's practical products, from clean water and microsurgery to consumer electronics, make that choice culturally automatic, even though the same system produces weapons of mass destruction.
- Religious fundamentalism is interpreted at least partly as a reaction against materialism, scientism, technological supremacy, military power, and the cultures attached to them.
- The authors reject both camps' superiority: religious fanatics claim divine authority, while scientists and atheists claim evidentiary authority.
- Sam Harris's nine-page *Atheist Manifesto* is the appendix's main case study in anti-religious argument. The authors characterize it as angry, reductionist, and too ready to make religious fanaticism the central cause of violence.
- Stalin, Mao, and Lenin are offered as atheist mass killers. The authors report Harris's answer that these men were irreligious to varying degrees but not rational, which they interpret as revealing rationality, not atheism alone, as his ideal.
- The appendix says Harris treats rationality and religion as polar opposites and criticizes several uses of language in his manifesto.
- Harris's observation that people do not identify as non-astrologers or non-alchemists is answered by noting that they also do not identify as non-astronomers or non-chemists.
- Astrology and alchemy may be called pseudosciences when they claim scientific standing, but the authors say they remain disciplines; they also say Judaism is not a Christian heresy, while Christianity could be described as a Jewish heresy.
- The authors distinguish belief from evidence: evolution and gravity do not need belief in the same way unsupported propositions do because scientific evidence supports them.
- They accuse Harris of scapegoating spirituality while omitting science's contribution to modern killing through small arms, poison gas, dirty bombs, and other technologies.
- The appendix does not deny violence done in religion's name, religious complacency toward disaster, or the attractiveness of Harris's interpretation to readers of recent news.
- It argues, however, that Al-Qaeda and the Islamic State cannot be explained solely through the Qur'an because European and American intervention, regional control, and oil also matter.
- Israel-Palestine is connected to World War I, European colonialism, and political manipulation of religious identity; Northern Ireland is interpreted through English hegemony over Ireland rather than missionary conflict alone.
- British presence in India and Malaya is likewise attributed to imperial interests rather than a primary wish to convert Brahmins or Muslims.
- The authors distinguish a tradition's essence from its implementation: atheists attack religious implementation through violence, fanaticism, and subjugation, while religious critics can attack scientific implementation through pollution, nuclear weapons, climate change, and resource depletion.
- Pearl Harbor, Hiroshima and Nagasaki, Hitler's invasions, Roman and Alexandrian expansion, Dutch Indonesia, Japanese expansion in Asia, and the destruction of Indigenous American populations are listed as wars of political or economic expansion rather than primarily religious wars.
- Religion may provide moral justification for conquest, but secular rulers often receive the territory and subjects; focusing only on religion can conceal state and scientific roles in violence.
- A separate conflict between science and the "irrational" may offer a path through the larger science-religion dispute because the paranormal side includes scientists as well as believers.
- Debunker literature has legitimate value when it identifies logical inconsistency, and the authors explicitly say there is nothing wrong with such criticism.
- They reject the stronger premise that reason and logic are the only measures of human experience and argue that analyzing the structure of irrational ideas helps explain their persistent force.
- Science and religion are both said to be weakest around anomalous experience: science because the authors insist events occur despite lacking accepted support, and religion because those events do not stay within denominations.
- Carl Sagan's *The Demon-Haunted World* is criticized for dismissing alien visitation through broad polemic, social/educational condescension toward witnesses, anomalous-propagation explanations for radar, and extrapolation from two crop-circle hoaxers to the wider subject.
- The authors see Sagan's critique as structurally parallel to elaborate theology: both protect an accepted system and devalue anomalous inquiry in advance.
- A centralized conspiracy is not required for suppression if scientists, contractors, and officials depend on the same institutional funding and internalize the same corporate culture.
- The appendix allows that a deliberate Cold War policy may have started the pattern but says it can persist as an unconscious reflex without current orders.
- If military or intelligence agencies withhold evidence for security reasons or uncertainty, civilian science also loses the data needed to test seemingly impossible behavior.
- The authors compare present dismissal of UFO capabilities with reported nineteenth-century scientific objections to manned flight and wireless radio, arguing that unknown permutations of physical law should not be ruled out solely by current understanding.
- A high-ranking military officer allegedly told Tom DeLonge in September 2015 that a broad UFO declassification effort would fail for bureaucratic rather than purely conspiratorial reasons.
- Psi and remote-viewing work moved among project names, departments, military branches, officers, civilians, purposes, and research directions, creating fragmented records compounded by office politics.
- The modern UFO era and Cold War are both dated to 1947, embedding official interest in national-security fears from the beginning.
- Kenneth Arnold's June 24 sighting occurred during the same period as the June 5 Marshall Plan, Hollywood communist investigations, and Truman's July 26 National Security Act creating the CIA, Department of Defense, and National Security Council.
- Early FBI investigation focused particularly on the West Coast and Pacific Northwest because unidentified craft might be Soviet machines or captured Nazi rocket technology.
- Swedish "ghost rockets" reported since 1946 were likewise interpreted as possible Soviet use of Nazi technology; the appendix says their nature remained unresolved.
- Once unidentified aircraft were judged not to pose an immediate threat, military attention shifted toward Soviet atomic and hydrogen bombs, ballistic missiles, and conventional war material.
- Disclosure advocates are said to overestimate presidential power: more than sixty years of dispersed investigations produced hundreds or thousands of files under incompatible project names, numbers, agencies, and committees rather than one master archive.
- A known project such as Project Blue Book can be declassified, but the authors argue a blanket order cannot identify and assemble all relevant fragments.
- Civilian scientists consequently lack a reliable stream of raw official data, while available publications often come from agenda-driven UFO organizations and may be distrusted.
- The Byelokoroviche and Minot nuclear cases are presented as anomalies known through reports and documents. The authors infer danger and a need for examination, not that the reports automatically prove extraterrestrial responsibility.
- David Ensor's professional standing and access are used to support the provenance of the Soviet report, but the authors explicitly state that his involvement does not require accepting the account at face value.
- The similarity between Soviet and American launch-system reports is used to challenge a simple religion-based explanation because one occurred in an officially atheist system and one in a majority-Christian country.
- A 2011 Pew source is cited for nearly 80 percent of Americans identifying as Christian, while the Soviet Union and People's Republic of China are characterized as officially atheistic and punitive toward religious membership.
- The Phenomenon is said to disregard belief and unbelief; when instruments react, the authors question whether separating its scientific and religious dimensions remains useful.
- Both believers and skeptics can work backward from conclusions, but skeptical sloppiness is considered especially damaging because skeptics claim to defend scientific method.
- The appendix questions Sagan's phrase "extraordinary claims require extraordinary proof" by asking who defines extraordinary and whether accepted knowledge is being protected as complete.
- It nevertheless grants science a decisive difference from religion: logic, physical observation, repeatable experiment, and prediction give science reliable power.
- UFO reports strain this system because propulsion, behavior, travel distance, and nonrepeatability appear impossible under accepted physical models, allowing the subject to be excluded from reality before discussion.
- The authors conclude that an alternate philosophical definition of reality must include the Phenomenon and that contemporary secret technology must be separated from historical UFO reports, a task deferred to *War*.

## Concepts
- **Military-industrial complex** — Eisenhower's term, used here for the government, military, intelligence, and corporate funding structure that supports research while potentially constraining information and policy.
- **Weaponization of the paranormal** — military and intelligence interest in turning Psi, remote viewing, and related capacities into operational tools, one reason the authors group paranormal and UFO research together.
- **Alien-astronaut compromise** — replacing supernatural gods with technologically advanced ancient Visitors so religious traditions preserve misunderstood events while atheism avoids a disembodied deity.
- **Experiencer as heretic** — the person whose anomalous observation is rejected because it conflicts with the dominant scientific worldview, paralleling religious punishment for doctrinal deviation.
- **Scientific inquisition** — the authors' polemical name for skeptical institutions and writers who treat paranormal inquiry as cultural and intellectual heresy.
- **Galileo's Revenge** — scientific authority's historical reversal of Galileo's subjection: once punished by religious orthodoxy, science now allegedly polices reality and excludes anomalous claims.
- **Anthropocentric displacement** — heliocentrism's broader cultural effect of removing Earth and humanity from creation's center.
- **Scientism** — the attitude, as used here, that scientific authority and materialist method exhaust legitimate knowledge rather than provide one exceptionally powerful mode of inquiry.
- **Implementation versus essence** — the distinction between a system's ideals and the harms enabled by institutions using it; applied symmetrically to religion and science.
- **Debunker literature** — skeptical work testing paranormal claims for error, fraud, and inconsistency. The authors accept its legitimate function but object when dismissal precedes case analysis.
- **Anomalous propagation** — Sagan's cited explanation for some radar traces through temperature inversions; used here as an example of a conventional explanation generalized too readily.
- **Institutional rather than commanded cover-up** — information loss produced by shared funding, corporate norms, classification, and dispersed bureaucracy without a single active order to conceal everything.
- **Fragmented classification** — records divided among changing programs, agencies, military branches, officers, civilians, identifiers, and purposes, making a complete subject file unlikely to exist.
- **Disclosure problem** — the mismatch between demands for one presidential declassification command and an archive whose relevant records cannot be comprehensively located under one heading.
- **Flying-saucer meme** — the public language inaugurated in the chapter's account by Kenneth Arnold's June 24, 1947 report near Mount Rainier.
- **Ghost rockets** — unexplained Swedish reports beginning in 1946, at the time interpreted as possible Soviet deployment of captured Nazi technology.
- **Byelokoroviche incident** — the reported October 4, 1982 concurrence of a huge UFO over a Soviet missile base and unauthorized activation of R-12 launch controls for fifteen seconds.
- **Minot AFB incident** — David Schuur's report that launch indicators activated as a UFO passed over US Minuteman sites in the late 1960s and normalized after he used inhibit-launch controls.
- **Extraordinary-claims threshold** — Sagan's demand for extraordinary proof, criticized here because the category "extraordinary" can encode existing orthodoxy.
- **Predictive science** — the capacity to derive reproducible expectations from observation, logic, and experiment; explicitly recognized as science's distinctive strength.
- **Alternate definition of reality** — a proposed philosophical framework broad enough to treat anomalous reports as objects of legitimate inquiry without presupposing their final explanation.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Government-funded research structure** — US science, military work, intelligence programs, and corporate R&D are described as financially interdependent. The authors infer that information norms can emerge from funding relationships without a written concealment order.
- **Ancient-alien translation thought experiment** — divine power, heavenly residence, cloud ascent, miracles, and superweapons are redescribed as technologically advanced contact remembered through an ancient vocabulary. It is offered as a potential atheist-believer compromise, not proven history.
- **Galileo, 1616** — the Roman Inquisition judged Galileo's Copernican heliocentrism scientifically, philosophically, and religiously unacceptable, confined him to house arrest, and prohibited publication for life. The appendix says his later writing circulated only after death.
- **Giordano Bruno, 1600** — the Inquisition burned Bruno sixteen years before Galileo's judgment for holding similar ideas, according to the source.
- **Telescope observations** — the Moon's surface, Venus's phases, and Jupiter's moons could be observed by priests, cardinals, astronomers, and physicists, shifting authority toward reproducible observation.
- **Cosmic-scale displacement** — Earth becomes the third planet of an insignificant star in the Milky Way, itself one part of a stated 170 billion galaxies. The authors connect that displacement with nihilism and existentialism.
- **Harris's *Atheist Manifesto*** — the nine-page essay is criticized for anger, reducing violence to religion, pairing rationality with atheism, and imprecise use of "heresy," "belief," and the disciplinary status of astrology and alchemy.
- **Atheist dictators** — Stalin, Mao, and Lenin are counterexamples to the idea that irreligion ensures peace. Harris's quoted qualification is that they were irreligious to varying degrees but not especially rational.
- **Conflict-context inventory** — Al-Qaeda/Islamic State, Israel-Palestine, Northern Ireland, British India, and British Malaya are interpreted through colonial, political, economic, and resource histories that interact with rather than reduce to religion.
- **Nonreligious-war inventory** — Pearl Harbor (1941), Hiroshima and Nagasaki (1945), Hitler's invasions, Roman conquest, Alexander's expansion to India, Dutch Indonesia, Japanese expansion across Asia, and conquest of Indigenous American populations are listed as imperial/economic cases in which religion was absent as primary motive or supplied secondary justification.
- **Sagan's alien chapter** — *The Demon-Haunted World* allegedly dismisses witnesses through educational hierarchy, explains radar with temperature-inversion propagation, and uses two British crop-circle hoaxers to generalize beyond their cases.
- **Manned flight and wireless comparison** — reported nineteenth-century scientific objections to flight and radio without wires are used to warn that current physical impossibility may mark incomplete knowledge. The appendix gives no named objector or detailed historical source here.
- **Southwest airport meeting, September 2015** — Tom DeLonge met secretly with a high-ranking military officer familiar with black budgets and Pentagon operations. Discussing hypotheticals, the officer allegedly said broad declassification would fail because the evidence, if it existed, was bureaucratically scattered.
- **Psi-program migration** — remote viewing and ESP research moved among names, agencies, branches, officers, civilian groups, budgets, purposes, and emphases over about fifty years, making continuity and accountability difficult.
- **1947 chronology** — the Marshall Plan was announced June 5; Kenneth Arnold reported objects near Mount Rainier June 24; Truman signed the National Security Act July 26, creating the CIA, Department of Defense, and National Security Council; the House Un-American Activities Committee investigated alleged Hollywood communism in the same period.
- **Early FBI response** — West Coast and Pacific Northwest agents interviewed witnesses and collected reports because unidentified craft might be Soviet intrusions or products of captured Nazi rocket research.
- **Swedish ghost rockets, from 1946** — remote-country reports were considered possible Soviet intimidation using Nazi technology; the source says their nature remained unknown.
- **Cold War period** — the appendix dates the Cold War from 1947 almost to the Soviet Union's fall on December 26, 1991, making modern UFO investigation inseparable from military threat assessment.
- **Distributed UFO archive** — more than sixty years of agency and military investigations allegedly produced hundreds or thousands of files under separate names and numbers. Project Blue Book is an identifiable subset, not a master collection.
- **ABC *Primetime* reports, October 5, 1994 and the following year** — Sam Donaldson hosted David Ensor's report based on cautiously released post-Soviet KGB files. Many sightings were considered dubious, but former Red Army lieutenant colonel Vladimir Plantonev's account stood out to the program.
- **Byelokoroviche, October 4, 1982** — at an IRBM base in present-day Ukraine about 150 kilometers or 94 miles southwest of Kiev, personnel reported a UFO roughly 900 feet wide and five stories tall, visible for hours. At the same time, underground control panels showed R-12 missile launch codes entering without human action.
- **Byelokoroviche control-room interval** — personnel who did not see the object endured fifteen seconds of apparent launch initialization before controls reset without human intervention. The authors emphasize the R-12's connection to the 1962 Cuban Missile Crisis and the possibility of catastrophic misinterpretation.
- **1982–83 tension context** — Ronald Reagan was president; in March 1983 he called the Soviet Union an "evil empire" and announced the Strategic Defense Initiative. The appendix uses this setting to establish the stakes of an unauthorized launch sequence.
- **Ensor provenance qualification** — David Ensor later directed Voice of America after serving in communications/public diplomacy at the US Embassy in Kabul. His status supports likely access to records and witnesses but, the authors explicitly say, does not make the report automatically true.
- **Minot Air Force Base, late 1960s** — First Lieutenant David H. Schuur of the 455th/91st Strategic Missile Wing told Robert Hastings that "Launch In Progress" indicators activated while a UFO passed over missile sites. Schuur used "Inhibit Launch" switches; indicators returned to normal after the object passed.
- **Minot documentary context** — a December 6, 1966 *Minot Daily News* story and a *Saturday Evening Post* article by J. Allen Hynek are cited for earlier base activity that may have related to Schuur's event.
- **Cross-bloc comparison** — Minot occurred about fifteen years before Byelokoroviche and only five years after the Cuban Missile Crisis. The authors emphasize trained personnel, simultaneous system effects, and similar nuclear stakes on both Cold War sides.
- **2011 Pew comparison** — nearly 80 percent of Americans are reported Christian, contrasted with official Soviet and Chinese atheism and penalties for religious organization membership. Similar anomalous reports across those systems are used against a purely religious explanation.
- **Repeatability limit** — UFO propulsion, distance, and behavior cannot be produced predictably in a laboratory; the authors accept this conflict with standard science but reject using it to define the observations out of existence.

## Thread
The Appendix turns the book's content problem into an authority problem. Earlier chapters asked how brains, genes, machines, altered states, and alien bodies might fit together; this essay asks why the institutions capable of investigating those links either divide them into science and religion or classify the relevant data. Galileo supplies the reversal: religious authority once excluded a correct astronomical model, while scientific authority may now exclude anomalous observations before inquiry. The appendix then moves from rhetoric to bureaucracy and finally to nuclear cases, using fragmented records and instrument-linked reports to argue for disciplined investigation without claiming that every report is true. Its final task is programmatic: enlarge reality's legitimate philosophical frame and separate historical anomalies from secret human technology in *War*.
