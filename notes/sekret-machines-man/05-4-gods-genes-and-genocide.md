# Gods, Genes, and Genocide

## In one paragraph
DeLonge and Levenda move from reported manipulation of individual bodies to the possibility that humanity, its genetic code, or both were engineered, while repeatedly stating that the chapter's conclusions are speculative rather than truth claims. An unnamed informant's most disturbing proposal is that genocide might eliminate ethnic groups carrying genes resistant to alien hybridization or control; the authors compare the idea with eugenics, name known population-linked illnesses only as an analogy, and provide no evidence that any genocide had such a cause. They then place modern alien-hybrid stories beside Genesis, Babylonian creation, alienation, alchemy, and directed panspermia, especially Francis Crick and Leslie Orgel's argument that life's code may have been deliberately seeded from elsewhere. A detailed DNA/RNA primer explains four bases, twenty canonical amino acids, three-base codons, sixty-four combinations, start/stop signals, molybdenum, and the near-universality and economy of the code. Finally, the authors present Vladimir shCherbak and Maxim Makukov's 2013 "Biological SETI" claim that positional decimal patterns, zero-like start/stop functions, symmetries, and nucleon sums in the genetic code are statistically suggestive of deliberate construction, while preserving evolution and natural selection after the code's proposed insertion.

## Core claims
- The authors explicitly continue the end of *Sekret Machines: Gods*, where Frankenstein and his Monster, the golem, and human rebellion against creator-gods supplied perspectives on current science's erosion of the human/artificial boundary.
- They say contemporary science and technology are moving humans from Earth-bound organisms toward spacefaring instruments and creating the robotic, androgynous beings found in occult nightmares and abduction reports.
- If consciousness is inseparable from brains, the authors reason, artificial brains should produce artificial consciousness and force social and legal reconsideration of life, awareness, and humanity; this is a conditional argument, not a reported experimental result.
- They ask whether reverse engineering concerns not only UFO craft but their pilots: human robots, androids, and cyborgs may be reengineered versions of the beings encountered in the Phenomenon.
- The chapter raises the possibility that parts of humanity were designed or assembled by another intelligence and asks what that would imply if consciousness is merely a product or "secretion" of an artificially generated brain.
- Patterns and symmetries in nature are interpreted by the authors as possible evidence of designing intelligence and a deliberately left "calling card," a claim later attached to DNA's structure.
- An unnamed informant asked the authors to consider whether some genocides served an ulterior genetic function: eliminating populations carrying traits resistant to alleged alien hybridization or control. The authors state this proposal but do not document an instance or endorse it as established history.
- The inverse question is whether traits removed by Earthly natural selection might be useful for survival off-planet.
- The authors acknowledge that the informant's hypothesis initially resembles eugenics and Nazi race science. They mention contactee reports of "Nordics" only as a suggestive association.
- Sickle cell anemia among African Americans and Tay-Sachs disease among Jews are used to establish the narrower fact, as the authors present it, that some populations carry characteristic disease-associated genes; they do not treat either illness as evidence of alien activity.
- They speculate about otherwise silent genes, perhaps in "junk DNA," that an alien race might find dangerous—"alien antibodies" enabling resistance to hybridization, detection of the Others, intellectual or physical capacities, or protection from their intentions.
- Racial polarization itself is posed as possibly manipulated to prevent such traits from spreading, but the chapter offers no evidence and frames the idea through repeated "what if" questions.
- The chapter then asks whether the genetic code was itself invented by the Others and labels the forthcoming argument outrageous, pseudoscientific-sounding, and necessarily speculative.
- DeLonge and Levenda explicitly distinguish data from conclusion: they say the cited data and documents are genuine primary-source material but do not claim their interpretation reflects "the Truth"; it is offered as a platform for research.
- Mendel's hybrid peas provide the conventional nineteenth-century entry to heredity, while Genesis 6 supplies an older hybrid narrative in which beings born of "sons of God" and human women are followed by the Deluge.
- The authors interpret Noah's Ark as a device preserving desirable plant and animal genetic stock after a divinely ordered destruction of humans and hybrids, calling Genesis 6 the Bible's first genocide.
- Babylonian creation stories are read as making humans artificial worker-beings from the blood of defeated gods, hence already cyborg or hybrid; contemporary transhumanism becomes, in their phrase, a possible glyph of the "trans-divine."
- A future mythology might recast the Holocaust as subhuman beings persecuted in a conflict among gods, with Israel imagined as a Noah's Ark for survivors. This is an analogy about mythic reinterpretation, not a historical causal claim.
- Beginning from the project's premise that the Phenomenon is real, the authors invite reinterpretation of history and the common feeling that humans are visitors, prisoners, or aliens on an inhospitable world.
- A purely mechanical Big Bang account makes everything inevitable and leaves no anomaly, but the authors say that formulation understates the human condition and its search for purpose.
- Human beings moved from environmental symbiosis to seeing themselves as creation's midwives, then from alchemical collaboration with nature to scientific control, domination, and mathematical abstraction.
- Zecharia Sitchin's literal claim that gods created humans to mine gold is not adopted. Instead, Mircea Eliade's history of metallurgy and alchemy is used to describe mining and transmutation as parallel outer and inner perfection.
- The rise of chemistry discredited alchemy and, in the authors' narrative, displaced cooperation with nature by hubris and separation; nature became servant or enemy.
- Biblical dominion and expulsion from Eden are treated as precedents for separation: Adam is given rule over Earth, not stars; after expulsion, life becomes "nasty, brutish, and short," and childbirth painful.
- Human needs, reproductive pain, social conflict, poverty, war, disease, and extensive advice literature are presented as signs that humans are unusually uncomfortable on Earth, a philosophical condition called alienation.
- Buddhism's diagnosis of life as sorrow caused by attachment is summarized as requiring release from world, existence, and even gods; existentialism and afterlife religions are presented as other responses to an apparently unfit world.
- The authors suggest moments of beauty may trigger an inherited memory of a lost home encoded in junk DNA. They label this a possibility and connect it with a repeated original trauma.
- Understanding human identity is made prerequisite to identifying the Others: "once we understand who we are, we can begin to figure out who they are."
- The authors state that evolution is a scientific fact and the dominant explanation fitting the evidence, despite unresolved questions about mutation and speciation.
- They say many mutations reduce capacities rather than expanding them, except in specific laboratory conditions, and that the timing and mechanism of speciation remain incompletely understood; these qualifications do not lead them to reject Darwinian evolution.
- Fundamentalist literalism is contrasted with evolution, and intelligent design is described as a political/religious attempt to introduce a Creator without naming God, not a scientific theory in the ordinary sense.
- The authors note a structural resemblance between intelligent design and directed panspermia but say the latter does not require the Biblical deity.
- They use religious and political pressure against 1980s military remote viewing and AATIP—where some officials reportedly considered UFOs demonic—as examples of ideology constraining inquiry.
- Scientific funding through universities, corporations, defense contractors, and government budgets creates professional risk for unconventional theories; tenure, major grants, or a Nobel Prize can protect figures such as Stephen Hawking or Michio Kaku when discussing aliens, star drives, or SETI risk.
- Francis Crick and Leslie Orgel proposed that terrestrial life may have been seeded deliberately from elsewhere; their directed-panspermia argument is the chapter's scientific anchor.
- Crick, Watson, Wilkins, and Franklin are associated with solving DNA's double-helix structure; the Nobel Prize went to Crick, Watson, and Wilkins in the chapter's account, while Franklin is also named as a collaborator.
- Crick's and Wilkins's wartime work is used to emphasize how World War II shaped twentieth-century science, not as evidence for panspermia.
- The DNA code uses four chemical bases in restricted pairs, sugar, and phosphate to form nucleotides arranged in a double helix; three-base codons produce amino acids and control signals.
- DNA uses thymine, whereas RNA generally uses uracil, is usually single-stranded, and can carry information as messenger RNA; RNA's simpler form leads some geneticists to place it earlier than DNA.
- Four bases arranged in groups of three create sixty-four possible codons, yet only twenty canonical amino acids build human proteins, plus initiation and termination signals.
- Crick and Orgel's problem was how chemical amino acids become life and eventually sentient life; the authors extend it to whether consciousness itself evolves toward super-consciousness as life moves from ocean to land to space.
- Molybdenum is described as necessary for genetic-code development and life but relatively rare on Earth's surface and more common in oceans. Crick and Orgel inferred an off-planet environment where it was abundant; the chapter reports their reasoning rather than independently validating it.
- Panspermia means life's seeds arrived from elsewhere, potentially as radiation-resistant microbial material on a meteorite that survived atmospheric entry and found nutrients on Earth.
- Alternatives include broad random distribution to many worlds or debris from a life-bearing planet struck by a comet; directed panspermia instead proposes intelligence, deliberate targeting, and an organic precursor.
- DNA's basic structure is said to be the same across terrestrial plants, animals, and humans, with limited exceptions mainly in mitochondria. Its universal economy is compared with binary bits producing many programming languages and applications.
- Universality does not identify the designer: the authors say an alien precursor must be considered before invoking the transcendent Abrahamic God.
- Ancient Egyptian and Indian seed-emission myths are presented as cultural analogues to panspermia, not scientific evidence proving it.
- Crick and Orgel presented directed panspermia in 1971 and published jointly in 1973; Orgel later modified his view, while Crick reportedly supported it throughout life.
- Three RNA stop codons—UAG, UAA, and UGA—and one usual start codon—AUG—separate sequences like punctuation or, in the chapter's analogy, zero.
- Zero is a human symbolic invention and positional placeholder absent as an object in nature, yet shCherbak and Makukov claim its functional and mathematical equivalent appears nonrandomly in the genetic code.
- The authors summarize ordinary heredity and modern testing to establish that DNA is ordered and predictive, then ask the additional question of whether the code's architecture was consciously designed.
- The 2013 Kazakh paper argues that arithmetic and ideographic patterns are highly improbable under random code evolution and more consistent with an intelligent source; the chapter abbreviates a mathematically dense argument and recommends the original paper.
- George Gamow's mid-1950s model incorrectly allowed codon bases in any order, but his search anticipated symmetries. The later finding that order changes the amino acid makes the genetic code positional like arithmetic.
- Start and stop codons provide essential organization rather than random interruption, keeping growing genetic sequences legible and functional.
- The Kazakh analysis counts nucleons in amino acids—arginine at 100 and lysine at 72 are the examples—and obtains patterned totals including 666, 888, and 777 under arrangements derived from Gamow.
- The reported numerical patterns appear specifically in positional decimal notation, according to the paper as quoted; the authors acknowledge that number symbols and bases are human constructions.
- The Kazakh authors' "ideographical" evidence includes DNA's double helix and RNA's single strand, forms the chapter says were prefigured in medieval Indian and classical Greek religious/esoteric imagery.
- Biological SETI proposes that life's chemical substrate existed first, then a deliberately structured genetic code was inserted; after insertion, ordinary evolution and natural selection could proceed.
- The authors stress that shCherbak and Makukov are not anti-Darwinist intelligent-design advocates and that their proposal need not contradict evolution.
- The chapter ends by foreshadowing another ancient code with four elements in three-part arrangements and sixty-four outcomes, asking how its match to genetics could be explained; it does not yet provide the answer in this assigned unit.

## Concepts
- **Ghost in the Sekret Machine** — the Phenomenon positioned at the hidden center of human technological evolution, where humans create the robotic beings associated with alien and occult encounters.
- **Reengineered pilots** — the authors' hypothesis that human robots, androids, and cyborgs may reproduce alleged UFO operators, so the pilots rather than only the craft are being reverse engineered.
- **Artificial consciousness** — consciousness expected under the conditional view that mind is inseparable from brain and therefore should emerge from an artificial brain, bringing legal and social consequences.
- **Genetic-resistance / genocide hypothesis** — an unnamed informant's speculation that some populations may carry genes resistant to alien manipulation and that genocidal conflict might eliminate them. The chapter supplies no confirming case and explicitly calls the ensuing conclusions speculative.
- **Alien antibodies** — the authors' metaphor for human genes that might resist hybridization or control, increase perception of nonhuman presence, or provide protection.
- **Eugenics / race science** — the coercive racial-selection ideology the authors say their informant's proposal initially resembles, especially the rationale used by the Nazi regime; the comparison is a warning, not support for the proposal.
- **Alien hybridization** — contactee reports of combining human and nonhuman heredity; in this chapter it supplies the alleged pressure against which speculative resistant genes might evolve.
- **Junk DNA / genetic memory** — DNA without a currently discerned function, proposed speculatively as a place for resistance traits or inherited memories of a lost home and original trauma.
- **Big Bang inevitability** — the mechanistic view that all later events and organisms follow from the universe's initial event, leaving no true anomaly; the authors regard it as logically valid but incomplete as an account of human meaning.
- **Trans-divine** — the authors' recasting of transhumanism through creation myths: humans already mix divine and artificial components, so technological transformation repeats a movement beyond the divine-human design.
- **Alienation** — the philosophical condition of human estrangement from environment, society, body, and existence; the authors treat it as potentially deeper than contrary human temperament.
- **Alchemy as midwifery** — the claimed alignment of spiritual self-perfection and material transmutation, especially lead toward gold, in which human and metal change together rather than one dominating the other.
- **Evolution / natural selection** — the established scientific framework the authors affirm: hereditary change and survival across generations explain life's development after any proposed origin or insertion of the code.
- **Mutation / speciation** — mutation is hereditary alteration, often described here as reducing rather than adding capacities; speciation is the emergence of species, whose mechanism and speed the authors say retain unanswered questions.
- **Intelligent design** — described here as a politically and religiously motivated Creator theory based on natural symmetry and elegance, but not scientific theory in the commonly accepted sense.
- **Panspermia** — the possibility that life's generating material came to Earth from elsewhere, perhaps on a meteorite or scattered debris, without necessarily being aimed here.
- **Directed panspermia** — Crick and Orgel's proposal that an intelligence deliberately selected Earth and seeded it with genetic material from an organic precursor.
- **DNA** — deoxyribonucleic acid, generally double-stranded and built from adenine, thymine, guanine, and cytosine base pairs joined with sugar and phosphate in nucleotides.
- **RNA** — ribonucleic acid, generally single-stranded, using uracil instead of thymine; messenger RNA carries genetic information to cells and its simpler structure may predate DNA.
- **Base pair** — the restricted chemical bond of adenine with thymine and guanine with cytosine in DNA; RNA substitutes uracil for thymine.
- **Nucleotide** — a base pair attached to a sugar molecule and phosphate molecule, forming the repeated units arranged along DNA's ladder-like helix in the chapter's primer.
- **Canonical amino acid** — one of twenty amino acids the chapter says occur in human DNA's protein-making system.
- **Codon** — a group of three bases coding an amino acid or serving a start/stop function; order matters, making the code positional.
- **Start/stop codons** — RNA signals that initiate or terminate sequences: start is usually AUG; stops are UAG, UAA, and UGA. They are compared with punctuation and zero.
- **Molybdenum argument** — Crick and Orgel's claim, as reported, that life's dependence on an element uncommon on Earth's surface suggests the genetic code developed where molybdenum was more plentiful.
- **Universal genetic code** — the same basic four-letter structure underlying terrestrial plants, animals, and humans, with a few mostly mitochondrial variations; its economy motivates design questions.
- **Binary-code analogy** — the comparison between four genetic letters generating terrestrial life's diversity and computer bits 0 and 1 generating bytes, languages, programs, and applications.
- **SETI** — the conventional Search for Extraterrestrial Intelligence using radio telescopes; Biological SETI relocates that search into genetics.
- **Zero** — a human-created symbol for place value or absence of quantity, treated by the Kazakh analysis as functionally present in genetic start/stop organization.
- **Positional notation** — a system in which order changes value, as 1-2-3 differs from 3-2-1; codon order likewise changes the produced amino acid.
- **Decimal and other number systems** — the modern base-10 positional system uses nine nonzero digits and zero; the chapter contrasts it with Babylonian base 6, binary, and sexagesimal systems because the reported genetic patterns appear specifically in decimal notation.
- **Gamow arrangement** — George Gamow's mid-1950s mathematical attempt to decode codons; its order-independence was wrong, but its symmetries inform later analysis.
- **Kazakh Configuration** — the authors' name for shCherbak and Makukov's claimed arithmetic and ideographic organization of the genetic code, including decimal positional patterns, zero-like separators, symmetry, and nucleon totals.
- **Biological SETI** — a search for extraterrestrial intelligence in the structure of terrestrial genetics rather than radio signals; DNA is treated as an equation or encoded text containing a possible designed signature.
- **Anthropocentrism** — rejecting extraterrestrial possibilities because they seem too much like science fiction or because Earth is treated as the universe's center; the authors compare it to preserving geocentric attitudes after heliocentrism.
- **Prima materia** — the preexisting chemical substrate of life into which the Kazakh hypothesis says the structured genetic code may have been inserted.
- **Genetic code as language** — codons compared with letters, nucleotide groups with words and sentences, and start/stop codons with punctuation that prevents an illegible stream.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Justin Benson's *Resolution* (2012)** — the epigraph lists aliens, ghosts, cults, gateways to hell, secret bases, and other dimensions, then proposes that any underlying reality may be none or all of them. It frames the chapter's refusal of single-category explanation.
- **Frankenstein, the Monster, golem, and rebel humans** — recalled explicitly from the end of *Sekret Machines: Gods* as narratives for creators losing control of artificial beings; no new textual case details are supplied here.
- **Artificial-brain thought experiment** — if brain and consciousness cannot be separated, building an artificial brain creates consciousness. The authors use this conditional case to anticipate legal and social disputes and humanity's demotion to a vulnerable evolutionary way station.
- **Unnamed informant's genocide proposal** — the informant asked the authors to investigate genetics in Phenomenon narratives and consider whether genocide eliminates alien-resistant traits. No person, document, population, gene, experiment, or confirmed genocide mechanism is identified.
- **Sickle cell and Tay-Sachs analogy** — sickle cell anemia among African Americans and Tay-Sachs among Jews illustrate population-linked genetic traits in the authors' argument. They are examples of known illness inheritance, not evidence for the alien-resistance proposal.
- **Gregor Mendel's hybrid peas** — the nineteenth-century Catholic monk's plant-crossing experiments are given as the conventional starting point for scientific heredity.
- **Genesis 6, Noah, and the Deluge** — human women and "sons of God" produce hybrid beings; immediately afterward Noah's Ark preserves desirable examples of plants and animals before a catastrophic flood removes much of the human and hybrid population. The authors interpret this sequence genetically and call it the Bible's first divinely ordered genocide.
- **Babylonian creation** — after one group of gods defeats another in cosmic war, humans are manufactured as laborers from blood of the slain. The authors read humanity as artificial worker, cyborg, and divine/nondivine hybrid.
- **Holocaust/Ark future-memory thought experiment** — the authors imagine distant people mythologizing the Holocaust as a gods' conflict over a subhuman group, with Israel as an Ark preserving survivors. It demonstrates how real events could become Biblical/Babylonian myth, not an alien account of the Holocaust.
- **Big Bang inevitability** — the mechanical view makes current writing and reading the inexorable result of events billions of years ago. The authors concede its logic but say it does not exhaust human purpose or suffering.
- **Zecharia Sitchin's gold-mining claim** — Sitchin proposed that gods made humans to mine terrestrial gold. The authors decline this literal version but retain mining and metallurgy as spiritually meaningful human collaboration with nature.
- **Mircea Eliade on metallurgy and alchemy** — *The Forge and the Crucible* (University of Chicago Press, 1956/1978) links ancient mines and metalwork to alchemy. Lead-to-gold transmutation externalizes and aligns the practitioner's inner search for perfection.
- **Eden, dominion, and childbirth** — God gives Adam dominion over Earth, explicitly noted as not extending to planets and stars; Adam and Eve first live as guests with one prohibited tree, then after expulsion face Hobbes's "nasty, brutish, and short" life and painful childbirth, with Genesis 3:16 quoted in a note.
- **Human-hostility inventory** — food, water, shelter, clothing, menstruation, pregnancy, childbirth, poverty, political constraint, conflict in love and family, and the proliferation of advice books are cited as evidence for alienation. Beauty—sunset, child laughter, embrace, music—is contrasted with disaster, drone strikes, addicted newborns, domestic violence, war, plague, famine, and drought.
- **Buddhist response** — life is sorrow because of attachment to world, ideas, existence, and gods; eternal bliss requires neutralizing attachment, which the authors summarize as leaving planet and universe.
- **Existential and afterlife responses** — existentialism faces unfitness directly, while other religions call this world the best possible but temporary posting before a better afterlife.
- **Genetic-memory family-album analogy** — beauty and immediate intimacy with a lover are compared with old photographs reawakening an inherited sensation, speculatively encoded in junk DNA and linked to original trauma.
- **Evolution's stated open questions** — negative rather than beneficial effects of many mutations and uncertainty about how or how quickly speciation occurs are called lacunae, while the authors maintain that Darwin's framework best fits the data.
- **Fundamentalist creationism** — literal Biblical dating, direct divine creation of humans, and rejection of primate descent are presented as religious objections, distinct from scientific controversy.
- **Intelligent-design campaign** — political and religious conservatives allegedly promoted a mathematically elegant Designer without openly saying God; the authors deny ordinary scientific status but note similarity to directed panspermia.
- **Ideological closure of programs** — religious zealotry and politics are blamed for shutting military remote-viewing work in the 1980s and for high-ranking officials' reported resistance to AATIP because UFOs might be demonic; a May 8, 2018 article is cited.
- **Scientific patronage system** — universities, large corporations, defense contractors, and governments fund salaries, laboratories, manufacturing, and exotic materials; bureaucracy makes unconventional work hazardous unless protected by tenure, grants, or prestige.
- **Hawking and Kaku** — offered as scientists whose status permitted public speculation about alien life, star drives, and SETI dangers without loss of professional standing.
- **DNA discovery team and wartime backgrounds** — Francis Crick (1916–2004), James Watson (born 1928), Maurice Wilkins (1916–2004), and Rosalind Franklin (1920–1958) are named in the double-helix story; Crick, Watson, and Wilkins shared the Nobel Prize in the authors' account. Wilkins worked on the Manhattan Project and was suspected by the FBI and MI6 of Soviet espionage, grouped with Oppenheimer, Jack Parsons, Frank Malina, and H. S. Tsien as later *War* material.
- **Crick's Admiralty mine** — during World War II, Crick designed an underwater mine intended to evade the German magnetic minesweeper called a *Sperrbrecher* and explode beneath it. The case supports the chapter's aside that war shaped scientific careers.
- **Crick and Wilkins after the war** — friendship with physicist/molecular biologist Wilkins led Crick toward genetics. Crick and Watson, described as not formally trained biochemists, used pattern visualization and logical analysis.
- **Crick and Leslie Orgel** — discussions with chemist/evolutionary biologist Orgel (1927–2007) about the jump from amino-acid chemistry to life produced the directed-panspermia proposal.
- **DNA base-pair primer** — adenine (A) bonds only with thymine (T), guanine (G) with cytosine (C); base pair plus sugar and phosphate forms a nucleotide, and nucleotides make the double-helix ladder shown in Figure 1.
- **Amino-acid/codon examples** — twenty amino acids are called canonical. Three bases make a codon: glycine can be GGG or GGA, while RNA's tryptophan codon is UGG and tryptophan is identified as a serotonin precursor.
- **RNA comparison** — uracil replaces thymine, RNA is usually one strand, and messenger RNA relays genetic information to cells. Some geneticists infer RNA preceded DNA because simpler systems precede complex ones.
- **Sixty-four combinations** — four bases in ordered groups of three yield $4 \times 4 \times 4 = 64$ possible codons, represented in Figure 2's universal genetic-code map.
- **Consciousness evolutionary questions** — the authors ask where life begins in chemicals, whether consciousness accumulates gradually from amoeba to Nobel laureate or arrives with the sea-to-land transition, and whether it will become super-consciousness during the land-to-space transition. No answers are claimed.
- **Molybdenum case** — molybdenum is described as essential to life's genetic development, rare at Earth's surface but more common in oceans. Crick and Orgel reasoned that DNA began where it was plentiful, implying elsewhere.
- **Undirected delivery scenarios** — DNA/RNA could be scattered toward thousands or millions of worlds; a comet could strike a living planet and send rock fragments outward; a microbial meteorite could survive interstellar radiation and Earth's atmosphere, land among nutrients, and replicate. The chapter labels all unproven possibilities.
- **Directed delivery scenario** — economy favors a deliberate choice of Earth or a few viable worlds by an intelligent organic precursor; molybdenum and code structure are the stated reasons.
- **Universal-code/binary analogy** — all terrestrial life reduces to four genetic letters despite enormous diversity, like software reducing to binary 0 and 1. Traditionally eight bits form a byte; bytes support hundreds of languages and millions of programs and apps.
- **Robert Temple's panspermia history** — Temple, author of the 1976 bestseller *The Sirius Mystery*, argued in a 2007 *International Journal of Astrobiology* article that panspermia appears in Egyptian Pyramid Texts and Sanskrit scripture.
- **Atum, Nu/Nut, and Shiva** — Atum creates through cosmic masturbation, with his seed taking root in empty space personified as the womb-goddess Nu or Nut; several Egyptian depictions are said to exist. Indian Vedas carry similar narratives, and Levenda's *Tantric Temples* (2011, pp. 70–71) describes Shiva's seminal emission or premature ejaculation in Tantric texts. These are mythic parallels.
- **Crick–Orgel publication history** — the directed-panspermia idea was presented in 1971 and published in *Icarus* 19(3), pp. 341–346, in 1973. Orgel later changed his position to some extent; Crick remained a supporter.
- **Start and stop codons** — UAG, UAA, and UGA terminate RNA sequences; AUG usually starts one. Their separating function is compared first to punctuation, then to zero.
- **Sumerian mathematics** — early writings record bookkeeping/accounting and dated astronomical lists. Tally lines representing cattle or days lead in the chapter's sketch toward arithmetic, geometry, algebra, trigonometry, and calculus.
- **Zero's operations and etymology** — zero distinguishes 1 from 10, represents absence such as $10-10$, and is called even because division by two can be represented as $+1$ and $-1$ without remainder. Sanskrit *sunya* (desert/emptiness) passed through Arabic *sifr* and Italian *zefiro* to English "zero" and "cipher."
- **Maya, Indian, Chinese, Sumerian, and Fibonacci zero** — the Maya independently developed zero by roughly the sixth century CE; Indian use was about five hundred years earlier than Western adoption. Chinese and Sumerian systems had awkward empty-set placeholders. Leonardo Bonacci/Fibonacci (1170–1250), raised in North Africa, carried Arab and Indian usage into Italian mathematics.
- **Everyday genetic testing** — Ancestry.com and 23andMe use cheek swabs or saliva to report ethnicity and sometimes probabilities for hair, eyes, illness, or addiction. TV cases show DNA identifying guilty people and freeing innocents; prenatal tests detect possible disease or disability. These examples establish order and predictability, not design.
- **shCherbak and Makukov, 2013** — Vladimir I. shCherbak of al-Farabi Kazakh National University and Maxim A. Makukov of the Fesenkov Astrophysical Institute published "The 'WOW signal' of the terrestrial genetic code" in *Icarus* on March 6, 2013 (pp. 228–242). They claim zero-like, positional, and nonrandom structures imply intelligence.
- **Gamow arrangement** — George Gamow (1904–1968), a Russian theoretical physicist trained at Göttingen and friend of Edward Teller and J. Robert Oppenheimer, defected west in 1933 and joined George Washington University in 1934. He consulted for the US Navy in World War II but is not known to have joined Manhattan; his mid-1950s codon model wrongly ignored base order while predicting important symmetry.
- **Positional codon finding** — discovery that rearranging three bases changes the amino acid made codons analogous to numbers whose digits change value by order, such as 1-2-3 versus 3-2-1 or 2-1-3.
- **Nucleon totals** — the chapter reports that arginine has 100 nucleons and lysine 72, and that Gamow-based groupings yield evocative sums of 666, 888, and 777.
- **Human number systems** — Babylonians used base 6; modern decimal uses nine digits plus zero; binary and sexagesimal systems are also named. A trailing zero makes 9 into 90, whereas a leading zero leaves 09 equivalent to 9.
- **Quoted decimal limitation** — shCherbak and Makukov state that the distinctive nucleon-sum notations appear "only in positional decimal system"; the note preserves the chapter's printed wording and qualification.
- **Ideographic structure and old imagery** — their argument includes visual form: double-stranded DNA and single-stranded RNA. The authors say double-helix imagery appeared in medieval India and classical Greece, but provide no itemized artifact in this chapter.
- **Statistical design claim** — the Kazakh paper reportedly argues that random evolution is less likely than deliberate construction for the code's organization. Start/stop locations prevent chaotic interruption, like punctuation appearing at sentence boundaries rather than inside words.
- **Genetic-text examples** — organized clusters yield traits such as blue eyes, disease, skin or hair color, and height; rats and swine are described as genetically only slightly different from humans. Start/stop boundaries keep hereditary "sentences" legible.
- **Code economy** — only twenty amino acids emerge from sixty-four possible three-base combinations, yet the system produces all life from microbes to mastodons; the authors call this economy breathtaking.
- **Biological SETI model** — life's primordial chemicals arise naturally, a structured genetic code is inserted later, and standard evolution follows. DNA can then be read as mathematical equations or an alphabetic text containing a possible message from intelligence.
- **Closing sixty-four-fold precursor** — the authors tease an ancient system, geographically and historically remote from Crick and Watson, with four components arranged in threes to create sixty-four possibilities and believed to encode life. Its identity and analysis are deferred to the next chapter.

## Key quotes
- "...what if our robots, androids, and cyborgs are nothing less than reengineered aliens?" — the opening reversal from craft to pilots.
- "What follows is necessarily speculative." — the authors' explicit status warning.
