# Government Documents

## In one paragraph
This final back-matter unit identifies four representative declassified records from a much larger body of CIA, DIA, US military, and foreign archival material used as background for *Man*. The documents concern Psi, extrasensory perception, psychokinesis, remote viewing, SRI, and the government programs that moved this research among agencies; they are not presented here as direct documentary proof of UFOs or alien contact. Because government files often have no named author or redact the creator, the authors organize the sample through titles, dates, agency/project descriptions, CIA locator numbers, original classification information, and release dates. The four items move from a 1977 CIA Office of Technical Services briefing summary, through a 1981 DIA GRILL FLAME question-and-answer memo, to an undated classified STAR GATE definitions document and a revised 1978 SRI remote-viewing protocol. Taken together, they substantiate the narrower archival claim that US intelligence and military bodies funded, defined, briefed, budgeted, and standardized remote-viewing research over time, while also displaying secrecy, compartmentation, and concern about institutional embarrassment.

## Source architecture
- **Scope statement:** The authors call the four records "representative examples" of a "vast library" of declassified CIA, DIA, and US military files, plus Russian, Chinese, and Japanese files in various archives.
- **Subject scope:** The named background subjects are Psi research, remote-viewing programs, SRI, and related topics. The source does not say these four files document alien beings, UFO craft, abduction, or missile-base incidents.
- **Access model:** The files are said to be available for free download from the relevant government websites. The unit prints CIA locator numbers so researchers can retrieve the records.
- **Authorship limits:** Three entries are listed as anonymous. The preface explains that government documents may lack an author or have the creator's name redacted.
- **Metadata carried:** Each inventory item includes some combination of title, document type, date, page count, originating organization, classification, release date, project name, institutional relationships, content summary, and CIA locator.
- **Release language:** Every record is marked "Approved for Release" with a date. Release dates in 2000 or 2005 are not the creation dates of the documents.
- **Representative, not exhaustive:** The four records cannot stand as a complete inventory of the agencies, countries, projects, experiments, personnel, or results described by the broader archive.
- **Program lineage:** OTS work with SRI and named viewers, DIA's GRILL FLAME, the STAR GATE program, and SRI's protocol are presented as connected stages or elements in a multi-agency remote-viewing history.

## Document inventory

### OTS briefing for the House Permanent Select Committee (1977)
- **Printed title:** "Memorandum for the Record; Subject: Briefing for House Permanent Select Committee Re: OTS Involvement in ESP Research."
- **Internal identifier and date:** OTS/BAB Memo #287-77, November 17, 1977.
- **Length and original classification:** Seven pages; originally classified SECRET.
- **Organizational provenance:** OTS is expanded in the unit as the Office of Technical Services, a CIA department. The document summarizes an anticipated or completed briefing for the House Permanent Select Committee.
- **Substantive content:** It summarizes OTS attempts to develop a viable intelligence capability from remote viewing in collaboration with SRI, particularly Pat Price; Ingo Swann is also named.
- **Institutional context:** The memo calls the work potentially "embarrassing" while the CIA and OTS were under investigation in connection with Watergate.
- **Locator:** `CIA-RDP96-00787R000500170001-0`.
- **Release:** Approved for release March 24, 2005.
- **What it supports:** The document, as summarized here, supports that OTS briefed or prepared to brief congressional overseers about CIA/SRI remote-viewing work and considered the political/reputational context.
- **What it does not establish here:** The entry gives no experiment scores, target descriptions, operational successes, UFO findings, or independent judgment that remote viewing worked.

### Project GRILL FLAME memo (1981)
- **Printed title and date:** "Project GRILL FLAME," August 20, 1981.
- **Length and agency:** Eight-page DIA memo.
- **Format:** One question and answer per page, with handwritten notes.
- **Institutional references:** CIA, SRI, and INSCOM are named, and financial budgeting is discussed.
- **Handwritten note on assessment:** One notation reads, "To me the spirit of the question indicates what are you doing to assess the Phenomenon?"
- **Handwritten note on disclosure:** Another reads, "Army guidance to O'Keefe: Tell them as little as possible." The source prints an exclamation mark after this quotation, signaling the authors' reaction.
- **Locator:** `CIA-RDP96-00788R001100210002-6`.
- **Release:** Approved for release March 24, 2005.
- **What it supports:** The record, as summarized, shows GRILL FLAME discussed through DIA questions and answers, with interagency references, budget concerns, assessment language, and restrictive communication guidance.
- **What it does not establish here:** "The Phenomenon" in the handwritten question occurs inside a remote-viewing-project memo; the unit does not demonstrate that the word refers specifically to UFOs or aliens in that document.

### Project STAR GATE definitions document (undated)
- **Printed title:** "Project STAR GATE."
- **Date and length:** Undated; thirty-six pages.
- **Classification string:** Secret NoForn Star Gate Limdis, expanded by the authors as Secret, No Foreigners, Star Gate, Limited Distribution.
- **Program status interpretation:** The authors say the markings indicate a Special Access Program.
- **Substantive content:** The file provides definitions of ESP, psychokinesis, remote viewing, and related topics.
- **Locator:** `CIA-RDP96-00789R003300210001-2`.
- **Release:** Approved for release August 8, 2000.
- **What it supports:** The document, as described, formalizes program vocabulary and records restricted distribution around STAR GATE subject matter.
- **What it does not establish here:** No author, creation date, experiment, result, target, operational use, or UFO-related content is supplied in this inventory description.

### SRI Standard Remote-Viewing Protocol (1978 revision)
- **Authors and title:** Harold E. Puthoff and Russell Targ, "Standard Remote-Viewing Protocol: Local Targets."
- **Date and version:** November 1978, revised.
- **Origin and length:** SRI International; sixteen pages.
- **Substantive content:** The document specifies procedures for remote-viewing tests with local targets.
- **Program relationship:** Puthoff and Targ are described as the people who created SRI's remote-viewing program, which later developed into government-funded projects including GRILL FLAME and STAR GATE.
- **Funding institutions:** CIA, DIA, and the Army are named as funders across the projects' multi-decade history.
- **Locator:** `CIA-RDP96-00788R001300050001-3`.
- **Release:** Approved for release August 7, 2000.
- **What it supports:** The record supplies a procedural layer absent from the three administrative/program documents: remote viewing was not only discussed but given a written testing protocol by SRI researchers.
- **What it does not establish here:** A protocol documents intended method, not successful performance; the inventory gives no outcomes, controls, replications, or external evaluation.

## Concepts
- **Declassified record** — a formerly restricted government document later approved for public release. Declassification confirms release status, not the truth of every statement within the document.
- **Representative sample** — a small set chosen to illustrate a much larger archive. Here, four records stand for wider domestic and foreign collections but cannot inventory them comprehensively.
- **CIA locator number** — the alphanumeric identifier printed for each file, enabling retrieval from an archive despite anonymous or redacted authorship.
- **Original classification** — the security level applied when a record was created or controlled, such as SECRET; distinct from the later release date.
- **Approved for release** — the archival status and date under which a document became publicly accessible; not the date of its research activity.
- **Psi** — the umbrella term in this unit for anomalous capacities studied through ESP, psychokinesis, and remote viewing.
- **ESP** — extrasensory perception, one of the topics defined or briefed in the files.
- **Psychokinesis** — anomalous influence on physical systems, named among STAR GATE's definitions.
- **Remote viewing** — the targeted information-gathering practice the agencies sought to assess and develop as an intelligence capability.
- **SRI** — the research institution collaborating with OTS and associated with Pat Price, Ingo Swann, Puthoff, Targ, and the standard protocol.
- **OTS** — the CIA Office of Technical Services, which attempted to develop a remote-viewing intelligence capability and brief congressional oversight.
- **GRILL FLAME** — a named DIA remote-viewing project represented here by a 1981 Q&A memo involving assessment, budgeting, and communication guidance.
- **STAR GATE** — a later named program represented by a restricted definitions document and described as one of the projects into which SRI's work evolved.
- **INSCOM** — an organization referenced in the GRILL FLAME memo; this unit does not expand the acronym.
- **NoForn** — expanded by the authors as "No Foreigners," part of the STAR GATE distribution marking.
- **Limdis** — expanded by the authors as "Limited Distribution," also part of STAR GATE's marking.
- **Special Access Program** — the status the authors infer from the STAR GATE classification string.
- **Standard protocol** — a written set of test procedures intended to make local-target remote-viewing trials consistent; it is methodological evidence, not a result.
- **Bureaucratic embarrassment** — OTS concern that ESP research could be politically or reputationally damaging during Watergate-related investigation.
- **Document provenance** — the combination of originating organization, authorship or anonymity, date, classification, locator, release date, and described content that allows a reader to place a record without overreading it.

## Important source groups
- **Oversight and briefing:** The 1977 OTS memorandum records how remote-viewing work was summarized for a House committee and framed under political scrutiny.
- **Program administration and budgeting:** The 1981 GRILL FLAME memo shows a question-and-answer administrative form, interagency references, financial discussion, and handwritten guidance.
- **Program vocabulary and access control:** The STAR GATE file defines ESP, psychokinesis, and remote viewing under restricted classification/distribution markings.
- **Experimental method:** Puthoff and Targ's SRI document supplies the actual local-target testing protocol.
- **Named participants:** Pat Price and Ingo Swann appear in the OTS summary; Puthoff and Targ author the protocol; O'Keefe appears only in a handwritten communication-guidance quotation.
- **Institutions:** CIA/OTS, DIA, Army, SRI, INSCOM, and a House oversight committee form the domestic network visible in the four examples.
- **International archive claim:** Russian, Chinese, and Japanese files are mentioned in the preface but none is individually inventoried in this unit.

## Provenance cautions
- Anonymous authorship can reflect missing bylines or redaction; it does not mean the agency itself is necessarily the author of every sentence.
- CIA locator numbers are archival handles even for records originating with DIA or SRI; archival custody should not be confused with original authorship.
- Original classification signals sensitivity, not evidentiary accuracy or paranormal validity.
- A government program's existence proves institutional interest and expenditure, not that the studied capacity was effective.
- A definitions document proves formal vocabulary existed, not that the defined phenomena were demonstrated.
- A protocol proves procedures were specified, not that trials succeeded.
- The handwritten phrase "the Phenomenon" should not be silently equated with this book's capitalized UFO/paranormal umbrella without more document context.
- The authors' phrase "eventually morphed into" describes continuity from SRI work to named projects at a high level; this four-item list does not provide a complete administrative lineage.

## Limitations
- Only four US-linked documents are itemized despite the stated international and multi-agency archive.
- No complete sets, website addresses, search instructions, or archive names are printed beyond locator numbers.
- The unit summarizes rather than reproduces the files, so all descriptions remain the book's characterization of the documents.
- Experimental data, scoring, controls, outcomes, and critical assessments are absent.
- The records concern Psi and remote viewing; using them as proof of UFO or alien claims would exceed what this unit says.
- Creation and release chronology must be kept separate: 1977, 1978, 1981, and undated source records were released in 2000 or 2005.

## Thread
The Government Documents unit supplies the narrowest and most concrete answer to the Appendix's claim that anomalous research became fragmented across intelligence and military bureaucracies. Its four records move through oversight, program management, controlled vocabulary, and experimental procedure, showing multiple institutions and project names rather than one continuous public science program. As the final unit of *Man*, it does not prove the book's larger synthesis; it gives readers retrievable archival footholds and demonstrates why provenance, locator numbers, classifications, and program lineage matter when evaluating claims about state involvement.
