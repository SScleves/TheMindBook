# Bibliography

## In one paragraph
This back-matter unit is an alphabetical bibliography rather than a continuation of the book's argument. Its source architecture mixes scholarly articles, academic and trade books, first-person contact and abduction accounts, specialist UFO and parapsychology periodicals, journalism, web publications, literary works, interviews, and institutional reports. The largest thematic bridges connect consciousness and neuroscience with cybernetics and AI; genetics with directed panspermia and Biological SETI; UFO sightings with abduction and contact testimony; and Psi research with intelligence history, witchcraft, religion, synchronicity, and philosophy of science. The list supplies authors, titles, venues, dates, editions, page ranges, URLs, and occasional access dates, but it does not annotate individual evidentiary roles or certify that one source validates another. Its value is therefore architectural: it shows the kinds of records from which *Man* was assembled and lets a reader distinguish peer-reviewed research, historical scholarship, testimony, journalism, specialist literature, and imaginative framing before following a citation back to the chapter where it was used.

## Source architecture
- **Alphabetical organization:** Entries run by author or institutional byline from George Adamski through Larry R. Vandervert. Repeated works by the same author are carried under an em-dash convention, as with John B. Alexander, Loren Eiseley, Mircea Eliade, John E. Mack, Andrija Puharich, and Jacques Vallee.
- **No internal subject headings:** Unlike the following Government Documents unit, this bibliography does not formally separate science, Ufology, philosophy, history, journalism, literature, or first-person testimony. The categories below are analytical aids derived only from titles, venues, and publication metadata.
- **Mixed evidentiary levels:** Peer-reviewed neuroscience and genetics articles appear beside witness narratives, specialist magazines, newspapers, web pages, philosophy, folklore history, and fiction. Co-presence in the list establishes use by the book, not equal reliability or mutual confirmation.
- **Primary and near-primary material:** First-person or participant works include Adamski's *Behind the Flying Saucer Mystery*, Morehouse's *Psychic Warrior*, Puharich's *URI*, Strieber's *Communion*, Swann's *Penetration*, and institutional or technical reports by Driscoll and Puthoff.
- **Secondary synthesis:** Works by Bryan, Bullard, Kean, Kelleher, Kripal, Mack, Swords and Powell, Vallee, and others collect, interpret, or historicize UFO, contact, abduction, paranormal, and government material.
- **Scholarly venues:** The list includes *Nature Neuroscience*, *Nature Reviews/Neuroscience*, *Frontiers in Psychology*, *PLOS Computational Biology*, *Physics of Life Reviews*, *Icarus*, *JBIS*, *Seminars in the Neurosciences*, *New Ideas in Psychology*, *European Journal of Parapsychology*, and *International Journal of Astrobiology*.
- **Academic-press books:** Oxford, University of Chicago, Princeton, Indiana, University of Michigan, University Press of Kansas, University of California, New York University, and University of Nebraska presses mark a substantial university-press layer.
- **Trade and specialist presses:** Penguin, Scribner, Ballantine, Simon & Schuster, Bantam, Dell, Avon, St. Martin's, Anomalist, Prometheus, Ibis, Trine Day, and others carry popular science, testimony, investigation, and specialist interpretation.
- **News and web layer:** BBC News, Reuters, *Hindustan Times*, *The Atlantic*, BuzzFeed, CNN iReport, a laboratory website, and online interviews supply contemporary reports and accessible summaries. Access dates are given for only some web entries.
- **Specialist field publications:** *MUFON Journal*, *Flying Saucer Review*, *UFO Afrinews*, *Alien Encounters*, *CenterPiece Magazine*, *ASPR Newsletter*, and *Intelligencer* preserve material from UFO, parapsychology, and intelligence-study communities.
- **Literary and cultural texts:** Arthur Machen's "The Great God Pan," Dean Koontz's *One Door Away from Heaven*, Cotton Mather's *Wonders of the Invisible World*, and Konstantin Stanislavski's *An Actor Prepares* are not presented as scientific evidence; their presence reflects the book's use of imaginative, historical, and performative language alongside research.
- **Edition history:** Several entries preserve original and later edition years, including Adamski (1967, 1974), Eiseley (1970, 1998), Eliade (1956, 1978), Foucault (1965, 1988), and Vallee's *Forbidden Science 2* (2008, 2017). Those paired dates indicate the edition cited may not be the first publication.

## Important source groups

### UFO sightings, government history, and contact
- **George Adamski, *Behind the Flying Saucer Mystery*** — an early contactee work used in the book's discussion of Nordic-type beings; the bibliography records New York, Warner Books, and 1967/1974 editions.
- **John B. Alexander, *UFOs: Myths, Conspiracies, and Realities* and *Reality Denied*** — two books spanning explicit UFO interpretation and first-person anomalous experience.
- **Thomas E. Bullard, *The Myth and Mystery of UFOs*** — a University Press of Kansas source whose title places myth and unexplained reports in one frame.
- **Leslie Kean, *UFOs: Generals, Pilots, and Government Officials Go on the Record*** — a trade-book source organized, by its title, around credentialed witnesses and officials.
- **Donald E. Keyhoe, *Aliens from Space*** and **Michael Swords and Robert Powell, *UFOs and Government*** — historical/institutional UFO sources representing both a 1973 popular account and a 2012 historical inquiry.
- **Jacques Vallee, the three *Forbidden Science* journal volumes** — dated journals covering 1957–1969 and later periods, central to the bibliography's long-duration investigator perspective.
- **Robert Hastings, "UFOs and Nukes"** and the **1966 *Minot Daily News* article** — specialist-journal and local-news sources supporting the nuclear-site thread.
- **H. E. Puthoff et al., "Inflation-Theory Implications for Extraterrestrial Visitation"** — a 2005 *JBIS* article that explicitly places visitation questions in a technical/theoretical venue.

### Alien abduction and experiencer testimony
- **John G. Fuller, *The Interrupted Journey*** — a 1966 account centered, in its subtitle, on two missing hours aboard a flying saucer.
- **C. D. B. Bryan, *Close Encounters of the Fourth Kind*** — a reporter's notebook on alien abduction, UFOs, and an MIT conference, used by the book for Gray descriptions.
- **David M. Jacobs, *Secret Life*** and **John E. Mack, *Abduction*** — two 1990s collections explicitly framed as firsthand human accounts.
- **Whitley Strieber, *Communion***; **Strieber and Jeffrey Kripal, *The Super Natural***; and **Whitley and Anne Strieber, *The Afterlife Revolution*** — first-person and collaborative works that bridge contact, unexplained experience, religion/philosophy, and reports of the dead.
- **John E. Mack and Dominique Callimanopulos, "The Ariel School Sighting"** and **Cynthia Hind, "Ariel School Report, Case No. 96"** — two 1995 periodical sources on the Zimbabwe school case, one in *CenterPiece Magazine* and one in *UFO Afrinews*.
- **Ahmad Jamaluddin's three articles** — reports on Malaysian mini-entities, humanoid encounters, and Southeast Asian encounters published across *MUFON Journal*, *Flying Saucer Review*, and *Alien Encounters*.
- **Roshni Nair, Neetup, and Reuters** — web/news sources for Indian and Malaysian reports; these entries show that some regional material reaches the book through journalism or personal online reporting rather than formal case monographs.

### Consciousness, neuroscience, and mind-brain theory
- **David Chalmers, *The Conscious Mind***; **Francis Crick, *The Astonishing Hypothesis***; and **Stanislas Dehaene, *Consciousness and the Brain*** — three book-length scientific/philosophical treatments explicitly centered on consciousness.
- **Francis Crick and Christof Koch, "Towards a Neurobiological Theory of Consciousness"** — a 1990 article in *Seminars in the Neurosciences*.
- **Stuart Hameroff and Roger Penrose, "Consciousness in the Universe"** — a 2014 review of Orch OR in *Physics of Life Reviews*, paired with Hameroff's 2015 SAND conference talk.
- **Masafumi Oizumi et al., "Integrated Information Theory 3.0"** and **Giulio Tononi et al., "Integrated Information Theory"** — 2014 and 2016 articles moving from phenomenology and mechanism to physical substrate.
- **Karl H. Pribram, *Brain and Perception*** — a 1991 work whose subtitle names holonomy and figural-processing structure.
- **Suzanne Corkin on patient H.M.** and **Roger Marek et al. on hippocampal/prefrontal fear relapse** — case- and circuit-level neuroscience used within the wider brain discussion.
- **Richard D. Emes et al.** and **J. Nithianantharajah et al.** — *Nature Neuroscience* articles on synapse-proteome complexity and vertebrate cognitive complexity.
- **Todd Feinberg and John Mallatt** and **John K. Grandy** — sources explicitly addressing the evolutionary/genetic origins or phases of consciousness.
- **Larry Vandervert, "Chaos Theory and the Evolution of Consciousness and Mind"** — a 1995 *New Ideas in Psychology* article whose subtitle proposes a thermodynamic-holographic mind-body resolution.

### Cybernetics, cyborgs, AI, and technological extension
- **Manfred Clynes and Nathan Kline, "Cyborgs and Space" (1960)** and **Kline and Clynes, "Drugs, Space, and Cybernetics: Evolution to Cyborgs" (1961)** — the earliest dated cyborg/space sources in the list.
- **Robert W. Driscoll, "Engineering Man for Space: The Cyborg Study" (1963)** — a final report identified by report number NASw-512 and a United Aircraft corporate research center.
- **Garet Garrett, *Ouroboros: Or the Mechanical Extension of Mankind* (1926)** and **Marshall McLuhan, *Understanding Media: The Extensions of Man* (1964)** — earlier and mid-century works framing technology as human extension.
- **Arthur Koestler, *The Ghost in the Machine*** — a 1967 conceptual source linking mind and mechanism in its title.
- **Ian Goodfellow et al., "Generative Adversarial Networks" (2014)** — an arXiv paper anchoring the AI discussion in a named technical architecture.
- **Mark Humphries, "Your Cortex Contains 17 Billion Computers" (2018)**; **Bryor Snefjella, "This Is the A-Bomb Moment for Computer Science" (2018)**; and **Danny Paez's Ray Kurzweil interview (2018)** — contemporary web and interview sources framing brains, AI, and DNA as computation or reprogrammable software.

### Genetics, evolution, and directed panspermia
- **Francis Crick and Leslie Orgel, "Directed Panspermia"** — an *Icarus* article with volume, issue, and pages but no publication year printed in this bibliography entry.
- **Vladimir I. shCherbak and Maxim A. Makukov, "The 'WOW Signal' of the Terrestrial Genetic Code"** — an *Icarus* article on the code patterns discussed at length in the main text.
- **Robert Temple, "The Prehistory of Panspermia: Astrophysical or Metaphysical?"** — an *International Journal of Astrobiology* article connecting the topic's scientific and historical/metaphysical framing at the title level.
- **Richard Dawkins, *The Selfish Gene*** — a 1976 evolution/genetics source from Oxford University Press.
- The synapse-evolution and consciousness-evolution papers listed above form a bridge from genetic and neural development to the book's consciousness argument; the bibliography itself does not state that they support alien intervention.

### Psi, remote viewing, and skeptical analysis
- **ASPR Newsletter, "Reception for Ingo Swann" (1972)** and **Ingo Swann, *Penetration* (1998)** — an institutional newsletter item and Swann's own later book.
- **Andrija Puharich, *Beyond Telepathy* and *URI*** — two participant-authored books on telepathy and Uri Geller-era research.
- **H. E. Puthoff, "CIA-Initiated Remote Viewing at Stanford Research Institute"** — a 2001 article in an intelligence-studies journal, explicitly joining an intelligence agency, remote viewing, and SRI.
- **David Morehouse, *Psychic Warrior*** and **Jim Schnabel, *Remote Viewers*** — a first-person memoir and a secondary history of American psychic intelligence work.
- **Richard Bentall, "Research into Psychotic Symptoms"** and **David Marks and Richard Kammann, *The Psychology of the Psychic*** — skeptical/psychological sources included alongside proponent and participant accounts.
- This mixed group shows that the authors consulted advocacy, participant testimony, institutional history, and skeptical psychology; the bibliography does not adjudicate their disagreements.

## Provenance and evidentiary roles
- A **journal citation** gives a venue, volume or issue, and often pages or DOI, but the bibliography does not state whether the main text relies on data, review, theory, or a single quoted phrase from that article.
- A **university-press imprint** identifies publication context but does not by itself verify every claim in the work.
- A **first-person account** provides testimony about an experiencer's own reported events; it does not independently establish mechanism or external cause.
- A **specialist UFO or parapsychology periodical** preserves field reports and investigator material that may not appear in mainstream venues; the list supplies no independent quality assessment.
- A **news report** can establish that a claim or event was publicly reported at a particular time, but the bibliography alone does not distinguish original investigation from repetition of another source.
- A **technical or institutional report** such as Driscoll's cyborg study or Puthoff's SRI history supplies organizational provenance; the separate Government Documents unit provides stronger locator-level examples.
- A **literary work** can furnish metaphor, epigraph, or cultural precedent without functioning as empirical evidence. The bibliography's inclusion of Machen, Koontz, Mather, and Stanislavski makes this role visible.
- A **later edition or reprint date** helps identify the text available to the authors but should not be mistaken for the original date of the idea.

## Independent context carried by the list
- The bibliography's dated sequence places mechanical-extension language in 1926, cyborg-and-space work in 1960–63, Jung's UFO book in 1959, the first listed abduction book in 1966, and modern AI/consciousness/genetics articles in the 1990s–2010s. This is chronology visible in the citations, not a claim about priority beyond the list.
- The same person can occupy different roles: Francis Crick appears in a consciousness book, a neurobiological article with Christof Koch, and directed-panspermia work with Leslie Orgel; John Mack appears as abduction author, interview subject, magazine essayist, and Ariel coauthor.
- Regional encounter material has distinct provenance: Indian cases come through a personal CNN iReport and *Hindustan Times*; Malaysian reports through Jamaluddin, Reuters, and specialist magazines; Zimbabwe through Mack/Callimanopulos and Cynthia Hind.
- The consciousness section relies much more heavily on named scholarly journals than the encounter section, which relies more heavily on witness books, field periodicals, journalism, and investigator syntheses.
- The list includes both supportive and skeptical sources: Bentall, Marks and Kammann, Sagan, and Bullard sit alongside contactees, abductees, remote viewers, and UFO investigators. Presence does not show how fully each counterposition was represented in the prose.
- Several entries are incomplete by modern bibliographic standards: some journal articles lack a year, some web items lack access dates, some entries use "et al." without full author inventories, and URLs are printed without persistent identifiers.
- The Harris URL is printed with `didg` in its path, while the Appendix footnote prints `dig`; the note records this as an internal bibliographic inconsistency, not a correction based on outside checking.
- Ahmad Jamaluddin's name in the bibliography differs from "Jamaludin" in the chapter prose and footnotes. The bibliography note preserves the form printed in this unit rather than deciding which spelling is externally correct.
- Joshua Trachtenburg's surname is likewise preserved as printed here, even though another chapter may render it differently.

## Limitations
- The bibliography contains almost no annotation, so subject categories and evidentiary roles must be inferred conservatively from titles, venues, and how cited works appear in the main text.
- It does not map entries to chapter, page, or footnote numbers.
- It does not distinguish peer review, editorial review, self-report, advocacy, or republication in a machine-readable way.
- It does not establish that a cited work supports the authors' interpretation, only that the work belongs to the source base they list.
- It is not the complete documentary architecture by itself: the next unit separately identifies four representative declassified government documents and explicitly says they stand for a much larger archive.

## Thread
The Bibliography makes visible the method used throughout *Man*: assemble conventional science, philosophy, intellectual history, intelligence-related reporting, encounter testimony, field literature, and imaginative texts in one research environment, then use the main chapters to propose connections among them. As back matter, it does not advance those connections or answer objections. Its proper role is provenance control: a reader can identify whether a passage arose from peer-reviewed neuroscience, a contactee memoir, a local newspaper, an investigator's synthesis, a literary epigraph, or a specialist field report before deciding how much weight to give it.
