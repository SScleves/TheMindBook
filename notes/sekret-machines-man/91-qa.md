# Sekret Machines Book 2: Man — Q&A

*Unit-pointer legend: pointers use the note unit numbers from each file's frontmatter, not always the book's printed chapter numbers. Unit 0 = Prologue. Section One (Genetics) = intro at unit 1 plus printed Ch. 1–5 at units 2–6. Section Two (Consciousness) = intro at unit 7 plus printed Ch. 6–10 at units 8–12. Section Three (Human-Machine Symbiosis) = intro at unit 13 plus printed Ch. 11–15 at units 14–18. Unit 19 = Appendix; units 20–21 = back matter. All answers state what Tom DeLonge & Peter Levenda claim in* Sekret Machines: Man *(2018); none are endorsed as fact.*

## Orientation and Method

**Q: Where does *Man* sit in the trilogy, and what is its brief?**
A: It is the middle volume and takes the *scientific* half of Harry Reid's December 2017 formula — "this is about science and national security" — reserving national security for *War*. Where *Gods* argued that religion is the ancient record of contact, *Man* asks what the Phenomenon's reported biological, neurological, and technological effects imply about the Visitors and about what humanity is becoming. (Unit 0)

**Q: What is the book's organizing reversal?**
A: To stop assuming that biological aliens pilot advanced machines and ask instead whether the apparent *pilots are themselves machines* — and whether humans, as gene-driven "survival machines" (Dawkins) or divinely "manufactured" creatures (Genesis), are already a kind of machine too. Its sharpened technology question is not "more advanced?" but "advanced by how much?" — and "what does that say about us?" (Units 0, 1)

**Q: What are the four research lines and the three sections?**
A: The Prologue names four lines — genetics, consciousness, brain-computer interfaces, and telepathy. The body runs three sections, each a scientific lens on the same Phenomenon: Section One (Genetics), Section Two (Consciousness), and Section Three (Human-Machine Symbiosis), with an Appendix ("Galileo's Revenge") and back matter. (Units 0, 1, 7, 13)

**Q: What is the volume's most important reading discipline?**
A: Keeping *evidence classes distinct* — refusing to let an accumulation of examples make different kinds of claim look equivalent. The authors repeatedly separate a reported experience/trauma from its interpretation, cited theory from their own extrapolation, and documented human programs from alien speculation, and they mark the boundary explicitly: experts supply the data, the authors and advisers supply the arguments, "the conclusions will be exclusively your own." (Units 0, 2, 11)

## Section One — Genetics

**Q: Why does the book take abduction testimony seriously at all?**
A: On two grounds stated in the Prologue and developed in "The Succubus in the Saucer": Garry Nolan's reported finding of observable, biologically traceable effects in contactees, and John Mack's 1994 observation of PTSD in ~200 otherwise healthy, adjusted, sometimes skeptical people. The authors treat these as evidence that *something* real occurred and left trauma — not as proof of literal extraterrestrial abduction. (Units 0, 2)

**Q: What is Hynek's "cardinal mistake," and why is it the governing rule?**
A: Substituting an *interpretation* of the Phenomenon for the Phenomenon itself. It licenses the book's core distinction between a real experience/trauma and its culturally conditioned imagery: the Hills' or Strieber's extraterrestrial framing can be wrong or media-shaped while the underlying event and its effects remain real. (Unit 2)

**Q: What are the contact "biomarkers," and how strong is the claim?**
A: At Rendlesham (1980) and Cash-Landrum (1980), witnesses reported close proximity to luminous craft followed by injury; Kit Green and Garry Nolan interpreted John Burroughs's heart damage and similar military cases through broad-band non-ionizing electromagnetic exposure, and Puthoff proposed a "blue-shifting" mechanism. The authors call bodily traces the "Man variable" — potentially more useful than videos — while keeping the *cause* attributed to the cited researchers rather than established. DOD/VA line-of-duty recognition for Burroughs is treated as administrative acknowledgment of injury, not of extraterrestrial cause. (Unit 2)

**Q: Are the Visitors machines?**
A: The authors *pose* it, repeatedly, without asserting it. Reported absences — no alien children, no visible aging, no ordinary empathy, no eating or excreting — are read as *consistent with* created rather than born beings ("closer to machines than organisms"). The golem, the jointless Gray, and the intelligence-vs-consciousness distinction develop the hypothesis; the Atacama skeleton (found to be a deformed human child) is used to show the authors preferring evidence over the wished-for alien. (Units 1, 4, 14, 17)

**Q: What is the "genocide" hypothesis, and how do the authors handle it?**
A: An unnamed informant asked them to consider whether some genocides served an ulterior genetic function — eliminating populations carrying genes resistant to alien hybridization or control. The authors state it, immediately liken it to eugenics and Nazi race science, provide *no* confirming case, use sickle cell and Tay-Sachs only as analogies for population-linked genetics, and call the ensuing conclusions speculative. They deliberately open "Gods, Genes, and Genocide" with this, their weakest and most dangerous idea, precisely to mark it as such. (Unit 5)

**Q: What is "directed panspermia," and does the book claim it's true?**
A: Crick and Orgel's proposal that life's genetic code was deliberately seeded on Earth by an organic-precursor intelligence, supported (as reported) by the molybdenum argument — that the code depends on an element rare on Earth's surface. The authors present it as a *published scientific proposal* and "a platform for research," not a demonstrated fact, and stress that ordinary Darwinian evolution proceeds *after* any code insertion. (Unit 5)

**Q: What is "Biological SETI" / the "Kazakh configuration"?**
A: shCherbak and Makukov's 2013 claim (the *Icarus* "WOW! signal of the terrestrial genetic code") that positional-decimal patterns, a zero-like start/stop function, symmetries, and nucleon sums are statistically more consistent with deliberate construction than with random evolution — the search for intelligence relocated into genetics. Reported at length, abbreviated as mathematically dense, and explicitly not endorsed as truth. (Units 5, 6)

**Q: What is the "sixty-four" argument in "The Chinese Configuration"?**
A: That four genetic bases in ordered groups of three produce 64 codons, while the *I Jing* pairs binary lines into 64 hexagrams, Ifa builds 256 odu (64×4), and Dee's angelic tablets yield 64-cell elemental structures and a 20-cell "Tablet of Union." The authors restrict the firm claim to the numerical *fit*, call the specific base-to-line assignments arbitrary, and label the twenty-cell/twenty-amino-acid correspondence "an outrageous (and unproven) claim." (Unit 6)

## Section Two — Consciousness

**Q: Does the book say DNA is the source of consciousness?**
A: It reaches that as an *inference*, not a proof. "The Structure of the Human Brain" ends: because DNA builds the neural system and neurotransmitter precursors, "DNA must be where consciousness comes from." The notes are explicit that the cited neuroscience establishes genetic *construction*, not that DNA experiences or generates subjective awareness — the final step is the authors' argument. The radio-receiver analogy is kept alongside precisely to hold open a "consciousness comes from elsewhere" alternative. (Units 8, 10)

**Q: What is the "hard problem," and how does it bear on the Visitors?**
A: The unresolved question of why physical processing has a felt, subjective dimension (*qualia*) at all. The authors argue qualia are *situated* — an alien on a different world would have different qualia — and that measuring a stimulus (a wavelength labeled "red") cannot transfer what it is *like*. This is why they say human and alien minds might only communicate through a shared, non-sensory channel (a universal field the brain "tunes" to). (Unit 9)

**Q: What are the two schools of consciousness the book stages?**
A: The emergent/computational school (Kurzweil, strong AI, the Singularity, and a Cambrian evolutionary origin in the lamprey/Pikaia), which expects sufficiently powerful computers to become conscious; and the quantum school (Penrose and Hameroff's Orch OR), which locates conscious moments in neuronal microtubules and treats consciousness as intrinsic to the universe. The authors don't declare a winner; they use quantum biology (photosynthesis, Rieper's DNA entanglement) as the bridge that makes the quantum account "discussable." (Unit 11)

**Q: Where does the book cross from cited science into speculation, and does it flag it?**
A: In unit 11, after Orch OR, into quantum alien telepathy, persistent "tagging," and different alien reproduction; in unit 12, from Dehaene's documented subliminal/TMS results into a conditional contact model (an external nudge amplified below awareness into a full encounter). The authors flag both: unit 11 carries an explicit *false-equivalence warning* (calling entanglement and astral projection both "spooky" joins two labels, not two mechanisms), and unit 12 preserves Dehaene's "correlation not causation." (Units 11, 12)

**Q: What is the "Joker in the deck"?**
A: The closing image of "Utter Chaos": the Gray as an anomalous element below ordinary conscious resolution — a hidden extra card that disrupts an otherwise coherent perceptual and psychological system, unnoticed during fast "shuffling." The authors are explicit it is a *metaphor* for their interpretation, not a diagnosis by Mack, a result of Dehaene's experiments, or evidence that a Gray literally exists outside awareness. (Unit 12)

## Section Three — Human-Machine Symbiosis

**Q: What is the section's governing distinction?**
A: Intelligence is not consciousness. "Machines can be intelligent, but that is not the same thing as conscious." GANs, deepfakes, and integrated information theory show high-performing intelligence and convincing simulation without demonstrated subjective awareness; the distinction is then applied to Gray anatomy (a mute, earless, non-eating being would integrate far less information) and to the machine-pilot hypothesis. (Unit 14)

**Q: What is the "original" cyborg, and why does the book recover it?**
A: Not a robot, but — in Clynes and Kline's 1960 sense — an organic human whose artificial components, drugs, and autonomic controls form an integrated "machine-derived unconscious" for surviving space. "I, Cyborg" grounds human-machine integration in Cold War institutions (Licklider's intelligence amplification, osmotic pumps, hypnosis/vestibular experiments) and ends on a deliberately unresolved "Chicken... or egg?" — whether contact reports inspired the science, the science shaped the reports, or both developed independently. (Unit 15)

**Q: What does the remote-viewing history actually establish?**
A: That prestigious societies, laboratories, physicists, intelligence agencies, and the military repeatedly investigated, funded, defined, and operationally deployed Psi (SPR, Rhine, SRI, the SCANATE→STAR GATE lineage) — while a 1974 CIA observer concluded "there exists at present no adequate theory of paranormal perception." It documents institutional *interest and expenditure*, not that Psi works. Swann's dramatic results are reported claims; the leaps to machine Psi, telepathic time travel, and "one shared science" of UFO and abductee motion are the authors' conditional extrapolations. (Units 16, 21)

**Q: What does the cross-cultural "Gray Anatomy" argue?**
A: That North American Gray/Nordic/Reptilian categories are not universal — India has *pariyaan* (fairies), Malaysia has three-to-six-inch mini-entities reported mostly by schoolchildren, the Philippines has six-foot beings — yet reported entities remain *recognizably humanoid* across huge variation, and witnesses rarely draw on the far wider library of film/comic forms. The authors infer that this *constrained* recurrence favors a common real experience over free invention — while explicitly noting possible cultural contamination (the 1992 Maharashtra case) and Ariel School's leading-question criticism. (Unit 17)

**Q: What is the "symbiosis" thesis?**
A: The claim closing "Gray Anatomy" and "House Guests" that humans and the alien possess different powers, limitations, and needs and therefore depend on each other — the authors' answer to "why haven't they invaded?" being "because they can't." The notes flag that this is asserted more strongly than the material supplies, and that Section Three carries an unresolved tension: the authors call the alien *real* while saying nearly every image of it is socially constructed, anthropocentric projection. (Units 13, 17, 18)

## Reading Critically

**Q: Where is the reader most likely to lose track of what kind of claim is being made?**
A: At the transitions the CONTINUE-HERE brief singles out. Watch: reported *trauma* → claimed *biomarkers* (unit 2); DNA/RNA *primer* → directed-panspermia *inference* (unit 5); Crick & Koch's *neural correlates* → "DNA must be where consciousness comes from" (unit 10); *cited* Orch OR → *speculative* quantum alien telepathy (unit 11); Dehaene's documented *subliminal/TMS* → the *conditional contact model* (unit 12). The authors mark most seams; a critical reader should re-mark them and never let a genetics, consciousness, brain, anomalous-experience, or symbiosis example silently upgrade a neighboring one. (Units 2, 5, 10, 11, 12)

**Q: What is the Appendix's argument?**
A: "Galileo's Revenge" turns the content problem into an *authority* problem: scientific orthodoxy, hardened since Galileo's punishment, can now dismiss anomalous testimony as heresy, while a funding-shaped, fragmented institutional structure loses the data without any central conspiracy. It criticizes Harris's *Atheist Manifesto* and Sagan's *Demon-Haunted World* for protecting an accepted system, and offers the Byelokoroviche and Minot nuclear-site cases as serious, instrument-linked anomalies — presented not as proof of alien intervention but as reasons inquiry should not be foreclosed. (Unit 19)

**Q: How reliable are the sources, by the book's own account?**
A: Deliberately mixed, and the back matter says so. Peer-reviewed neuroscience and genetics sit beside witness books, field periodicals, journalism, philosophy, and literature; co-presence establishes *use*, not equal reliability or mutual confirmation. The four Government Documents are explicit that a program's existence proves institutional interest, a definitions file proves vocabulary, and a protocol proves procedure — none proves efficacy, and none of the four concerns UFOs or aliens. (Units 20, 21)

**Q: Are the comparisons to other shelf books (simulation, informational reality, machine consciousness) the authors' own?**
A: No. Every such link in the source notes is explicitly the knowledge base's reader-side resonance, *not* a comparison DeLonge & Levenda draw. (Units 1, 8, 11)

**Q: Does *Man* answer what the Phenomenon is?**
A: No. It is the *scientific* stage: it brings genetics, consciousness studies, and human-machine technology to bear, models the discipline of keeping evidence classes distinct, leaves the "organic cyborg" versus "collective breakdown" question open, and defers the national-security synthesis — and the crucial separation of secret human technology from genuine historical anomaly — to *Sekret Machines: War*. (Units 17, 18, 19)
