# The Structure of the Human Brain

## In one paragraph
DeLonge and Levenda provide the anatomical primer they consider necessary before comparing human consciousness with the biology of alleged extraterrestrial biological entities. They move from the central and peripheral nervous systems through the cerebrum, lobes, hemispheres, thalamus, glands, amygdala, cerebellum, brain stem, and spinal cord, repeatedly noting that correlations between structures, sensory processing, injury, sleep, emotion, and behavior still do not explain how consciousness is produced. The chapter then makes neurons the focus: roughly 17 billion in the cortex and as many as 30 billion in the full brain, communicating electrochemically through dendrites, axons, synapses, neurotransmitters, ions, and myelin. A 2007 *Lancet* case of a functioning forty-four-year-old man whose brain had reportedly contracted to 50–75 percent of normal is used to demonstrate functional reassignment and to challenge simple anatomical localization, while Mark Humphries's 2018 account of single neurons as multilayered nonlinear networks intensifies the brain's computational complexity. The authors finally infer that because DNA directs construction of neurons and supplies precursors for neurotransmitters, DNA must contain the commands underlying the system and therefore may be the source of consciousness; this is their argumentative conclusion, not a finding established by the anatomy or cited cases.

## Core claims

### Reported anatomy, functions, and observations
- Most scientists, according to the chapter, consider consciousness an artifact of the brain and nervous system because sensory inputs begin in the nervous system, arrive in the brain, and are joined there with memory management. The authors immediately qualify this as not universally accepted, partly because consciousness has no standard definition.
- Brain or nervous-system injury can alter mental abilities, emotion, and behavior. A physically induced loss of consciousness after a blow to the head differs from being called unconscious during deep sleep: one follows damage, the other is natural, and recovery differs.
- The cerebrospinal or central nervous system (CNS) consists of the brain and spinal cord. The peripheral nervous system (PNS) consists of nerve channels through the arms, legs, torso, and the rest of the body.
- The PNS includes the autonomic nervous system (ANS), which manages involuntary processes such as breathing, heart rate, and peristalsis, and the somatic nervous system (SNS), which controls voluntary movement.
- The ANS includes the sympathetic system, called "fight or flight"; the parasympathetic system, called "rest and digest"; and the enteric system, sometimes called the "second brain." The enteric system extends through the gastrointestinal tract from esophagus to anus and can operate independently of the other two.
- Although those systems are peripheral, CNS operations still regulate them. The thalamus's regulation of the ANS is the chapter's example.
- The cerebrum is the familiar walnut-like brain structure seen in television autopsies. It weighs about three pounds; its gray outer surface is the cortex or gray matter, while tissue a few millimeters below appears white.
- Cortical grooves and folds look random but are sufficiently consistent between brains to let researchers map areas and their functions. The named regions are the occipital lobe at the rear, frontal lobe behind the forehead, temporal lobe above the ear, and parietal lobe across the top beneath the scalp.
- The cerebrum has right and left hemispheres. The chapter gives the usual qualified account for right-handed people: the right hemisphere controls the body's left side and is associated with creativity, while the left controls the right side and is associated with logic and reason. The authors warn that the art/science analogy greatly simplifies the reality.
- The corpus callosum forms a bridge or wall between the hemispheres and enables cross-hemisphere communication.
- The thalamus, whose Greek name means "chamber," is described as regulating sleep, wakefulness, and therefore consciousness. It receives all sensory inputs except smell and has two halves.
- The pineal gland lies between the thalamus's halves. Modern mystics associate it with the third eye, while Rene Descartes associated it with vision and the soul.
- The hypothalamus, literally "under the thalamus," regulates hunger, thirst, fatigue, and body temperature. The pituitary gland at its bottom secretes hormones governing blood pressure, metabolism, sex organs, pregnancy, childbirth, and other functions.
- The paired almond-shaped amygdalae lie in the temporal lobes and are associated with emotion. The chapter describes one as responsible for fear and the other as handling positive and negative emotion; more generally, the amygdala processes perceived threats and reward/punishment responses, making fear a survival mechanism.
- During sleep, the amygdala remains active while many other brain centers become quiet. The authors describe the emotional self as active while the rational self and waking inhibitions are reduced, invoking Freud's phrase that dreams are the "royal road to the unconscious."
- The cerebellum, or "little cerebrum," has folds, grooves, and a cortex and chiefly governs fine motor control. Cerebellar damage can produce tremors and shaking hands.
- The brain stem continues the spinal cord. Sensory information passes upward through it to the brain, and brain commands travel downward through the spinal cord to the body. The cerebrum and cerebellum are said to have evolved from the brain stem and spinal cord.
- The spinal cord handles external sensory inputs such as cold, heat, smell, touch, taste, and sound, as well as internal sensations such as stomach pain and phantom-limb experience.
- The medulla, glossed as "middle," extends from the brain stem, and the pons, meaning "bridge," extends from the medulla. The chapter describes the pineal and pituitary glands as appendages, in a sense, of the pons.
- Historical phrenology assigned qualities and capacities to mapped areas of the head and used the map for fortune-telling. Its particular divisions were naive, but the broad localization claim—that functions and capabilities can be associated with cortical sites—is said to have survived.
- The neuron is the brain's basic cell. The chapter gives approximately 17 billion neurons in the human cerebral cortex and as many as 30 billion in the brain overall.
- A neuron contains a DNA-bearing nucleus; cytoplasm containing RNA-bearing ribosomes and glucose-oxidizing mitochondria; a cell membrane; dendrites; and an axon.
- Dendrites, glossed as "little fingers," receive information and pass it through the neuron and along the axon. The axon communicates with another neuron's dendrites across a synapse, a word meaning "to clasp," by electrochemical impulse.
- About fifty neurotransmitters are said to occupy vesicles in the axon and cross the synapse to receptors on another neuron's dendrites. Neurotransmitters can excite or inhibit responses.
- Named neurotransmitters include adrenalin, dopamine, serotonin, endorphins, and unconventional endocannabinoids. Their effects include mood, energy, motor function, sleep and wakefulness, and body temperature.
- Some neurotransmitters have genetic precursors: dopamine is synthesized from tyrosine, and serotonin's precursor is tryptophan. Endocannabinoids bind cannabinoid receptors that also respond to cannabis and THC, a substance the chapter says directly affects consciousness.
- A message received by dendrites passes through the soma and down the axon, triggering neurotransmitter release through a chemical and electrical process involving positively and negatively charged ions across the membrane in pump-like action.
- Axons in white brain tissue are insulated by fatty myelin sheaths. Myelin keeps neighboring signals from contaminating each other and increases propagation speed; unmyelinated axons transmit more slowly.
- Myelin deficits are linked in the chapter to neurodegenerative disease, with multiple sclerosis named, and organophosphate pesticides are said to cause demyelination.
- Neural pathways make sensory processing possible. If neurons cease firing, there is no functioning brain and no discernible consciousness; yet large interconnected populations can shift computing work when damage requires it.
- Trauma, pollutants, degeneration, drugs, and exercise can alter the physical brain and nervous system. The chapter asks, but does not establish, whether an alteration of the physical platform necessarily alters consciousness itself.

### Cited cases and researcher reports
- Lionel Feuillet and colleagues reported in *The Lancet*, volume 370, p. 262, a forty-four-year-old married civil servant with two children whose brain had reportedly shrunk to 50–75 percent of normal size while he remained conscious and functional. The chapter's footnote says the report was referenced in *New Scientist* on July 20, 2007.
- The man had hydrocephalus in infancy. A shunt drained the fluid and remained until adolescence; fluid later accumulated again while the brain gradually contracted to less than half its original size.
- Max Muenke, identified as a pediatric brain-defect specialist at the National Human Genome Research Institute in Bethesda, Maryland, explained the continued function by saying different regions assume duties normally performed by displaced regions.
- The chapter cites Mark Humphries's February 12, 2018 "Your Cortex Contains 17 Billion Computers," retrieved February 24, 2018. It reports recent work suggesting the brain is not itself simply a computer; individual neurons function like computers with processing capacity beyond any then-available computer.
- In that account, the cortex contains 17 billion computers operating massively in parallel and forms "a neural network of neural networks." Some neurons themselves compute nonlinear functions as multilayered neural networks.

### DeLonge and Levenda's inferences
- The authors argue that human neuroanatomy is a prerequisite for reasoned comparison with alleged extraterrestrial biological entities (EBEs): one cannot infer alien biology responsibly without first understanding terrestrial cerebrospinal systems.
- They infer that alien-abduction reports focus strongly on amygdala functions because the reports combine dreamlike content and intense fear. They acknowledge psychologists' sleep-disorder interpretation but add the speculative thought that if dreams lead to the unconscious, the unconscious might lead "somewhere else."
- They regard phrenology's map as wrong in detail but potentially right in its central intuition that aspects of consciousness and capability can be localized in the brain.
- They use the 2007 hydrocephalus case to say simple classical brain anatomy does not fully explain consciousness. The cited specialist reported functional reassignment; the stronger claim that the case "falsifies" existing assumptions is the authors' interpretation.
- They repeatedly distinguish a platform from an explanation: identifying brain regions, cables, drives, and a motherboard does not show how subjective consciousness appears, just as recognizing computer hardware does not explain to an untrained observer how screen images were produced.
- The chapter asks whether consciousness is a program running on a brain-computer, whether brain damage stops that program, whether consciousness is computation, or whether computation merely provides a platform. These are questions, not results.
- The authors infer from the Humphries account that the brain's actual processing system is vastly more complex than the ordinary computer analogy: billions of neuron-computers, some internally multilayered, communicate with billions of others.
- DNA constructs the body's neurons and cerebrospinal organization, while neurotransmitters have genetic precursors. The authors therefore reason that the genome contains commands for building the 17-billion-neuron cortical system.
- **Authors' strongest inference:** Because DNA must encode the construction instructions for the neural system, they conclude that DNA "must be where consciousness comes from." The chapter does not cite an experiment showing consciousness in DNA, and the preceding evidence establishes genetic construction of neural components rather than the asserted identity of DNA as consciousness's source.
- Understanding DNA and the human system is presented as the route to understanding both ourselves and the Others.

### Explicit caveats and open questions
- No universal definition of consciousness exists, so words such as conscious, unconscious, sleep, injury, and recovery can conceal unlike states.
- Functional localization is real in the chapter's account but not absolute: other areas may take over after illness or injury, and hemisphere descriptions are analogical simplifications.
- The chapter calls its anatomy a basket capable of accounting for "everything (or almost everything)" associated with consciousness, but admits many structures, functions, and capabilities have been omitted.
- The abduction/amygdala link is interpretive. Dreamlike content and fear overlap with amygdala functions, but the chapter reports no imaging experiment on abductees and no causal result.
- The hydrocephalus case demonstrates exceptional preserved function amid extreme anatomy; it does not identify consciousness's mechanism.
- The chapter states that myelin deterioration leads to disorders "such as Alzheimer's disease" and separately names multiple sclerosis. This note preserves the source's claim without adding outside correction or extension.
- The neuron-as-computer account is summarized because its technical explanation lies beyond the chapter's scope. Processing complexity does not by itself define consciousness or its purpose.
- DNA's instructions for neural development do not, within the cited material, experimentally demonstrate that DNA experiences or generates consciousness. That final step belongs to the authors' argument and motivates the next inquiry.

## Concepts
- **Central/cerebrospinal nervous system (CNS)** — the brain and spinal cord, treated as the primary receiving, processing, and regulating system for sensation, memory, movement, and candidate mechanisms of consciousness.
- **Peripheral nervous system (PNS)** — the body's non-CNS nerve channels, divided here into autonomic involuntary and somatic voluntary systems.
- **Autonomic nervous system (ANS)** — involuntary control of breathing, heart rate, peristalsis, and related processes; it includes sympathetic, parasympathetic, and enteric branches.
- **Somatic nervous system (SNS)** — the peripheral system controlling voluntary movement.
- **Sympathetic / parasympathetic systems** — respectively the "fight or flight" and "rest and digest" branches of the ANS.
- **Enteric nervous system** — the relatively independent gastrointestinal network from esophagus to anus, nicknamed the second brain but still regulated in the larger CNS relationship.
- **Cerebrum** — the roughly three-pound, walnut-like major brain organ with a gray cortical surface, white tissue beneath, two hemispheres, and four named lobes.
- **Cerebral cortex / gray matter** — the cerebrum's folded gray outer layer; regularities in its grooves provide the topographic basis for functional mapping.
- **White matter** — lighter tissue a few millimeters below the cortex, where axons are seen with insulating myelin.
- **Occipital, frontal, temporal, and parietal lobes** — mapped cortical regions at the rear, forehead, above the ear, and top of the brain, respectively.
- **Right and left hemispheres** — paired cerebral halves controlling opposite sides of the body and conventionally associated with creative versus logical activity; the chapter marks that division as only partly useful.
- **Corpus callosum** — the bridge between hemispheres responsible for cross-hemisphere communication.
- **Thalamus** — a paired deep-brain structure receiving all senses except smell and regulating sleep, wakefulness, and the ANS; the authors consequently associate it with consciousness.
- **Pineal gland / third eye** — the gland between the thalamic halves, historically or mystically associated with vision, soul, and a third eye rather than given a consciousness mechanism here.
- **Hypothalamus** — the regulator of hunger, thirst, fatigue, and body temperature beneath the thalamus.
- **Pituitary gland** — the hormone-secreting gland at the hypothalamus's base, linked to blood pressure, metabolism, sex organs, pregnancy, and childbirth.
- **Amygdala** — one of two almond-shaped temporal-lobe nuclei associated with fear, emotion, threat processing, and reward/punishment; it remains active during sleep in the chapter's account.
- **Unconscious** — used both for reduced awareness after injury or during sleep and, in Freud's formulation, the suppressed psychic material reached in dreams; the chapter warns these senses are not equivalent.
- **Cerebellum** — the folded "little cerebrum" chiefly responsible for accurate fine motor action.
- **Brain stem and spinal cord** — the central relay between brain and body, with incoming sensory signals and outgoing motor commands; larger brain structures are said to have evolved from them.
- **Medulla and pons** — structures extending from the brain stem in sequence; *pons* means bridge.
- **Phrenology** — a discredited fortune-telling map that linked traits and extraordinary capacities to head regions. Its specific scheme failed, but the chapter sees an anticipation of genuine functional localization.
- **Functional reassignment** — one brain region taking over work from another after displacement, illness, or injury, as proposed for the hydrocephalus case.
- **Neuron** — a nerve cell with nucleus, cytoplasm, membrane, dendrites, and axon; it receives, processes, and transmits electrochemical information.
- **Dendrite / axon / synapse** — dendrites receive signals, the axon carries output, and the synapse is the space across which one cell communicates chemically and electrically with another.
- **Neurotransmitter** — one of about fifty signaling substances released from axonal vesicles to dendritic receptors; examples include adrenalin, dopamine, serotonin, endorphins, and endocannabinoids.
- **Electrochemical signaling** — neural communication combining chemical transmitters with movement and pumping of positive and negative ions across cell membranes.
- **Myelin** — fatty axonal insulation that limits cross-signal contamination and accelerates transmission; its loss slows or damages neural operation.
- **Neural pathway** — interconnected firing routes that process sensory data and can sometimes redistribute computation after damage.
- **Brain-computer analogy** — comparison of nervous systems with input devices, controllers, memory, processor, software, and cabling. The authors use it while emphasizing that hardware identification does not solve consciousness.
- **Neuron-as-computer** — the reported model in which each cortical neuron computes, and some neurons themselves implement multilayered nonlinear networks.
- **Neural network of neural networks** — the Humphries formulation for billions of interconnected neurons whose individual internal operations may also be network-like.
- **DNA consciousness inference** — the authors' conclusion that because DNA encodes neural construction and biochemical precursors, it is consciousness's source. This is the chapter's hypothesis-producing endpoint rather than cited proof.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Shakespeare's *Hamlet*, Act 5, Scene 1** — the Yorick-skull epigraph foregrounds a physical object once associated with a person of "infinite jest" and "excellent fancy," setting up the question of where those qualities went and how they related to the brain.
- **Head injury versus sleep** — both a concussed person and a deeply sleeping person may be called unconscious, though one state follows physical damage and the other is ordinary, and they differ in recovery. The case exposes semantic looseness before anatomy begins.
- **EBE comparison** — "extraterrestrial biological entities" are the reason the authors give for learning human biology. Applying CNS knowledge may, they suggest, reveal possible Visitor structures and characteristics; no alien anatomy is supplied here.
- **Autonomic examples** — breathing, heart rate, and peristalsis represent involuntary regulation, while voluntary movement belongs to the SNS. The digestive tract's enteric network can function relatively independently despite central regulation.
- **Television-autopsy cerebrum** — *CSI* and other postmortem programs provide the familiar image of the walnut-like brain lifted from the skull. The example identifies the cerebrum, not the full nervous system.
- **Figure 11 brain diagram** — an old illustration is described as showing the brain's structure and complexity; it accompanies the discussion of the cerebrum and its internal departments.
- **Cortical topography** — repeated groove and fold patterns across brains allow researchers to recognize lobes and associate sites with functions. Apparent surface disorder therefore conceals consistent organization.
- **Art/science hemisphere analogy** — for a typical right-handed person, right is simplified as art/creativity and left as science/logic, with each controlling the opposite body side. The authors explicitly warn that reality is more complicated.
- **Descartes and the pineal gland** — modern mystics call it the third eye; Descartes linked it to vision and the soul. These are historical associations, not findings endorsed as anatomy.
- **Amygdala during sleep** — the emotional system remains alert while rational inhibition quiets. Freud's dream theory is used to connect this state with suppressed psychic material.
- **Abduction/amygdala comparison** — dreamlike reports plus extreme fear lead the authors to say alien-abduction scenarios seem focused on the amygdala. Psychologists' sleep-disorder explanation is acknowledged; the authors' "road to somewhere else" is explicitly speculative.
- **Cerebellar injury** — tremors and shaking hands after damage exemplify localization of fine motor control.
- **Spinal sensory inventory** — cold, heat, smell, touch, taste, and sounds arrive from outside; stomach pain and phantom limbs arise from inside. Together they make the spinal cord look like a computer's input architecture.
- **Computer hardware inspection** — an observer can follow cables and identify hard drives, CD or floppy drives, and the motherboard without understanding how an image appeared onscreen. Brain anatomy likewise maps parts without locating consciousness.
- **Phrenology heads (Figure 12)** — ceramic or porcelain heads divided in black ink into sites for prediction, messages, love, dreams, pain, death, and other traits represent a superstitious map. Their exact divisions failed, but cortical localization survived as a general idea.
- **The 2007 *Lancet* hydrocephalus case** — Feuillet and colleagues' patient was forty-four, married, a father of two, and a civil servant, despite a brain said to be 50–75 percent of ordinary size. Infant hydrocephalus had been drained through a shunt left until his teens; later fluid buildup compressed the brain to below half normal size. Muenke's explanation was regional reassignment of displaced functions.
- **Neuron counts** — 17 billion neurons in the cortex and up to 30 billion throughout the brain establish the scale on which the chapter's later computer comparison operates.
- **Cell-content inventory** — the neuron nucleus contains DNA; cytoplasm holds RNA-bearing ribosomes and glucose-oxidizing mitochondria; dendrites receive, the soma relays, and the axon sends across a synapse.
- **Synaptic communication** — roughly fifty neurotransmitters wait in axonal vesicles, are released on command, and bind receptors across the gap. Excitation and inhibition enable different response types.
- **Figure 13 neuron diagram** — the image labels the neuron, dendrites, axons, and neurotransmitters, visually collecting the chapter's cell-level signaling vocabulary.
- **Neurotransmitter examples and precursors** — dopamine derives from tyrosine and serotonin from tryptophan; adrenalin, endorphins, and endocannabinoids are also named. Cannabis/THC acts through cannabinoid-responsive systems and is used as an example of chemical alteration of consciousness.
- **Myelinated cable analogy** — fatty insulation keeps one axon's signal from corrupting another's and raises speed. Uninsulated transmission is slower; multiple sclerosis and organophosphate-linked demyelination illustrate vulnerability.
- **Milky Way comparison** — cortical interconnections are often said to outnumber stars in the Milky Way. The authors use the image to convey scale, not provide an astronomical count.
- **Environmental effects on the platform** — trauma, pollutants, neurodegeneration, drugs, and exercise can change brain operation. The unresolved issue is whether consciousness is identical with that changing computation or only uses it.
- **Mark Humphries, February 12, 2018** — the cited account says a neuron can exceed contemporary computers in sophistication and function as a nonlinear multilayered network. The retrieval date is February 24, 2018.
- **Seventeen-billion-computer consequence** — if every cortical neuron computes and neurons communicate massively in parallel, the cortex is not one machine but billions of machines, some internally networked, connected into a larger network. The authors joke that an abacus has too few beads for the multiplication.
- **Genome command-chain argument** — DNA builds the body's structures, including neurons; neurons make the brain and spine; neurotransmitters have genetic precursors. The authors infer that the microscopic genome contains construction commands for the whole system and then make the further, unproven step to DNA as consciousness's source.

## Thread
Chapter 8 constructs the physical platform required by Section Two's later theories. It begins with systems and regions because injury, sleep, sensory processing, emotion, and motor control make the brain an obvious consciousness candidate, but every successful localization also sharpens the missing explanation: no mapped lobe, gland, cable, or chemical tells the reader how subjective awareness appears. The hydrocephalus case disrupts a rigid one-site/one-function picture, and the neuron-as-computer model replaces a simple central processor with billions of adaptive processors. The chapter then pushes one level deeper, from neurons to the genetic instructions that build them, turning anatomical complexity into the next hypothesis: if the genome contains the construction plan, perhaps consciousness begins in DNA. The evidence supports genetic construction and distributed computation; the proposed source of consciousness remains the authors' inference.
