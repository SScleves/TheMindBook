# The Succubus in the Saucer

## In one paragraph
DeLonge and Levenda argue that alien abduction is the Phenomenon's most controversial domain because it usually lacks corroborating witnesses and leaves its clearest traces in experiencers' psyches, though sometimes also in unusual physical symptoms. Through the Hills, Budd Hopkins, Whitley Strieber, and John Mack, they distinguish a reported experience and trauma from the experiencer's culturally conditioned interpretation, rejecting both automatic literalization and blanket dismissal as sleep paralysis, media contamination, or deception. They describe Mack's roughly 200 psychologically healthy interviewees with PTSD symptoms as evidence that "something" occurred without claiming that this proves extraterrestrial abduction. The chapter then moves from subjective reports to the Rendlesham Forest and Cash-Landrum cases, where witnesses reported close proximity to luminous craft and suffered injuries that researchers Kit Green, Garry Nolan, and Hal Puthoff interpreted through electromagnetic exposure and radiation effects. The authors conclude that biological, neurological, cardiac, dermatological, oncological, and possibly genetic markers may preserve more scientifically useful "fingerprints" of contact than videos alone, while keeping the cause attributed to the cited witnesses and researchers rather than established independently.

## Core claims
- The authors call alien abduction and adverse close contact the most difficult and controversial aspect of the Phenomenon: stories of nocturnal removal, transport to a craft, invasive examination or insemination, and return to bed are harder to accept than sightings or even crash-retrieval speculation.
- They acknowledge the evidentiary weakness directly: most abduction accounts have virtually no corroborating eyewitnesses and leave few discernible traces beyond psychological effects and occasional strange physical symptoms.
- The Hills' case illustrates "high strangeness," a Hynek term for a terror combining fear, dislocation, awe, and bodily symptoms that does not fit an obvious everyday danger.
- The authors reject the universal use of sleep paralysis, hypnagogic hallucination, deprivation, or suggestibility as explanations. Those states can resemble aspects of abduction, but applying them to every witness is presented as observer bias rather than evidence of witness deceit.
- Cultural material may shape how an experience is described without creating or negating the underlying event; the authors compare changing actors and costumes in a touring musical whose songs and plot remain the same.
- Citing Hynek, they identify the field's cardinal error as substituting an interpretation of the UFO Phenomenon for the phenomenon itself.
- They connect this interpretive problem explicitly to *Sekret Machines: Gods*: people perceive the same Phenomenon through their cultural conditioning, and experiencers lack a vocabulary for an ineffable encounter, much as mystics struggle to report experiences beyond ordinary language.
- The authors say modern scientific and technological language has displaced consciousness, religion, mysticism, dreams, and psyche; using that limited vocabulary for the Phenomenon can make sincere reports sound foolish or vague.
- Publicity did not initially motivate Barney and Betty Hill: they delayed public discussion for eighteen months, accepted hypnosis later for memory recovery, and became famous only after a session tape was reportedly leaked.
- Sincerity is not truth, but the authors treat it as evidence against conscious deception in the Hills and Whitley Strieber.
- They reject unsupported "cultural contamination" claims as insufficient to explain an experience, comparing media-shaped misidentification of an assailant with denial that a shooting happened at all.
- Budd Hopkins's interviews led him to regard abduction accounts as consistent and as evidence of dispassionate nonhuman examination; the authors report his role in creating the modern abduction "meme" without treating his conclusions as established fact.
- Strieber does not insist that extraterrestrials caused his experiences; he maintains that the experiences and the intelligences represented in them are real, while considering screen memories and possible links to human mind-control projects.
- The authors treat the UFO/consciousness relation as unavoidable because many abductees first report a physical UFO sighting, sometimes on the same day, followed by psychological effects disproportionate to merely seeing a disk in the sky.
- John Mack's open approach avoided binary traps such as real/unreal, internal/external, and happened/didn't happen. The authors say he found about 200 otherwise healthy, intelligent, adjusted, sometimes skeptical people displaying PTSD symptoms from an unexplained shared class of experience.
- Mack did not claim that literal alien abduction or UFO explanations were proven; he claimed a powerful real phenomenon was present that he could not otherwise account for. The authors present criticism and Harvard's investigation as failures to preserve that nuance.
- Close contact with craft may produce measurable physical injury even without an abduction. Rendlesham and Cash-Landrum, occurring within days of one another in December 1980, are presented as especially important because witnesses reported UAP proximity and subsequent harm.
- Kit Green and Garry Nolan reportedly linked John Burroughs's cardiac injury and similar military cases to broad-band non-ionizing electromagnetic radiation and identified possible contact biomarkers affecting the brain, heart, skin, cancer risk, and DNA.
- The Department of Defense and Veterans Administration's coverage of Burroughs's medical costs is characterized as a tacit administrative admission that he was injured on active duty during an unexplained encounter, not as an official declaration that extraterrestrials were responsible.
- The authors frame Cash-Landrum as a legal Catch-22: the government's denial that its aircraft caused the harm helped defeat the victims' lawsuit but left the injuring diamond-shaped craft and reported twenty-three Chinook-like objects unidentified.
- Hal Puthoff's 2018 proposed mechanism says engineered vacuum effects could blue-shift normally invisible infrared into visible luminosity and visible light into ultraviolet or soft X-rays, potentially causing burns or radiation poisoning near a powered craft.
- The chapter's final methodological claim is that bodily traces in experiencers may reveal more than pursuit videos or gun-camera footage because they are the "Man" variable—the Phenomenon's alleged biological fingerprints.

## Concepts
- **Alien abduction** — the reported removal of a person, often from bed or a vehicle at night, followed by invasive physical procedures, missing memory or time, and return; the authors separate the reported experience and its effects from a literal extraterrestrial interpretation.
- **Contactee / experiencer** — a person reporting interaction with the Phenomenon, including craft proximity or nonhuman beings; the terms encompass more than complete abduction.
- **High strangeness** — J. Allen Hynek's term, invoked from his November 27, 1978 United Nations speech, for encounters whose emotional and physical quality exceeds familiar danger: fear, dislocation, awe, feverishness, nausea, dizziness, trembling, or breathing difficulty.
- **Missing time** — an interval for which witnesses lack conscious memory; in the Hill case, approximately two hours and thirty-five miles of travel were unaccounted for.
- **Cultural conditioning** — the shaping of an experiencer's vocabulary and imagery by the surrounding culture. The authors allow that it may alter description while denying that it necessarily generates the underlying experience.
- **Interpretation versus experience** — the chapter's main epistemic distinction: an account's language, images, or extraterrestrial explanation can be wrong or culturally filtered while the event and trauma remain real.
- **Sleep paralysis / hypnagogic hallucination** — recognized psychological and sleep states that can resemble abduction features, especially paralysis near sleep; the authors object only to using them as automatic explanations for every report.
- **Recovered memory** — details recalled under hypnosis after an event; the Hills and Strieber used separate professional hypnosis, but the chapter does not claim hypnosis guarantees factual memory.
- **Screen memory** — Strieber's possibility that remembered imagery conceals or protects consciousness from a more disturbing underlying reality, potentially including human mind-control experimentation.
- **Consciousness aspect** — the psychological and perceptual effects linked to apparently physical craft; the authors say this combination distinguishes the Phenomenon from ordinary traumatic causes.
- **Post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD)** — symptoms Mack reportedly found in otherwise healthy abductees, indicating trauma without identifying its literal external cause.
- **Physical contact effects** — injuries following close proximity to reported craft, including burns, inflammation, heart damage, gastrointestinal illness, lesions, alopecia, and possible neurological or genetic changes.
- **Broad-band non-ionizing electromagnetic radiation** — Kit Green's proposed exposure category, including radio waves, thermal radiation, infrared, ultraviolet, and other broad spectral effects capable of burning or inflaming tissue.
- **Biomarkers** — measurable neurological, cardiac, dermatological, oncological, or genetic traces that Green, Nolan, Puthoff, and colleagues reportedly investigate as evidence of contact effects.
- **Blue-shifting** — Puthoff's proposed process in which infrared is shifted into visible light while visible light is shifted into ultraviolet or soft X-rays, explaining both brilliant luminosity and possible injury.
- **Legal Catch-22** — the military's dilemma, as framed by the authors: denying responsibility requires identifying an unknown craft in US airspace, while accepting line-of-duty responsibility can expose unexplained encounter details; in Cash-Landrum, denial also prevented plaintiffs from proving government ownership.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **George Adamski's early-1950s contact claims** — cited as the era's recurring, relatively benign contactee model: Adamski claimed an ongoing relationship with a Venusian. The later Hill case publicly established a more frightening abduction narrative.
- **Barney and Betty Hill sighting and missing time, September 19–20, 1961** — while returning from Montreal to New Hampshire on Route 3, south of the Old Man of the Mountain site, the interracial couple saw an object around midnight. Barney, initially annoyed, viewed a large windowed object and apparent occupants through binoculars, felt terror, returned to the car, and after an unexplained beep the pair apparently lost awareness for roughly two hours; a second beep preceded recovery, with Barney still driving, and full awareness returned near a "Concord 17 miles" sign. They later determined that the missing interval had carried them about 35 miles farther along the highway.
- **Immediate Hill aftermath** — at home the Hills felt disoriented; Betty inexplicably placed luggage by the back door, both watches had stopped and would not restart, and Barney felt compelled to inspect his genitals though he found nothing unusual. These behaviors are offered as outlines of high strangeness, not proof of a specific cause.
- **Betty Hill's reporting sequence** — the day after the incident she borrowed Donald E. Keyhoe's UFO book from the library, later contacted him, and reported the sighting to Pease Air Force Base on September 21 while omitting humanoid occupants. Major Paul Henderson called the next day and filed a September 26 report attributing the sighting to Jupiter; it entered Project Blue Book.
- **NICAP investigation, October 21, 1961** — a National Investigations Committee on Aerial Phenomena member visited the Hills. NICAP, cofounded in 1956 by Keyhoe and Thomas Townsend Brown and lasting until about 1980, was then the leading civilian UFO body; Barney reported gaps and possible suppressed memories, appearing more anxious than Betty.
- **Betty's five dreams, early October 1961** — over five consecutive nights she dreamed of a spaceship, humanoids, and medical examinations, motifs later associated with abduction accounts.
- **Delayed disclosure and hypnosis** — the Hills first mentioned the event publicly at their Unitarian church in March 1963, eighteen months later, and agreed to separately conducted hypnosis in January 1964. Therapist Benjamin Simon, a prior UFO witness but not an abduction believer, first suspected Barney's childhood trauma and ultimately called the narrative a new psychological disorder.
- **Hill press leak and publication** — at least one hypnosis tape reportedly reached the press; Boston papers put the story on front pages in October 1965, four years after the event. John G. Fuller's *The Interrupted Journey* (Dial Press, New York, 1966) supplied the chapter's basic case outline and made the Hills emblematic of abduction; Betty accepted celebrity more readily than Barney.
- **Racial-anxiety explanation** — some psychiatrists attributed Barney's distress to being a Black man married to a white woman in early-1960s New Hampshire. The authors note that Simon himself did not reach that conclusion.
- **Television and pulp contamination claim** — debunkers proposed *The Twilight Zone*, *The Outer Limits*, magazines, or UFO articles as sources of details. The authors say there is no evidence Betty had seen even one *Twilight Zone* episode before the encounter and argue shared cultural and witness imagery might instead derive from a common source.
- **Crime-media analogy** — exposure to police procedurals and violent films does not mean real shootings do not occur; culture might cause a witness to mistake a neighbor for a Mafia killer or terrorist without invalidating the shooting. This is the authors' analogy for separating alien interpretation from encounter.
- **Budd Hopkins's career and sighting** — Hopkins (1931–2011) was a nationally recognized painter, Guggenheim fellow, National Endowment for the Arts grantee, and 1994 full member of the National Academy of Design. In August 1964 he and two friends saw a daylight UFO over Truro, Massachusetts, beginning his lifelong research interest.
- **Hopkins's North Bergen investigation and books** — as a NICAP member he investigated a North Bergen, New Jersey incident and published a 1976 *Village Voice* article that drew many readers. *Missing Time* (1981) helped create the abduction meme; *Intruders* (1987) and *Witnessed* (1996) extended his conclusion that consistent survivors reported genuine, dispassionate examination by entities from elsewhere.
- **Whitley Strieber's background** — before *Communion*, Strieber wrote *The Wolfen* (1978) and *The Hunger* (1981), both filmed. *Communion* appeared in 1987 and became a 1989 feature starring Christopher Walken, bringing abduction into the mainstream.
- **Strieber's October and December 1985 experiences** — at his upstate New York cabin, despite an activated burglar alarm, a disturbance woke him; fear preceded sight of a Small Gray near the bedroom door. It rushed him, after which he lost consciousness, later sensed being carried while paralyzed, and found himself in a small chamber with tiny beings where a hair-thin needle entered his cranium and a rectal probe was used. He described squat beings in blue coveralls and one perceived as feminine, with emotions shifting among fear, terror, and anger.
- **Strieber's aftermath and hypnosis controls** — he awoke in bed with a sense something was wrong and declined emotionally; Anne Strieber connected it to a similar rough period months earlier. Hopkins suggested hypnotists, but Strieber chose his own to avoid contamination and printed session transcripts in *Communion* so readers could assess leading questions.
- **Strieber's screen-memory and Randolph AFB speculation** — later memories extended into childhood; in conversations with Levenda, Strieber considered whether memories conceal an awful reality and whether 1950s–60s mind-control experiments were involved. His childhood proximity to Randolph Air Force Base in San Antonio—described as hosting a major Operation Paperclip contingent specializing from aviation medicine to psychology—prompted speculation, not a documented causal finding.
- **Strieber's religious and esoteric context** — a Catholic whose titles include *Communion* and *Confirmation*, he also had a Gurdjieff/Ouspensky background and investigated the Process Church of the Final Judgment at its Mayfair, London headquarters in 1968, discussed in an interview with Levenda.
- **Strieber and Jeffrey Kripal, 2016** — *The Super Natural* paired the famous abductee with a religious-studies professor to examine the experience academically and psycho-spiritually after decades of ridicule and hostility.
- **John Mack's change of hypothesis** — Mack (1929–2004), Harvard Medical School psychiatry professor and author of the Pulitzer-winning T. E. Lawrence biography *A Prince of Our Disorder* (1977), began studying abductees expecting a new disorder. Following Thomas Kuhn's advice to avoid binaries such as real/unreal and internal/external, he interviewed roughly 200 people and reported PTSD in otherwise healthy, intelligent, adjusted, skeptical subjects.
- **Mack's *Abduction* and Harvard proceedings, 1994** — *Abduction: Human Encounters with Aliens* brought criticism and a Harvard inquiry into revoking his tenure, despite his narrower claim that interviewees had experienced something and that an unexplained real phenomenon was involved. Attorney Daniel Sheehan and legal scholar Alan Dershowitz challenged the proceeding's legality; Mack retained tenure.
- **James McDonald comparison** — physicist McDonald testified to Congress in 1968 that the UFO Phenomenon was real, was heavily ridiculed, and died by suicide in 1971. The authors compare his professional exposure with Mack's.
- **Mack's death, September 27, 2004** — at age 74 he was killed by a drunk driver in London. The chapter rejects subsequent conspiracy theories and describes the death as an accident.
- **Thomas E. Bullard and later scholarship** — Indiana University folklore professor and member of several UFO organizations Bullard treated reports seriously in *The Myth and Mystery of UFOs* (2010); Rice University religious-studies scholar Jeffrey Kripal continued the trend with Strieber in *The Super Natural* (2016). Their contribution is compared with Mack's and Jacques Vallée's.
- **Rendlesham Forest chronology, December 26 and 28, 1980** — at RAF Woodbridge in England, then used by the USAF, a security patrol near the east gate saw what looked like a fireball or downed aircraft. John Burroughs, Penniston, and Cabansag followed a light into the forest; although skeptics invoked a nearby lighthouse, the authors say that cannot explain why trained airmen initially searched for a crash or all observed lights.
- **Rendlesham close approach** — Burroughs was assigned as radio relay but followed from fear and curiosity; Penniston reported a craft in the woods and touched it, while Burroughs saw and sketched it. The authors attribute Burroughs's later rare medical condition to proximity.
- **Kit Green's radiation analysis** — Green is described as a physician, psychiatrist/radiologist, behavioral and neuroscience specialist, remote-viewing researcher, former CIA analyst, and former Assistant National Intelligence Officer for Science and Technology. He interpreted Burroughs's symptoms as broad-band non-ionizing electromagnetic exposure capable of burns and inflammation.
- **Burroughs's classified medical record** — despite long-held TS/SCI clearance, Green reportedly could not access it; he said the only comparably classified medical files he knew were John F. Kennedy's autopsy and Adolf Hitler's records. References to Special Access Programs allegedly complicated declassification until senators pressed for release of files or salient details.
- **Green–Nolan findings and Burroughs outcome** — Green and genetics specialist Garry Nolan agreed that this and similar unexplained military cases reflected electromagnetic exposure. They attributed Burroughs's damaged heart valve to prolonged close exposure; DOD and the Veterans Administration ultimately recognized line-of-duty injury and granted medical benefits.
- **Proposed contact biomarkers** — Green, Nolan, and colleagues reportedly identified neurological and cardiological effects, skin lesions, some cancers, and possible DNA changes among patients. The authors label the claim astonishing but soberly made.
- **Cash-Landrum sighting, December 29, 1980** — around 9 p.m. on FM 1485 near Huffman outside Houston, Betty Cash, Vicky Landrum, and seven-year-old Colby Landrum saw a bright, approximately upright-diamond-shaped craft descend to treetop level. Accounts differed on contours, but other local witnesses corroborated an unusual sky event.
- **Twenty-three Chinook-like objects** — witnesses consistently counted twenty-three twin-rotor helicopter-like objects surrounding the craft at a distance. Military branches denied ownership or involvement; the authors regard a classified escort mission as more plausible than all twenty-three being alien craft, but state this as inference.
- **Cash-Landrum sensory and religious interpretation** — intense light and heat, loud roaring, intermittent buzzing, and flames from beneath the object led the two born-again Christian women to interpret it initially through the Second Coming and End Times. The authors explicitly connect this to *Gods* and its claim that cultural religion shapes contact interpretation.
- **Exposure and departure** — all three exited or were exposed near the car; Colby wanted its protection, but even its surface grew too hot to touch. After roughly twenty minutes, stronger flames accompanied the craft's ascent and the reported helicopters followed.
- **Cash-Landrum injuries** — corroborating witnesses included a police officer and his wife. Cash and Landrum quickly developed nausea, diarrhea, burns, unusual scalp and bodily growths, and alopecia; Cash, who stood closest in front of the car, was most severely affected. Illness cost both women their jobs and produced very large medical bills.
- **Cash-Landrum lawsuit** — a $20 million action against the US government was dismissed because plaintiffs could not prove government ownership or operation of the injuring device. With military and corporate ownership denied, the cause remained an unidentified craft in the authors' presentation.
- **Hal Puthoff's June 8, 2018 Las Vegas presentation** — at an SSE/IRVA conference, the laser physicist, propulsion and alternate-energy specialist, longtime government adviser, remote-viewing pioneer, and To The Stars Academy vice president proposed infrared-to-visible and visible-to-ultraviolet/soft-X-ray shifting in an engineered vacuum. His stated practical outcome was sunburn or radiation poisoning near a powered landed craft; he advised against approaching one.
- **Moses in Exodus 34** — as a source-labeled aside, the authors compare contact burns with Moses descending Sinai after time with God, his face shining so strongly he wore a veil; they note the Hebrew word *qaran* is rare and appears nowhere else in the Bible.
- **David Ferrie alopecia aside** — Ferrie, linked in allegations about the Kennedy assassination and known for fake eyebrows and a wig, had alopecia of unknown origin. The authors connect this only as a "bizarre" coincidence to Guy Banister and Fred Crisman, whom they say had UFO backgrounds reaching to the 1947 Kenneth Arnold and Maury Island affairs; no causal claim is established.

## Key quotes
- "...there is a compelling powerful phenomenon here that I can't account for in any other way..." — John Mack's deliberately limited conclusion.
- "A cardinal mistake... has been the almost universal substitution of an interpretation... for the phenomenon itself." — Hynek's epistemic warning.
- "For two hours." — the Hill case's central missing-time interval.
- "...the actors may be different and the costumes not identical... but the songs are all the same..." — the cultural-filter analogy.
- "In other words, they had experienced something." — the authors' summary of Mack's finding.
- "He told me not to worry about science and to watch out for the traps of language..." — Thomas Kuhn's advice to Mack.
- "A succubus in the saucer." — the chapter's compression of invasive modern encounter and older sexual-demon imagery.
- "...the only medical files he knows of that were ever classified that high were the autopsy of President John F. Kennedy and the medical records of Adolf Hitler..." — Green's comparison for Burroughs's file.
- "There were twenty-three." — the reported count of Chinook-like craft at Cash-Landrum.
- "If you run across one of these sitting on the ground and it's powered up, I recommend you don't rush up." — Puthoff's practical warning.
- "It's the 'Man' variable in Sekret Machines..." — the authors' reason for prioritizing biological traces.
- "...the Phenomenon leaving fingerprints at the scene of the crime." — the final biomarker metaphor.

## Thread
Chapter 1 supplies the human evidence needed for the book's scientific program. It first protects a narrow proposition—that trauma and anomalous experience deserve study—from both credulous literalism and reflexive psychological dismissal, then follows a historical chain from the Hills through Hopkins, Strieber, and Mack. The final move is decisive: Rendlesham and Cash-Landrum connect subjective high strangeness to claimed measurable injury, allowing the authors to propose biomarkers as a bridge among craft, consciousness, and genetics. This makes the experiencer's body the instrument through which later chapters can ask what the Visitors are, what their technology does, and whether contact modifies human biology.

## Connections
- **Explicit trilogy link:** The authors invoke *Sekret Machines: Gods* for the claim that culture conditions the imagery used to report a persistent Phenomenon; Cash and Landrum's End Times interpretation is offered as a modern repetition of ancient divine-contact framing.
- **Explicit link to this volume's Prologue:** Garry Nolan's biological research and John Mack's PTSD observation are the two evidentiary reasons the Prologue gave for including contactees and abductees; this chapter supplies their case context.
- **Explicit forward link:** Strieber's possible screen memories and the Randolph AFB/Operation Paperclip speculation are said to lead into later discussion of UFOs, consciousness, and mind-control projects.
- **Explicit forward link:** The biomarker argument prepares the following treatment of Grays, implants, genetic manipulation, and hybrid claims.
- **Reader-side speculative connection:** The distinction between an experience and its explanatory imagery could be compared with other books about culturally mediated anomalous states, but no such cross-book identification is made by the source.
