## The thesis in one page

*Man* is the middle nonfiction volume of the *Sekret Machines* trilogy, and it takes the **scientific** half of former senator Harry Reid's formula — "this is about science and national security" — while reserving national security for *War*. Where *Gods* argued that religion is the ancient record of contact, *Man* asks what the Phenomenon's reported **biological, neurological, and technological** effects imply about the Visitors *and about what humanity itself is becoming*. Its organizing reversal, stated in the Section One introduction, is to stop assuming that biological aliens pilot advanced machines and to ask instead whether **the apparent pilots are themselves machines** — and whether human beings, gene-driven "survival machines" or divinely "manufactured" creatures, are already a kind of machine too. The book therefore reframes the stock claim that UFOs are "far in advance" of us into a sharper question: **advanced by how much**, now that human genetics, computing, and AI have reached former science-fiction levels — and what does *that* say about us?

The volume advances through **three linked sections**, each a different scientific lens on the same Phenomenon, and its most important interpretive discipline — the one the authors repeatedly insist on and repeatedly test themselves against — is to **keep evidence classes distinct** and refuse to let an accumulation of examples make different kinds of claim look equivalent. **Section One (Genetics)** protects a narrow proposition — that experiencer trauma and reported biological effects deserve study — from both credulous literalism and reflexive dismissal, then moves through abduction testimony (the Hills, Hopkins, Strieber, Mack), reported contact biomarkers (Nolan, Green, Puthoff at Rendlesham and Cash-Landrum), the golem/Gray artificial-being comparison, and finally the boldest and most explicitly speculative step: directed panspermia (Crick & Orgel) and the shCherbak/Makukov claim that the genetic code carries a designed "signature," extended through the *I Jing*, Ifa, and Dee's angelic tablets. It deliberately opens with its *weakest* and most ethically dangerous idea — an unnamed informant's "genocide of alien-resistant genes" — and marks it speculative before proceeding. **Section Two (Consciousness)** treats consciousness as science's remaining "black box" and tests the DNA-as-source inference against the neural (Crick & Koch), evolutionary (Cambrian origin), and quantum (Penrose & Hameroff's Orch OR, quantum biology, DNA entanglement, chaos theory, holonomics) accounts — always distinguishing *cited theory* from the authors' *conditional extrapolations* to alien telepathy, "tagging," and time. **Section Three (Human-Machine Symbiosis)** turns to brain-computer technology, the historical cyborg (Licklider, Clynes & Kline), the institutional history of parapsychology and remote viewing (SRI, STAR GATE), and a cross-cultural anatomy of the Gray, ending in the twin images of humanity converging on the machine-like Visitor and living in "symbiosis" with an alien that "needs" us. The **Appendix** ("Galileo's Revenge") reframes the whole as an *authority* problem — a scientific orthodoxy that can dismiss testimony as heresy — and the back-matter **Bibliography** and **Government Documents** make the volume's mixed evidentiary base legible. The recurring caution throughout is the authors' own: experts supply reported *data*; DeLonge, Levenda, and their advisers construct the *arguments*; "the conclusions will be exclusively your own."

## How the argument builds

**Prologue (unit 0) — the scientific charter.** Harry Reid's December 2017 statement divides the trilogy's remaining labor. The authors resist compartmentalizing spirituality, science, and security ("since Newton"), defend integrating contactee/abductee testimony with physical UFO data on two grounds — Nolan's reported biological effects and Mack's PTSD observation — and announce four research lines: genetics, consciousness, brain-computer interfaces, and telepathy. They set the volume's epistemic boundary: data from experts, arguments from the authors.

**Section One — Genetics and the Extraterrestrial Hypothesis (units 1–6).** The intro (unit 1) poses the machine-reversal: are the Visitors sentient machines, and are we? "**The Succubus in the Saucer**" (unit 2) supplies the human evidence — separating a real experience/trauma from its culturally conditioned interpretation (Hynek's "cardinal mistake"), following the Hills → Hopkins → Strieber → Mack, and then pivoting from subjective report to *claimed measurable injury* at Rendlesham and Cash-Landrum, proposing biomarkers as the "Man variable." "**Opening the Door**" (unit 3) turns event-vs-interpretation into a history of *authority* (church, state, science, psychology) that decides what counts as real, comparing abduction with succubi, jinn, and the Witches' Sabbat. "**Of Golems and Grays**" (unit 4) builds an intermediate category — the artificial messenger/control device — from angelology, golem lore, Gray anatomy, and documented human mind-control programs, while testing itself against hard limits (Leir's unvalidated implants, the Atacama skeleton being human). "**Gods, Genes, and Genocide**" (unit 5) scales from manipulated bodies to the *architecture of life*: it front-loads its most dangerous speculation (the genocide hypothesis), marks it speculative, then delivers a DNA/RNA primer and the directed-panspermia and Biological-SETI arguments. "**The Chinese Configuration**" (unit 6) answers the "sixty-four" riddle with the *I Jing*, Ifa, and Dee's tablets — explicitly labeling the twenty-cell/twenty-amino-acid correspondence "outrageous (and unproven)" — then turns to maternal-fetal conflict and the conflict between the "genetic imperative" and conscious will (the "ghost in the sekret machine").

**Section Two — Consciousness (units 7–12).** The intro (unit 7) frames consciousness as the "black box." "**DNA Consciousness**" (unit 8) tests whether consciousness is encoded in, generated by, or *received* by the biological system, using Crick & Koch's neurobiological program and Grandy's genetic proposal, with Alzheimer's and a radio-receiver analogy keeping the "received from elsewhere" option open. "**The Hard Problem**" (unit 9) distinguishes measurable structure from subjective *qualia*, moves down through the four forces to the quantum domain, and floats the brain-as-receiver and top-down-causation possibilities. "**The Structure of the Human Brain**" (unit 10) is the anatomical primer, ending in the authors' strongest *inference*: because DNA builds the neural system, "DNA must be where consciousness comes from" — explicitly their argument, not a cited result. "**The Evolution of Consciousness**" (unit 11) stages the decisive contrast: the emergent/computational school (Kurzweil, the Singularity, Cambrian origin) versus the quantum school (Orch OR), with quantum biology (photosynthesis, DNA entanglement) as the bridge — then crosses from cited theory into project-specific speculation about quantum alien telepathy, "tagging," and reproduction, held in check by an explicit false-equivalence warning. "**Utter Chaos**" (unit 12) adds chaos theory and holonomics (Vandervert, Pribram, Melzack's phantom limb) and Dehaene's subliminal/TMS work to propose a *conditional contact model* — an external nudge that grows below awareness into a full encounter — ending on the Gray as the "Joker in the deck."

**Section Three — Human-Machine Symbiosis (units 13–18).** The intro (unit 13) turns to brain-computer technology and parapsychology, argues for race- and gender-inclusive, multicultural inquiry because "the Phenomenon resists all forms of English-language description," and makes the section's governing tension explicit: the authors assert the alien is *real* while warning that nearly every image of it is socially constructed projection. "**The Brain-Computer Model**" (unit 14) makes the intelligence-vs-consciousness distinction the section's diagnostic (strong AI, panpsychism, GANs, deepfakes, integrated information theory), applies it to Gray anatomy, and motivates the machine-pilot hypothesis (G-forces). "**I, Cyborg**" (unit 15) recovers the *original* cyborg — a human integrated with machine homeostasis for space (Licklider, Clynes & Kline) — and its documented hypnosis/vestibular results, ending on the "chicken or egg" question of whether contact reports inspired the science. "**Can You Hear Me Now?**" (unit 16) is the institutional history of parapsychology and remote viewing (SPR, Rhine, SRI, STAR GATE, the 1974 CIA verdict of "no adequate theory"), plus trauma-driven neural rewiring, then a series of conditional leaps (machine Psi, telepathic time travel, one shared UFO-abductee "science"). "**Gray Anatomy**" (unit 17) tests the Gray taxonomy cross-culturally (India, Malaysia, Philippines, Zimbabwe's Ariel School) and argues the *constrained* recurrence of humanoid types favors real experience over free invention, landing on the "organic cyborg" versus "collective breakdown" alternatives and the closing thesis of symbiosis. "**House Guests**" (unit 18) abandons linearity for a web image, compares abduction to disaster and rape, floats a *collective* repressed trauma, and ends on the guest room and the "ticking" left-behind gift.

**Back matter (units 19–21).** The **Appendix** ("Galileo's Revenge") reframes content as authority: scientific orthodoxy, hardened since Galileo, can dismiss testimony as heresy, while a fragmented, funding-shaped institutional structure loses the data without any central conspiracy; the Byelokoroviche and Minot nuclear cases are its strongest reported anomalies. The **Bibliography** (unit 20) and the four-item **Government Documents** inventory (unit 21) make the mixed source base and the remote-viewing archive concrete, and mark the boundary between what the records establish (institutional interest and expenditure) and what they do not (efficacy, or any UFO content).

## The evidentiary strategy

*Man*'s method is to run **three scientific lenses** over the same Phenomenon and to insist, at every step, on keeping **evidence classes distinct**. The single most important reading discipline the book models — and the one it repeatedly turns on itself — is refusing to let an accumulation of examples make different kinds of claim look equivalent.

- **Experiencer testimony, held at arm's length.** Abduction and contact reports (the Hills, Strieber, Mack's ~200 PTSD cases) are retained as reports of a real *experience and trauma*, explicitly separated from any literal extraterrestrial *interpretation*. Hynek's "cardinal mistake" — substituting an interpretation of the Phenomenon for the Phenomenon itself — is the governing rule.

- **Reported biological effects as the "Man variable."** Nolan, Green, and Puthoff's work on contact biomarkers (Rendlesham's John Burroughs, Cash-Landrum's injuries) is the volume's attempt to move from subjective report to *measurable* trace — while the authors keep the *cause* attributed to the cited researchers rather than established independently.

- **Peer-reviewed science as the backbone of the consciousness sections.** Crick & Koch, Feinberg & Mallatt (Cambrian), Penrose & Hameroff (Orch OR), Tononi (integrated information), the photosynthesis and DNA-entanglement work, and Dehaene are cited with venues and page numbers. The consciousness sections lean far more on named journals than the encounter sections, which lean on witness books and field periodicals — an asymmetry the Bibliography note makes explicit.

- **Directed panspermia and "Biological SETI" as the genetic anchor.** Crick & Orgel's seeding proposal (the molybdenum argument) and the shCherbak/Makukov genetic-code "signal" are the volume's scientific bridge to design — introduced as *reported* claims and "a platform for research," with the authors stressing that Darwinian evolution is preserved *after* any code insertion.

- **Documented human programs as terrestrial baselines.** MKULTRA, Operation Paperclip, the cyborg/space research (Clynes & Kline), the MIT thought-control device, GANs, and the SRI/STAR GATE remote-viewing archive are used to show that implants, mind-control, silent communication, and Psi were pursued by *humans* — making the alien versions "discussable" without demonstrating them.

- **Conditional speculation, explicitly flagged.** The volume's boldest moves — the genocide hypothesis, DNA-as-source, quantum alien telepathy and "tagging," telepathic time travel, machine Psi, the "one shared science" of UFO and abductee motion, the collective planetary trauma — are marked by the authors as questions, inferences, or "what ifs," and are frequently guarded by warnings (the false-equivalence caution in unit 11; Dehaene's "correlation not causation" in unit 12).

How the authors want it weighed: experts supply the *data*, the authors and their advisers supply the *arguments*, and "the conclusions will be exclusively your own." The recurring intellectual test is whether a reader can tell, at any point, which layer a claim belongs to — reported experience, reported biological effect, cited theory, documented program, or authorial extrapolation.

## Reading it critically

The notes flag numerous hedges, tensions, and open questions — many raised by the authors, some by the note-takers.

- **The transitions are where evidence classes are most likely to blur — and the book knows it.** Watch the seams: from reported *trauma* to claimed *biomarkers* (unit 2); from the DNA/RNA *primer* to the directed-panspermia *inference* (unit 5); from Crick & Koch's *neural correlates* to the authors' "DNA must be where consciousness comes from" (unit 10); from *cited* Orch OR to *speculative* quantum alien telepathy (unit 11); from Dehaene's documented *subliminal/TMS* results to the *conditional contact model* (unit 12). The authors mark most of these seams, but a critical reader should re-mark them.

- **The most dangerous idea is stated first, and disowned.** "Gods, Genes, and Genocide" opens with an unnamed informant's proposal that genocides might eliminate "alien-resistant" genes, likens it to eugenics and Nazi race science, provides *no* confirming case, and calls the ensuing conclusions speculative. It is a warning, not a claim — but it uses real population-linked illnesses (sickle cell, Tay-Sachs) as analogies, which a careless reading could weaponize.

- **The consciousness argument reaches its conclusion by inference, not by experiment.** The claim that DNA is the source of consciousness (unit 10) rests on the *construction* fact that DNA builds neurons and neurotransmitter precursors; the cited neuroscience establishes genetic construction, not that DNA experiences or generates subjective awareness. The radio-receiver analogy (units 8–9, 11) deliberately keeps a "consciousness comes from elsewhere" option open that the biology does not require.

- **Quantum strangeness is used to make paranormal possibilities *discussable*, not to demonstrate them.** Unit 11's own false-equivalence warning is the key: calling entanglement and astral projection both "spooky action at a distance" joins two labels, not two mechanisms. The leaps to quantum alien telepathy, "tagging," and time travel are the authors' extrapolations several steps beyond the conference theory (unit 16).

- **Cross-cultural convergence is argued as favoring real experience — but is not a demonstrated elimination of cultural transmission.** In "Gray Anatomy" (unit 17) the authors explicitly note that the 1992 Maharashtra account's American-like structure "may indicate cultural contamination," and that Ariel interviewers were accused of leading questions. Their "constrained repertoire" argument (why not octopus-aliens or Predator?) is a reasoned inference, not proof.

- **The Section Three tension is structural and unresolved.** The authors say the alien is *real* while also saying nearly every image of it is socially constructed, anthropocentric projection (unit 13). The final "symbiosis" and "the alien needs us" claims (units 17–18) are asserted more than evidenced; the notes flag that they are stronger than the material supplied.

- **The remote-viewing and government material documents interest and expenditure, not efficacy.** The Government Documents unit (21) is explicit: a program's existence proves institutional interest, a definitions file proves vocabulary existed, a protocol proves procedures were specified — none proves the studied capacity worked, and none of the four files concerns UFOs or aliens. Swann's extraordinary results (unit 16) are *reported claims*.

- **Numbering, spelling, and figure flags.** Note unit numbers (0–21) diverge from printed chapter numbers (1–15) by the three section intros. The notes preserve source inconsistencies rather than silently correcting them: "Jamaludin"/"Jamaluddin" and "Trachtenberg"/"Trachtenburg" spelling variants; the DIA "Designed Target" wording; the shCherbak/Makukov "only in positional decimal system" qualification; and the unit-11 "four amino acids in groups of three" phrasing.

- **Cross-book shelf connections are the knowledge base's, not the authors'.** Where the source notes compare *Man* to informational- or simulated-reality books, machine-consciousness, or posthuman-identity works, those are explicitly reader-side resonances, **not** comparisons DeLonge & Levenda draw.

## What *Man* establishes / what it does not

### What it establishes (within its own frame)

- That a **real experience and measurable trauma** underlie a class of abduction/contact reports (Mack's PTSD finding), and that some close-contact witnesses suffered **documented physical injury** (line-of-duty recognition for Burroughs; Cash-Landrum's illness), whatever the cause.
- That **directed panspermia** and the genetic-code "signature" claim are **published scientific proposals** worth examining, and that the *I Jing*, Ifa, and other systems share the code's "sixty-four" arithmetic (a numerical fit the authors distinguish from any demonstrated meaning).
- That consciousness is a genuine **unsolved problem** with multiple live models (neural, evolutionary, quantum) and no consensus definition, origin, or distribution.
- That **human institutions** did pursue mind-control, cyborg/space research, and remote viewing, documented in declassified files that show institutional interest and expenditure.
- That the reported **Gray taxonomy** is cross-culturally *constrained* rather than freely various, and that reported UFO maneuvers would impose G-forces destructive to ordinary biology.

### What it does not establish

- It does **not** demonstrate that the Visitors are extraterrestrial, machines, cyborgs, or projections; each is offered as a hypothesis, and the "organic cyborg" and "collective breakdown" endpoints are left unresolved.
- It does **not** prove that DNA is the source of consciousness, that consciousness is received from a universal field, that quantum entanglement supports alien telepathy or "tagging," that telepathy implies time travel, or that UFO motion and abductee levitation are "the same science" — all are the authors' explicitly conditional extrapolations.
- It does **not** validate remote viewing, the reality of Psi, alien implants (Leir's objects went unexamined), or hybridization (the Atacama body was human) — and it says so.
- It does **not** confirm the informant's genocide hypothesis, alien programming bias, or a collective planetary trauma; these are flagged as speculation.
- It does **not** identify the Phenomenon's nature or intentions; the closing "symbiosis" claim is asserted, not evidenced, and the sorting of secret human technology from historical UFO reports is deferred to *War*.

*Man* is, on its own terms, the **scientific** stage of the project: it brings genetics, consciousness studies, and human-machine technology to bear on the Phenomenon, models the discipline of keeping evidence classes distinct, and hands the national-security synthesis — and the separation of classified human tech from genuine anomaly — to *Sekret Machines: War*.
