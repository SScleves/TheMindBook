# The Hard Problem

## In one paragraph
DeLonge and Levenda open Section Two's sustained treatment of consciousness by distinguishing the physical ingredients used to describe a world from the subjective qualities through which a consciousness experiences it. They move from ancient atoms, Greek and Chinese elements, and the modern four-force model to quantum particles whose behavior resists ordinary intuition, then speculate that this subatomic domain may help explain consciousness, telepathy, remote viewing, and reported contact. Their central conditional proposal is that if consciousness is not manufactured by the physical brain, the brain may function as a receiver for a universal field, making communication across radically different biological sensory systems conceivable. They cite Hal Puthoff's remote-viewing and UFO research and Garry Nolan's measurement of immune-system effects in contactees as reasons to connect consciousness studies to the Phenomenon, while neither item is presented as proof of universal consciousness or alien telepathy. The chapter defines no settled solution to the hard problem; instead, it lays out the book's forthcoming comparison of DNA, neurons, quantum effects, chaos theory, and information theory, culminating in the authors' speculative question of whether causation may run top-down rather than from particles upward.

## Core claims

### Reported philosophical and scientific background
- Ancient and modern attempts to explain the physical world share a building-block assumption: basic materials or components combine to produce everything in creation.
- The ancient Greeks postulated atoms through logic and reason despite lacking technology with which to see them. Plato developed an older elemental idea into four basic states of matter—fire, earth, air, and water—plus the less definite aether.
- The chapter contrasts that ancient scheme with the modern periodic table but stresses that comparable elemental classifications appeared in many cultures. The Chinese system named five basic elements or processes: fire, wood, water, earth, and metal.
- The authors say these ancient names functioned as placeholders for qualities or forces rather than necessarily naming literal substances. Western descriptions such as "saturnine," "mercurial," and "martial" refer to qualities associated with Saturn, Mercury, and Mars; descriptions such as fiery temperament, wooden affect, or airy personality operate similarly.
- In neurobiology, *qualia* denotes subjective experiences of objects. The standard example in the chapter is red: measurable light of a given frequency can be identified as red, but shades, associations, and experiences vary between people, and the experience itself cannot simply be transmitted to a blind person through a scientific description.
- The Greek elements are presented as likely beginning in qualia. Fire, for example, gathers experienced heat, light, destruction, and dryness into a single category; those qualities are subjectively encountered even when some can be quantified.
- Aristotle, Galen, Avicenna, and unnamed others are said to have taken up and amplified these qualitative elemental ideas, helping form the beginnings of natural science.
- The chapter reports the twentieth-century scientific model of four forces: electromagnetic, strong nuclear, weak nuclear, and gravity. Gravity is described as the outlier whose relation to the other three remains unresolved.
- A Grand Unified Theory (GUT), as the chapter defines it, would express all four forces through one central pattern or formula and thereby join general relativity to quantum mechanics. The authors describe those frameworks as incompatible partners and say gravity has prompted theoretical approaches such as string theory.
- Matter can be divided into progressively smaller components, but at the subatomic or quantum scale the components do not always behave in ways compatible with ordinary experience; therefore, mysteries multiply rather than disappear as analysis moves downward.
- The chapter says there is no agreement among physicists, neurobiologists, psychologists, or mystics about what consciousness is, where it originates, when it began, whether other organic beings possess it, whether the universe or every object is conscious, or whether consciousness can examine itself.
- John Mack's, David Jacobs's, and others' collected abductee accounts are described as a substantial body of experiential data. The authors report that scientists often classify such accounts as hoax, fantasy, delusion, or insanity, while the authors regard their consistency as material for inquiry rather than proof of the witnesses' interpretation.

### DeLonge and Levenda's inferences
- Ancient elemental systems should be read as classifications of experienced qualities, not dismissed only because their labels are not entries in the periodic table. The authors treat them as early natural science grounded in human perception.
- Qualia are necessarily situated. The elemental experiences described in terrestrial traditions belong to human beings on Earth; nonhuman organisms on another planet would have different qualia unless environment, physics, chemistry, topology, and evolved life were all identical to Earth's.
- The inability to communicate the experience of red illustrates a broader consciousness problem: objective measurement of a stimulus does not explain or transfer what the stimulus is like for the subject.
- The authors infer that learning more about human consciousness and studying close-encounter reports can operate symbiotically: each may supply questions and interpretive clues for the other.
- They treat the consistency of abductee reports as suggesting "fertile lines of inquiry" into the Other, but do not claim that consistency establishes alien abduction as the cause.
- They argue that the Phenomenon requires a multidisciplinary approach combining consciousness theory, neurobiology, genetics, artificial intelligence, information theory, quantum physics, remote viewing, and contact testimony.
- They state that the chapter's purpose is introductory rather than exhaustive. Challenging terminology will be necessary, in their view, because the Phenomenon forces questions conventional categories have not answered.

### Explicitly conditional or speculative proposals
- **Authors' speculation:** The smallest known particles may hold a key to consciousness and may eventually explain telepathy, remote viewing, and other phenomena called psychic. The chapter offers this as a possibility, not a demonstrated result.
- **Authors' speculation:** If those mechanisms were identified, reported contact or communication with nonhuman entities in close encounters and abductions might become scientifically explainable or verifiable. No verification is supplied here.
- **Reported objection:** Scientists, especially linguists and anthropologists, are said to regard extraterrestrial communication as impossible when separately evolved beings share no sensory hardware or perceptual ranges—perhaps no comparable eyes, ears, wavelengths, or sound ranges at all.
- **Authors' conditional reply:** If consciousness is not produced by the physical brain, it need not be confined to human neurobiology. The brain could be a receiver analogous to a radio, with more sophisticated integration allowing access to a broader signal range.
- **Authors' speculation:** If consciousness is universal—or if, as unnamed scientists speculate, the universe itself is conscious—human and alien minds might communicate by tuning to a common signal despite incompatible sensory systems.
- **Authors' inference from affiliations:** Hal Puthoff's work in remote viewing for private companies and the US government, his role as a To The Stars Academy founder, and his UFO research are taken to imply a connection between the Phenomenon and unexplained features of consciousness. The affiliation is factual within the chapter's account; the connection is the authors' inference.
- **Reported research, bounded claim:** Garry Nolan and other To The Stars Academy-affiliated scientists are said to isolate and measure observable immune-system effects specifically in contactees. The chapter supplies no sample size, protocol, values, diagnosis, or causal demonstration, so this cannot establish what produced the effects.
- **Authors' speculative reversal:** The conventional account builds the universe bottom-up from atoms and subatomic particles. The authors ask whether that mechanism could be correct while its direction is wrong—that causation may instead proceed top-down.
- **Questions reserved for later chapters:** The authors ask whether DNA is conscious or is consciousness's physical substrate, whether neurons generate consciousness, whether quantum effects occur at genome or neural scale, and whether chaos and information theory can explain consciousness. They do not answer any of these questions in this chapter.

## Concepts
- **Qualia / quale** — subjective qualities of experience rather than merely measurable properties of an external object. A *quale* is one such quality; *qualia* is plural.
- **Red as a quale** — the chapter's main hard-problem example. A wavelength can be measured and labeled red, but that procedure does not reveal what red means to a consciousness or permit one person to give the experience to someone who cannot see.
- **Platonic elements** — fire, earth, air, water, and aether. The authors interpret the first four as old qualitative descriptions of forces and experiences, not simply failed chemical elements.
- **Chinese five elements/processes** — fire, wood, water, earth, and metal, presented as another culture's basic qualitative system.
- **Aether** — Plato's more amorphous fifth element in the account given here; the chapter does not develop its physical or metaphysical properties.
- **Four fundamental forces** — electromagnetic force, strong nuclear force, weak nuclear force, and gravity. Their incomplete unification replaces the older elemental question at a more technical level.
- **Grand Unified Theory (GUT)** — the hoped-for pattern or formula that would reduce the four forces to one and reconcile general relativity with quantum mechanics.
- **Hard problem of consciousness** — the unresolved question of how physical structures or processes give rise to subjective awareness and experience. The chapter also uses the phrase as an umbrella for disputes about consciousness's definition, origin, location, and distribution.
- **Quantum domain** — the subatomic scale at which components behave in ways unlike familiar macroscopic matter. Here it is both a scientific domain and the authors' candidate territory for a consciousness mechanism.
- **Brain-as-receiver model** — the conditional analogy that a brain receives consciousness as a radio receives a broadcast rather than manufacturing it. Receiver sophistication would determine available range.
- **Universal consciousness** — the reported speculation that consciousness belongs to the universe, or that the universe is itself conscious. The chapter treats this as an open hypothesis.
- **Alien-human communication problem** — the objection that separately evolved species may share no sensory medium, set against the authors' proposal that nonlocal or universal consciousness could provide a common channel.
- **Quantum consciousness** — the proposed involvement of quantum-scale effects in consciousness. This chapter announces rather than establishes the theory.
- **DNA consciousness** — the later question of whether DNA itself is conscious or supplies the physical basis from which consciousness arises.
- **Neural consciousness** — the conventional possibility that neurons are the source of human consciousness, to be tested against DNA- and quantum-level alternatives.
- **Chaos theory and information theory** — two later frameworks the authors promise to use in explaining consciousness; neither is defined in this chapter.
- **Top-down causation** — the closing possibility that atoms and subatomic particles compose the known universe but do so under a pattern or cause proceeding from above rather than generating everything upward by themselves.
- **Contact / alien abduction evidence** — accounts of communication and encounter used here as a source of recurring phenomenological questions. Their consistency is evidence of a recurring report pattern, not proof of an extraterrestrial mechanism.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Henri Bergson epigraph (1928)** — "The mystical summons up the mechanical" comes from *The Two Sources of Morality and Religion* (Greenwood Press, Westport, Connecticut, p. 268). It frames the chapter's attempt to connect subjective or mystical questions with physical mechanism.
- **Ancient Greek atom inference** — Greek thinkers reasoned that unseen atoms must exist even though they had no observational technology capable of displaying one. The authors use the case to show that scientific concepts can begin as logical postulates.
- **Plato's fivefold matter scheme** — fire, earth, air, water, and aether are contrasted with the periodic table familiar to modern schoolchildren. The authors call the old assumption naive by present standards but historically widespread in form.
- **Chinese elemental/process scheme** — fire, wood, water, earth, and metal provide a parallel fivefold classification. The word "processes" limits an overly literal reading of these categories as chemical substances.
- **Planetary adjective examples** — saturnine, mercurial, and martial attribute qualities associated with planets in astrology and mythology; they do not claim the described person's extraterrestrial origin. Fiery, wooden, and airy personality descriptions make the same distinction vivid.
- **Communicating red to a blind person** — a speaker can describe red scientifically but cannot communicate the experience itself. Countless shades and each person's associations make the quale variable even among sighted observers.
- **Fire as a bundle of qualities** — heat, light, destructive force, and dryness are collected under "fire." Some effects can be quantified, yet their experiential unity remains subjective.
- **Nonhuman qualia thought experiment** — any difference in another planet's physics, chemistry, topology, environment, or evolved organisms would alter how effects are experienced. Only an Earth-identical world with human-identical life would guarantee the same qualia.
- **The modern four-force problem** — electromagnetic, strong nuclear, weak nuclear, and gravitational forces replace the ancient list, but gravity's failure to fit the other three prevents a GUT and the marriage of relativity with quantum mechanics. String theory is named as one intriguing direction prompted by the mismatch; no string-theory result is reported.
- **Progressively smaller components** — reducing matter toward quantum particles does not yield a simple final explanation because the smaller entities behave unlike objects in ordinary human experience. This observation motivates, but does not prove, the chapter's consciousness speculation.
- **Cross-species communication objection** — imagined aliens may lack eyes, ears, or any apparatus responsive to human-visible wavelengths and audible frequencies. Linguists' and anthropologists' reported objection grounds the authors' turn to a nonsensory consciousness channel.
- **Radio-receiver analogy** — consciousness is the broadcast and the brain the radio. A more integrated receiver accesses more stations; alien communication would require tuning the human receiver to the appropriate one. This is an analogy supporting a hypothesis, not a described experiment.
- **Hal Puthoff research example** — Puthoff is described as an award-winning physicist, leading remote-viewing pioneer, researcher for private firms and the US government, UFO investigator, and To The Stars Academy founder. The authors regard this convergence as suggestive of a consciousness–Phenomenon link.
- **Garry Nolan contactee measurements** — Nolan and colleagues are said to be isolating observable effects in contactees' immune systems. Because no methods or results are detailed, the example supplies a research direction rather than an independently assessable experiment.
- **Mack and Jacobs collections** — their volumes of abductee experience provide the chapter's claimed data set. The key stated feature is consistency in confrontation-with-the-Other narratives despite scientific dismissal; no count, coding method, or individual case is supplied here.
- **Planned research sequence** — the authors say later units will examine reliable abduction accounts with attention to how communication is described; introduce information theory, AI, quantum physics, and recent neurobiology; review documented remote viewing and related phenomena; and test the top-down alternative.
- **Announced consciousness-theory roster** — chaos theory, quantum consciousness, and DNA consciousness will be discussed through work by David Chalmers, Larry Vandervert, Stanislas Dehaene, Sir Roger Penrose, and unnamed others. Their specific positions are deferred.
- **Genome-to-neuron progression** — the planned analysis asks first whether DNA is conscious or supports consciousness, then whether neurons generate it, and finally whether quantum effects occur at either level. Chaos and information theory are proposed as explanatory supplements.

## Thread
This chapter is the conceptual threshold for Section Two. It begins with the gap between measurable structure and lived quality, then shows the same explanatory incompleteness at increasingly small physical scales: ancient elements give way to forces, forces fail to unify around gravity, and quantum components cease behaving like ordinary objects. That sequence lets the authors formulate their governing possibility without presenting it as a result: consciousness might be universal, brains might receive rather than generate it, and a shared quantum-level medium might make otherwise impossible contact conceivable. The named researchers and promised theories are a roadmap, not yet evidence for a solution; the chapter's work is to define the problem and identify the levels—genome, neuron, quantum domain, and reported encounter—at which later chapters will look for one.
