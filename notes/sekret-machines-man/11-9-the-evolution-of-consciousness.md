# The Evolution of Consciousness

## In one paragraph
DeLonge and Levenda contrast two broad accounts of consciousness: the emergent/computational view associated here with Ray Kurzweil, in which evolving brains eventually yield conscious machines at the Singularity, and the quantum view represented by Penrose and Hameroff's Orch OR, in which consciousness arises from events at the Planck scale and may be intrinsic to the universe. They first detail a hypothesized Cambrian origin more than 500 million years ago, citing lamprey and Pikaia research, synaptic gene expansion, sensory development, and coevolution of bodies and nervous systems, but stress that this origin remains hypothetical and depends on the assumption that consciousness requires a physical substrate. The chapter then introduces quantum probability, particle-wave behavior, observation, uncertainty, non-locality, entanglement, photosynthesis, and a 2010 DNA-entanglement theory to argue that warm biological systems can exhibit quantum effects, while warning that paranormal extrapolations from subatomic results often make false equivalences. Orch OR locates orchestrated quantum computation in 25-nanometer neuronal microtubules and treats conscious moments as objective reductions linked to fine-scale space-time; the cited theory is distinguished from the authors' further speculation that comparable structures could support alien telepathy, persistent abductee "tagging," and different forms of nonhuman consciousness. The chapter ends by extending Hameroff's proposed universal pleasure/reward loop to alien reproductive curiosity, cattle mutilations, and one-way contact, but these are explicitly questions and inferences rather than reported demonstrations of alien motive or quantum communication.

## Core claims

### Two reported schools of consciousness
- The first school treats consciousness as an emergent property of evolved brains. It identifies consciousness with computation and expects sufficiently powerful computers to cross a technological threshold called the Singularity, after which machines and humans will be indistinguishable. Ray Kurzweil is named as an influential scientist and philosopher in this camp.
- The second school rejects the equivalence of consciousness and computation. Its objection is that the first school has not defined consciousness or identified and quantified a threshold at which computation turns into awareness, so claims of future machine consciousness remain speculative rather than experimentally grounded.
- The second view locates consciousness outside the classical domain, in the quantum world at the Planck scale: $1.6 \times 10^{-35}$ meters, where classical physics ceases to apply. Orch OR is presented as this chapter's principal representative.
- The chapter does not declare either school settled. It develops evolutionary evidence for a physical substrate and quantum evidence that may complicate a purely classical computational account.

### Reported Cambrian and genetic research
- Peer-reviewed papers published in 2012 and 2013 reportedly placed the evolutionary beginning of what humans call consciousness in the Cambrian Period, more than 500 million years ago.
- The theory uses vertebrate fossils containing rudimentary spinal cords and brains and focuses on the lamprey, a jawless, eel-like fish with a toothed sucker mouth. A modern descendant survives; the chapter calls it both a pest and a food in some cultures, adding that lamprey pie is a delicacy for the Queen of England.
- The inference depends on a premise: a brain and spinal column are prerequisites for consciousness. If the earliest known brain-and-spine system belongs to a Cambrian lamprey ancestor, then consciousness began there, and no terrestrial consciousness existed earlier.
- Todd E. Feinberg and John Mallatt's article, "The evolutionary and genetic origins of consciousness in the Cambrian Period over 500 million years ago," appeared in *Frontiers in Psychology* in October 2013, volume 4, article 667.
- The chapter explicitly calls the proposed Cambrian origin of vertebrate consciousness a hypothesis rather than a definitive finding.
- Feinberg and Mallatt are said to agree with Francis Crick and Christof Koch's earlier conclusion that the visual system is the most productive place to study consciousness's origins and the brain's construction of conscious experience.
- Crick and Koch's "Towards a neurobiological theory of consciousness" appeared in *Seminars in Neuroscience*, volume 2, in 1990, pp. 263–275.
- Edinburgh-based researchers also located consciousness's origin in the Cambrian but attributed increased brain size and intelligence to a genetic accident that expanded the number of "brain genes" in a Pikaia, described as similar to a hagfish and possessing an early spinal column.
- A footnote preserves an important uncertainty: there is no present consensus in the chapter over whether Pikaia was a vertebrate or invertebrate.
- The same genetic expansion reportedly increased vulnerability to mental disease. The researchers suggested that autism and schizophrenia can be traced along the evolutionary path to that initial accident.
- The work traces genes specific to post-synaptic density (PSD) and MAGUK-associated signaling complexes (MASC) from invertebrates to vertebrates and then to cranium-specific development.
- MAGUK is expanded as "membrane-associated guanylate kinases," a protein superfamily. PSD/MASC systems regulate protein synthesis and structural plasticity and underlie learning and memory.
- Protein synthesis is treated as a prerequisite for genetic development and complexity. The progression from spinal structure and rudimentary brain to developed brain and cord is used as a proxy for an increasingly complex consciousness.
- The cited papers are J. Nithianantharajah and colleagues, "Synaptic scaffold evolution generated components of vertebrate cognitive complexity," *Nature Neuroscience*, January 2013, 16(1), pp. 16–24, doi:10.1038/nn.3276; and Richard D. Emes and colleagues, "Evolutionary expansion and anatomical specialization of synapse proteome complexity," *Nature Neuroscience*, July 2008, 11(7), pp. 799–806, doi:10.1038/nn.2135.

### Assumptions and implications of the physical-substrate model
- The central assumption is materialist: consciousness requires a body, specifically a brain and nervous system. Brain death counts as death because the person and consciousness are assumed to cease when the brain stops, even if machines keep the rest of the body alive.
- A footnote raises near-death experience (NDE) as a challenge to the language of death: people revived after short clinical death retain memories and self-awareness, but medicine calls the event "near" death because return is taken to mean they were not truly dead. The authors call that logic debatable.
- Increasing brain and nervous-system complexity is said to produce increasingly sophisticated consciousness. The chapter ranks a lamprey below a cat or dog and those below a human in forms humans can appreciate, while acknowledging that this assumes human consciousness is superior.
- The materialist chain is body to consciousness, genes to body, and chemistry to genes. The authors identify the chain as an assumption rather than an explanation of subjective awareness.
- A jaw and four limbs are presented as markers in the development of human-type consciousness. Bilateral limb symmetry corresponds to the two hemispheres, which the chapter conventionally associates with logical/rational and imaginative/creative modes.
- Limbs and senses expand environmental information gathering and generate binary distinctions: right/left, up/down, forward/backward, front/behind, and possibly past/future. Ground travel expands range; the opposable thumb adds toolmaking.
- The authors reject a one-way picture in which limbs and senses merely extend a preexisting brain. Anatomy and nervous system coevolved, each reinforcing the other.
- Evolution selected out capacities that did not aid survival or that created weakness, while cooperation between both brain sides increased physical, intellectual, and creative range.
- Over roughly 500 million years, Cambrian pre-vertebrates and vertebrates led to a being able to communicate instantly over great distances and travel to the Moon and beyond.
- Greater complexity also creates vulnerability. The chapter says antimicrobial peptides in an ancient brain immune system protect the brain but, with age, produce a molecule that forms dementia-associated plaque; the process is not understood and is linked here to Alzheimer's disease.
- The authors ask whether consciousness altered by Alzheimer's, autism, schizophrenia, or hundreds of other conditions is "real" and whether a baseline consciousness exists. These questions expose the difficulty of grading consciousness by healthy human behavior.

### DeLonge and Levenda's evolutionary inferences
- The authors speculate that evolution may have discarded capacities useful in the modern world because those capacities once impaired survival amid predators, climate instability, and hostile neighbors.
- They claim there is "some evidence" of a lost characteristic in ancient literature but do not identify a particular text in this chapter. Their proposal is that modern consciousness may be a scaled-down or altered version of an earlier capacity.
- They ask whether consciousness preceded brains and was already present in genes, driving nervous systems toward complexity. They then ask why inquiry should stop even at genes.
- The radio analogy distinguishes consciousness's existence from biological access: a receiver that catches one station does not prove only one station exists. Biological complexity may make consciousness more accessible rather than create it.
- The computer-operator analogy likewise separates an organized mechanism from an animating source. CPU, RAM, ROM, sensors, and limbs remain inert until an operator turns the machine on and supplies instructions.
- Zecharia Sitchin's account of aliens creating humans to mine gold and reports of Grays controlled by taller Nordics are introduced as expressions of the suspicion that humans or Visitors may be machines. The authors do not validate either account.
- If DNA was seeded, the authors ask whether a machine performed the seeding and whether its purpose was to create self-generating automata. Their culminating formulation—whether humanity is a "sekret machine"—is speculative.

### Reported quantum-mechanical principles
- Quantum mechanics (QM) is probabilistic. Einstein's reported statement that God does not play dice with the universe expresses resistance to replacing deterministic Newtonian expectation with chance.
- The chapter says QM has been demonstrated repeatedly but remains difficult to reconcile with Newtonian mechanics and especially Einsteinian relativity, creating apparently contradictory physical regimes.
- The chapter contrasts the classical worlds associated with Newton and Einstein with the quantum tradition of Max Planck, Niels Bohr, Heisenberg, Schrödinger, and Pauli. Its point is that both descriptions must somehow belong to one reality.
- Schrödinger's Cat and the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle are cited as familiar fragments that do not by themselves provide a complete account of QM.
- Particle-wave duality means quantum entities can appear as particles or waves depending on observation. The chapter suggests that this dependence gives consciousness a role, though it does not establish the nature of that role.
- In the double-slit account, photons sent one at a time through one narrow slit produce one vertical dot pattern. Two parallel slits unexpectedly produce multiple bands: an interference pattern associated with wave behavior.
- A ball analogy clarifies the surprise: two apertures should produce two directly aligned marks, but wave-like ripples interfere and distribute marks across several bands.
- When experimenters place a sensor at the slits to observe the photon's passage, the photon remains particle-like and the interference pattern disappears. The act of measurement changes the outcome; the chapter calls the transition the collapse of the wave function.
- The authors add that the entity may be neither particle nor wave but a disturbance in a quantum field with both kinds of qualities.
- The standard schoolroom picture of electrons orbiting an atomic nucleus like planets is described as a teaching schematic. The chapter instead gives a probability cloud and says no quantum result or electron location can be predicted except statistically.
- The uncertainty principle is summarized as the inability to locate a particle precisely in both space and time.
- Non-locality and entanglement mean two particles that interact can remain described as connected across enormous separation. An operation on one immediately affects the other, without a speed-of-light delay.
- The chapter's illustrative example places entangled particles in New York and Tokyo and says making one spin clockwise makes the other do so immediately. It says the effect has been tested repeatedly.
- Because instantaneous transfer appears to violate relativity's speed limit, some physicists are reported to interpret the event as information transfer, with information treated as neither matter nor wave and thus not constrained by light speed.
- Matter particles named are electrons, quarks, neutrinos, and muons; force carriers named are photons, bosons, and gluons. Their laws differ from macroscopic behavior, and the chapter says integrating QM with the standard model remains impossible.

### Caveats about quantum and paranormal extrapolation
- Many scientists reject or ridicule "quantum consciousness" because non-locality, superposition, and other poorly understood ideas are often used by nonspecialists to support telepathy, ghosts, UFOs, ESP, remote viewing, teleportation, or astral projection.
- The chapter says quantum discoveries also excited mystics, gurus, and con men; the grouping is the authors' warning that suggestive vocabulary can be exploited without a valid physical bridge.
- The authors concede that many enthusiasts lack training in classical or quantum physics and cannot defend their applications. They also criticize dismissive scientific arrogance for increasing conflict and misunderstanding.
- Critics object to taking an individual subatomic result such as double-slit behavior and projecting it directly into ordinary experience. On the conventional boundary described here, Planck-scale effects disappear at atomic and molecular scales, where classical rules forbid faster-than-light travel, macroscopic superposition, and a living Schrödinger cat.
- Linking entanglement to astral projection merely joins two things labeled "spooky action at a distance" and can be a false equivalence. The chapter explicitly states that this is a considerable leap.
- Early objections to quantum processes in the brain emphasized that brains are warm, wet, and noisy, whereas quantum effects were assumed to require extremely cold, dry conditions. This objection was aimed at Penrose and Hameroff's late-1990s Orch OR proposal.

### Reported quantum biology
- The chapter says the debate changed by 2014 as peer-reviewed research reported quantum effects in photosynthesis and in migratory-bird brains, encouraging Penrose and Hameroff to defend Orch OR more forcefully.
- Articles beginning in 2007 proposed that plants convert sunlight into energy through quantum-mechanical molecular vibrations and energy exchange involving negative values in probability distributions, whereas classical physics recognizes only positive probability distributions.
- By 2014, the chapter says, evidence had made the quantum account of photosynthesis widely accepted. Its citation is "Quantum mechanics explains efficiency of photosynthesis," January 9, 2014, at phys.org.
- Photosynthesis occurs at biological temperature and in a visible organism, so it challenges the claim that quantum effects require only super-cold, super-dry laboratory conditions or remain wholly inaccessible at macroscopic scale.
- The chapter's mechanism begins with solar photons entering plant cells. Chlorophyll absorbs a photon, loses an electron, and transfers it down an electron chain while synthesizing food from carbon dioxide and water.
- Because photons and electrons are subatomic, the process already occupies quantum territory. The authors say classical explanations of photosynthesis had not succeeded.
- **Authors' inference:** If food, oxygen, and life depend on quantum energy transfer, other quantum mechanisms may connect humans with nature, one another, and terrestrial or nonterrestrial species. The cited photosynthesis work does not itself demonstrate any such interpersonal or extraterrestrial connection.

### Reported DNA-entanglement theory
- Elisabeth Rieper and colleagues at the National University of Singapore theorized in 2010 that a DNA nucleotide can be modeled as a cloud of negatively charged electrons around a positively charged nucleus.
- Interaction between the negative cloud and positive nucleus creates an oscillation called a phonon. To form a base pair, the two nucleotides' clouds must oscillate in opposite directions.
- Phonons are quantum objects and can become entangled. The theory therefore proposes that entanglement holds DNA's double helix together.
- The chapter carefully says this is not yet "DNA consciousness." It is evidence, in the account presented, for a quantum aspect of the genetic code and another route by which quantum effects could occur at atmospheric or biological conditions.
- The authors note implications for constructing quantum computers and infer that comparable quantum effects may occur in human brains, potentially mediating consciousness.

### Penrose and Hameroff's reported Orch OR theory
- Strong-AI advocates regard the brain as an advanced computer and predict that algorithms and faster processing will generate consciousness. Kurzweil is again named as a representative.
- The chapter says this view generally allows degrees of animal consciousness but denies plant consciousness. Plants react to light, water, and temperature but do not actively relocate or possess human senses; a compass pointing north provides the analogy for response without choice. "Vegetative state" supplies the human linguistic comparison.
- The authors infer that an embodied AI approaching human consciousness may need sensors analogous to the CNS. A fixed desktop or laboratory computer might remain "vegetative," though strong-AI proponents emphasize algorithm and processing growth instead.
- Sir Roger Penrose is identified as a mathematician and physicist; Stuart Hameroff as an anesthesiologist and professor emeritus of anesthesiology and psychology. They argue that brain processes are nonalgorithmic and that consciousness requires joining quantum mechanics with classical theory.
- The chapter philosophically describes physical laws as inventions or projections through which consciousness organizes experience rather than concrete objects. Since both classical and quantum physics are held true and both were discovered by conscious minds, the authors reason that both may be needed to explain consciousness.
- Orch OR's physical substrate is the microtubule (MT), a hollow structure about 25 nanometers in diameter found in cytoskeletons from single-celled organisms through brain neurons. A nanometer is one billionth of a meter.
- Microtubules consist of hexagonal protein lattices and support movement, cell division, dendrite and axon extension, and synaptic formation.
- The microtubule protein tubulin exhibits dynamic switching patterns compared with cellular automata in John Horton Conway's 1970 Game of Life. Microtubules are also reported to participate in electrochemical signaling and communication, not merely structural support.
- Penrose and Hameroff propose that microtubules bridge classical and quantum processes and make non-locality, entanglement, and superposition foundational to consciousness.
- Their 2014 review, "Consciousness in the universe: A review of the 'Orch OR' theory," appeared in *Physics of Life Reviews*, volume 11, pp. 39–78. The chapter's extended definition of Orch OR is cited specifically to p. 73, while its proto-consciousness account is cited to pp. 39–78.
- The cited theory connects biomolecular brain processes with the universe's basic structure. Consciousness consists of discrete objective-reduction (OR) events terminating quantum states or computations; when appropriately orchestrated in neuronal microtubules, these events become conscious moments.
- Objective reduction is said to occur ubiquitously as a bridge between quantum and classical worlds. In brains, quantum computations in microtubules connect biology to the fine-scale geometry of space-time.
- Penrose and Hameroff describe proto-conscious physical events as always having existed. Biology evolved mechanisms that orchestrated them, coupled them to neurons, gave them experiential qualities, and allowed conscious causal control of behavior.
- On this account consciousness is an intrinsic feature of the universe's action, or in the authors' paraphrase an emergent property of the universe rather than merely the brain.

### Hameroff's reported consciousness-and-pleasure proposal
- In "A brief history of (a study of) consciousness," a talk delivered October 22, 2015, at the Science and Nonduality (SAND) Conference, Hameroff proposed that the universe arises from consciousness and that consciousness preceded life and drove life's origin and evolution.
- The authors note that Asian scriptures contain older versions of consciousness preceding the material world; they distinguish Hameroff's proposal by its scientific framing.
- Hameroff's evolutionary account is contrasted with Richard Dawkins's "selfish gene" model and compared with Sigmund Freud's pleasure principle.
- Reproduction of mammals or proteins is said to proceed through reward and pleasure rather than a gene's abstract aim of immortality. The authors reason that a gene cannot predict long-term reproductive strategy but can respond to immediate pleasure/pain and reward/punishment.
- The proposed feedback loop is universal at a basic level but could appear differently in different species.

### DeLonge and Levenda's quantum/contact extrapolations
- The authors describe gods, spirits, demons, jinn, UFOs, and EBEs as invisible ideas or entities that have become confabulated in human accounts. UFOs and Visitors are said to be visible, experienced, tracked, and investigated only intermittently.
- Ordinary perception contextualizes a bird or unknown person against prior examples, rapidly supplying identity, gender, age, and other categories. The Phenomenon resists this neuronal cleanup because it arrives unpredictably, lacks the ordinary historical context provided by climate, economy, violence, technology, politics, race, and other everyday forces, behaves outside familiar physical and cultural norms, and disappears without warning.
- UFOs and Visitors are reported to follow commercial and military aircraft as if inviting pursuit or attack. The authors ask whether their apparently impossible behavior and communication exploit undiscovered quantum effects rather than violate physics.
- The quantum/classical relation is compared with chess: the flat sixty-four-square board is the quantum background, pieces are Newtonian objects, and rules plus interaction approximate Einsteinian relativity. Board and pieces can be examined separately, but only together constitute the game.
- The authors call the board or quantum background "the matrix" and warn that extending the analogy to colored squares as particles/waves or matter/dark matter would take it too far.
- They connect the chessboard's 64 squares with the genetic code's 64 possible three-part combinations, the I Jing's 64 hexagrams, and the Yoruban Ifa system's 256 odu ($64 \times 4$). In this chapter the source refers to four amino acids in groups of three; the note preserves the source's formulation without silently correcting or expanding it.
- **Authors' speculation:** DNA may be a pathway into the quantum world linking genetics, consciousness, and QM, while life events may be pieces on an existential board and the quantum domain the unconscious mind of classical physics.
- **Authors' synthesis:** Quantum biology may reconcile reductionist science with spiritual or intuitive approaches, analogous to bringing the rational left and creative right halves into a middle way.
- **Authors' alien-communication inference:** If consciousness is intrinsic to universal quantum events, other beings could be conscious through microtubules or analogous structures even while possessing different qualia and interpretations.
- **Authors' speculation:** Human and alien microtubules could communicate through quantum entanglement. The authors use this to suggest a mechanism for abductees' claims that abductors remain in contact and can locate them anywhere, as though they were tagged. Orch OR itself, as quoted here, does not report an alien-contact test.
- Quantum computation and quantum teleportation are named as active applications, with teleportation limited so far to individual particles rather than organisms.
- **Authors' alien-reproduction speculation:** If Hameroff's pleasure loop operates differently under another planet's environment, an alien species may reproduce only in cycles, favor logic over pleasure, or find continuous human sexuality puzzling.
- Cattle mutilations associated with UFO reports are proposed as a possible comparison case for alien study of reproduction. The chapter does not establish who mutilated cattle or why.
- A nonorganic android or robot Visitor might be especially curious. Human grave robbing and corpse dissection are used as an analogy for investigating another body's inner workings.
- The authors ask whether Visitors seek human pleasure centers and sexual motivation rather than merely reproductive organs, and whether their consciousness follows neither Hameroff's pleasure model nor Dawkins's selfish-gene model.
- Different alien neural structures could produce a consciousness unequal to and unlike a human's. Reported one-way communication from alien to abductee implies, in the authors' view, some similarity, but neither the structure nor vulnerabilities of alien consciousness are known.

## Concepts
- **Emergent property of the brain** — consciousness understood as an outcome or "secretion" of sufficient neural complexity rather than an independent feature of reality.
- **Consciousness as computation** — the strong-AI premise that brain processing and algorithmic machine processing differ in degree rather than kind.
- **Singularity** — the proposed technological threshold at which machine capability and consciousness make a computer indistinguishable from a human.
- **Planck scale** — the quantum boundary given as $1.6 \times 10^{-35}$ meters, below which classical physics no longer applies.
- **Cambrian-consciousness hypothesis** — the reported proposal that consciousness began more than 500 million years ago with the first fossil evidence of a brain and spinal column.
- **Lamprey** — the jawless, sucker-mouthed fish whose Cambrian ancestor is used as the earliest vertebrate-consciousness candidate.
- **Pikaia genetic accident** — the Edinburgh researchers' proposed expansion of brain genes in a hagfish-like early spinal creature, increasing intelligence, brain size, and mental-disease risk.
- **Post-synaptic density / MASC** — synaptic gene and protein systems tied to protein synthesis, structural plasticity, learning, and memory; their evolutionary expansion tracks increasing neural complexity.
- **Physical substrate** — a material body, brain, nervous system, gene, microtubule, or other structure required to instantiate consciousness.
- **Brain-as-receiver** — the alternative to emergence: evolving nervous systems increase access to preexisting consciousness like better radios receiving more stations.
- **Bicameral/bilateral development** — the chapter's association of two brain hemispheres, paired limbs, cross-body control, and binary categories with the expansion of environmental intelligence.
- **Baseline consciousness** — a hypothetical standard against which consciousness altered by disease or different biology might be measured; the authors question whether such a standard exists.
- **Sekret machine** — the chapter's question whether a human is a biologically self-generating automaton designed or seeded by another intelligence.
- **Quantum mechanics** — the probabilistic physical framework governing subatomic particles, characterized here by particle-wave duality, uncertainty, non-locality, entanglement, and measurement-sensitive outcomes.
- **Particle-wave duality** — the same quantum entity's particle-like or wave-like behavior under different measurement conditions.
- **Double-slit experiment** — photons passing two unobserved slits form wave interference, while measurement at a slit yields particle-like bands, illustrating measurement's relation to outcome.
- **Collapse of the wave function** — the transition from a spread of quantum possibilities or wave behavior to a measured particle outcome.
- **Uncertainty** — the limit on simultaneously precise location in space and time, represented through an electron probability cloud rather than a fixed orbit.
- **Non-locality / quantum entanglement** — shared quantum description and immediate correlated change across distance, apparently unconstrained by light-travel time in the chapter's account.
- **Quantum field disturbance** — the authors' alternative language for something that may not literally be either particle or wave but can display both kinds of behavior.
- **Quantum biology** — evidence or theory that biological systems such as photosynthesis and DNA use quantum processes at ordinary biological temperatures.
- **Negative probability distribution** — the nonclassical probability feature invoked in the chapter's account of molecular vibration and energy transfer during photosynthesis.
- **Chessboard matrix** — analogy in which the quantum domain is the board, Newtonian objects are pieces, and relativistic rules/interactions produce the game-like whole.
- **DNA entanglement** — Rieper and colleagues' theory that opposite electron-cloud oscillations and entangled phonons bind nucleotide base pairs and stabilize the double helix.
- **Phonon** — the quantum oscillation produced by interaction between a nucleotide's negative electron cloud and positive nucleus.
- **Strong AI** — the prediction that larger algorithms and faster processing will cause computers to acquire consciousness and integrate with humanity.
- **Microtubule / tubulin** — a 25-nanometer hollow protein-lattice structure in cells and neurons; its tubulin switching and signaling functions make it Orch OR's proposed quantum substrate.
- **Cellular automata / Game of Life** — Conway's 1970 rule-based switching model, used as an analogy for dynamic tubulin patterns.
- **Orchestrated objective reduction (Orch OR)** — Penrose and Hameroff's theory that orchestrated terminations of quantum computations in neuronal microtubules produce discrete conscious moments linked to space-time geometry.
- **Proto-conscious events** — noncognitive objective reductions said to be intrinsic to physical action before biology evolved means to orchestrate them.
- **Fine-scale space-time geometry** — the universe's basic structural level to which microtubule computations are proposed to connect brain biology.
- **Pleasure principle / universal feedback loop** — Hameroff's proposed reward/punishment driver of reproduction and evolution, compared with Freud and contrasted with gene-level foresight.
- **Selfish gene** — Dawkins's model of genes pursuing persistence through hosts, criticized here because genes cannot know future strategies.
- **Alien qualia** — the different subjective world expected from unlike biology, nervous systems, environments, and sensory structures.
- **Quantum alien communication** — DeLonge and Levenda's speculation that entanglement through microtubules or analogous structures could support telepathy or persistent contact; it is not a reported Orch OR result.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Jack Parsons letter, January 27, 1950** — the epigraph calls humans "not brains but fields" and says inside and outside, guts, blood, and skin must speak. Parsons is identified as a UFO experiencer, rocket scientist, and Jet Propulsion Laboratory founder.
- **Lamprey fossil reasoning** — rudimentary brain and cord in Cambrian vertebrate fossils are treated as the earliest physical marker of consciousness. The authors stress that the conclusion follows from a materialist premise and remains hypothesized.
- **Lamprey descriptions** — the animal resembles an eel, has a toothed sucker compared with smaller versions of the sandworms in *Dune*, survives today, is often treated as a pest, and is eaten by some cultures, including in royal lamprey pie.
- **The plate perception sequence** — an eye receives a circle as an ellipse from perspective; within nanoseconds neurons compare possible shapes, correct it to a circle, identify its function as a plate, and maintain identity while it is lifted, rotated, washed, and set to dry.
- **Millions of coordinated neurons** — they recognize geometry, assign "plate," track movement, identify color, estimate weight and size, register sound if dropped, and coordinate stereoscopic depth and hand movement. Neurology understands the macro process better than the micro/neuronal details.
- **Why wash the plate?** — the decision introduces memory, personal history, cultural valuation of dirty versus clean, and motivation, showing that accurate sensory computation does not solve voluntary intention.
- **Pikaia brain-gene expansion** — increased PSD/MASC complexity reportedly produced larger brains and learning capacity but also greater vulnerability, with autism and schizophrenia traced speculatively to the Cambrian genetic event.
- **Brain death and NDE caveat** — materialism identifies brain cessation with the person's death, while revival with memory intact is redescribed as near death. The authors challenge the circularity without providing an alternative medical model.
- **Lamprey–cat–dog–human ranking** — escalating body complexity is used to infer escalating consciousness, but the authors expose its anthropocentric dependence on what humans recognize as sophistication.
- **Jaw, limbs, and information collection** — bilateral limbs support travel, touch, spatial binaries, and extensive data gathering; the opposable thumb enables tools. Nervous and bodily systems are described as mutually developing.
- **Five-hundred-million-year trajectory** — simple Cambrian organisms eventually yield humans who communicate over vast distances instantly and travel to and beyond the Moon.
- **Antimicrobial-peptide reversal** — an ancient immune protection becomes harmful with age by producing plaque-forming material associated with dementia. The example makes neural complexity inseparable from new failure modes.
- **Lost-capacity questions** — traits selected out as liabilities under prehistoric danger might be useful now. Ancient literature is said to preserve signs, but no specific text or measured gene is given.
- **One-station radio** — limited biological reception cannot establish that consciousness itself is limited. Complexity could expand access rather than manufacture the signal.
- **Computer operator** — an intelligent-looking machine with CPU, memory, inputs, and outputs remains inert until operated. The analogy supports an external source but does not identify one.
- **Sitchin, Grays, Nordics, and other proposed races** — Sitchin's gold-mining creators and tales of Grays commanded by taller Nordics exemplify nested creator/machine suspicions; the authors place them among dozens of proposed alien races. They are reported narratives, not verified biology.
- **Bird and stranger perception** — prior birds and humans furnish a context from which neurons instantly categorize unfamiliar instances. The Phenomenon allegedly denies this stable historical library.
- **Aircraft pursuit behavior** — UFOs reportedly track commercial and military aircraft as if daring pilots to pursue or fire. The authors ask whether undiscovered quantum control explains the display.
- **Double-slit experiment** — one slit creates one vertical distribution; two unmeasured slits create multiple interference bands; sensors restore particle-like behavior. The authors use the repeatable result to introduce measurement, not to prove consciousness causes collapse.
- **Ball and pond analogies** — balls should map directly through two slits, while intersecting ripples from two stones naturally create interference. Together they make particle-to-wave behavior intuitively strange.
- **Electron cloud** — atomic orbital diagrams are teaching aids; a probability cloud better expresses uncertain location. The chapter summarizes the limit as locating a particle in space or time, but not both.
- **New York/Tokyo entanglement** — partnered particles are said to change spin immediately across the cities. The chapter labels the effect repeatedly tested and discusses information as a possible nonmaterial transfer.
- **Quantum menagerie** — electrons, quarks, neutrinos, muons, photons, bosons, and gluons demonstrate that the periodic table does not exhaust smaller matter and force components.
- **False paranormal equivalence** — saying entanglement and astral projection are both spooky does not make them the same mechanism. This is the chapter's clearest restraint on its own later speculation.
- **Classical-scale boundary examples** — at the macro level, the chapter says faster-than-light travel and communication are impossible, objects cannot occupy more than one place, and the cat shut in a box with plutonium is always dead. The deliberately blunt examples contrast ordinary expectations with quantum descriptions.
- **Warm/wet/noisy objection** — critics said quantum brain effects were impossible outside cold, dry environments. Penrose and Hameroff's late-1990s proposal was dismissed on this basis.
- **Photosynthesis timeline, 2007–2014** — publications beginning in 2007 proposed quantum molecular vibration and negative-probability energy exchange; by 2014 the chapter says the evidence was widely accepted.
- **Chlorophyll electron chain** — absorbed sunlight removes an electron from chlorophyll and passes it along while plants make food from carbon dioxide and water. The result sustains food and oxygen at biological temperature.
- **Two thrones in Heaven** — a phrase borrowed from Jewish mysticism expresses the intolerability of two unrelated realities, one quantum/paranormal and one classical/ordinary.
- **Chessboard model** — sixty-four flat squares enable three-dimensional pieces and strategic action. Board without pieces and pieces without board do not produce chess; quantum background, Newtonian objects, and relativistic rules are likewise made mutually necessary by analogy.
- **Genetic/divinatory sixty-four pattern** — chess, the genetic matrix, and the I Jing each use 64; the Table of Ifa expands to 256 odu. The authors treat the recurrence as suggestive, not as a demonstrated quantum mechanism.
- **Rieper DNA theory, 2010** — opposite nucleotide-cloud oscillations create entangled phonons that reportedly bind the helix. National University of Singapore is the named institution; no consciousness outcome is claimed.
- **Compass and plant responses** — pointing north or turning toward sunlight can arise mechanically without choice, illustrating why simple environmental response does not automatically establish consciousness.
- **Embodied-computer scenario** — a stationary computer may lack the active sensory engagement thought necessary for mind, whereas a system duplicating CNS sensors could approach the relevant biological conditions.
- **Microtubule structure** — 25-nanometer hollow lattices occur from single cells to neurons, help movement and division, extend dendrites and axons, make synapses, and carry electrochemical signals.
- **Conway comparison, 1970** — tubulin switching resembles the Game of Life's cellular automata, suggesting computation inside the neuron's support structure.
- **Orch OR event sequence** — microtubules sustain quantum computation; objective reduction terminates a state; biological orchestration turns terminations into meaningful experience and behavior linked to fine space-time.
- **Quantum technology boundary** — quantum computing is under development, and teleportation has reached particles, not whole organisms. The limitation separates actual application from science-fiction extension.
- **Tagged-abductee hypothesis** — claims that abductors can always locate experiencers are compared with persistent entanglement. This is DeLonge and Levenda's conjecture; no abductee, microtubule measurement, or entanglement test is reported.
- **Hameroff SAND talk, October 22, 2015** — consciousness precedes and drives life; reward and pleasure create a basic feedback loop. The chapter contrasts this with Dawkins's gene immortality and Freud's psychological pleasure principle.
- **Gene foresight argument** — a gene cannot calculate its future immortality but can respond to immediate reward or pain. The authors present this as why feedback appears more plausible than strategic foresight.
- **Seasonal mating comparison** — some terrestrial animals reproduce only during set cycles. A differently evolved alien species could likewise lack or misunderstand continuous human sexual motivation.
- **Cattle-mutilation comparison** — UFO-linked mutilation reports are associated speculatively with study of animal and human reproduction. No responsible actor or experimental result is identified.
- **Human body-dissection analogy** — historical grave robbery and corpse study show that a species can violate bodies from intellectual curiosity. The analogy is used to make alien or robotic examination conceivable, not to prove it occurs.
- **One-way telepathy problem** — reported contact flows mainly from Visitor to abductee. The authors infer enough consciousness similarity for communication but leave alien neurobiology and motive unknown.

## Thread
Chapter 9 tests the DNA-source inference from Chapter 8 against two competing histories. A material history begins with Cambrian neural anatomy, gene expansion, sensory reach, learning, and ever more complex brains; a quantum history begins with universal physical events that biology eventually learns to orchestrate. The authors do not erase the evolutionary account: they retain its fossils, genes, coevolving limbs, and vulnerabilities, but reinterpret biological complexity as a better receiver for a preexisting consciousness rather than necessarily its creator. Quantum biology supplies the bridge by placing photosynthesis and, theoretically, DNA entanglement in warm living systems; Orch OR then locates conscious reductions within neuronal microtubules. From there the chapter crosses from cited theory into project-specific speculation: if universal quantum events support human awareness, analogous structures might support alien awareness, telepathy, persistent contact, and radically different motives. The explicit false-equivalence warning remains the necessary check: quantum strangeness makes those possibilities discussable in the authors' view, but it does not by itself demonstrate them.
