# Prologue

## In one paragraph
DeLonge and Levenda open with a dramatized encounter in which a Stone Age people interpret twentieth-century military transport, machines, supplies, and personnel as divine beings and gifts, then reveal the setting as the South Pacific in 1942 and name the resulting religious pattern a cargo cult. A dated sequence then links Walter Dornberger's June 1947 arrival at Wright Field and the New Mexico crash, William Dudley Pelley's occult and pro-Nazi career, the July 1952 Washington UFO overflights and official response, and Andrija Puharich's June 1953 Round Table seance with a network of wealthy, scientific, entertainment, military, and intelligence-adjacent participants. The authors state that their project assumes rather than tries to prove the reality of UFOs and long-running human contact with non-human beings, and defines the Phenomenon broadly enough to include physical evidence, anomalous communication, trauma, religious visions, paranormal events, and institutional confusion. Guided by Jeffrey Kripal's refusal to sharply separate the real, religious, and fictional, they propose a multimedia, multidisciplinary project intended to change the worldview through which humanity interprets both contact and reality. Their historical thesis is that civilization over at least ten thousand years may itself be a cargo cult formed after an original contact, the *ur-punkt* or Alien Genesis, while their practical claims reframe the loaded word *alien*, reject government disclosure as a sensible thing to await, and leave open whether reported re-engineering concerns alien machines or something much closer to humanity. The prologue ends by explaining *sekret* as street-inflected, Eastern European and Cold War-linked, suggestive of secretion or a "secreted" machine, and mystical in the Greek-derived sense of *secret*.

## Core claims
- The authors use the 1942 South Pacific cargo-cult scenario to argue that people without a technological vocabulary can understand unfamiliar machines through inherited religious categories, preserve artifacts as sacred relics, and restrict the resulting knowledge to initiates.
- They suggest that cargo-cult dynamics may not be a local modern exception but a recurring human pattern that could have existed everywhere since the beginning of recorded history.
- The prologue places Dornberger's arrival at Wright Field in June 1947 beside a New Mexico crash described variously as a weather balloon, Project Mogul, or an initially reported flying disk, and says that whatever crashed "ushered in a new reality."
- It places Pelley's occult alien communications and Puharich's later contact circle in a pattern where alleged beings from the sky offer guidance intended to produce political, spiritual, and cultural change.
- The authors characterize the July 1952 Washington overflights as a provocation that would have risked war if conducted by a terrestrial power, but say the Air Force publicly whitewashed the event while the government privately pursued propaganda, psychological warfare, and surveillance responses.
- They present Puharich's 1953 Round Table group as an extraordinary convergence of wealthy families, medicine, engineering, occultism, military work, entertainment, and proximity to intelligence and later assassination history, all organized around claimed instructions from beings in near-Earth orbit.
- The authors explicitly say the project is not intended to convince skeptical readers that UFOs are real and will not rehearse the usual mass of sighting reports.
- The project's declared premise is an "understanding" rather than a belief: UFOs are real, humans and non-human beings have interacted since recorded history began, and outcomes have ranged from edifying to dangerous and terrifying.
- The authors claim contact has expanded dramatically over the preceding seventy years and occurs daily around the world to ordinary people, so disclosure should not be expected from political or military leaders.
- They argue that official denial and citizen experience have produced a crisis of confidence and dangerous inaction before something that might be either a universal threat or a lost opportunity for extraordinary growth.
- **The Phenomenon** is deliberately broader than UFO, UAP, or flying saucers: the authors use it for a cross-historical complex of sightings, physical traces and contact, communications, apparent violations of physical law, unfamiliar propulsion, observer disorientation and trauma, religious visions, anomalies, telepathy, telekinesis, and political, military, and industrial confusion.
- Following Kripal, the project refuses sharp boundaries among the real, religious, and fictional and uses both fiction and nonfiction, films, mass media, social media, and a comprehensive platform in pursuit of something "analogous to the truth."
- The authors hope a multidisciplinary approach will legitimize open study of the Phenomenon and generate scientific, technological, philosophical, and educational advances.
- They argue that technological convergence has outpaced the psychological and philosophical mindset needed to inhabit it: people use twenty-first-century technology while thinking like seventeenth-century citizens.
- They describe the familiar worldview as an obsolete image or construct sustained by prejudice, cynicism, violence, and despair about human agency, and offer the project as an alternative intended to produce a cultural revolution in consciousness.
- The authors say accepted alien contact would inevitably require religious, cultural, political, scientific, and military thought leaders to overhaul and reframe their knowledge.
- They distinguish their thesis from an "ancient aliens" program: they do not intend to recycle that material or attribute every terrestrial anomaly to alien action.
- Their proposed historical framework is that ten thousand or more years of human civilization may be a cargo cult formed after one or more original contacts; they call the origin point the *ur-punkt* or Alien Genesis.
- The authors acknowledge that *alien* has become a pejorative used to demonize people and beliefs and to ridicule off-planet contact, but retain it provisionally because they lack a better term.
- They say alien motives and intentions cannot be fathomed directly and can only be deduced from collected evidence; even with that caveat, they reject waiting for government disclosure because no human government can control alien access or communication as it controls human contact, governments have failed to do so, and admitting that failure would expose institutional impotence.
- They treat alien-technology re-engineering as possible and say evidence suggests secret machines may have existed for decades or longer, but caution that the machines may not be what people think and that the object being re-engineered may be "much closer to home."
- They attribute the project's seemingly hyperbolic quality to humanity's weak understanding of itself and its place, not to the subject alone, and compare current cultural geocentrism to the worldview Galileo displaced scientifically in the seventeenth century.
- The authors disclose that Tom has seen UFOs and Peter has not. Peter's conviction rests on evidence; they argue that science does not normally require every investigator to observe a phenomenon personally, using bacteria and subatomic particles as analogies.
- They divide institutional resistance into two motives: deliberate withholding under the label of national security and a nonscientific cultural bias.
- They present their alternative as a modern, twenty-first-century reboot of old ideas intended to probe the mystery behind the Sekret Machines.
- The spelling *sekret* is deliberate: the authors connect it to street usage, Eastern Europe and Soviet Cold War machines, words meaning secretion in several European languages, the idea of a "secreted" machine, and the mystical sense of *secret*.

## Concepts
- **Cargo cult** — The anthropological and journalistic name the authors give to a religion formed when people interpret technologically delivered goods and their operators as divine. The prologue makes it a model for civilization itself: copied clothing, tools, roads, towers, rituals, and prayers preserve the appearance of an encounter whose mechanisms were not understood.
- **Light Beings** — The South Pacific witnesses' religious interpretation of personnel associated with brilliant lights, aircraft, vehicles, supplies, and amplified voices. Their illumination is both literal and initiatory in the reconstruction.
- **Sacred relics and restricted knowledge** — Leftover artifacts become repositories of power and knowledge, separated from ordinary people and displayed only on sacred days. Those who witnessed the beings first claim privileged access.
- **Secret machines** — Technology whose essence remains hidden when observers lack the words and concepts needed to describe it; ritual and arcane language preserve the encounter while concealing its operation.
- **Soulcraft** — The ideology Pelley developed from theories about race, spirituality, UFOs, and alien presence on Earth after his pro-Nazi activism and claimed contact.
- **Star Guests** — Pelley's term for the alien intelligence from which he said guidance had arrived through a trance medium since 1928.
- **Project Blue Book** — The Air Force UFO investigation that the authors say began in March 1952, months before the Washington overflights.
- **Robertson Panel** — The panel established in January 1953 after private government exploration of propaganda and psychological-warfare responses. According to the prologue, its twelve hours of meetings ended with rejection of further UFO investigation and a recommendation to surveil civilian UFO groups as possible subversive sources.
- **Apport** — An object said to materialize from nowhere in a seance; at the Round Table gathering it was cotton string distributed as the cord worn by Brahmins after a ceremony.
- **The Nine** — Nine claimed principles or forces that communicated through an Indian medium, proposed to use the participants, and were associated with beings aboard a spacecraft in near-Earth orbit. Nine participants gathered six months after the earlier communication.
- **ESP weaponization** — The military-linked ambition the authors attribute to Puharich, an Army medical officer, scientist, and occultist who wanted to make extrasensory perception operational as a weapon.
- **Alien contact** — Interaction between humans and non-human beings which the project treats as real, ancient, globally recurrent, and variable in effect rather than as a belief awaiting official confirmation.
- **Disclosure** — Public acknowledgment of alien contact. The authors regard government-centered disclosure as both strategically unlikely and conceptually absurd because it assumes governments control contact that they have demonstrably failed to control. They distinguish that argument from certainty about alien intentions, which they say can only be inferred from evidence.
- **The Phenomenon** — The authors' umbrella designation for experiences that cross historical, scientific, and religious contexts and blur real, religious, and fictional categories. It includes observable traces and encounters as well as physical, psychological, religious, paranormal, and institutional effects.
- **Kripal framework** — Jeffrey J. Kripal's refusal to draw sharp distinctions among the real, religious, and fictional, adopted as the rationale for the project's mixture of media and modes.
- **Convergence of technologies** — The contemporary joining of technologies which, the authors argue, has not been matched by the psychological and philosophical preparation needed for the resulting environment.
- **Worldview** — A culturally inherited image or construct that sets the limit of what people can see and accept. The authors call the current form obsolete even though scientific and technological horizons have expanded.
- **Cultural revolution in human consciousness** — The project's stated ambition: to use a reframed understanding of contact and reality to force revision across culture, religion, politics, science, and the military.
- **Alien Genesis / ur-punkt** — The proposed original contact or contacts from which the authors intend to reconstruct and re-imagine human history as a ten-thousand-year cargo cult.
- **Alien** — A necessary but inadequate provisional term. The authors say its pejorative associations demonize people or belief systems and reduce speakers to stereotypes of people in tinfoil hats or people hearing voices, thereby degrading discussion.
- **Re-engineering** — Reported government attempts to infer the operation of alien technology and derive human technologies from it. The authors retain the possibility but hint that the thing being re-engineered may be closer to human identity than an external machine.
- **National security** — One motive the authors assign to scientific, political, and military resistance: civilian information is withheld under this justification.
- **Cultural bias** — Their second explanation for institutional rejection, distinguished from evidence-based scientific judgment.
- **Sekret / secreted machine** — The project's chosen spelling, intended to distinguish its subject from ordinary secrecy and to evoke Cold War Eastern Europe, secretion, hidden production or emergence, and mystical meaning.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Dramatized first encounter under the tropical canopy** — The authors stage an unnamed Stone Age community that knows its tropical land intimately, fishes, gathers herbs and tubers, builds bamboo-and-grass huts, and raises families. On a clear, moonless, oppressively hot night, thunder-like sound, vibration, light that turns night into day, frightened animals, amplified inhuman voices, and moving lights are interpreted without a technological vocabulary. Most people flee; elders crawl toward the source because their social role requires them to understand. After moving about a kilometer down a mound sacred to their ancestors for reasons no one remembers, they see light-clothed beings, multidirectional spheres and faces, symbols without referents, and a gourd- or canoe-like vessel whose throbbing sound has continued for hours. The elders experience the event as spiritual initiation.
- **Morning supplies, aircraft, and infrastructure** — In daylight the elders see human-looking beings, a huge moving "building" distributing wrapped packages, and a second flying building that lands and disgorges more cargo. They observe food, clothing, water, unfamiliar implements, lights, a temple-like layout with a broad avenue, and a wooden high place from which beings seem to address the Father of the Sky. The contrast between scales is explicit: distance the people cover in an hour is a second's work for the machines.
- **Ritual imitation after departure** — The witnesses return with stories of sky beings and gifts, become initiated or "illuminated," and reason that only people accustomed to darkness could appreciate the divinity of light. They make ceremonial clothes and imitations of communication, flight, vision, and sound-making equipment. Artifacts left on the ground become sacred machines held apart and shown only on special days. The People build a broad jungle avenue and high watchtower, station elders to scan the sky, and pray, "Spirit of the Sky, Remember!"
- **The 1942 reveal** — The authors identify the community as a Stone Age people living during the twentieth century on South Pacific islands contested by Japanese, British, Australian, and American forces in the Second World War. They had not seen aircraft, motor vehicles, Japanese people, or Europeans. The resulting religion joins gods from the sky with wisdom, knowledge, Coca-Cola, hot dogs, machine guns, and medicine. Anthropologists and journalists call it a cargo cult; the authors say it still exists and may have existed everywhere since recorded history began.
- **John Frum shrine image** — A caption identifies a 1960s shrine on Tanna Island, Vanuatu, devoted to the cargo cult of John Frum. The authors ask whether its red cross copied the Red Cross markings seen on mysterious sky ships. Followers are said to believe John Frum was a World War II serviceman who will return with wealth and prosperity, or cargo, from the sky; the caption's cross interpretation is presented as a question, not a conclusion.
- **Walter Dornberger at Wright Field, June 1947** — The prologue describes the fifty-two-year-old, balding, middle-aged German major-general with an autocratic air and slight mustache arriving at Wright Field, Ohio, after two years in British custody as a war criminal. More than one thousand compatriots and former subordinates had traveled from Peenemunde to America. He is assigned guided-missile research because he developed the V-1 "buzzbomb" and V-2, described as the first long-range guided missile; more than three thousand V-2s struck London and Antwerp in the war's last months and killed thousands of civilians.
- **The former missile team in America** — The authors say SS Major Wernher von Braun was headed to White Sands Proving Ground while the rest of the team formed at Fort Bliss, Texas. Americans kept Dornberger away from direct command, but the narrative attributes to him confidence that physical separation could not undo the men's allegiance, ideals, or political and spiritual beliefs, despite wages of six dollars per day and poor food and lodging.
- **The New Mexico crash and Bell Aerospace** — The authors say Dornberger would become a Bell Aerospace executive three years later. At the time of his June 1947 arrival, roughly one thousand miles away in New Mexico, something had crashed: they list weather balloon, Project Mogul, and the Air Force's initial "flying disk" report as competing descriptions and do not resolve them. Whatever it was, they say, it established a new normal.
- **Operation Paperclip image caption** — A caption identifies Dornberger and von Braun as two of many Nazis brought to the United States under Operation Paperclip. It says von Braun went to Huntsville, Alabama, and Dornberger to Wright Field near Dayton, Ohio, in June 1947, the same month the caption says Roswell debris was sent there.
- **William Dudley Pelley's release, February 14, 1950** — At the federal prison in Terre Haute, Indiana, Pelley is released after eight years for sedition and returns to Noblesville. The authors call him a crazed mystic and say his ideas about race, spirituality, UFOs, and alien presence became Soulcraft.
- **Silver Legion and pro-Nazi network** — Pelley had led the Silver Legion in unconditional support of Hitler and opposition to the US government, especially Franklin D. Roosevelt's administration. The prologue associates him with Father Coughlin and Fritz Kuhn of the German-American Bund and says Pelley's had once been the largest pro-Nazi organization in the United States. After the war, his path, like George Adamski's, turned toward the stars; George Hunt Williamson is identified as both Pelley's friend and Adamski's confidant.
- **Pelley's Star Guests** — *Star Guests: Design for Mortality* appeared in 1950, the year of Pelley's release. The authors say his alien communications began at 9:00 p.m. on October 28, 1928, in a New York City apartment, where a trance medium delivered the first of many messages. As with Puharich's group three years after the book's publication, the guidance was intended to change American and global politics, spirituality, and culture.
- **Pelley wanted poster image** — The caption calls Pelley an American Nazi imprisoned for wartime sedition and says he believed in benign Star Guests after a 1928 New York seance.
- **Washington, DC overflights, July 1952** — The authors describe a squadron of UFOs passing over the US capital on two successive July weekends, recorded on film and seen by many witnesses. Public pressure forced the Air Force to hold what the prologue calls the largest press conference in American history to that date in order to debunk the sightings. The authors say the same act by a terrestrial nation would have been a gross provocation and possible incitement to war.
- **Blue Book and the Robertson Panel** — Project Blue Book had begun in March 1952. After the overflights, the authors say officials privately explored propaganda and psychological-warfare measures, modeled on methods recently used against European civilians in World War II, to discredit later civilian reports. The Robertson Panel convened in January 1953, met for twelve hours total, concluded further investigation did not merit resources, and recommended surveillance of civilian study groups as potential subversives. The prologue says the policy backfired but does not explain the mechanism here.
- **Round Table Foundation seance, June 27, 1953** — At 12:15 a.m. on a full-moon night near Augusta, Maine, a man from Puna, India, sits in a wooded house, handles beads, and channels alleged sky beings. A voice naming itself only as R announces an intention to create Brahmins. Cotton string appears as an apport and is distributed as the cord Brahmins wear after initiation; participants are told they have become Brahmins.
- **The earlier message and nine-person gathering** — Six months earlier, the same beings had called themselves nine principles and forces, offered to work with the group, distinguished peace from mere warlessness, and said they intended to "utilize" the humans. Nine people later gather to contact the nine beings and undergo the Brahmin ceremony.
- **Henry and Georgianna Jackson** — Both join the circle; Henry Jackson is described as later becoming an important, respected California hospital administrator.
- **Alice Bouverie, nee Astor** — She is characterized as wealthy, entitled, and mystically preoccupied. A later image caption identifies her as Ava Alice Muriel Astor, daughter of John Jacob Astor, who died aboard the Titanic, and names her as one of the Nine.
- **Carl Betz and Vonnie Beck** — Betz later becomes a Hollywood actor known as Donna Reed's television husband. The source says little is known of Beck.
- **Marcella Du Pont** — A friend of H. L. Mencken who later becomes important in another channeled movement by translating *The Urantia Book* into French.
- **Andrija Puharich** — The group's leader is described as a US Army medical officer at Edgewood Arsenal, scientist, occultist, advocate of weaponizing ESP, and later discoverer of Israeli psychic Uri Geller.
- **Arthur Young** — An engineer, occultist, and inventor credited with the Bell Helicopter. The image caption says he left Bell at the end of World War II to study the paranormal.
- **Ruth Forbes Young and her network** — The body calls Arthur's wife Ruth Forbes Young, a socialite and best friend of Mary Bancroft, who was Allen Dulles's mistress. Ruth is mother to Michael Paine, a Bell Aerospace employee in Dallas, and mother-in-law to Ruth Paine, who obtained Lee Harvey Oswald's job at the Texas School Book Depository. A later caption calls her Ruth Forbes Paine; the note preserves the body's naming in frontmatter while recording the caption's wording here.
- **The Nine as an elite network** — The authors emphasize the presence of a Forbes, Du Pont, and Astor; medical men Henry Jackson and Puharich; engineer Arthur Young; and Ruth Young's intelligence-adjacent and assassination-linked social connections. They say the group believed it was receiving instructions from beings aboard a spacecraft in near-Earth orbit.
- **Arthur and Ruth Young image caption** — The caption calls Arthur the Bell Helicopter designer and says Arthur and Ruth belonged to the Nine, described there as nine influential Americans, including an Astor and DuPont, who believed they contacted UFO inhabitants in a 1953 seance.
- **Project premise and excluded sighting catalog** — The authors decline to assemble dozens or hundreds of familiar cases from books, articles, television, and websites. They invoke instead records presented by sober members of world governments and military observers, then say readers still needing proof will not find the project addressed to them.
- **Jeffrey J. Kripal as methodological source** — The prologue quotes *Authors of the Impossible: The Paranormal and the Sacred* (University of Chicago Press, 2010), page 34, on the inability to separate the real, religious, and fictional. The authors adopt that statement as the framework for merging fictional and nonfictional modes, mass and social media, three works of fiction, three works of nonfiction, documentary and feature-film treatments, and a comprehensive social-media platform.
- **The Phenomenon's evidence and effects** — The source inventory includes sightings, photographs, film, radar traces, physical contact with Earth and humans, multiple forms of communication, apparent violations of physical law, impossible propulsion, observer disorientation and trauma, religious visions, miscellaneous anomalies, telepathy, telekinesis, and confusion in political, military, and industrial sectors. The authors say these features recur from the earliest recorded history without significant change across eras.
- **Disciplines invited into the project** — Medicine, physics, biology, chemistry, astronomy, astrophysics, engineering, information technology, psychology, sociology, anthropology, and religious studies are all named. The authors hope open study will also stimulate students and teachers and lead to advances in science, technology, and philosophy.
- **Galileo and worldview lag** — The authors use Galileo's demonstration that Earth revolves around the sun to argue that culture still behaves as if everyday experience exhausts reality. They say Galileo shifted the scientific center of gravity from Earth to sun in the seventeenth century but that culture remains geocentric while using twenty-first-century tools, repeatedly falling back on old prejudices, badly informed cynicism, violence toward neighbors and the planet, and a sense that human action cannot produce change.
- **Ten-thousand-year cargo-cult proposal** — Beginning with alien-contact accounts in core religious texts, the authors intend to reconstruct initial contact from available data and reinterpret ten thousand years or more of civilization as its ritual and cultural aftermath. They disclaim any attempt to make every anomaly evidence of alien intervention.
- **Tom's observation and Peter's evidentiary conviction** — Only Tom reports seeing UFOs. Peter has not, but says the evidence convinces him. The authors compare this to accepting bacteria and subatomic particles without personal observation and argue that requiring direct encounter would make UFOs uniquely exempt from ordinary scientific inference.
- **Four senses of *sekret*** — The unusual spelling invokes street associations; distinguishes the project from ordinary secrecy; alludes to Eastern Europe, Soviet machines, and the Cold War; resembles words for "secretion" in some Eastern European and Scandinavian languages and German; supports the thesis of a machine that is "secreted"; and recalls a Greek-derived mystical meaning of *secret*. The authors defer fuller treatment of the secreted-machine idea to the second and third volumes.

## Thread
The prologue supplies the nonfiction trilogy's operating model and stakes. The cargo-cult reconstruction makes technological incomprehension into a mechanism for religion, ritual, sacred objects, and restricted authority; the 1947-1953 sequence then places modern aerospace, occult contact, official denial, psychological warfare, and elite networks inside the same frame. From there the authors define the Phenomenon, explain why their project combines fiction and nonfiction, and announce an inquiry beginning with religious origin texts and an *ur-punkt* of contact. Its closing discussions of alien, disclosure, re-engineering, observation, bias, and *sekret* establish cautions and questions that later volumes are meant to develop rather than answers the prologue claims already to possess.

## Connections
- **Foreword by Jacques Vallée** — Vallée's call to reconsider history and physical reality becomes the prologue's explicit cargo-cult history, expanded Phenomenon, and alternative worldview.
- **Peter's Acknowledgments** — The prologue enacts the multidisciplinary approach Levenda attributes to Vallée and expands the acknowledgments' spectrum from military and mind-control interpretations to spiritual ones.
- **Sekret Machines fiction and nonfiction works** — Explicit series connection: the project is planned as three works of fiction and three of nonfiction, plus documentary, feature-film, mass-media, social-media, and platform components.
- **Later volumes** — Explicit trilogy reference: the authors say the "secreted machine" resonance of *sekret* will be elaborated in volumes two and three, and that this and following volumes will provide a framework for re-examining human knowledge after contact.
- **Alien Genesis** — The prologue names the initial contact or *ur-punkt* after the first chapter's subject, positioning "Alien Genesis" as the starting point for the proposed reconstruction of civilization.
- **Reader-side connection (speculative; not attributed to the authors)** — The distinction between an encounter and the conceptual system later built around it may serve as a useful reading test throughout the book: each later claim can be examined for what is presented as event, interpretation, ritual inheritance, or institutional framing.
