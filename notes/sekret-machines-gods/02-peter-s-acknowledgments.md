# Peter's Acknowledgments

## In one paragraph
Peter Levenda begins by honoring earlier researchers and speakers who risked reputations and sometimes lives in studying what he calls the Phenomenon, and states the project's hope that people will recognize this mystery as a missing piece of the human condition. He vouches for a group of about ten mostly unnameable agency and department advisors whose contributions he calls substantive and important. Among those he can identify, he gives extended thanks to Jacques Vallée, J. Allen Hynek, Whitley Strieber, Andrew J. Hartley, and Tom DeLonge, as well as researchers, interviewers, friends, family, and readers. His descriptions stress a multidisciplinary method, a spectrum of interpretations from military and mind-control models to spiritual ones, a fiction series that nearly became an exposé, and DeLonge's unusual access to senior military, intelligence, and corporate figures. Levenda closes by connecting roughly two years of travel, research, and reflection to a hoped-for new understanding of humanity's role and the extension of consciousness into realms previously imagined by scientists and shamans.

## Core claims
- Levenda says many earlier investigators of the Phenomenon researched, wrote, and spoke at risk to their reputations and, at times, their lives.
- He calls it inconceivable that the enduring mystery is not taken more seriously and states the project's hope that readers will see the puzzle's missing piece as "the missing piece of the human condition."
- Levenda states that about ten project advisors exist, that most cannot be named because of their standing in agencies and departments, and that their contribution has been substantive and important; he explicitly asks readers to accept his personal assurance rather than supplying their identities.
- He presents Vallée's combination of UFO research, astronomy, and computer science as the beginning and model of the multidisciplinary approach the project seeks to implement.
- Levenda credits his long correspondence with Whitley Strieber with producing insights across a broad interpretive range, from military and mind-control readings to purely spiritual ones.
- He says Andrew J. Hartley's fictional rendering of the nonfiction series' people, places, and events has come so close to the underlying material that seasoned advisors found it intriguing and startling, "very nearly an exposé."
- Levenda says the project would not exist without Tom DeLonge and attributes its expanded scope to DeLonge's unmatched access to high-ranking military, intelligence, and corporate figures.
- He frames the project as an effort to reconsider humanity's role in the world and the possible extension of consciousness into realms imagined by both scientists and shamans.

## Concepts
- **The Phenomenon** — Levenda's term for the enduring mystery usually discussed through UFOs, alien contact, and related experience; here it is presented as both under-investigated and potentially central to understanding the human condition.
- **Advisors** — A group of about ten people connected with the project, mostly men whose agency or department standing prevents their identification. Levenda vouches for their existence and describes their contribution as substantive and highly important.
- **Multidisciplinary approach** — The method Levenda associates with Jacques Vallée's conjunction of Phenomenon research, astronomy, and computer science, and which the project is determined to follow.
- **Abduction experience** — One part of Whitley Strieber's UFO-related work whose sincerity Levenda calls inspirational.
- **Interpretive spectrum** — The range explored in Levenda and Strieber's correspondence: military and mind-control interpretations at one end, the purely spiritual at the other, with positions between them.
- **Fictional interpretation** — Hartley's transformation of names, places, and events from the nonfiction series into a coherent narrative that, according to Levenda, approached exposé-level proximity to the source material.
- **Human consciousness** — The faculty whose extension beyond familiar limits Levenda says the project's implications may help readers consider.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Earlier Phenomenon researchers** — Levenda opens with an unnamed collective of predecessors who researched, wrote, and spoke despite possible damage to reputation or life. Their work is the foundation for the present project.
- **The advisory group** — About ten individuals are called "the advisors." Most are gentlemen whom Levenda says cannot be identified because of their positions in respective agencies and departments. He asks for patience with the omission, personally vouches that they exist, and calls their contribution substantive and of great importance.
- **Jacques Vallée and J. Allen Hynek** — Levenda says Vallée had been at the forefront of the post-World War II UFO Phenomenon for nearly fifty years and that Vallée and Hynek were experts when serious, respected researchers were scarce. Vallée's astronomy and computer-science expertise made his work, in Levenda's account, the origin and model of the needed multidisciplinary method. Levenda also thanks him for the foreword, continuing friendship, and advice.
- **Whitley Strieber** — A friend since the 1990s whose sincerity about UFOs and abduction Levenda found inspirational. Their voluminous correspondence over the preceding twenty years generated new insights and approaches ranging from military and mind-control interpretations through purely spiritual ones.
- **Researchers and discussion partners** — Levenda names Jim Marrs, Richard Dolan, Timothy Good, and Hal Puthoff as friends and colleagues with whom he shared convivial meetings and discussions about the subject.
- **Radio and podcast interviewers** — He thanks Jimmy Church, Ian Punnett, the late Georgeanne Hughes, Dr. J. Ilias, Gordon White, Micah Hanks, and Joseph Farrell for in-depth interviews. Levenda says such people keep the discussion alive when many would prefer to forget it and move on.
- **Andrew J. Hartley** — Levenda calls Hartley an incomparable raconteur, a best-selling novelist, and "king of Shakespeare studies," adding playful disclaimers about Lear, Richard III, and the Scottish king. Their conversations range widely and include wry humor and ironic asides. Hartley's fictional use of nonfictional names, places, and events produced a coherent story that some seasoned project advisors found startlingly close to an exposé.
- **Tom DeLonge** — Levenda says no project would exist without him and credits DeLonge with a determined challenge to established theories of alien contact. He identifies access to high-ranking military, intelligence, and corporate people as what made the project three-dimensional, "if not four-dimensional," in scope.
- **Roughly two years of project work** — Levenda thanks family, friends, and devoted readers for tolerating his unavailability during approximately two years of travel, research, long reflection on relationships among events, persons, and phenomena, and consideration of their implications.

## Key quotes
- "the missing piece of the human condition" — Levenda's statement of what the Phenomenon may supply.
- "a group of about ten individuals" — the approximate size of the unnamed advisory circle.
- "they do, indeed, exist" — Levenda's personal assurance regarding the advisors.
- "the model of the multi-disciplinary approach" — his description of Vallée's contribution.
- "from military and mind-control interpretations to the purely spiritual" — the range explored with Strieber.
- "very nearly an exposé" — how close Hartley's fiction came to the nonfictional material, in Levenda's view.
- "three-dimensional in scope if not four-dimensional" — his characterization of the effect of DeLonge's access.
- "realms only dreamed of by scientists and shamans" — the territory into which Levenda imagines human consciousness extending.

## Thread
Levenda's acknowledgments establish the human network behind the series and the methodological posture that the foreword and prologue develop: long-term UFO research, multiple disciplines, openness to military and spiritual interpretations, creative fiction alongside nonfiction, and restricted guidance from unnamed insiders. The section also makes the project's ambition unusually broad by treating the Phenomenon as a route toward rethinking the human condition and consciousness.

## Connections
- **Foreword by Jacques Vallée** — Levenda explicitly says Vallée's foreword places the book where it needs to be and treats Vallée's multidisciplinary career as the project's methodological model.
- **Tom's Acknowledgments** — Both authors credit unnamed government or agency-linked advisors and identify Hartley's fiction as the accessible narrative expression of the project's esoteric nonfiction.
- **Sekret Machines fiction series** — Explicit series connection: Levenda says Hartley fictionalized the names, places, and events in the nonfiction series, producing a narrative that nearly functioned as an exposé.
- **Prologue** — Reader-side connection (speculative; not attributed to Levenda): the prologue expands the acknowledgments' claims about multidisciplinarity, the Phenomenon, fiction/nonfiction, and transformed human consciousness into the project's formal program.
