# Foreword by Jacques Vallée

## In one paragraph
Jacques Vallée presents *Sekret Machines: Gods* as a bold reconsideration of unexplained aerial phenomena at a moment when the field has reached a crossroads. He says continuing reports survive academic neglect, political dismissal, church censorship, military classification, and media ridicule, and organizes the resulting challenge around two questions: what humanity's myths conceal or express about human origins, and whether physical reality itself must be reconsidered if supposedly impossible UFO reports persist. Vallée develops a deliberate paradox: conceding the skeptics' claim that UFOs do not exist removes the stated reason to hide the reports and secret files, yet opening them confronts us with hundreds of thousands of testimonies incompatible with the accepted model. He credits Tom DeLonge and Peter Levenda, whom he says have privileged access to long-denied information, with reopening both questions while refusing to impose a replacement ideology on difficult ancient material. The foreword ends by inviting a new research phase that joins old and recent sources to decipher the message of the "Sekret machines."

## Core claims
- Vallée says the study of unexplained aerial phenomena has reached a crossroads because reports continue despite institutional neglect, censorship, classification, and ridicule.
- He frames the first central question historically: when origin myths are tested against environmental and biological fact, what do they reveal about humanity's birth and the forces or influences that shaped its development?
- He leaves open two alternatives within that historical question: gods may have been present on Earth "in illo tempore," or humans may have invented them to satisfy an ancient and increasingly urgent spiritual desire to reach the stars.
- Vallée frames the second question as a more scientifically basic and psychologically troubling challenge to reality itself.
- For the sake of argument, he grants SETI experts and academic consensus their claim that UFOs do not exist and that reports conflict with modern science's anthropocentric model of physical reality.
- He then argues that this denial should permit open acknowledgment of the reports and release of even secret files, since stories declared meaningless and impossible should pose no danger; he quotes the book's formulation that the UFO can be known only by not asking what it is.
- Vallée's next inference is that the persistence of "impossible" UFO reports may require renegotiating the accepted concept of physical reality rather than simply dismissing the reports.
- He says the Phenomenon's persistence forces fundamental re-examination of human history and humanity's presence on Earth and may offer a breakthrough beyond the confusions and contradictions of modern physics.
- Vallée warns that such inquiry enters a difficult field of old records, tentative readings, and mutually irreconcilable ideologies, which Jeffrey Kripal characterizes as gnosis or forbidden knowledge beyond reason and belief.
- He cautions that the ancient material is "brittle," making analytical restraint especially important when testing scholarly hypotheses and established traditions.
- He praises DeLonge and Levenda for questioning scholarly hypotheses and established pieties while retaining analytical restraint and declining to impose a new ideology.
- Vallée says the authors invite readers into a new research phase informed jointly by the oldest and newest sources.

## Concepts
- **Unexplained aerial phenomena** — The field whose meaning and nature the book re-examines; Vallée emphasizes that reports persist even while major institutions marginalize them.
- **Historical question** — The inquiry into what origin mythology, confronted with environmental and biological fact, can disclose about humanity's birth and the influences on its development.
- **Anthropocentric physical reality** — Vallée's description of modern science's accepted framework, centered on human ideas of what is physically possible and allegedly incompatible with UFO reports.
- **Strategic denial** — Vallée's logical maneuver of provisionally accepting the skeptical claim that UFOs are impossible: if the accounts are meaningless, he argues, there is no reason not to acknowledge them and open classified records.
- **Persistence of the Phenomenon** — The continued arrival of reports and experiences despite official impossibility; for Vallée, this persistence is what puts pressure on received history and physical reality.
- **Gnosis / forbidden knowledge** — Jeffrey Kripal's characterization, quoted by Vallée, of the domain beyond ordinary reason and belief into which this inquiry leads.
- **Analytical restraint** — The authors' stated virtue in Vallée's account: they display brittle ancient material and competing interpretations without forcing a new ideology on the reader.
- **New phase of research** — The collaborative inquiry Vallée says the authors propose, combining the oldest records with the most recent sources.
- **Sekret machines** — Vallée's name for the book's object of inquiry, treated as carrying a message that now needs to be deciphered.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Five institutional responses to sightings** — Vallée says academics have ignored reports, most politicians have brushed them off, churches have censored them, the military has classified them, and the media has ridiculed them. The reports nevertheless continue, deepening the experience of an uncanny intrusion into a modern world of convenient machines and superficial entertainment.
- **Human-origin alternatives** — Vallée asks readers to compare cultural origin mythology with environmental and biological fact. His paired possibilities are that gods existed on Earth in remote time or that humans invented them to answer a spiritual need to touch the stars, a need he says began millennia ago and has become more urgent in the present century.
- **The skeptical concession** — As a thought experiment, Vallée accepts the assurances of SETI experts and the academic consensus that UFOs do not exist and assumes the reports are inconsistent with modern scientific reality. He argues that this very conclusion eliminates the rationale for suppressing accounts or secret files: reports deemed meaningless can safely be brought into daylight.
- **Hundreds of thousands of worldwide sightings** — Vallée invokes testimony gathered from hundreds of thousands of reports around the world as the evidence that skeptical denial would newly permit society to consider. Their continued presence is the basis for his proposal to reconsider physical reality.
- **The Derrida-school comparison** — Vallée says the reversal by which denial enables acceptance is a subtle logical twist that would have delighted philosophers of the Derrida school.
- **DeLonge and Levenda's access and method** — Vallée says the authors have privileged access to information long denied to others. They reopen historical and reality questions, work among dusty records and incompatible ideologies, treat ancient material as brittle, challenge scholarly hypotheses and established pieties, and present complex material without demanding adherence to a new doctrine.
- **Jeffrey Kripal's formulation** — Vallée cites Kripal's description of this field as a form of gnosis or forbidden knowledge beyond reason and belief to convey the epistemic difficulty readers face after accepting the inquiry.

## Key quotes
- "the field has reached a crossroads" — Vallée's reason for treating the invitation to write the foreword as both an honor and a challenge.
- "Yet the reports continue to come." — his answer to institutional dismissal.
- "Were there gods on the earth" — one side of his question about human origins.
- "Let us assume" — the provisional move by which he grants the skeptics' position for argument's sake.
- "The UFO can be 'known' only by not asking what it is." — the authors' paradox as quoted by Vallée.
- "re-negotiate physical reality" — Vallée's proposed response if persistent UFO reports conflict with the accepted model.
- "not going away" — his description of the supposedly impossible UFOs.
- "gnosis or forbidden knowledge" — Jeffrey Kripal's phrase for the territory beyond reason and belief.
- "do not try to force upon us a new ideology" — Vallée's praise for the authors' restraint.
- "The time has come to decipher it." — his closing invitation regarding the message of the Sekret machines.

## Thread
The foreword supplies the book's epistemic license. Rather than first trying to prove UFOs, Vallée argues that the official declaration of impossibility should remove barriers to examining testimony and opening records; the persistence of the reports can then test both received history and the boundaries of physical reality. His emphasis on difficult evidence, competing interpretations, and resistance to a replacement dogma prepares readers for the prologue's mixed historical, religious, scientific, and fictional method.

## Connections
- **Prologue** — The prologue develops Vallée's two questions into the project's program: it interprets human civilization through contact and cargo-cult models and defines the Phenomenon as exceeding historical, scientific, and religious categories.

- **Sekret Machines project** — Explicit trilogy connection: Vallée describes this book as a research invitation and closes by assigning the "Sekret machines" a message to be deciphered.
