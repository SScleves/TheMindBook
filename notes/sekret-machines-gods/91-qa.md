# Sekret Machines Book 1: Gods — Q&A

*Unit-pointer legend: pointers use the note unit numbers from each file's frontmatter, not the book's printed chapter numbers. Units 0–4 = front matter (dedication, acknowledgments, Vallée's foreword, prologue); units 5–12 = the eight main chapters ("Alien Genesis" = printed Ch. 1 … "Planet Earth: The Hot LZ" = printed Ch. 8); unit 13 = endnotes. All answers state what Tom DeLonge & Peter Levenda claim in* Sekret Machines: Gods *(2017); none are endorsed as fact.*

## Orientation and Method

**Q: What is the book's central thesis in one sentence?**
A: That religion is humanity's oldest record of contact — that the gods, scriptures, rituals, and monuments of the world preserve, in each culture's own vocabulary, a real and recurring encounter with a non-human intelligence the authors call the Phenomenon, and that ten thousand years of civilization may be a Cargo Cult built to remember and re-summon that original contact (the *ur-punkt*, or "Alien Genesis"). (Units 4, 5)

**Q: Does the book try to prove UFOs are real?**
A: No — and it says so plainly. The premise is "not the belief, the understanding": the authors *assume* that UFOs are real and that humans and non-human beings have interacted since recorded history, decline to rehearse the usual catalog of modern sightings, and tell readers who still need that proof that the project "is not addressed to them." Much of the book's force therefore depends on granting the premise. (Unit 4)

**Q: What does Vallée's foreword contribute?**
A: It supplies the epistemic license. Rather than first proving UFOs, Vallée grants — for argument's sake — the skeptics' claim that they don't exist, then argues that this very concession removes the reason to suppress the reports and secret files: accounts declared meaningless can safely be examined, whereupon hundreds of thousands of testimonies confront the accepted model. He proposes that the persistence of "impossible" reports may require "renegotiating physical reality." (Unit 3)

**Q: Why "sekret" with a k?**
A: The prologue gives four senses: street usage that distinguishes the subject from ordinary secrecy; Cold War Eastern Europe and Soviet machines; words for "secretion" in several European languages, supporting the idea of a machine that is "secreted" or hidden; and the Greek-derived mystical sense of *secret*. The "secreted machine" resonance is deferred to later volumes. (Unit 4)

**Q: What is the "Cargo Cult," and why is it the book's master analogy?**
A: The prologue reconstructs a 1942 South Pacific people who interpret WWII aircraft, cargo, and personnel as gods and gifts, preserve leftover artifacts as sacred relics, and build bamboo runways and ritual imitations to bring the beings back. The authors generalize it: incomprehensible technology becomes religion, and civilization itself may be the ritual afterlife of an original contact. It recurs as the reading of the Works of the Chariot and of Egyptian funerary practice. (Units 4, 7, 9)

**Q: How do the authors want the Phenomenon studied?**
A: Through a "multidisciplinary approach" that holds the physical and the consciousness dimensions together, following Kripal's refusal to separate "the real, the religious, and the fictional." Their strongest methodological claim is that science and a non-denominational mysticism must be developed together — "a shotgun wedding" that would transform both — because the Phenomenon manifests in both dimensions. (Units 4, 5, 10)

**Q: What is the reversed-conjuration thought experiment, and how seriously do the authors mean it?**
A: In "Alien Genesis" they invert a magician's summoning rite: if the lit, marked, smoking magic circle with its compelled visible spirit is reversed, the apparent flying saucer becomes *the other world's* magic circle and humans become the summoned spirits ("we may be the 'spirits' for the magicians who fly the ships"). They call it "outrageous" and state explicitly, "there is no implication or insistence that this experiment is in any way 'true.'" Its purpose is to open consciousness-centered hypotheses, not to assert a mechanism. (Unit 5)

## Origins, Creation, and the Ancient Record

**Q: What do the authors say the Sumerian and biblical texts record about human origins?**
A: They read the *Enuma Elish* and Genesis 6 as recording a consistent structure: humans made from divine substance (Kingu's blood) for *labor* — a "slave race" — taught civilization by non-human sages (the apkallu/*bene elohim*), and interbred with the "sons of God" to produce the Nephilim (interpreted as literal giants, not "fallen ones"). They insist this is the texts' own account of a hybrid humanity, needing none of Sitchin's gold-mining addition. (Unit 6)

**Q: Do the authors accept Zecharia Sitchin's "ancient aliens"?**
A: No. They state plainly that Sitchin's Annunaki-as-astronauts, slave-hybrid, and "twelfth planet" claims are not supported by the Sumerian texts or cylinder seals, and criticize his philology throughout. But they treat his worldwide popularity as evidence of a genuine, unmet hunger for an origin account beyond institutional science, and they build their own case from better-attested sources like the *Enuma Elish*. (Units 5, 6)

**Q: What is the "Gate of the Gods," and why does it matter?**
A: "World" is defined as a culturally bounded order; the Phenomenon occupies the narrow overlap between "This World" (human, ordered, visible) and an "Other World" (non-human, chaotic, unrecognized). Babylon's ziggurat — its blue-glazed summit chamber where Marduk descends yearly to a high priestess — is that overlap made architecture; *Babili* means "Gate of the Gods." (Unit 6)

**Q: Why do the authors say governments *cannot* disclose the truth about UFOs?**
A: Following Wendt & Duvall's "Sovereignty and the UFO," they argue modern authority is anthropocentric — constituted by reference to humans alone — so it cannot acknowledge a superior non-human authority without undermining the basis of its own power. The result is an "authoritative taboo": "the UFO can be known only by not asking what it is," and it becomes a "non-object," institutionally unseen. (Unit 6)

**Q: What is the "stairway to heaven" argument?**
A: That the ancient world preserved not only stories of gods descending but *repeatable ritual technologies* by which selected humans tried to ascend and re-establish contact — Jacob's ladder, Muhammad's *Mi'raj*, the Daoist Pace of Yu, Masonic ritual, Ezekiel's chariot, the *merkava*. Across them recurs a package of solitude, sensory deprivation, seven guarded levels, passwords, celestial vehicles, and permanent transformation. (Unit 7)

**Q: Do the authors claim consciousness controls the craft?**
A: They *pose* it as a question, not a finding. Noting that ancient accounts rarely describe anyone physically steering celestial vehicles, and citing Bob Lazar and unnamed reverse-engineering reports of a "control problem" (no visible steering), they wonder whether trained consciousness — not a visible dashboard — is the interface. They explicitly decline to judge the captured-craft claim and use it only to raise the hypothesis. (Unit 7)

## Sacrifice, Egypt, and the Comparative Sweep

**Q: What is the "psycho-biological connection" behind blood sacrifice?**
A: In "The Cult of the Dead Gods," the authors argue that the near-universal practice of blood and human sacrifice is a category of evidence in itself: many unrelated cultures believed human blood/life carried a divine substance that had to be returned to the gods. Aztec religion is the systematic extreme (blood "is of divine origin, not human"; the *istli* freed by violence flies to the Palace of the Fleshless). They read it as a debt owed to absent, "dead" creators. (Unit 8)

**Q: How does the sacrifice chapter connect to genetics?**
A: Speculatively and with explicit caveats. The authors ask whether pre-scientific peoples intuited that blood carries unique identity (introducing Grandy's reported "DNA consciousness"), whether selecting beautiful/royal/mutated victims functioned as genetic selection, and whether the shCherbak/Makukov stop-codon "message" and "planned obsolescence" of mortality point to an engineered origin. All of this is flagged as fanciful and deferred to Book Three. (Unit 8)

**Q: In what sense is an Egyptian pyramid a "machine"?**
A: The authors adopt Egyptologist Toby Wilkinson's description of Djoser's north-aligned Step Pyramid as "a resurrection machine designed to propel its royal owner" to the undying circumpolar stars. They generalize: Egyptian funerary religion (the *ka*, *ba*, *akh*; the Opening of the Mouth; stellar alignments) is a *technology of identity* — an attempt not at bodily immortality but at continuity of consciousness — with priests as "Mission Control." (Unit 9)

**Q: Do they claim aliens built the pyramids?**
A: No — they explicitly resist it. A long controversies section (Cagliostro, Bauval/Hawass/Krupp, Temple's Dogon, *Hamlet's Mill*) is used as methodological self-critique: they concede terrestrial answers to construction questions (arsenical copper), reject arbitrary monument-decoding as devaluing the broader question, and prefer Egyptian *texts* as direct testimony over alien-builder speculation. (Unit 9)

**Q: What do the Chinese and Indian/Tibetan chapters add?**
A: "The Star Walkers" carries the stellar-ascent complex into *living* Daoist practice — the Pace of the Stars, treading the Big Dipper with "the body as the launch pad" and consciousness as vehicle — and into the diffusionism debate (Hummel, Oppenheimer's Sundaland, Schoch). "Kala Chakra/Chakra Vartin" internalizes the Dipper's stations as bodily *cakras* and argues that the wheel, mandala, and flying disk are cognate images of one real object. (Units 10, 11)

**Q: What is the Kalacakra–UFO correlation, and how strong do the authors say it is?**
A: They lay Vallée's 1947–1962 "Schedule of Reinforcement" sighting graph beside mass Kalacakra initiations and add later event/initiation pairs (Lhasa 1954/1956, Ladakh 1976, Bihar 1985, West Bengal 1996). But they hedge it repeatedly and emphatically: "This is not to say that the Dalai Lama was in any way 'creating' the UFO sightings," the mechanism is unknown, the evidence is circumstantial, and "much more research and analysis is needed." It is the volume's most novel and most explicitly limited evidence. (Unit 11)

## Reading Critically

**Q: Do the authors accept the crude reading that Ezekiel's chariot was a flying saucer?**
A: No. In "Planet Earth: The Hot LZ" they reject it as backward projection. Instead they argue the reverse direction: modern disks, triangles, and lights are "Ezekiel's Chariot projected forward into the present" — each era's technology rendering the same underlying Phenomenon. The distinction protects their thesis from depending on any one hardware shape. (Unit 12)

**Q: What is the "hot LZ," and what posture does the book end on?**
A: A Vietnam-era term for a landing zone under fire. The authors picture a divided, weaponized, internally hostile Earth as a "hot LZ" — dangerous to any visitor and, they speculate, easy for a dominating force to exploit while humans "do the heavy lifting" of destroying one another. The recommended posture is *advaita* (nonduality beyond science/religion binaries) and the endnote thesis that "all religions are UFO religions." (Units 12, 13)

**Q: Where should a skeptical reader be most careful?**
A: Where the authors themselves flag it. The whole argument rests on an *assumed* premise, not a demonstrated one. The most striking claims — reversed conjuration, consciousness-as-interface, DNA-consciousness, the genetic-code "message," genetic selection through sacrifice, and the Kalacakra synchronicities — are explicitly labeled speculation, analogy, or research directions. And a persistent *inference layer* (that recurring myths preserve real non-human contact) sits atop the documentary layer; the reader should keep asking which claims are the texts' and which are the authors' interpretation. (Units 5, 6, 8, 11)

**Q: How reliable are the book's sources, by the authors' own account?**
A: Deliberately mixed, and the endnotes say so. They range from primary ancient texts and page-specific university scholarship, through declassified government files (the "giggle factor" memo, GRILL FLAME, a Gulf War remote-viewing scorecard), to contested authors the notes openly criticize (Waddell's Aryan-Sumerian diffusionism, Evans-Wentz's Theosophy, the disputed Aryan Migration Theory) and web-only or unpublished-thesis material. The unevenness is itself substantive: it marks where a claim is documented, merely proposed, or deferred to Books Two and Three. (Unit 13)

**Q: Are the comparisons to other books on the shelf (holographic universe, simulation, My Big TOE) the authors' own?**
A: No. Every such link in the source notes — consciousness-primary ontology, rendered or projected reality — is explicitly marked as the knowledge base's reader-side thematic resonance, *not* a comparison DeLonge & Levenda draw. They should be read as shelf-neighbor observations, never as the authors' citations. (Units 5, 6)

**Q: Does *Gods* answer what the Phenomenon is?**
A: No, and it does not claim to. It presents itself as the *groundwork* volume: it assembles the ancient record and the interpretive method, insists the Phenomenon's nature, origin, and intentions can only be inferred (never awaited from "disclosure"), and defers the modern institutional and scientific detail to *Sekret Machines: Man* and *Sekret Machines: War*. (Units 4, 5, 13)
