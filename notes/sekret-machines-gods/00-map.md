## The thesis in one page

*Gods* is the opening nonfiction volume of the *Sekret Machines* trilogy, and its governing claim is that **religion is humanity's oldest and most complete record of contact** — that the gods, angels, demons, and sky-beings of the world's origin stories, scriptures, rituals, and monuments preserve, in the only vocabulary their cultures possessed, a real and recurring encounter with a non-human intelligence the authors call **the Phenomenon**. The book's premise is deliberately stated as "not the belief, the understanding": DeLonge & Levenda **assume rather than argue** that UFOs are real and that humans and non-human beings have interacted since recorded history began, and they decline to rehearse the usual catalog of modern sightings. From that premise they propose a startling historical frame — that **ten thousand or more years of human civilization may itself be a Cargo Cult**, a ritual afterlife built to remember and re-summon an original contact they name the **ur-punkt** or **Alien Genesis**. The trilogy's title is glossed in the prologue: *sekret* (with a k) evokes Cold War Eastern Europe, "secretion," a machine that is "secreted" or hidden, and the Greek-derived mystical sense of *secret*.

Around that spine the authors build a **method** as much as an argument, borrowed from Jacques Vallée's foreword and Jeffrey Kripal's refusal to separate "the real, the religious, and the fictional." They insist the Phenomenon manifests in **two dimensions at once** — the physical and the consciousness dimension — and therefore that science and a non-denominational **mysticism** must be developed together ("a shotgun wedding" that would transform both). Their most-repeated interpretive move is the **reversed-conjuration thought experiment** of "Alien Genesis": if a magician's summoning circle, with its lights, smoke, symbols, and compelled visible spirit, is inverted, the apparent flying saucer becomes *the other world's* magic circle and **humanity becomes the summoned spirits** — a suggestion they label "outrageous" and explicitly disclaim as true, using it only to open consciousness-centered hypotheses. Sub-claims braid across the volume: that Sumerian and biblical texts record humans **created from divine substance for labor** (a "slave race"), taught civilization by non-human sages (the **apkallu**), and interbred with the **sons of God** to produce the **Nephilim**; that ritual technologies of **celestial ascent** (Jacob's ladder, Ezekiel's chariot, the *merkava*, the Daoist *Pace of the Stars*) may be Cargo-Cult reconstructions of contact whose real interface is trained consciousness; that **blood sacrifice** across cultures encodes a "psycho-biological" debt of divine substance owed back to absent creators; that **Egyptian funerary religion** is a "resurrection machine," a technology of preserved identity; and that the same wheel, mandala, flying disk, and winged-disk imagery recurs from Sumer to Tibet as culturally transformed memory of a real object. The volume closes by picturing a divided, weaponized modern Earth as a **"hot LZ"** — a landing zone under fire — and by rejecting the crude ancient-astronaut reading of Ezekiel: modern saucers are not proof that his chariot was a spacecraft; rather, each era's craft is "Ezekiel's Chariot projected forward," the same Phenomenon in period dress. Its recommended posture is inquiry over dogma and *advaita* (nonduality) over the binaries of science/religion, fact/fantasy, alien/human, captured in the endnote thesis that **"all religions are UFO religions."**

## How the argument builds

**Front matter (units 0–4) — premise, method, and frame.** The **dedication** addresses readers who already sense "a whole lot more to our human story." **Tom's and Peter's acknowledgments** stake the project on family support, Levenda's research, A. J. Hartley's fictionalization ("very nearly an exposé"), DeLonge's access to senior military/intelligence/corporate figures, and a circle of "about ten" mostly unnameable government-linked advisors. **Vallée's foreword** supplies the epistemic license: rather than first proving UFOs, he argues that the official declaration of impossibility should *remove* the barrier to examining testimony and opening records, and that the persistence of "impossible" reports may force a renegotiation of physical reality itself. The **prologue** then lays out the whole operating model: the 1942 South Pacific **Cargo Cult** as a template for how incomprehensible technology becomes religion; a dated 1947–1953 sequence (Dornberger at Wright Field, the New Mexico crash, Pelley's Soulcraft, the 1952 Washington overflights, Puharich's Round Table séance with "the Nine"); the broad definition of **the Phenomenon**; the Kripal method; and the ten-thousand-year Cargo-Cult / *ur-punkt* proposal.

**The method chapter (unit 5) — "Alien Genesis."** The first main chapter establishes the book's core vocabulary and its central caution. It defines religion, via Jacobsen and Jonas, as confrontation with a power experienced as "alien" and terrifying; tests popular ancient-astronaut writers (Sitchin, von Däniken) against better-attested Gnostic and Sumerian sources and finds their particulars unsupported but their audience's hunger genuine; and moves the inquiry from disputed artifacts to **consciousness** — near-death experience, remote viewing (SRI, Puthoff, Targ, Swann), shamanism, the Tibetan state oracle, and ceremonial magic. Its climactic reversed-conjuration thought experiment is the model for the whole volume: keep documentary evidence, textual interpretation, author inference, and speculation **visibly separate**.

**The ancient dossier (units 6–8) — descent, ascent, and sacrifice.** "**The Gate of the Gods**" builds the paleocontact case from the *Enuma Elish*, Genesis, *Gilgamesh*, the apkallu, the *bene elohim*, and the Nephilim, and pairs it with Wendt & Duvall's "Sovereignty and the UFO" to explain why modern anthropocentric states *cannot* acknowledge a higher non-human authority — "the UFO can be known only by not asking what it is." "**Stairway to Heaven**" turns the Gate from a place gods descend into a **method** by which selected humans ascend (Jacob, Muhammad's *Mi'raj*, Ezekiel's chariot, the *merkava*), proposing that altered consciousness, not a visible dashboard, may be the control interface. "**The Cult of the Dead Gods**" reads global **blood sacrifice** — Aztec religion its principal case — as psycho-biological evidence of a debt of divine substance, then extends into speculative genetics (Grandy's "DNA consciousness," the shCherbak/Makukov genetic-code "signal").

**The comparative sweep (units 9–12) — Egypt, China, India/Tibet, Greece.** "**Let Us Now Raise Famous Men**" recasts Egyptian funerary religion as a **resurrection machine** — a technology of preserved identity — while using a long controversies section (Bauval, Temple, *Hamlet's Mill*) as methodological self-critique. "**The Star Walkers**" carries the stellar-ascent complex into living Daoist practice (the *Pace of the Stars*, the Big Dipper) and the diffusionism debate. "**Kala Chakra/Chakra Vartin**" internalizes the Dipper's stations as bodily *cakras*, reads the wheel/mandala/flying-disk as cognate images of a real object, and offers the volume's most novel and most hedged evidence: circumstantial synchronicities between mass Kalacakra initiations and UFO events. "**Planet Earth: The Hot LZ**" gathers Greek *Gigantomachy*, Gnosticism, Prometheus, and Frankenstein into a closing diagnosis of a divided, godlike-but-dangerous humanity.

**Back matter (unit 13) — the endnotes.** Far more than citations, the 141 notes expose the volume's evidentiary spine: where a claim rests on a primary text, a page-specific scholar, a declassified file (the "giggle factor" memo, GRILL FLAME, a Gulf War remote-viewing scorecard), a contested author (Waddell, Evans-Wentz), an interpretive analogy, or a promise deferred to Books Two and Three. They make the volume's unevenness of support legible, and state the series thesis outright: "all religions are UFO religions."

## Unit index

**[00-dedication.md]** — unit 0 (Front matter) — *Dedication*. A single sentence dedicating the book to anyone who already sensed "a whole lot more to our human story." It identifies the intended audience before the argument begins.

**[01-tom-s-acknowledgments.md]** — unit 1 (Front matter) — *Tom's Acknowledgments*. DeLonge thanks his family for supporting his interest in "all things weird," credits Levenda's research and A. J. Hartley's accessible storytelling, and addresses unnamed government advisors, telling them the project has already begun "waking people up." It stakes the book on insider sourcing and an ambition to change public self-understanding.

**[02-peter-s-acknowledgments.md]** — unit 2 (Front matter) — *Peter's Acknowledgments*. Levenda vouches for "about ten" mostly unnameable agency/department advisors, names Vallée as the model of the multidisciplinary method, credits Strieber, Hartley, and DeLonge's access, and frames the project as an effort to reconsider humanity's role and extend consciousness "into realms only dreamed of by scientists and shamans."

**[03-foreword-by-jacques-vall-e.md]** — unit 3 (Front matter) — *Foreword by Jacques Vallée*. Vallée frames two questions — what origin myths reveal about human beginnings, and whether persistent "impossible" UFO reports require renegotiating physical reality — and executes his "strategic denial" reversal: granting the skeptics that UFOs don't exist removes the reason to suppress the reports, whereupon hundreds of thousands of testimonies can be examined. He praises the authors for refusing to impose a new ideology.

**[04-prologue.md]** — unit 4 (Front matter) — *Prologue*. The trilogy's operating model. The 1942 Cargo Cult reconstruction makes technological incomprehension into a mechanism for religion, ritual, sacred objects, and restricted authority; the 1947–1953 sequence places modern aerospace, occult contact, official denial, and elite networks (the Nine) in one frame. It defines the Phenomenon, adopts Kripal's real/religious/fictional method, proposes the ten-thousand-year Cargo-Cult / *ur-punkt* thesis, cautions about the word *alien* and about "disclosure," and glosses the four senses of *sekret*.

**[05-alien-genesis.md]** — unit 5 (printed Ch. 1) — *Alien Genesis*. Establishes method and vocabulary: religion as confrontation with the alien (Jacobsen, Jonas); Gnosticism's Demiurge and Alien God; a documentary critique of Sitchin against the *Enuma Elish*; and a shift from artifacts to consciousness (NDE, SRI remote viewing, shamanism, the Tibetan oracle, ceremonial magic). Its reversed-conjuration thought experiment — "we may be the 'spirits' for the magicians who fly the ships" — is offered explicitly as a model, not a claim.

**[06-the-gate-of-the-gods.md]** — unit 6 (printed Ch. 2) — *The Gate of the Gods*. Defines the Phenomenon spatially as the narrow overlap of "This World" and an "Other World" and locates it architecturally in Babylon's ziggurat. Wendt & Duvall's sovereignty argument explains the "authoritative taboo." The paleocontact dossier — creation-for-labor from Kingu's blood, the *bene elohim* / apkallu, the Nephilim as giants, Flood survival, Gilgamesh — advances an author inference while explicitly rejecting Sitchin's translations.

**[07-stairway-to-heaven.md]** — unit 7 (printed Ch. 3) — *Stairway to Heaven*. Converts descent into a repeatable technology of **ascent**: Jacob's ladder, Muhammad's *Mi'raj* and Buraq, the Daoist Pace of Yu, Masonic ritual, and Ezekiel's chariot converge on a package of isolation, sensory deprivation, seven levels, passwords, and permanent transformation. The chapter proposes — using Bob Lazar and unnamed reverse-engineering reports only to *pose* the question — that trained consciousness may be the craft's control interface, and reads the Works of the Chariot as a Cargo-Cult reconstruction.

**[08-the-cult-of-the-dead-gods.md]** — unit 8 (printed Ch. 4) — *The Cult of the Dead Gods*. Reads worldwide **blood sacrifice** as "psycho-biological" evidence of a debt of divine substance owed to absent creators, with Aztec religion (Aztlan, Chicomoztoc, Huitzilopochtli, Xipe Totec) as its systematic extreme. It extends, explicitly as speculation, into genetics: Grandy's "DNA consciousness," sacrifice as genetic selection, the shCherbak/Makukov stop-codon "message," and mortality as "planned obsolescence."

**[09-let-us-now-raise-famous-men.md]** — unit 9 (printed Ch. 5) — *Let Us Now Raise Famous Men*. Shifts the goal of contact from bodily immortality to **continuity of identity** and reads Egyptian funerary religion as a "resurrection machine" (Wilkinson's phrase for Djoser's Step Pyramid), with *ka*/*ba*/*akh*, the Opening of the Mouth, and stellar alignments as its components. A long controversies section (Cagliostro, Bauval/Hawass/Krupp, Temple's Dogon, *Hamlet's Mill*, Tompkins) separates textual paleocontact from weak alien-builder claims; Lenin's mausoleum, torpor, relativity, and drones bring the system into the present.

**[10-the-star-walkers.md]** — unit 10 (printed Ch. 6) — *The Star Walkers*. Extends the Egyptian ascent into living Chinese Daoist practice: the underworld-paradise mirror, Chinese pyramids and Niuheliang, the diffusionism-versus-independent-invention debate (Hummel, Oppenheimer's Sundaland, Schoch), and the **Pace of the Stars** — treading the Dipper's seven visible and two invisible stars with the body as "launch pad" and consciousness as vehicle. It cautiously compares Daoist "dark stars" with dark matter and proposes the nervous system as the "sekret machine of consciousness."

**[11-kala-chakra-chakra-vartin.md]** — unit 11 (printed Ch. 7) — *Kala Chakra/Chakra Vartin*. Internalizes the Dipper's stations as bodily *cakras*; rejects the *Vymanika Shastra* as a 20th-century channeled text; builds from Vedic, Avestan, Manichaean, and Buddhist sources (the winged disk, the consubstantial asura/deva conflict, the flying *ratana-cakka*); and proposes that wheel, mandala, and flying disk are cognate images of a real object. Its most novel and most hedged evidence is a set of Kalacakra-initiation / UFO-event synchronicities, repeatedly qualified as circumstantial, non-causal, and requiring far more research.

**[12-planet-earth-the-hot-lz.md]** — unit 12 (printed Ch. 8) — *Planet Earth: The Hot LZ*. The closing diagnosis. Greek *Gigantomachy* gives the recurring divine-war a Western name; Gnosticism (Nag Hammadi, the Alien God, the Book of Giants) explains embodied alienation; abduction, Prometheus, and *Frankenstein* make humans participants in the same manufacture and rebellion they attribute to gods. It rejects the crude "Ezekiel's chariot = saucer" reading, calls for *advaita*, and pictures a divided, weaponized Earth as a "hot LZ" that visitors would find dangerous and easy to dominate.

**[13-endnotes.md]** — unit 13 — *Endnotes*. The 141-note apparatus (32/14/6/9/29/23/18/10 across the eight printed chapters). Beyond citations, it adds source criticism (Waddell's Aryan-supremacist liabilities, the *Picatrix* dating, Evans-Wentz's Theosophy), declassified government material (the "giggle factor" DIA note, GRILL FLAME/remote-perturbation, a Gulf War remote-viewing scorecard), and repeated deferrals to Books Two and Three. It states the series thesis: "all religions are UFO religions."

## The evidentiary strategy

The authors braid several distinct *kinds* of evidence and argument, and — following Vallée and Kripal — insist they be weighed **together** rather than filtered through any one discipline. The volume's own recurring discipline is to keep those kinds visibly labeled, most explicitly in "Alien Genesis," which tags its paragraphs as documentary source, myth/text interpretation, author inference, or speculation.

- **Ancient texts as the primary record.** The *Enuma Elish* (excavated by Layard at Nineveh, 1849), Genesis, the *Gilgamesh* traditions, the *Atra-Hasis*, the Pyramid and Coffin Texts, the *Book of Gates*, Ezekiel, the *Book of Giants*, Hesiod's *Theogony*, and the Manu/Matsya flood account are treated as the oldest written evidence for contact. The authors repeatedly separate what a text *says* from their own inference that it preserves a memory of non-human beings.

- **Comparative religion and ritual.** Gnosticism, Merkava/Hekhalot mysticism, the *Mi'raj*, the Daoist Pace of the Stars, Kalacakra, ceremonial magic, and the Tibetan state oracle supply cross-cultural patterns (the Gate, the ascent, the seven levels, blood sacrifice, the resurrection machine) that the authors argue deserve explanation rather than dismissal.

- **Documentary criticism as a filter.** The volume is as notable for what it *rejects* as what it advances: Sitchin's Sumerian translations, the *Vymanika Shastra*, the Zündel Antarctica myth, and blanket alien-builder claims about the pyramids are all disallowed. This screening is meant to protect the stronger paleocontact inference from association with weak evidence.

- **Consciousness and paranormal research.** SRI remote viewing (Puthoff, Targ, Swann), the AMP/"Anomalous Mental Phenomena" programs, near-death studies, and the declassified STAR GATE/GRILL FLAME lineage are used to argue that governments themselves treated the Phenomenon as consciousness-implicating.

- **Modern scientific speculation, clearly flagged.** Crick and Orgel's directed panspermia, Grandy's DNA-consciousness, and the shCherbak/Makukov genetic-code "signal" are introduced as reported hypotheses and "platforms for research," repeatedly labeled speculative and deferred to Book Three.

- **The Cargo Cult as master analogy.** The 1942 South Pacific reconstruction is the volume's organizing figure: incomprehensible technology becomes gods, gifts become sacred relics, and ritual imitation preserves an encounter whose mechanism was never understood. It is applied to civilization itself, to the Works of the Chariot, and to Egyptian funerary practice.

- **The reversed-conjuration model.** The "Alien Genesis" thought experiment — inverting the magician and the summoned spirit — is the interpretive engine, offered as a way to hold the physical and consciousness dimensions together, explicitly disclaimed as literally true.

How the authors want it weighed: hold the physical and consciousness dimensions **simultaneously**; adopt "the understanding, not the belief"; treat myth as culturally interpreted memory ("myths tell history") without reducing it to literal chronicle; and accept *advaita* — that the Phenomenon belongs wholly to neither science nor religion, fact nor fantasy.

## Reading it critically

The notes flag numerous hedges, tensions, and open questions — many raised by the authors, some by the note-takers.

- **Assumed premise, not demonstrated conclusion.** The book states at the outset that it will *not* try to convince skeptics UFOs are real; it "assumes" contact and reads history through that lens. Much of its force therefore depends on granting the premise. The authors are candid about this ("we are using that premise to reinterpret evidence rather than deriving contact deductively").

- **Explicit, repeated self-labeling of speculation.** The reversed-conjuration experiment ("neither asserted nor implied to be true"), the child-sacrifice-as-genetic-selection idea, the *ur-chromosome*, the consciousness-controls-the-craft hypothesis, the DNA-consciousness and genetic-code "message" claims, and the Kalacakra/UFO synchronicities are all marked by the authors as conjecture, analogy, or a research direction rather than a finding.

- **The inference layer sits on top of the documentary layer.** In "The Gate of the Gods" especially, the authors reject Sitchin's translations yet advance a "stronger author inference of their own" that the same texts record non-human contact. A critical reader should keep asking which claims are the texts' and which are the authors' interpretation of them.

- **Contested and single-source authorities travel with the argument.** The endnotes themselves flag Waddell's discredited Aryan-Sumerian diffusionism, Evans-Wentz's Theosophy, the disputed Aryan Migration Theory, the uncertain Greek reading of alleged "I-beam" symbols, and web-only or unpublished-thesis sources. The Bauval/Hawass and Temple/Dogon controversies are shown to be entangled with personality and politics on all sides.

- **The Cargo Cult analogy cuts both ways.** It powerfully explains how technology becomes religion, but it is an analogy, not a demonstrated transmission chain. The authors use it to argue that ritual imitation preserves a real event; a skeptic can use the same structure to argue that ritual generates the appearance of one.

- **Provocative political and ethical analogies are the authors' own.** God's promise to Jacob framed as *Lebensraum*, the Auschwitz-orchestra meditation carried in from *War*'s companion argument, and "planned obsolescence" are rhetorical moves the notes attribute to the authors, distinct from documentary claims.

- **Numbering and transcription flags.** The note-unit numbers (0–13) diverge from the printed chapter numbers (front matter, then Chapters 1–8, then endnotes); the endnotes reconcile 141 notes as 32/14/6/9/29/23/18/10. The notes also record likely transcription artifacts (the Gilgamesh cubit-to-inch rounding in unit 6; "shCherbak" spelling; the Trachtenberg/Jamaludin spelling variants noted in *Man*).

- **Cross-book shelf connections are the knowledge base's, not the authors'.** Every link in the source notes to *the-holographic-universe*, *my-big-toe*, and *the-simulation-hypothesis* (consciousness-primary ontology, rendered reality) is explicitly marked as reader-side thematic resonance, **not** a comparison DeLonge & Levenda draw.

## What *Gods* establishes / what it does not

### What it establishes (within its own frame)

- That the world's origin myths, scriptures, and rituals contain a **recurring structure** — creation from divine substance, non-human teachers and sky-beings, interbreeding and hybrids, celestial ascent and descent, blood debt, and monumental technologies of immortality — that can be laid side by side and compared.
- That this structure was consistently understood by its own cultures as an encounter with something **alien, superior, and terrifying**, in a religious rather than a "scientific" vocabulary.
- That popular ancient-astronaut particulars (Sitchin's translations, the *Vymanika Shastra*, alien pyramid-builders) are **weakly supported** and can be set aside without abandoning the broader question.
- That governments and intelligence agencies did in fact fund **consciousness and paranormal research** (AMP, remote viewing, STAR GATE), documented in declassified files.

### What it does not establish

- It does **not** demonstrate that any specific myth is a literal transcript of an extraterrestrial event, or that the gods were physically present astronauts.
- It does **not** prove that consciousness is the control interface of anomalous craft, that DNA is conscious, that the genetic code carries a designed "message," or that mass ritual can manifest UFOs — all are flagged by the authors as speculation or research directions.
- It does **not** identify the Phenomenon's nature, origin, or intentions; the authors insist these can only be inferred, and reject waiting for government "disclosure" as both unlikely and conceptually confused.
- It does **not** endorse its own most provocative analogies (reversed conjuration, *Lebensraum*, planned obsolescence, genetic selection through sacrifice) as fact; they are interpretive instruments.

*Gods* is, on its own terms, the **groundwork** volume: it assembles the ancient record and the interpretive method, defers the modern institutional and scientific detail to *Man* and *War*, and asks the reader to hold the physical and the consciousness dimensions of the Phenomenon together — "the understanding, not the belief."
