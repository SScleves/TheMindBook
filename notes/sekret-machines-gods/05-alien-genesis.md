# Alien Genesis

## In one paragraph
DeLonge and Levenda argue that religion is humanity's oldest framework for confronting a power or intelligence experienced as superhuman, alien, and terrifying, and that modern inquiry into the Phenomenon therefore cannot simply discard the mystical vocabulary in which such encounters were preserved. Reading Gnostic, Sumerian, biblical, shamanic, and magical materials comparatively, they distinguish documented texts and practices from their own inference that these traditions may carry transformed memories of contact; they repeatedly criticize unsupported ancient-astronaut translations even while maintaining that those writers respond to a genuine explanatory gap. The chapter proposes that human origin stories, altered states, near-death and UFO experiences, remote-viewing research, initiation rituals, spirit possession, and ceremonial magic all point toward a porous boundary between ordinary reality and an "other world" mediated by consciousness. Its boldest move is an explicitly labeled thought experiment: reverse a magician's summoning ritual and imagine the apparent flying saucer as the other world's magic circle, with humans as the summoned spirits. The authors do not present that reversal as fact, but use it to justify their programmatic claim that science and a non-denominational mysticism must be developed together if the physical and consciousness dimensions of the Phenomenon are to be understood.

## Core claims
- **[Documentary source]** Aimé Michel's epigraph identifies a methodological problem: humanity historically placed supposedly superhuman thought in a religious context, while Ufology asks investigators to study a superhuman phenomenology scientifically without lapsing into mysticism. The authors adopt the difficulty but reject the clean exclusion of mysticism.
- **[Documentary source]** Thorkild Jacobsen, writing about third-millennium-BCE Babylon, defined religion through confrontation with "power not of this world"; Hans Jonas described the Gnostic God as absolutely alien to the universe. The authors stress that neither scholar was writing about astronauts, but treat both formulations as evidence that ancient divine experience was understood as alien, strange, and terrifying.
- **[Documentary history]** Gnosticism is presented as a loose family of mystical groups flourishing in Egypt and the Middle East in the first centuries CE, with some descendants surviving and incorporating Islamic elements. Its Jewish, Christian, and later Islamic currents claimed *gnosis*, privileged knowledge of the esoteric truth beneath exoteric scripture.
- **[Myth/text interpretation]** In the Gnostic rewriting of Genesis, the material cosmos was made not by the transcendent God but by the Demiurge, a subordinate Platonic creator opposed to spirit. The Serpent is recast as the Supreme or Alien God who offers knowledge, while the Demiurge seeks to preserve human ignorance; Adam and Eve therefore "fell awake" rather than fell from grace.
- **[Myth/text interpretation]** The Gnostic cosmos is a prison with Earth as its deepest dungeon, surrounded by seven or, in some systems, 365 concentric spheres. The authors note the latter number's evident symbolism: solar days and the 365 years Enoch lived before bodily translation to heaven.
- **[Author comparison]** The authors say this ancient scheme should interest modern believers in multiple interfering beings or hybrid humanity because it makes both humanity's lesser creators and the true God alien, nearly two millennia before current ancient-alien literature. They present resonance, not proof of literal extraterrestrials.
- **[Documentary criticism]** Zecharia Sitchin (1920-2010), an Azerbaijan-born former London journalist who moved to New York and taught himself Sumerian, produced idiosyncratic translations criticized by specialists in Sumerian and Akkadian. His books nonetheless became worldwide bestsellers translated into forty-five languages.
- **[Documentary criticism]** The authors state plainly that Sitchin's slave-hybrid theory and "twelfth planet" are not supported by the Sumerian texts or cylinder seals. They nonetheless describe him, using Jeffrey Kripal's category, as a writer-medium or intuitive whose story appealed because evolution, astrophysics, and genetics have not filled every perceived gap in human origins.
- **[Author inference]** The popularity of Sitchin, Erich von Däniken, Giorgio Tsoukalos, and David Hatcher Childress is attributed to a sense that science has not explained a continuing presence in the world. The authors do not endorse those writers' particulars; they say audiences feel that there is truth "somewhere" beneath errors and exaggerations.
- **[Author social analysis]** Distrust of government, academia, military institutions, and science has expanded conspiracy culture from political assassinations into metaphysics, ancient astronauts, Holy Grail narratives, Ufology, magic, art, and literature. The resulting underground mixes domains that official knowledge keeps separate.
- **[Documentary source]** The *Enuma Elish*, found at Nineveh near modern Mosul in 1849 by Austen Henry Layard (1817-1894), is described as an approximately one-thousand-line cuneiform creation epic whose authenticity and broad translation are not academically controversial.
- **[Myth/text summary]** In the epic, fresh-water Abzu and salt-water/Chaos Tiamat precede Earth and sky; the younger gods' noise provokes Tiamat, Marduk leads their revolt, kills and bisects her to form Earth and sky, kills Kingu, and makes humans from Kingu's blood and Marduk's breath.
- **[Author interpretation]** The authors read that creation as ancient Sumer's culturally accepted account of humans as a hybrid of opposed divine substances: establishment blood and rebel breath, matter and spirit. They explicitly deny that cultural acceptance proves scientific truth.
- **[Comparative interpretation]** *Hamlet's Mill* is cited for reading mythic gods, demons, and serpents as coded astronomy; L. A. Waddell for comparing Sumerian, Chinese, and Egyptian language in support of common racial origins; Robert Temple and others for the controversial claim that the Dogon knew inaccessible astronomy. These works are evidence of interpretive strategies, not settled proof in the chapter.
- **[Author comparative claim]** Across Sumerian tablets, ancient-astronaut literature, Indian Tantra, Egyptian narratives, the Bible, Kabbalah, pranayama, and European alchemy, the authors identify recurring motifs of astral/divine origin, creation by divine sexuality or substances, and breath as a divine life principle. They argue that cross-cultural recurrence deserves explanation while allowing ordinary shared experiences such as birth, death, and puberty to generate common ritual themes.
- **[Author claim]** Origin stories matter because people use them to locate present identity and future possibility. The authors treat superhero culture as a popular religion whose rescuing superhumans encode moral values, then use secret identities to connect modern myth with Enlightenment secret-society models.
- **[Historical/cultural analysis]** Superman, created in 1933 and published by DC from 1938, arrived from an exploding star, acquired powers through interaction between terrestrial environment and alien heredity, and hid as Clark Kent. Because this predates the post-1947 UFO era, the authors read him instead through World War II, Nazi secret weapons, and Nietzsche's *Übermensch*: an Allied, godlike, and literally "illegal" alien.
- **[Historical interpretation]** The Rosicrucian Manifestos of the seventeenth century popularized a hidden fraternity of morally elevated adepts with extraordinary knowledge. Its founder Christian Rosenkreutz was said to have traveled east, probably to Yemen, learned gold-making and the *elixir vitae*, founded a benevolent order, died, and been hidden in the Vault of the Adepts; Freemasonry then embodied a related secret community grounded more in solidarity and intellectual liberty than miraculous chemistry.
- **[Author inference]** The authors ask whether Enlightenment secret societies became the lens through which later Europeans imagined Sumerian and Egyptian hidden knowledge, and whether the popularity of secret superheroes expresses an unconscious conviction that concealed superhuman custodians are real. These are questions and inferences, not documentary conclusions.
- **[Documentary source]** The first-millennium-BCE Akkadian *Maqlu* contains extensive protective incantations against feared sorcerers and witches. Biblical parallels include the Witch of Endor (1 Samuel 28:3-25), Exodus 22:18, and Job 3:8's people skilled in rousing Leviathan.
- **[Comparative myth interpretation]** The authors trace the creation-serpent motif from Tiamat through Leviathan, Behemoth, Marduk, Saint George, Eden, Gnostic serpent reverence, Kundalini, and the caduceus. Orthodoxy makes the serpent Satan; Gnosticism makes it the awakening interloper, so the authors compare Gnosticism to an early conspiracy theory and modern conspiracy theory to a new Gnosticism.
- **[Documentary/text interpretation]** In the Mandaean narrative summarized here, the Alien God, Stranger, or Messenger descends from space through planetary orbits to free humanity through knowledge while planetary Archons or Aeons resist. The authors preserve this as sectarian cosmology and use it to formulate two alien forces struggling over an unconscious humanity.
- **[Speculative synthesis]** Combining Ufology, conspiracy, mysticism, psychology, politics, and science could yield a grand theory of consciousness involving a multiverse, parallel universe, or hyperspace such as mathematically modeled superstring/supergravity spaces. The chapter presents this as a desired research direction requiring specially trained consciousness, not an achieved theory.
- **[Documentary archaeology with author inference]** Recent Genesis/Eden research is reported as proposing a pre-Sumerian Proto-Euphratian or Ubaidian people whose language supplied the names Adam and Eden; "Eden" may mean fertile plain and "Adam" a settlement on that plain. LANDSAT images reportedly identified two submerged riverbeds in the Persian Gulf, supporting a historical memory of rising seas and displacement.
- **[Author distinction]** A climate-and-migration account can explain a real fertile plain and survival story, but the authors argue it cannot exhaust texts that also describe other worlds and beings. Eden is therefore treated as both terrestrial place and a simulacrum where heaven contacted Earth; the emotional and non-human content is taken as evidence that the texts do more than code ordinary disasters.
- **[Myth/text interpretation]** The Hebrew Bible's wrathful deity, destroyed cities, empowered armies, and demanded blood are treated as expressions of terrifying power from beyond. Genesis records a rupture produced by human will and knowledge, while many religions describe present existence as degeneration from an earlier blessed condition.
- **[Author philosophical contrast]** Evolution describes adaptive change serving species survival rather than individual fulfillment; genes require reproduction, while self-aware consciousness desires personal continuity and immortality. The authors pun on Genesis/genes and ask whether religion supplies an individual horizon absent from the collective logic of genetic propagation.
- **[Speculation]** Near-death experience is proposed as a clue to the conflict. Medicine tentatively attributes tunnels, light, loved ones, peace, and reduced fear of death to endorphins, but the authors ask what evolutionary benefit a pleasant death gives genes; they offer two possibilities: consciousness continues beyond "Act One," or genes use pleasure to discourage costly individual immortality.
- **[Documentary research report]** Studies in the 1980s reportedly compared NDE survivors and UFO experiencers and found similarities in how both groups understood life, death, and the paranormal. The authors connect this with government contractors' early recognition that UFOs implicated consciousness.
- **[Documentary history]** Jacques Vallée participated early in Stanford Research Institute remote-viewing work with Hal Puthoff, Russell Targ, and Ingo Swann, before US Army interest classified the project and ended Vallée's direct participation. Vallée said Swann taught him rudimentary remote viewing but that he was not especially gifted.
- **[Contextual history]** The chapter notes that Swann was a Scientologist, a movement historians of religion classify as a UFO religion, and that Vallée and Project Blue Book's J. Allen Hynek had Rosicrucian interests. These associations are presented as intellectual context, not as discrediting evidence.
- **[Author account of Vallée]** Vallée accepts physical UFO evidence but, after decades of research, interprets the Phenomenon as an artifact or control mechanism of consciousness. The authors emphasize the observers' profound, life-changing psychological effects as potentially the point of encounters rather than incidental fallout.
- **[Documentary institutional history]** US Army, Air Force, Navy, CIA, NSA, and DIA paranormal investigations used contractors including SRI and SAIC. To reduce the "giggle factor," official language replaced ESP, psychic powers, and telekinesis with AMP, "Anomalous Mental Phenomena."
- **[Documentary/conceptual distinction]** AMP included precognition, telepathy, psychokinesis, remote viewing, and "remote perturbation." Remote viewing gathered intelligence; remote perturbation was envisioned for sabotage by mentally throwing switches or disrupting aircraft, missiles, submarines, navigation, and control equipment.
- **[Documentary report plus caveat]** The authors say many government AMP/RV programs were curtailed after decades of research and missions, but report only a persistent rumor that psychic personnel remained active or returned after September 11, 2001. They explicitly acknowledge uncertain science and suggest laboratory conditions may have been counterproductive.
- **[Programmatic claim]** The project seeks a revolution in hard and social sciences by reassessing human function, cosmic purpose, opportunities, and threats. It makes the working hypothesis that mysticism is a necessary adjunct to science for the Phenomenon and warns that this "shotgun wedding" would transform both.
- **[Historical/anthropological claim]** Shamanism is described as the earliest generally recognized form of religion and as neglected by architecture-focused ancient-alien media. Neolithic changes from geometric marks to humans, humanoids, surreal animals, and astronomically aligned ritual megaliths such as Stonehenge are interpreted as a possible transition toward awareness of off-planet and unseen realities.
- **[Author inference]** Because shamanic labor manipulates invisible forces without obvious cause-and-effect, the authors infer that hunter-gatherer communities recognized an unseen layer of reality and specialists able to engage it. This inference does not establish alien contact but supplies a social precedent for it.
- **[Phenomenological comparison]** Modern contact usually occurs as Michel's "non-contact": UAP appear without public landings or mass interaction. The July 1952 Washington, DC overflights are contrasted with Hollywood landings; close encounters instead tend to involve isolated individuals in remote settings, resembling shamanic initiation contexts.
- **[Author interpretive caution]** Cultural vocabularies may make the same Phenomenon appear differently, and later commentators may misread old terms through their own categories. The chapter therefore treats religion, parapsychology, and encounter testimony as different descriptions requiring translation rather than automatic equivalence.
- **[Documentary ethnography]** Shamanic flight can ascend to celestial regions or descend underground and may involve danger, death, dismemberment, illness, disability, trance, drums, rattles, sensory manipulation, or entheogenic plants/fungi. Mircea Eliade's synthesis is acknowledged as criticized, while the authors say many of its underlying reports have not been similarly rejected.
- **[Author psychological model]** Initiation drama carries data beyond intellectual propositions. Blindfolds, binding, threat, isolation, altered time/space, symbols, and emotional trauma make a liminal space in Victor Turner's sense; the authors compare this structure with UFO encounters, whose content is dramatic and traumatic rather than primarily discursive.
- **[Historical psychiatric case]** R. D. Laing's 1960s claim that nervous breakdown can be nervous breakthrough and schizophrenia a spiritual initiation briefly became influential before drug-centered psychiatry reframed disorders as chemical imbalances. The authors invoke his "politics of experience" to challenge institutions that pathologize unusual states, without claiming all illness is initiation.
- **[Documentary ethnographic case]** Tibet's state oracle preserves a Bön-derived shamanic role inside a Buddhist society. The head of Nechung Monastery, in exile with the Dalai Lama at Dharamsala, becomes the *kuten* or "material basis" only in a summoning ritual, wears a costume exceeding seventy pounds, moves vigorously while possessed, speaks coded predictions, and may require prolonged convalescence afterward.
- **[Author inference]** The oracle is offered as a "laboratory case" for a permeable boundary between waking reality and another domain variously named parallel universe, aether, or astral plane. The authors admit these names are culturally overloaded placeholders rather than scientific identifications.
- **[Historical concept]** The astral plane, literally the starry plane, is traced through Plato, Plotinus, and Neoplatonism: the soul leaves the Earth-bound body, travels through planetary and stellar spheres, and returns with knowledge; conversely, spirits enter through a human material basis. Mediumship and channeling are presented as secularized descendants.
- **[Documentary ritual description]** European ceremonial magic uses fasting, prayer, sensory focus, robes, implements, two concentric circles, divine names and seals, four lights at cardinal points, incense, bells or gongs, *voces magicae*, gestures, and sometimes a separate triangle to summon a spirit to visible appearance while the conscious magician remains protected at the *axis mundi*.
- **[Author Cargo Cult comparison]** The authors compare the lit circle, smoke, sounds, arcane markings, clothed occupant, and triangular spirit-space with UFO reports of luminous circular or triangular craft, strange symbols, unusual clothing, and telepathy. This is a formal analogy, not a documentary claim that grimoires describe spacecraft.
- **[Documentary textual history]** The early-fourteenth-century *Liber Iuratus Honorii* or *Sworn Book of Honorius* is identified as an early standardized grimoire, later than the eleventh-century *Picatrix* and forms of the *Key of Solomon*. It requires a stone or brick circle with a central earth mound on which planetary spirits appear; the authors note its visual likeness to an iconic saucer.
- **[Speculative thought experiment]** Reversing the rite, the authors imagine an alien inside a saucer penetrating our world to summon humans, with knocks, lights, telepathy, threats, pain, scars, repeated abductions, and compelled return corresponding to magical coercion. They call the suggestion outrageous and explicitly say it is neither asserted nor implied to be true; its purpose is to open alternative consciousness-centered hypotheses.
- **[Author synthesis]** Vallée's control-system model fits ceremonial magic because the magician controls the rite and summoned being. Mutual visible appearance would mark a temporary breach between worlds that changes at least the magician's mind; the chapter's provocative formulation is that humans might be spirits summoned by the ships' operators.
- **[Documentary institutional framing]** The authors defend occult comparison by noting that US military and intelligence bodies investigated occultism, parapsychology, and mediumship in separate Cold War streams: military psychic intelligence/sabotage and CIA psychological warfare, behavior modification, mind control, and brainwashing.
- **[Programmatic proposal]** Their proposed "spiritualized refinement" is explicitly non-denominational and rejects organized religion's corruption and dogma. It is meant as a mystical partner to material science because the Phenomenon, in their view, manifests in both physical and consciousness dimensions.

## Concepts
- **Religious attitude / super-human phenomenology** — Aimé Michel's problem of applying scientific methods to a category of thought previously treated only as superior, divine, or religious. The chapter uses it to define the disciplinary impasse around Ufology.
- **Gnosis / Gnosticism** — hidden or special knowledge claimed by diverse Jewish, Christian, and later Islamic mystical currents; here, a counter-reading of scripture in which material creation conceals a deeper spiritual truth.
- **Demiurge** — the subordinate Platonic creator of the material universe in Gnostic Genesis, opposed to spirituality and mistaken for the highest God.
- **Alien God** — Hans Jonas's wholly transcendent Gnostic deity and the Mandaean Stranger/Messenger who descends through planetary spheres to liberate humans through knowledge. "Alien" here is a theological quality in the source traditions before the authors compare it with contemporary contact ideas.
- **Consensus reality** — the socially agreed material world from which Gnostic alienation begins; the authors contrast it with spiritual, paranormal, and anomalous experience.
- **Cosmic prison** — the Gnostic universe of concentric spheres with Earth as its lowest dungeon and planetary Archons guarding escape.
- **Ancient-astronaut theory** — modern interpretations that read gods and cultural transformations as extraterrestrial intervention. The authors criticize Sitchin's philology and astronomy while retaining paleocontact as a hypothesis worth investigating.
- **Hybrid humanity** — in this chapter, first the Gnostic-modern resonance of humans made by lesser beings for service, then the *Enuma Elish* reading of humans formed from Kingu's blood and Marduk's breath. It is an interpretation of myths, not demonstrated biology.
- **Ur-mythos** — a hypothetical shared primal narrative behind cross-cultural creation, breath, astral, birth, death, and puberty motifs. The authors acknowledge that shared human life events can generate common myths without contact.
- **Origin story** — a narrative that explains where a being or people comes from and thereby frames identity and destiny; superhero stories supply a secular comparison.
- **Secret society** — a hidden body holding knowledge or powers, exemplified by Rosicrucians and Freemasons and proposed as a later interpretive lens for ancient texts.
- **Maqlu** — first-millennium-BCE Akkadian anti-witchcraft incantations and rituals, documentary evidence that secret harmful specialists were feared in Mesopotamia.
- **Archons / Aeons** — planetary powers in Gnostic systems that maintain the world's prison and oppose liberating knowledge.
- **Proto-Euphratian / Ubaidian** — proposed names for a pre-Sumerian Near Eastern population whose words may survive in "Adam" and "Eden."
- **Eden as fertile plain** — an archaeological-linguistic interpretation in which rising Persian Gulf waters displaced inhabitants of a real plain; the authors add, as interpretation, that Genesis turns it into a contact point with Heaven.
- **Genetic imperative** — the chapter's personification of genes as favoring reproduction and species continuity rather than individual identity or immortality.
- **Near-death experience (NDE)** — reports of tunnel, light, dead loved ones, peace, and reduced fear after temporary clinical death; compared with UFO experiencer transformations and used to ask whether consciousness exceeds biological utility.
- **Anomalous Mental Phenomena (AMP)** — official neutral term designed to reduce ridicule around precognition, telepathy, psychokinesis, remote viewing, and related claimed abilities.
- **Remote viewing / remote perturbation** — respectively, alleged mental intelligence gathering at a distance and alleged mental interference with physical switches or systems; the latter was considered for sabotage.
- **Shamanism** — the chapter's candidate for earliest religion: a socially recognized specialist enters altered states, communicates with invisible forces, travels between worlds, heals or divines, and preserves communal symbolic knowledge.
- **Initiatory dismemberment** — the shaman's experienced death, bodily destruction, and reconstruction, compared with Eleusinian, alchemical, Masonic, and encounter trauma as transformative dramaturgy rather than ordinary injury.
- **Liminal space** — Victor Turner's set-apart threshold where normal time, space, identity, and sensory expectation are suspended, allowing initiation or contact to be experienced.
- **Material basis (*kuten*)** — the Tibetan state oracle's human vehicle for a spirit during a specific possession rite.
- **Astral plane** — the starry intermediary realm through which souls ascend and beings descend; an ancient cosmological term the authors retain cautiously because of New Age baggage.
- **Mediumship / channeling** — forms of communication in which a human becomes a temporary vehicle for another intelligence; presented as simplified descendants of older possession systems.
- **Ceremonial magic** — deliberate, conscious ritual penetration of another realm to compel a spirit into visible appearance, using strict sensory and symbolic controls.
- **Magic circle / axis mundi** — the protected ritual vehicle and center of creation occupied by the magician; in the authors' analogy, it resembles a lit, marked aerial craft.
- **Visible appearance** — the grimoire's objective of rendering a normally invisible entity mutually perceptible, interpreted as a temporary breach between worlds.
- **Cargo Cult analogy** — imitation of an incompletely understood technology through ritual form; applied here to magic circles and later reversed so the apparent craft may be the other side's ritual device.
- **Control system** — Vallée's conception of the Phenomenon as shaping human belief and behavior; linked here to the magician's control of ritual and summoned entity.
- **Science-and-mysticism partnership** — the chapter's stated method: combine material investigation with disciplined study of consciousness without imposing religious dogma.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **[Documentary intellectual history: Jacobsen and Jonas]** Thorkild Jacobsen's analysis of third-millennium-BCE Babylon and Hans Jonas's study of Gnosticism in the first centuries CE independently use otherworldly or alien language for the divine. The authors say the separated contexts show a stable human response of estrangement and terror, while emphasizing that neither scholar proposed alien astronauts.
- **[Documentary reception history: Zecharia Sitchin, 1920-2010]** Sitchin's self-taught readings produced more than a dozen books, worldwide bestsellers in forty-five languages. Specialists disputed his Sumerian, Akkadian, Aramaic, seals, slave-hybrid thesis, and twelfth planet; the authors say the outcome demonstrates not accuracy but a large unmet demand for an origin account beyond institutional science.
- **[Reported scientific speculation: Thomas Van Flandern]** The late astronomer proposed that one or more solar-system planets exploded, using the idea to account for the dinosaur-killing asteroid strike sixty-five million years ago and a civilization on Mars. The chapter presents this as the kind of astronomical weakness/speculation Sitchin intuitively touched, not as established fact.
- **[Documentary archaeology: Austen Henry Layard, Nineveh, 1849]** Layard discovered the tablets of the roughly one-thousand-line *Enuma Elish* near present-day Mosul. Its uncontested status and relatively stable translation allow the authors to separate the documented epic from Sitchin's disputed reconstructions.
- **[Myth case: Enuma Elish]** Abzu and Tiamat generate the gods; conflict follows their noise; Marduk kills Tiamat and uses her halves for sky and Earth, kills Kingu, and makes humanity from divine blood and breath. The authors say it records a five-thousand-year-old culturally accepted model of humans as hybrid matter/spirit born from divine war, not a scientific account.
- **[Comparative scholarship: Hamlet's Mill, Waddell, Temple]** *Hamlet's Mill* decodes myth as astronomy; L. A. Waddell compares languages across Sumer, China, and Egypt to posit common racial origins; Robert Temple's Dogon argument claims astronomical knowledge requiring an advanced external source. The authors use the cases to show repeated efforts to recover technical information from myth, while calling them criticized or controversial.
- **[Comparative religious examples: breath and divine sexuality]** Indian Tantras and Egyptian stories create the cosmos through divine sexual acts; Genesis mixes mud and God's breath; Jewish Kabbalah, Indian Tantra and pranayama, and European alchemy treat breath as divine. The authors say the recurrent blood/breath/sexuality motifs may preserve a common account of astral origin, while shared human experience remains an alternative explanation.
- **[Cultural case: superhero origin stories, 1933-1938 onward]** Superman is sent by spacecraft from an exploding world, gains powers through Earth-heredity interaction, saves humanity, and hides as Clark Kent; Batman provides the darker secret counterpart. The authors say this functions like popular religion and, because Superman predates modern UFO culture, reflects World War II, Nazi secret weapons, and secret identity more directly.
- **[Historical case: Rosicrucian Manifestos, seventeenth century]** Christian Rosenkreutz allegedly traveled to Yemen, learned transmutation and universal medicine, founded an ethical fraternity, and was hidden in the Vault of the Adepts. The later rise of Freemasonry supplied a community-and-liberty version of secret power; the authors say these movements normalized hidden enlightened custodians.
- **[Documentary texts: Maqlu and biblical witchcraft]** The Akkadian *Maqlu* supplies extensive anti-sorcery rites; 1 Samuel presents the Witch of Endor raising Samuel for Saul; Exodus orders that a witch not live; Job links cursers with rousing Leviathan. The authors say these preserve fear of specialists who manipulate the unseen.
- **[Comparative myth case: serpent conflict]** Tiamat, Leviathan and Behemoth, Marduk, Saint George's dragon, Eden's serpent, the Gnostic Alien God, Kundalini, and Hermes/Mercury's caduceus place reptilian forms at the boundary of chaos, knowledge, rejuvenation, and spiritual power. The authors say moral polarity varies: the serpent may enslave, threaten, or awaken.
- **[Myth case: Mandaean Alien God]** In the sectarian account, the Stranger descends through planetary orbits, opposed by planetary gods, to wake and liberate humanity. The authors say it offers a remarkably explicit two-force contact model but treat it as Gnostic narrative rather than independent event documentation.
- **[Archaeological-linguistic case: submerged Eden]** Modern scholarship reportedly traces Adam and Eden to an older Proto-Euphratian/Ubaidian language and interprets them as settlement and fertile plain. LANDSAT images found two submerged river courses south of Iraq; the outcome supports a flood/displacement memory, which the authors combine with the text's spiritual actors rather than using it to erase them.
- **[Textual-historical case: Sumerian material in Genesis]** Flood narratives and other Mesopotamian themes appear in abbreviated or altered biblical form after the Babylonian captivity. The authors say technological tools such as satellite imagery and ground-penetrating radar increasingly show that scriptural stories can encode real places and events, though the non-human layer remains interpretive.
- **[Biological-philosophical case: genes versus consciousness]** Species-level propagation makes individual self-awareness unnecessary, yet religions promise individual judgment, survival, and resurrection. The authors use this mismatch to ask whether consciousness has a role not reducible to genes; no experiment resolves the question.
- **[Reported medical case: near-death experience]** People revived after minutes of apparent clinical death report tunnels, light, loved ones, peace, and reduced fear. Medicine tentatively proposes endorphin release, with no conclusive evidence; the authors challenge its adaptive rationale and speculate either post-body consciousness or a gene-level anti-immortality mechanism.
- **[Reported comparative studies: NDE/UFO, 1980s]** Studies comparing near-death survivors and UFO experiencers reportedly found similar changes in beliefs about life, death, and paranormal reality. The authors say this supports treating the Phenomenon as consciousness-related, though the chapter gives no sample sizes or named institutions for these studies.
- **[Institutional case: SRI remote viewing]** Jacques Vallée worked with Hal Puthoff, Russell Targ, and Ingo Swann at Stanford Research Institute before US Army classification; Vallée described his own ability as rudimentary. The authors say this links a major physical-UFO investigator directly to experimental consciousness research.
- **[Institutional history: AMP programs, Cold War through post-9/11 rumor]** Army, Air Force, Navy, CIA, NSA, and DIA work through SRI, SAIC, and others adopted the AMP label. Remote viewing promised intelligence and remote perturbation sabotage of aircraft, missiles, submarines, and switches; programs were later curtailed, while continued or renewed service after September 11, 2001 remains rumor in the chapter.
- **[Documentary sighting contrast: Washington, DC, July 1952]** Urban UAP appeared as overflights rather than public landings with humanoid occupants. The authors use the case to distinguish cinematic contact from the Phenomenon's usual oblique visibility.
- **[Archaeological period case: Neolithic transformation]** The chapter contrasts earlier geometric art with Neolithic humans, humanoids, surreal animals, and megaliths combining astronomy and ritual, exemplified by Stonehenge. The authors infer a possible boundary where sky and unseen forces became culturally central; this is not offered as direct alien evidence.
- **[Cross-cultural initiation case]** Shamanic dismemberment/death is compared with Eleusinian initiation, Renaissance alchemical images, and Masonic ritual. A First Degree Mason is blindfolded and meets a sword point; a Third Degree Mason undergoes mock burial and resurrection. The authors say emotional dramaturgy carries subconscious data that propositions alone cannot.
- **[Psychiatric history: R. D. Laing, 1960s]** Laing called nervous breakdown potential breakthrough and schizophrenia a spiritual initiation. Drug-centered psychiatry displaced this approach by treating behavioral change under medication as proof of chemical imbalance; the authors say the outcome exemplifies institutional control over acceptable experience.
- **[Ethnographic case: Tibetan state oracle]** At Nechung/Dharamsala, the monastery head becomes the *kuten* during a complex rite, wears over seventy pounds of costume, moves freely while possessed, gives coded predictions consulted by the Dalai Lama, and can be unable to walk for long periods afterward. The authors offer it as observable evidence of a culturally bounded altered state and possible world-boundary permeability, not proof of a particular spirit ontology.
- **[Historical cosmology: Plato to Plotinus]** Ancient and Neoplatonic models place planetary and stellar spheres around Earth; trained souls travel outward and spirits travel inward through a material basis. The authors say this codifies the shamanic journey and supplies the lineage of the "astral plane."
- **[Documentary ritual case: ceremonial magic]** The magician fasts and prays, controls light, color, scent, sound, clothes, implements, words and gestures, stands inside concentric protective circles, and compels an entity to appear in a triangle. Any unplanned event becomes contact evidence within the closed sensory field; the authors compare this designed liminality with encounter reports.
- **[Textual history: Sworn Book of Honorius, early fourteenth century]** Later than the eleventh-century *Picatrix* and alongside *Key of Solomon* traditions, the *Sworn Book* standardizes lengthy preparation and circles. Its planetary-spirit rite uses a brick/stone circle and central earth mound for the apparition; the authors say the plan resembles a saucer and makes the circle the spirit's vehicle.
- **[Speculative thought experiment: reversed conjuration]** The authors reverse magician and spirit: an alien in a circular vehicle enters our plane and summons isolated humans. Encounter knocks, lights, telepathy, pain, marks, repeat abductions, and promises to return are mapped onto grimoire coercion. The stated outcome is conceptual expansion only; they explicitly disclaim truth.
- **[Historical policy case: two Cold War streams]** Military organizations investigated psychic collection and sabotage, while CIA work pursued psychological warfare, behavior modification, mind control, and brainwashing. The authors say official willingness to examine occult and parapsychological methods legitimizes a more disciplined consciousness-centered inquiry, not the programs' ethics or success.
- **[Illustrated historical case: Simon Magus, Nuremberg Chronicle, 1493]** Simon flies over Rome in a magical contest with Saint Peter and is cast down when Peter's angel attacks Simon's demons. The caption treats the scene as an example of celestial conflict between classes of beings operating through human surrogates.
- **[Illustrated biblical case: Witch of Endor]** William Blake's image of 1 Samuel 28:3-25 shows Saul compelling a ritual specialist to summon Samuel. It is juxtaposed with Joseph Smith's Moroni encounter and Dee/Kelly's angelic work to emphasize repeated human efforts to call non-human or dead intelligences.
- **[Illustrated religious history: Joseph Smith Jr.]** Smith's multi-year magical work at Hill Cumorah culminates in the Angel Moroni and the recovery of sacred material. The authors use it as a modern American comparison to older evocation narratives.
- **[Illustrated occult history: John Dee and Edward Kelly]** Their prolonged Elizabethan operations reportedly produced contact with angels and an "Angelic" or Enochian language with alphabet, vocabulary, and grammar that occultists still use. The case shows contact represented through new language and ritual technology.
- **[Illustrated ethnography: Lapland and British Columbia shamans]** A Lapland shaman's symbol-covered drum and a twentieth-century British Columbia shaman visually extend the discussion of isolation, disorientation, initiation, and star travel across distinct cultures.
- **[Illustrated grimoire history: Heptameron, Key of Solomon, The Magus (1801)]** Peter of Abano's circle shows concentric rings and four stars; the *Key of Solomon* adds coiled serpent, four candle-stars, and eastern triangle; Francis Barrett's 1801 *The Magus*, compiled partly from Cornelius Agrippa, is proposed as a likely source for Joseph Smith. The captions say orientation and celestial symbolism make the circles mechanisms for other-world travel.

## Thread
This opening chapter establishes the book's core method and vocabulary. It begins with the alien quality of divinity, tests popular ancient-astronaut claims against better-attested Sumerian and Gnostic texts, and shifts the investigation from disputed artifacts to consciousness, ritual, and altered experience. The sequence moves deliberately from origins (Gnostic and Sumerian creation), through cultural afterlives (superheroes, secret societies, serpents, Eden), into modern boundary phenomena (NDE, UFO experience, remote viewing), then back to the proposed first religion (shamanism) and a technical ritual analogue (ceremonial magic). Its reversed-conjuration thought experiment is not a conclusion but a model for the rest of the volume: preserve physical evidence, add the neglected consciousness dimension, and keep documentary evidence, textual interpretation, author inference, and speculation visibly separate.
