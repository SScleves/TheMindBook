# The Star Walkers

## In one paragraph
In this assigned unit 10, printed as Chapter 6, DeLonge and Levenda argue that ancient Egyptian funerary ascent and living Chinese Daoist celestial flight preserve a shared complex linking the North, the circumpolar stars, death, rebirth, immortality, and transformation of consciousness. They place Chinese pyramids, Niuheliang's astronomically aligned ritual landscape, the *Pace of Yü*, alchemy, shamanic sensory withdrawal, and claims of Egypt–Asia or Sundaland diffusion into one comparative history, while acknowledging controversy over diffusionism and uncertainty about particular routes and dates. Their central practical example is the Daoist *Pace of the Stars*: a living adept internalizes, circumambulates, and treads the seven visible and two invisible stars of the Big Dipper as gates toward the polestar, using the body as a launch pad and consciousness rather than the physical body as the vehicle. The authors compare Daoist "dark stars" cautiously but suggestively with modern dark matter or antimatter theories, and ask whether disciplined imagination can disclose realities outside ordinary sensory bandwidth. They conclude that shamanic and initiatory systems may be inherited training technologies for deliberate contact, that modern alien-abduction and UFO-religion reports continue the same pattern in a different vocabulary, and that the human nervous system may be the "sekret machine" through which humanity eventually undertakes consciously controlled contact with its alleged celestial origins.

## Core claims
- The authors argue that Egyptian travel through the Underworld and ascent to the circumpolar stars are complementary aspects of one postmortem journey, analogous to the Great Pyramid's north-facing shaft toward the Big Dipper and south-facing shaft toward Orion and Sirius.
- They distinguish the sunlike cycle of setting and rising, associated with the pharaoh's death and resurrection, from the circumpolar stars' unceasing revolution around the pole, which symbolizes immortality and corresponds to the Chinese "Garden of the Immortals."
- They say Daoism formalizes the underworld and the realms above as mirror images and treats celestial flight as a route to immortality, healing, longevity, knowledge, and powers.
- They contrast Egypt, where the sealed Pyramid Texts apparently instructed the dead king's soul, with China, where a living ritual specialist could undertake an interior ascent through the *Pace of Yü*; in both, something other than the physical body travels and the embodied person may benefit.
- The authors stress that ascent is structured and dangerous: the traveler must be physically and psychologically prepared, pass gates, overcome obstacles, and follow exact instructions.
- They connect Egyptian, Chinese, Middle Eastern, and European alchemy through material and spiritual transformation, return to *prima materia*, accelerated regeneration, and psycho-biological imagery involving blood, semen, breath, germination, and reproduction.
- They argue that alchemical and creation traditions converge around a dimly perceived proposition: humanity was created by beings not of Earth and must survive through apotheosis, becoming more than it presently is.
- They warn that the split between science and consciousness makes humanity vulnerable to a force that has united both, and that either scientific suppression of religion or religious suppression of science produces distortion.
- They present Asian traditions as especially useful because they describe consciousness in the living practitioner, whereas the Egyptian textual system discussed in the previous chapter was primarily designed for the dead.
- They state that sacred architecture, stellar alignment, celestial flight, and consciousness trace a single historical arc from early myth to modern spaceflight; they preview Qian Xuesen's recognition of ancient systems' relevance to aeronautics for Book Two.
- They hedge the Phenomenon's role along that arc: it has intervened, or seemed to intervene, perhaps encouraging humanity, perhaps monitoring it, and perhaps thwarting progress.
- They argue that Chinese pyramid complexes show the Middle East did not have a monopoly on pyramidal royal tombs or astronomical alignment.
- They report that the Xi'an region contains roughly seventy to one hundred tombs, many pyramidal and some forming a Chinese analogue to Egypt's Valley of the Kings; most probably date from the first millennium BCE through the first millennium CE, though some have been claimed to be as old as Egypt's Fifth Dynasty monuments.
- They do not endorse a single-builder ancient civilization outright, calling that theory potentially far-fetched, but insist that scholarly proposals of Egypt–China contact, influence, or descent from a common *ur-culture* deserve acknowledgment.
- They define the main archaeological dispute as diffusionism versus independent inventionism: either geographically distant similarities reflect contact and transmission, especially by sea, or comparable cultures invented similar forms separately.
- They criticize modern disciplinary specialization as a possible institutional reason independent inventionism feels natural, because archaeologists, geneticists, astronomers, biologists, physicists, and linguists often work in isolation.
- They use Seigbert Hummel's comparison of Egyptian and Eurasian scripts, iconography, narratives, and temple plans as a serious diffusionist case, while presenting it as research and comparison rather than settled proof.
- They use Stephen Oppenheimer's disease-immunity and myth correlations to support the proposal that dispersed populations carried both genetic profiles and creation/flood traditions from submerged Sundaland.
- They contrast Toby Wilkinson's religious reading of Eastern Desert boat petroglyphs with an Oppenheimer-informed historical reading in which the boats may also preserve an actual migration from an inundated homeland.
- Their methodological aim is to dissolve a strict religion/history binary: a spiritual image may encode an actual event, so a petroglyph can be simultaneously ritual expression and historical memory.
- They cite Robert Schoch as supporting a Southeast Asian origin for pyramid-building culture and possible links among Southeast Asia, India, Mesoamerica, Peru, Sumer, Mesopotamia, and Egypt, while conceding that particular astronomical measurements remain contestable.
- They argue that if Oppenheimer and Schoch are entertained, Aztec traditions of arrival by boat from a land surrounded by water become possible historical memories rather than mere invention.
- They state their larger hermeneutic directly: myths explain how the world was and became what it is, and therefore can tell history.
- They describe ecstatic flight as an early and widespread religious technology using drums, horns, dance, trance, sensory restriction, and sometimes psychoactive plants or fungi to redirect awareness into a visionary world whose information is then applied to healing or prediction.
- They link death/rebirth and stellar flight through the *axis mundi*, the world-pole represented by trees, ritual poles, the body's central axis, and the north–south orientation of monuments.
- They say early Chinese *wu* combined celestial contact, healing, divination, astronomy, astrology, and political authority; the earliest leaders may have been shamans whose secular authority derived from celestial power.
- They date the persistence of this shaman-star-state association through the Shang and Zhou dynasties from circa 1560 BCE to about 200 BCE.
- They interpret the square-Earth/round-Sky plan and the yin-Earth/yang-Heaven trigrams as a practical cosmology of alignment, not merely abstract symbolism.
- They argue that Hongshan and Niuheliang evidence carries this cosmology back to at least about 3500 BCE, if not earlier, and shows continuity across Chinese ethnic and linguistic differences.
- They treat Doumu, Shangdi, the Big Dipper's seven named stars, and Chinese royal burial alignment as parallels to Egyptian northward orientation, priestly transformation, and immortal ascent.
- They describe the *Pace of the Stars* as a living continuation of state funerary transformation: the Chinese practitioner begins the crossing before death through the older shamanic *Pace of Yü*.
- They argue that the Great Yü and Yellow Emperor traditions join Orion, the Dipper, bodily sacrifice, a reptilian sea monster, weapons, war, flood, and cosmic ordering into a Chinese version of a widespread creation-conflict pattern.
- They note without explaining it that Chinese astrologers add two invisible stars, Fu and Bi, to the seven visible Dipper stars; they suggest a possible analogy to Egypt's nine-member Ennead but do not establish a historical connection.
- They identify ecstatic flight as the common denominator among Daoist, Egyptian, Jewish *merkava*/*hekhalot*, and possibly other initiatory systems, while explicitly saying it is too early and too controversial to declare all of them shamanic in origin.
- They place the rise of individual ascent systems in a broad chronology: Ptolemaic and Han periods, first-century BCE/CE Gnosticism, Mithraism, and alchemy, organized Daoism around the second century CE, seventh-century Shangqing, and developing Indian Tantra.
- They say Indian identification of seven bodily *cakras* with the Dipper's seven stars further internalizes the celestial route.
- They interpret the Daoist identity of Underworld and Celestial Palace as a change of perspective: induced bodily denial and deathlike stillness prepare consciousness for movement through stellar gates toward immortality.
- They argue that *Pacing the Void* is a repeatable, taught system, not an unstructured fantasy: visualizations, stars, deities, directions, foot placements, garments, secrecy rules, and reversals are precisely specified.
- They read the Shangqing statement that pacing is "the truth of all movements of humanity" as assigning the adept a collective role, an advance guard for humanity's aspiration rather than a private seeker alone.
- They claim Shangqing sources describe seven invisible and intangible "dark" counterparts to the Dipper's stars, distinguish these from the unseen but tangible Fu and Bi, and situate them in a mirrored celestial network.
- They say comparison with contemporary dark matter or antimatter is tempting, but their treatment shifts between alternatives: the Chinese dark-star concept appears closer to nonluminous dark matter, while a universal system of opposites could suggest a theoretical anti-universe.
- They ask rather than settle whether ancient Chinese practitioners imagined dark stars, inherited the idea, or perceived something through initiation.
- They defend disciplined imagination as potentially epistemic: dreams, visions, and insights need not be accidental neurological noise if produced within a systematic mental practice.
- They argue sensory withdrawal reduces ordinary light, sound, and bodily signals, allowing consciousness to notice connections or data outside its normal narrow bandwidth.
- They reject a blanket fraud explanation for early shamanic authority, arguing that hunting, plants, seasons, healing, and communal survival made the shaman's claimed knowledge socially consequential before later exploitation emerged.
- They assert that humans repeatedly associate extraordinary events with space and nonhuman beings because a buried memory of stellar connection persists in consciousness.
- They characterize Daoists, Tantrikas, alchemists, witches, sorcerers, and other antinomian practitioners as beginning from an extraterrestrial premise and searching within the body–not-body boundary for a route to the stars.
- They propose that bodily controls—darkness, silence, fasting, breath regulation, restricted movement, sexual abstinence, solitude, timing, atmosphere, and orientation—imitate conditions of modern spaceflight; the body is the launch pad, not the vehicle.
- They ask where detailed Egyptian Otherworld itineraries came from if no living person had already made the journey, and offer shamanic voyage, bodily travel, or inherited refugee knowledge as possibilities rather than conclusions.
- They reframe the difference between a modern alien abductee and a Daoist star-walker as primarily one of context, suggesting that both may report travel structured by the cultural vocabulary available.
- They use a flight-simulator analogy: ritual may train practitioners by reproducing an earlier experiencer's journey rather than itself mechanically accomplishing literal immortality or space travel.
- They treat modern UFO religions as evidence that an experiencer can become a shaman or priest of "Alien Gods," and advance the book's thesis that religion is fundamentally UFO religion.
- They place Lao Ze in the fourth century BCE and the *I Jing* around 1100 BCE, probably developing from Shang oracle-bone divination; although not strictly Daoist, the latter supplies the yin-yang and change framework later associated with Daoism.
- They criticize the modern division in which measurable waking reality is treated as real while dreams, memory, emotion, imagination, mysticism, and religion are demoted as unreal.
- They argue that dependence on measuring machines alienates people from direct nature, human relations, and the possible capacities of consciousness.
- They describe the human nervous system as an unmatched machine and suggest consciousness studies might bypass limits of conventional engineered technology.
- They read the *wu* character and its older cross-potent form as encoding two human figures around a central pole or *axis mundi*, and regard related motifs on second-millennium-BCE bronzeware as evidence for the antiquity of the shaman–pole–heaven connection.
- They suggest oracle-bone writing, developed for divination, links writing's communication across time and space to the Phenomenon, as Egyptian hieroglyphs also retained an occult character.
- They interpret astronomical timing and alignment as a form of spiritual dead reckoning: Earth and Heaven must mesh accurately or the traveler becomes lost, just as a rocket can fail or an astronaut drift.
- They conclude that early religion, art, and writing record contact with beings or forces outside accepted human evolutionary context and that this Phenomenon jump-started civilization.
- They call the brain the contact point between humanity and the craft or agencies in the sky, and predict that consciously trained ascent technologies will be necessary for interstellar travel and cautious contact with the alleged gods of human genesis.

## Concepts
- **Underworld–paradise mirror** — The chapter's opening Chinese formulation says the deepest northern underworld reflects the highest heaven, making death below and immortality above two perspectives on one realm; the authors use it to illuminate Egyptian ascent.
- **Celestial flight** — Travel by a nonphysical or inward aspect of the person from Earth through staged celestial gates, associated across the chapter with death, rebirth, healing, power, knowledge, and immortality.
- **Ecstatic flight** — Shamanic travel induced through trance, rhythm, dance, sensory manipulation, fasting, or psychoactive substances; its visionary information is subsequently used in ordinary life.
- **Prima materia and psycho-biological alchemy** — A return to original matter followed by accelerated transformation; its blood, semen, breath, germination, and reproduction imagery binds bodily and spiritual creation together.
- **Apotheosis** — Humanity's elevation to divine or kingly status; the authors treat it as both the goal of ascent systems and the revolutionary replacement of an old ruler by a transformed human.
- **Diffusionism** — The position that ancient peoples traveled and exchanged symbols, technologies, religious narratives, and perhaps genes across regions before Columbus; the authors contrast it with independent invention and present several disputed cases.
- **Independent inventionism** — The conventional view, as the chapter describes it, that similar forms such as pyramids arose separately in distant cultures without cross-fertilization.
- **Ur-culture** — A hypothesized common ancestral civilization from which otherwise separated cultural forms may descend; Oppenheimer and Schoch place a candidate in flooded Sundaland.
- **Sundaland** — The now largely submerged land spanning continental Southeast Asia and the Indonesian archipelago; Oppenheimer identifies it as the source of a flood-displaced civilization whose people, genes, and myths dispersed west and east.
- **Myth as history** — The authors' method of treating sacred narrative not as a choice between literal chronicle and fantasy but as culturally interpreted memory of concrete events with spiritual meaning.
- **Axis mundi** — The world-axis joining underworld, Earth, and sky, represented by the pole star, mountains, trees, ritual poles, a shaman's climb, a monument's north–south line, or the central axis of the body.
- **Wu** — The Chinese shaman: a healer, diviner, astronomer, astrologer, trance specialist, and mediator between Heaven and Earth whose celestial authority may have underwritten early kingship.
- **Square Earth / round Heaven** — Chinese cosmological topology pairing square, yin Earth with circular, yang Sky; ritual, state, and architecture seek their harmonious fit.
- **Pace of Yü** — A halting ritual step attributed to the Great Yü, who had sacrificed part of his body for supernatural power; later Daoist adepts use it to tread the Dipper's stars.
- **Pace of the Stars / Treading the Dipper** — A structured Daoist ascent in which the adept visualizes, circles, and steps on each Dipper star, meets associated deities, and advances through stellar gates toward the pole.
- **Nine Heavens** — The expanded Dipper of seven visible stars plus the invisible Fu and Bi, associated with the Mystic Woman who teaches the Yellow Emperor the star-treading technology used against Ch'ih-yu.
- **Pacing the Void** — Shangqing ritual variants that use a Dipper diagram or embroidered silk, directional circumambulation, exact footwork, secrecy, visualization, and divine salutations to enact ascent.
- **Dark stars** — Invisible and intangible mirrors of the visible Dipper stars, reportedly perceptible to an advanced adept; the authors distinguish them from invisible but tangible Fu and Bi and compare them cautiously with modern dark matter and antimatter models.
- **Sensory withdrawal** — Deliberate reduction of normal bodily and environmental input through darkness, silence, stillness, fasting, breath work, abstinence, and isolation so consciousness can fix on a more distant target.
- **Flight-simulator model of ritual** — The proposal that precise otherworld rites reproduce an earlier voyager's itinerary to instruct and train later practitioners, just as a simulator prepares a pilot without itself being the destination.
- **Doubleworld / Otherworld** — The realm paired with ordinary Earth and reached through controlled alteration of consciousness; early shamans bridge the two rather than treating sacred and secular reality as separate.
- **Paleocontact** — The authors' inferred early contact with nonhuman or celestial powers, used to explain why star-derived authority and deliberate Heaven–Earth connection appear in Neolithic ritual structures.
- **Sekret machine of consciousness** — The brain and nervous system as an interior contact technology whose trained operation may connect with the unexplained machines in the sky and eventually enable interstellar travel.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Egyptian–Chinese ascent comparison**: The Great Pyramid's two shafts are presented as a paired celestial map—north to the circumpolar Dipper, south to Orion and Sirius. Egyptian Pyramid Texts were sealed with the king and apparently available only to his soul after death; Chinese Daoism extends comparable ascent instructions to a living adept. The authors say both systems require a nonphysical traveler, gates, preparation, danger, and a goal of immortality.
- **Cross-cultural ecstatic-flight scholarship**: Historians of religion Mircea Eliade and Ioan Coulianu are cited for finding ecstatic flight among Siberian shamans, Jewish mystics, and ancient Greeks, supporting the chapter's claim that it crosses large differences in time and geography.
- **Chinese pyramid complexes around Xi'an**: The region is said to hold seventy to one hundred royal tombs, many pointed or truncated pyramids aligned astronomically like Egyptian royal tombs. The authors give a broad probable range from the first millennium BCE to the first millennium CE, while noting claims that some reach back to the era of Egypt's Fifth Dynasty.
- **James Gaussman and the "White Pyramid" (1945)**: During World War II, American aviator James Gaussman reportedly saw a shimmering, flat-topped white pyramid near Xi'an while dealing with engine trouble. After several passes, he described a crystal or jewel-like summit object; a TWA pilot later confirmed the story and published Gaussman's 1945 photograph. The structure was identified as the first-century-BCE Maoling Mausoleum of Wu Di (140–87 BCE), fifth king of the Western Han; its base exceeds Gizeh's Great Pyramid but it is shorter and truncated. The authors acknowledge that no crystal is now visible and do not claim it survived.
- **Chinese official reticence and tree covering**: The chapter offers several reasons the government may initially have denied the pyramids: incomplete official knowledge, concern that builders might not have been Han Chinese, a politically awkward rumor of Turkish origin, remoteness and restrictions after the Revolution. It also reports that at least the so-called White Pyramid was planted with trees to resemble an ordinary hill.
- **Seigbert Hummel (1908–2001)**: The German Tibetologist, Sinologist, Egyptologist, and Lutheran minister published numerous books and hundreds of peer-reviewed articles. He studied Asian languages and Egyptology at Munich, Rostock, and Tubingen while spending the war in Leipzig and Dresden; curated Leipzig's Ethnographic Museum Asian collection from 1947 to 1955; then served as pastor in Vogtland while publishing through the 1990s. His only English-translated book was *On Zhang-zhung*; Guido Vogliotti assembled Italian translations of his Egypt–Eurasia studies as *Tracce d'Egitto in Eurasia*. The authors treat his comparisons of scripts, iconography, narratives, and floor plans as a scholarly exception to anti-diffusionism.
- **Naxi and Egyptian scripts**: Hummel compared a minority script from the Himalayan foothills with Egyptian signs and found ideograms similar in both design and meaning though not pronunciation. The authors note that ideograms carry meaning without a fixed phonetic value, using Mandarin and Cantonese readings of the same Chinese character as illustration.
- **"The Osiris Myth in Tibet"**: Hummel's Italian-titled article *Il mito di Osiri in Tibet* argues that an Egyptian narrative, not only visual motifs, reached the Himalayas. The authors regard common ideas as more persuasive than architectural resemblance alone but still frame the matter as investigation and speculation.
- **Stephen Oppenheimer's immunology-to-myth research**: Working in Southeast Asia, Indonesia, and the South Pacific, medical doctor Oppenheimer reportedly found that clans sharing an island could differ in genetic susceptibility to tropical disease and that the difference tracked distinct creation or flood myths. By following genetic profiles toward Europe, he proposed flooded Sundaland as an ancestral civilization whose survivors carried culture to Africa, India, and the Middle East.
- **Sundaland flood comparison**: Oppenheimer's *Eden in the East* treats globally similar flood stories as memories of one massive inundation rather than unrelated local floods. The authors use the proposal to join genetic dispersal, sea migration, and mythic continuity, not as an uncontested conclusion.
- **Toby Wilkinson's Eastern Desert expeditions (book published 2003)**: Egyptologist Wilkinson surveyed petroglyphs east of the Nile and interpreted oared boat carvings, similar to later dynastic art, as religious Underworld imagery created by ancestors of Egyptian civilization. He rejects extraterrestrial explanations and assumes a locally developed ritual tradition. The authors counter that under Oppenheimer's thesis the boats might record actual refugees from a literally submerged civilization and need not be solely religious.
- **Robert M. Schoch's geological diffusion case (book published in the same year as Wilkinson's)**: Schoch, described as holding Yale doctorates in geology/geophysics, grants some merit to Robert Bauval, Graham Hancock, and Adrian Gilbert on pyramid astronomy while allowing measurement disputes. He argues that similarities among Mayan, Indian, Indonesian, and former-Indochinese architecture justify revisiting human history and proposes an outward Sundaland migration between 6000 and 4000 BCE, northwest toward Sumer, Mesopotamia, and Egypt and east perhaps to Peru, after a flood possibly triggered by a cosmic event.
- **Aztec watery-homeland tradition**: The authors recall that Aztecs said their culture came by boat from a land surrounded by water. Academics often place it north of Mexico, perhaps in a southwestern-US lake; Oppenheimer and Schoch let the authors consider a much older flooded homeland and interpret the account as history-bearing myth.
- **Hongshan and Niuheliang (discovered in the 1980s)**: Northern China/Inner Mongolia contains monuments aligned astronomically by about 3500 BCE or earlier. Niuheliang yielded many tombs, a pyramid, and a large underground "Goddess Temple" with enormous sculpture, including one figure three times average human height and life-sized heads. A commonly labeled goddess head has green-jade eyes, though the authors note no ancient writing proves that identification. Jade dragons and cloud forms are presented as a continuum into later Daoist and Ming art.
- **Niuheliang's north–south axis and Chinese cultural continuity**: The pyramid and tombs align north–south like Egyptian pyramids and mastabas. The authors argue that such ideas spread despite no single language linking northeastern Niuheliang, Xi'an, and the wider tomb landscape, demonstrating durable religious and political forms across diversity.
- **Doumu and Shangdi**: Doumu, "Mother of the Chariot," personifies the Big Dipper and is worshipped with Shangdi, the "Primordial Emperor" or, in the closing illustration caption, "Father of the Plough." The Dipper's seven stars have individual names and divine associations across China, India, and other Asian cultures.
- **Great Yü's conception and gait**: Yü's mother conceives him after seeing a shooting star in Orion. He later walks with a strange, halting step because he sacrificed part of his body for supernatural power. The story joins Orion and the Dipper as Egyptian stellar traditions also do.
- **Yellow Emperor versus Ch'ih-yu**: The Yellow Emperor uses the secret of Treading the Dipper, taught by the Mystic Woman of the Nine Heavens, to defeat Ch'ih-yu, a horned sea monster with eight fingers, eight toes, and metal weapons. Ch'ih-yu's name is linked to *ch'ung*, reptile; he is rain god and war god and lends his name to martial art *ch'ih-yu-hsi*. The authors compare the water-covered world, monster, victorious ordering deity, and star technology with Indian and Babylonian creation conflicts.
- **Han/Ptolemaic and later initiatory chronology**: State death-and-resurrection rites become more individualized around the first century BCE/CE through Mediterranean Gnosticism, Mithraism, and alchemy. Organized Daoism begins around the second century CE; seventh-century Shangqing develops elaborate ascent and alchemy while Tantra grows in India with comparable creation, immortality, transformation, and internal-flight themes.
- **A detailed Pacing-the-Void sequence**: One version has the adept sleep on a Dipper diagram and experience it as Tai Yi's chariot descending. Another requires counterclockwise circumambulation of nine stars, one-foot-at-a-time star treading with the Pace of Yü, reverse treading, clockwise circumambulation, then two-footed treading. A silk version keeps an embroidered star map locked from outsiders until ritual use, clothes the adept in the stars, approaches the Dipper from its dark side, and salutes each resident deity.
- **Shangqing dark-star system**: Seven visible Dipper stars have silent, invisible, intangible mirrors disclosed during ascent; these differ from tangible but unseen Fu and Bi. Some cosmologies give every planet and star a dark counterpart. The authors compare this with an anti-universe and with dark matter but do not maintain a technically consistent identity between all three.
- **Katherine Freese's late-2007 dark-star theory**: The University of Michigan professor is cited for proposing that the universe's earliest stars were fueled by dark matter. The text first says dark matter is roughly eighty-five percent of total matter, then later says it comprises about twenty-three percent of the universe; both figures and their different denominators are source claims preserved here rather than harmonized.
- **Matter/antimatter contrast**: The authors explain that a Big Bang should produce equal matter and antimatter, that little antimatter has been detected, and that contact between opposite particles of equal mass but reversed properties causes mutual annihilation and energy release. Dark matter, by contrast, emits no light and is not antimatter.
- **Dogon/Sirius comparison**: Dogon knowledge that Sirius has an unseen companion is described as controversial, with independent discovery, ancient transmission, and European astronomical contact all offered as competing explanations. The authors contrast this uncertainty with what they regard as the demonstrable antiquity of Chinese dark-star belief.
- **Bohr and Pauli as imagination cases**: Niels Bohr and Wolfgang Pauli are previewed for Book Three as scientists whose dreams or imagination aided discovery, supporting the broader question whether a disciplined inner image can correspond to reality.
- **Egyptian parts of the person**: The chapter recalls *ka*, *ba*, *akh*, *ren*, *sheut*, and *ib* as bodily, named, soulful, spiritual, or composite aspects that become especially consequential after death. It asks whether living priests must have generated the Pyramid Texts' route knowledge, but acknowledges the surviving texts do not show whether such living ascent was practiced.
- **Robert Temple's northwest-African proposal**: Temple is cited for locating the pyramid builders' origin among Morocco's stone-circle megalithic culture. The authors offer possible shamanism there as one route into Egypt, in parallel with Schoch/Oppenheimer's alternative Sundaland refugee route.
- **Alien abductee versus Daoist adept**: The chapter says the distance traveled may be less important than cultural context. Both report nonordinary transit; a modern abductee experiences involuntary alien contact, while the adept seeks and structures it through an inherited method.
- **Flight simulator and transmission chain**: An original experiencer reports with available vocabulary; later practitioners reproduce body chemistry, rhythm, trance, and itinerary. The rite may be a simulator that trains followers to recognize or repeat the journey.
- **Wu ideogram and bronzeware**: The current form shows two human figures around a central sign usually read as "work" (*gong*), but older forms may show an axis. The still older cross-potent resembles a Templar-style equal-armed cross and appears on Chinese bronzeware from the mid-second millennium BCE; the authors interpret it as magician/shaman imagery rather than proof of a Templar connection.
- **Oracle bones and the Dalai Lama's State Oracle**: Oracle-bone divination is called the more rational ancient Chinese method compared with chaotic shamanic trance and provides the earliest Chinese writing evidence. As a modern comparison, the Dalai Lama's State Oracle enters an intense possession trance and speaks a language said to be intelligible only to the Dalai Lama.
- **Greek language of mathematics**: The authors state that *mathematikos* means astrologer and *mathesis*, normally learning, can mean astrology, using the linguistic point to argue that early formal knowledge was knowledge of the stars.
- **FuXi and NuWa illustration**: A Xinjiang image shows humanity's creators with human heads and intertwined serpent bodies amid constellations. FuXi holds an implement compared to the Egyptian Opening-of-the-Mouth adze; NuWa holds apparent compasses, producing a resemblance to Masonic imagery; the Big Dipper appears above FuXi. The authors offer these as visual resonances, not a demonstrated institutional lineage.

## Key quotes
- "There is an essential unity between the underworld and paradise" — the opening formulation that makes below and above mirror perspectives.
- "travel to the stars is an essential prerequisite for longevity" — the authors' cross-cultural summary of celestial flight, health, and immortality.
- "we cannot remain as we are and survive" — the chapter's evolutionary and revolutionary imperative.
- "every time science and reason suppress religion, and every time religious fervor overwhelms science and reason, a demon gets his horns" — warning against either side of the modern split becoming absolute.
- "myths tell history" — the authors' compact statement of their interpretive method.
- "Tours of space were a commonplace of ancient China" — Edward H. Schafer's epigraph to the Pace-of-the-Stars section.
- "the source of immortality" — the polestar as the destination beyond the seven or nine Dipper gates.
- "Pacing the celestial network . . . is the essence of the flight into the heavens" — Shangqing liturgical language the authors read as unusually explicit.
- "Does a work of the imagination qualify as true? Can one imagine the real?" — the chapter's epistemological challenge.
- "the body was the launch pad" — the distinction between bodily preparation and the traveling consciousness.
- "The difference is one of context rather than distance" — the comparison between an abductee and a Daoist star-walker.
- "all religion is UFO religion" — the chapter's strongest statement of the series thesis.

## Thread
This chapter extends the previous chapter's Egyptian funerary ascent into a living Asian technology of consciousness. Its first movement broadens the archaeological and historical frame through Chinese pyramids, the diffusionism dispute, Hummel's Egypt–Eurasia comparisons, and the Sundaland proposals of Oppenheimer and Schoch; its second moves inward through Chinese shamanism, Niuheliang, the *Pace of Yü*, the Dipper, and Shangqing ritual; its third treats imagination, sensory withdrawal, and the nervous system as a possible contact technology. The argument deliberately oscillates between history and interior experience because the authors' method rejects their strict separation: migration can become flood myth, a stellar itinerary can become ritual, and an anomalous encounter can become religion. The chapter prepares the next assigned unit by moving from the Dipper as a celestial sequence to the Indian identification of stellar stations with bodily *cakras*, and it previews Book Two on Qian Xuesen and Book Three on scientific consciousness, genetics, and figures such as Bohr and Pauli.
