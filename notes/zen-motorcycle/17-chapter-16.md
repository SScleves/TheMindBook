# Chapter 16
## In one paragraph
Opening Part III, the narrator and Chris begin their multi-day climb up a pine-forested canyon toward the snow line, and the physical mountain becomes an explicit allegory for the spiritual one: Phaedrus's exploration of the meaning of Quality is announced as "a route through the mountains of the spirit." The narrator explains that this exploration had two phases — a happy, nonmetaphysical first phase during his teaching in the valley below, and a disastrous second, metaphysical phase that ended with electrodes and the loss of everything — and that his own Chautauqua is a reconstruction pieced together from thousands of fragments of Phaedrus's debris. The bulk of the chapter reconstructs the first phase: Phaedrus's classroom discoveries at Bozeman, beginning with the blocked student cured by "the upper left-hand brick," continuing through the thumb and coin exercises against imitation, and culminating in the quarter-long "demonstrator" experiment in which he withheld all grades to argue for abolishing the degree-and-grading system. The experiment produces striking class participation and a revealing vote split by grade level, but it also exposes a "huge and frightening vacuum" — students punished without being told what to do, one girl suffering a nervous breakdown — and Phaedrus, unable to fill the vacuum without authoritarian teaching, retreats to regular grading, near resignation, waiting for a missing "seed crystal" of thought. Interleaved trail scenes (the partridge, cutting aspen poles, Chris refusing work, the escape-route plan) keep the narrative layer moving.
## Core claims
- The narrator claims the allegory of a physical mountain for the spiritual one between each soul and its goal appears in Zen literature and the tales of every major religion; most people stand in sight of the spiritual mountains all their lives and never enter them, and those who do make it learn "there's no single or fixed number of routes. There are as many routes as there are individual souls."
- He claims the old Western religious routes over the mountain have been "undercut and almost washed out" over three centuries by scientific truth — not because Jesus or Moses spoke untruth, but because language rigidity has made their chosen routes lose everyday relevance ("Heaven above" fades when space-age consciousness asks where "above" is) — yet the mountain itself remains as long as consciousness exists.
- He states Phaedrus's exploration of Quality had two distinct phases: a happy, creative first phase with no rigid systematic definition, and a second phase of systematic, rigid statements and an enormous hierarchic structure, which he calls "a total disaster" — before the electrodes Phaedrus had lost money, property, children, and citizen's rights, keeping only the dream of Quality; after them he lost that too.
- The narrator warns that his Chautauqua is a reconstruction from thousands of amorphic fragments (many supplied by friends), bound to contain errors and inconsistencies: "If something is wrong there's a good chance that the error isn't in what he thought but in my reconstruction of it."
- Phaedrus concluded, per the narrator, that his blocked students weren't lazy — they were trying to repeat things already heard rather than looking and seeing freshly for themselves, and that "imitation was a real evil that had to be broken before real rhetoric teaching could begin"; little children lack it, so it likely comes from school itself.
- He claims schools teach imitation via grades: college merely sophisticates this — you imitate the teacher so as to convince the teacher you are not imitating — while originality "could get you anything—from A to F," so the whole grading system cautions against it.
- The demonstrator's argument holds that grades and degrees sustain a hypocrisy (students are there for the degree, not the education), and their elimination would let the grade-conditioned "mule" flunk himself out harmlessly, learn in the school of hard knocks, and possibly return as a knowledge-motivated "free man" needing no external pushing — the "Church attitude" being that civilization is best served by free men, not mules.
- Phaedrus, the narrator reports, refused to give the grade-withholding experiment scientific value, since in a classroom no cause can be held constant and the observer is himself one of the causes who "can never judge his effects without altering his effects."
- He claims the experiment's vote (54% opposed, 37% in favor, 9% neutral) told another story when broken down by grade: A students 2-to-1 in favor, B/C evenly split, D/F unanimously opposed — supporting his hunch that brighter students, interested in subject matter, least desire grades, while dull or lazy students most desire them because grades tell them if they're getting by.
- He claims a sinister function of grades is that they "cover up failure to teach"; removing them forces the class to ask what is really being learned and "exposes a huge and frightening vacuum."
- The experiment's collapse yields the claim that individual creativity and expression in the classroom is "really basically opposed to the whole idea of the University" — students are there precisely because they don't know what is good or bad — and that when really good original work appears it is "in spite of the instruction, not because of it."
- On the trail, the narrator claims that after enough trips into high country the YMCA-camp desire for adventure diminishes in favor of "the more substantial benefits of cutting down risks," and notes that Chris turning down work is "not a good sign in the mountains."
## Concepts
- **Quality** — the term whose meaning Phaedrus explored as "a route through the mountains of the spirit"; in this chapter it appears as a working concept in teaching rhetoric (first phase), not yet defined — the chapter announces the exploration rather than the definition.
- **Chautauqua** — the narrator's ongoing philosophical talk; here he states it is "clearly based on" the thousands of jotted-down fragments of Phaedrus's thought, and thus "probably a long way from what he thought."
- **Spiritual-mountain allegory** — the physical mountain standing for the spiritual one between each soul and its goal; valley-dwellers avoid it, some climb with guides on established routes, a few make their own routes and learn there are as many routes as individual souls.
- **Nonmetaphysical phase (first phase)** — Phaedrus's period of using Quality without rigid, systematic definition: "happy, fulfilling and creative," solid and pragmatic, lasting most of his teaching time at the school; the narrator says it deserves judgment on its own merits.
- **Metaphysical phase (second phase)** — the later period of systematic, rigid statements about what Quality is, supported by "an enormous hierarchic structure of thought"; Phaedrus felt he'd explained existence better than anyone before, but the narrator calls the phase "a total disaster."
- **Amorphic fragments** — the debris of Phaedrus's thought that the narrator jotted down "without regard to form, in the order in which they occurred," many supplied by friends; thousands exist, and the reconstruction from them may err.
- **Imitation** — the "real evil" blocking students: repeating in writing what has already been heard rather than seeing for oneself; an external compulsion absent in little children, seemingly instilled by school, where not imitating the teacher earns bad grades.
- **Direct seeing** — the cure for blockage: looking and seeing "freshly for herself, as she wrote, without primary regard for what had been said before"; narrowing to one brick "destroyed the blockage because it was so obvious she had to do some original and direct seeing."
- **Infinity of hypotheses** — the maxim the narrator attaches to Phaedrus's earlier dismissal from the University for having too much to say: "For every fact there is an infinity of hypotheses. The more you look the more you see."
- **Degree-and-grading system** — the institution Phaedrus's demonstrator argued to abolish; it conditions students to work for grades rather than knowledge, cautions against originality, and covers up failure to teach.
- **Demonstrator** — a piece of persuasive writing Phaedrus worked up day by day in front of and with the class's help, used instead of teaching principles of composition (all of which he doubted); this quarter's demonstrator argued for eliminating grades and degrees, while he simultaneously withheld all grades (entering them privately in a book).
- **Slave / mule mentality** — the conditioning "built into him by years of carrot-and-whip grading": "If you don't whip me, I won't work"; the grade-conditioned student is a "mule" supposedly trained to pull the cart of civilization.
- **Free man** — the knowledge-motivated person the mule can become: pushed from inside, needing no external discipline, likely to "shape up" slacking instructors with rude questions; motivation of this sort, "once it catches hold, is a ferocious force."
- **Church attitude** — the view (opposed to the common vocational "location" point of view) that civilization or "the system" is best served not by mules but by free men; abolishing grades exists not to punish mules but to provide an environment where a mule can turn into a free man.
- **School of hard knocks** — "another kind of education quite as valuable" that the self-flunked student gets: low-status shopwork whose boredom and thousands of hours of frustrating mechanical problems reawaken his stifled creative intelligence.
- **Grade motivation vs. knowledge motivation** — the demonstrator's central contrast: the student conditioned to work for a grade rather than for the knowledge the grade was supposed to represent, versus the returned student who "would need no external pushing to learn."
- **Goalless vacuum** — what grade-withholding created for many students: a "Kafkaesque situation" of punishment for failing to do something no one would name; "You cannot withhold grades and sit there and create a goalless vacuum" — a goal must fill it, and Phaedrus could not supply one without authoritarian, didactic teaching.
- **Seed crystal** — what Phaedrus, stopped and depressed at chapter's end, was waiting for: "that missing seed crystal of thought that would suddenly solidify everything."
## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The morning climb** — after a good night's sleep the narrator and Chris pack carefully and hike an hour up a pine-forested canyon (aspen and broad-leafed shrubs below, steep canyon walls, springy pine-needle duff, a canyon stream); sunlight through pines creates "a cathedral effect." The scene grounds the mountain allegory.
- **The partridge** — a whirr sounds and a bird disappears through the trees; the narrator tells Chris it's a partridge because "they rock back and forth like that when they fly" and stay close to the ground — while privately admitting "I'm not sure of this but it sounds right."
- **The girl with strong-lensed glasses (the Opera House brick)** — a serious, disciplined, hardworking but "extremely dull" student wants to write 500 words on the United States; can't. Narrowed to Bozeman: nothing. To the main street of Bozeman: tears, still nothing. Furious ("You're not looking!"), Phaedrus narrows it to the front of one building — the Opera House — "Start with the upper left-hand brick." She returns with a 5,000-word essay, having sat in the hamburger stand across the street: "by the third brick it all started to come and I couldn't stop." Phaedrus concludes she was blocked by trying to repeat what she'd already heard — the same blockage that paralyzed him on his own first day of teaching — and the one brick forced original, direct seeing.
- **The thumb and coin exercises** — Phaedrus has one class write all hour about the back of the thumb: funny looks, but everyone writes and no one complains of "nothing to say." In another class the subject is a coin ("Do you have to write about both sides?"), yielding a full hour's writing from every student, and the same in other classes. Confidence-building because the writing, though seemingly trivial, was "their own thing, not a mimicking"; coin-exercise classes were "always less balky and more interested." From this he concludes imitation is the evil to break.
- **The psychology professor next door** — an "extremely imaginative teacher" who responds to Phaedrus's grading critique: "Right. Eliminate the whole degree-and-grading system and then you'll get real education."
- **The bright student's term paper** — weeks later Phaedrus assigns the grade-abolition topic to a very bright student (entrance exams in the upper one percent) who can't think of a subject. Within two weeks she has a superb paper and delivers it to a hostile class with "old-time religious fervor": "I'm not saying this for him... It's for you." Her fervor prompts Phaedrus to adopt the topic as his next quarter's demonstrator.
- **The hypothetical mule student (inside the demonstrator)** — a typical grade-conditioned student in a gradeless school drifts, skips assignments without penalty, understands less and less, and flunks himself out "with no hard feelings" — "Good! This is what should have happened," no stigma, no burned bridges. He becomes a low-status mule (maybe a mechanic; "Actually his real status would go up"), and in six months to five years shop boredom reawakens his creative intelligence; blocked by lack of theory, he discovers respect for mechanical engineering and returns knowledge-motivated, branching into physics, mathematics, metallurgy, electrical engineering and beyond — "the real thing" instead of the imitation of education.
- **The grade-withholding quarter** — all quarter papers return with comments but no grades (grades secretly entered in a book). One candid student lays the system open: "Of course you can't eliminate the degree and grading system. After all, that's what we're here for." Sequence of phenomena: A students initially contemptuous but working from acquired self-discipline; B/high-C students sloppy; low-C/D students absent. Asked what he'll do, Phaedrus answers, "Outwait them." Weeks 3-4: nervous A students turn in superb work and fish for standing; B/C students improve; D/F students show up to see what's going on. After midquarter: A students relax into uncommon friendliness, B/C panic into painstaking work, D/F turn in satisfactory assignments. Final weeks: free-for-all discussion "like a successful party" — except D's and F's "frozen in their chairs, in a complete internal panic." Students later explain the relaxation: they met outside class and decided "the best way was just to figure you were going to fail and then go ahead and do what you could anyway."
- **The end-of-quarter evaluation vote** — students essay-evaluate the system without knowing their grades: 54% opposed, 37% in favor, 9% neutral. Broken down by recorded grade (grades in line with predictions): A students 2-to-1 in favor, B and C evenly divided, D and F unanimously opposed — supporting Phaedrus's hunch about who wants grades and why. He nonetheless denies the experiment scientific value: classroom causes are uncontrollable and the observer alters his own effects.
- **The vacuum and the breakdown** — grade-withholding exposes that grades "cover up failure to teach"; for many students it creates a Kafkaesque punishment-without-instructions, and "one girl suffered a nervous breakdown." Unable to supply a goal without authoritarian teaching, Phaedrus drops the idea the next quarter, returns to regular grading, and is ready to resign.
- **Reed College** — Phaedrus hears Reed College in Oregon withholds grades until graduation and visits during summer vacation, but is told the faculty is divided on its value and "no one was tremendously happy about the system." His mood turns depressed and lazy; he and his wife camp in the mountains, she asks why he is so silent, and he waits, stopped, for the seed crystal.
- **The aspen poles and Chris's refusal** — at a rest stop the narrator hands Chris the machete to cut two straight aspens for hiking sticks and tent poles; Chris starts to rise, settles back: "You cut them." The narrator cuts them himself (one swing each, bark severed with the back hook) but notes "Chris is turning down work. Not a good sign in the mountains."
- **Route planning and the escape road** — the snow above the ridge is three days hard, four easy (cross the forested ridge, follow another canyon to its end, angle back up); DeWeese will search if they're not back in nine days. DeWeese says straight south runs seventy-five miles of nothing but forests and snow without a road. The narrator has secretly arranged to be near a road at the end of day two in case things go badly — hidden from Chris to spare his "YMCA-camp sense of adventure" — because "You take one bad step in a million, sprain an ankle, and then you find out how far from civilization you really are."

## Thread
This chapter opens Part III and pivots the Chautauqua from the Part II survey of classical reason (the analytic knife, Hume, Kant, the Church of Reason) into the book's central subject: Phaedrus's exploration of Quality, framed here as a new route over the spiritual mountain now that the old religious routes are washed out. It performs three structural jobs: (1) it announces the two-phase shape of the whole Quality story — the pleasant nonmetaphysical phase begun here versus the disastrous metaphysical phase to come — and flags in advance the total cost (electrodes, everything lost); (2) it establishes the narrator's method and its limits, a reconstruction from amorphic fragments that may misrepresent Phaedrus; (3) it supplies the pedagogical origin of the Quality enquiry — blockage, imitation, direct seeing, and the grade-withholding experiment whose exposed vacuum ("what should they work toward?") is exactly the question Quality will answer. The narrative layer mirrors the argument: the actual climb toward the snow with Chris begins, complete with route choices, hidden escape plans, and the first warning sign of Chris turning down work. The chapter ends with Phaedrus stopped and waiting for a "seed crystal," setting up the moment when Quality crystallizes as the goal that fills the vacuum.
