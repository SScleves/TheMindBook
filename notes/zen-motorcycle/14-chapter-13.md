# 13

## In one paragraph
As the party rides down the canyon toward Livingston and on toward Bozeman, the narrator loses his appetite and feels a mounting dread: today they reach the school where "an enormous coalescence of things occurred" in Phaedrus's life, and he compares himself to an archeologist about to open a forgotten tomb. The chapter then recovers Phaedrus's time as a teacher at a Montana "teaching college" during an outbreak of ultra-right-wing state politics that gutted academic standards and funding. Out of a student's misconception — that police could be posted to stop the school from losing accreditation — Phaedrus wrote his carefully elaborated "Church of Reason" lecture, distinguishing the real University (a state of mind, the continuing body of reason itself) from the legal corporation that merely houses it. The narrator admires the analytic craftsmanship of this church-versus-location split, then confronts the one thing the lecture notes leave unexplained: Phaedrus's fanatic intensity. His answer is that Phaedrus's lack of faith in reason was precisely why he was so fanatically dedicated to it — you are never dedicated to something you have complete confidence in. The chapter closes back on the road, the narrator wondering how much Chris knows and remembers, and whether some tombs are better left shut.

## Core claims
- The narrator claims Phaedrus's apparent calm surroundings conceal a "haunted place," and frames his return to Bozeman as archeology: he has only fragments of memory and pieces of what people tell him, and wonders "if some tombs are better left shut."
- Pirsig, through the narrator, claims a "teaching college" is "a very clever way of running a college on the cheap while giving a false appearance of genuine education" — endless teaching with no time for research or contemplation dulls the mind, kills creativity, and turns the teacher into an automaton whom students lose respect for.
- Through Phaedrus's lecture, the book claims the real University is not a material object: "It is a state of mind," "that great heritage of rational thought that has been brought down to us through the centuries," "nothing less than the continuing body of reason itself." No legislature can dictate to it and no police can defend it.
- The lecture claims loss of accreditation carries no legal penalties, fines, or jail; classes go on as before. It would merely be official recognition of an existing condition — "similar to excommunication": the real University declares the place no longer "holy ground" and vanishes from it, leaving only bricks, books, and the material manifestation.
- Phaedrus claims confusion arises in people who fail to see the difference between the two universities and "think that control of the church buildings implies control of the church" — who see professors as corporate employees who should "abandon reason when told to and take orders with no backtalk."
- Phaedrus claims the primary goal of the Church of Reason "is always Socrates' old goal of truth, in its ever-changing forms, as it's revealed by the process of rationality," and that true churchmen, like a true minister facing trustees' funding threats, "must act as though they had never heard these threats."
- The narrator claims the standard psychological explanations of Phaedrus's intensity (stage fright, redemption for his earlier expulsion) are inadequate; there is no evidence Phaedrus regarded his expulsion as a failure, "just an enigma."
- The narrator's central claim: the apparent discrepancy between Phaedrus's lack of faith in scientific reason in the laboratory and his fanatic faith in the Church of Reason lecture "wasn't a discrepancy at all. His lack of faith in reason was why he was so fanatically dedicated to it."
- Generalized: "You are never dedicated to something you have complete confidence in… When people are fanatically dedicated to political or religious faiths or any other kinds of dogmas or goals, it's always because these dogmas or goals are in doubt."
- The narrator claims Phaedrus felt deep kinship with the failing students in his back rows: their contemptuous faces mirrored his own feelings toward the rational, intellectual process — "they were contemptuous because they didn't understand it. He was contemptuous because he did." His lecture told them to have faith in reason "because there isn't anything else" — a faith he didn't have himself.
- The narrator claims Phaedrus was a fifties anachronism — "A pre-Reformation Loyola," militantly defending an institution no one in Bozeman doubted — and that after the sixties, "a decade in which reason has been assailed and assaulted beyond the wildest beliefs of the fifties," the Chautauqua based on his discoveries can be better understood, perhaps even as "a solution for it all," though so much is lost there's no way of knowing.

## Concepts
- **Church of Reason** — Phaedrus's name for the school, and more broadly for the real University understood on the model of a church: an institution whose primary goal "is always Socrates' old goal of truth, in its ever-changing forms, as it's revealed by the process of rationality," with everything else subordinate. Its "true churchmen" must ignore threats from trustees and legislators, since their primary goal is never to serve the community ahead of everything else but "to serve, through reason, the goal of truth."
- **The real University (the first university)** — Not a material object and not a group of buildings defensible by police: it "has no specific location," owns no property, pays no salaries, receives no material dues. "The real University is a state of mind… that great heritage of rational thought that has been brought down to us through the centuries," regenerated by people who traditionally carry the title of professor (though even the title is not part of it); "nothing less than the continuing body of reason itself."
- **The second university (the legal corporation)** — A legal entity "unfortunately called by the same name": a nonprofit corporation, a branch of the state with a specific address, which owns property, pays salaries, receives money, and responds to legislative pressures. It "cannot teach, does not generate new knowledge or evaluate ideas" — it is "just a church building, the setting, the location at which conditions have been made favorable for the real church to exist."
- **Church-versus-location split** — The analytic cleavage Phaedrus made instead of the traditional splits (by fields/departments, or into students/faculty/administration). The narrator praises its "analytic craftsmanship": once the institution is split between "the church" and "the location," the dull, imponderable institution of the official bulletin "suddenly is seen with a degree of clarity that wasn't previously available," and puzzling but normal aspects of University life become explicable.
- **Teaching college** — Euphemism for the kind of school where Phaedrus taught: "you teach and you teach and you teach with no time for research, no time for contemplation, no time for participation in outside affairs," until the mind grows dull, creativity vanishes, and the teacher becomes an automaton — "a very clever way of running a college on the cheap while giving a false appearance of genuine education."
- **Accreditation (as excommunication)** — In Phaedrus's account, loss of accreditation shuts nothing down: no legal penalties, no fines, no jail sentences; classes continue and students get the same education. It is "simply an official recognition of a condition that already existed," like excommunication: the real University declares the place no longer "holy ground" and vanishes, leaving only the material manifestation.
- **Fanaticism born of doubt** — The narrator's explanatory principle for Phaedrus's intensity: "You are never dedicated to something you have complete confidence in. No one is fanatically shouting that the sun is going to rise tomorrow." Fanatic dedication to dogmas or goals always marks that they are in doubt; historically, Jesuit zeal "stems not from the strength of the Catholic Church but from its weakness in the face of the Reformation." Phaedrus's lack of faith in reason made him its fanatic teacher.
- **Desanctified church** — From the newspaper article opening the lecture: a former Catholic church sold and used as a bar, with a beer sign over the entrance. The priest's point: bricks, boards, and glass do not constitute a church; the building "had been desanctified," and those complaining about the beer sign were, "posing as piety," exhibiting the very materialism the church opposed. Phaedrus uses this to model the University.
- **Chautauqua** — The narrator's ongoing philosophical talk; here he says that "in this Chautauqua based on his discoveries" — after the sixties' assault on reason — we can understand a little better what Phaedrus was talking about, perhaps "a solution for it all."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Breakfast and the ride toward Bozeman** — John and Sylvia eat hot cakes and drink coffee, still in the mood of last night; the narrator can hardly get food down. They ride down the canyon toward Livingston (sagebrush "like sagebrush you see all the way from here into Mexico," warmer softer sunlight at lower altitude), then reach the intersection where the park road joins the main east-west highway, turn onto it, and head west over a low pass toward Bozeman. Shows the mounting tension of approaching Phaedrus's "haunted place" — and, in the last line, a sudden shift to looking forward to what's ahead.
- **The archeologist analogy** — The narrator recalls reading about an archeological excavation in the Near East and the archeologist's feelings opening tombs shut for thousands of years; he feels like that archeologist, with only fragments of memory and pieces of what people tell him, wondering "if some tombs are better left shut."
- **Phaedrus's classroom presence** — Many mornings the tension was so intense he would throw up everything before reaching his first classroom; he loathed appearing before classes — a complete violation of his lone, isolated way of life. His stage fright never showed as such but as "a terrific intensity about everything he did"; students told his wife it was "just like electricity in the air." All eyes followed him to the front of the room; conversation died to a hush for minutes before class began and eyes never strayed for the hour. He became a controversial figure; the majority of students "avoided his sections like the Black Death."
- **Montana's ultra-right-wing outbreak** — The book compares it to Dallas, Texas, just prior to President Kennedy's assassination: a nationally known professor from the University of Montana at Missoula was barred from speaking on campus lest it "stir up trouble"; professors were told all public statements must be cleared through the college public-relations office. The legislature had already prohibited refusing entry to any student over twenty-one with or without a high-school diploma, and then passed a law fining the college eight thousand dollars for every student who failed — "virtually an order to pass every student."
- **The governor's vendetta** — The newly elected governor tried to fire the college president (a personal enemy and a Democrat); the governor's campaign manager doubled as state coordinator for the John Birch Society, and he was the same governor who supplied the list of fifty subversives heard about a few days earlier in the trip. Funds to the college were cut, and the president passed an unusually large share of the cut to the English department — Phaedrus's department, vocal on academic freedom.
- **Phaedrus's accreditation campaign** — Having given up on internal remedies, Phaedrus exchanged letters with the Northwest Regional Accrediting Association about the violations of accreditation requirements and publicly called for an investigation of the entire school situation.
- **The police-and-accreditation exchange** — Students in one class bitterly asked if his efforts to stop accreditation meant he was trying to prevent them from getting an education; Phaedrus said no. A student partisan of the governor said angrily the legislature would prevent loss of accreditation; asked how, the student said "they would post police to prevent it." Phaedrus realized "the enormity of the student's misconception" — the trigger for writing, that night, the long and carefully elaborated Church of Reason lecture (in contrast to his usual sketchy notes).
- **The church-turned-bar newspaper story** — The lecture opened with an article about a country church building sold and used as a bar, an electric beer sign over the front entrance; complainants went to church officials, and the irritated priest replied that the criticism revealed "an incredible ignorance of what a church really was" — the building was desanctified, not holy ground, and the complaint was materialism posing as piety. (The narrator guesses classroom laughter, the college being well-known for drunken partying.) Phaedrus said the same confusion existed about the University.
- **The minister-and-trustees analogy** — Citizens build a church for the community; the minister goes along with community goals but knows his primary goal "is always to serve God." When trustees oppose his sermons and threaten to cut funds, "a true minister… must act as though he'd never heard the threats." Phaedrus maps this onto professors facing trustees and legislators who threaten funding — with Socrates named as the case where the conflict between truth and "improving the citizenry" arose.
- **Why Phaedrus escaped censure** — Regarded as a troublemaker but never censured in proportion to the trouble he made: partly his unwillingness to give any support to the enemies of the college, partly a begrudging understanding that his troublemaking was motivated by "the mandate to speak the rational truth," which those around him were never free from themselves.
- **The Jesuit/Loyola case in point** — Jesuit militancy historically stems from the Catholic Church's weakness in the face of the Reformation, not its strength — the narrator's historical evidence that fanaticism marks doubt. The narrator stresses this "must always be remembered" as "the nineteen-fifties, not the nineteen-seventies": there were only "rumblings from the beatniks and early hippies at this time about 'the system' and the square intellectualism that supported it," but "hardly anyone guessed how deeply the whole edifice would be brought into doubt." Phaedrus in fifties Bozeman is "A pre-Reformation Loyola. A militant reassuring everyone the sun would rise tomorrow, when no one was worried."
- **The failing back-row students** — Phaedrus felt deep kinship with them: their contempt for the rational, intellectual process mirrored his, except "they were contemptuous because they didn't understand it. He was contemptuous because he did." They could only fail and remember it with bitterness; he "felt fanatically obliged to do something about it," telling them to have faith in reason "because there isn't anything else" — a faith he lacked.
- **Chris behind him** — Chris suddenly comes to the narrator's mind, and he wonders "how much he knows, how much he remembers" — planting the question of Chris's memory of Phaedrus.

## Thread
This chapter opens the Bozeman approach that dominates the middle of Part II: the trip's geography now converges physically on Phaedrus's old life, and the narrator's tension (nausea at breakfast, the tomb imagery) makes the recovery of Phaedrus feel dangerous rather than academic. Argumentatively, it supplies the institutional backstory promised by earlier fragments — the "teaching college," the right-wing political siege (including the governor with the list of fifty subversives mentioned days earlier on the trip), and above all the Church of Reason lecture, which is Phaedrus's fullest public statement before his later crisis. Crucially, the chapter plants the paradox that drives everything that follows: Phaedrus's fanatical defense of reason grew from his laboratory-era loss of faith in it (the doubts recounted in the earlier hypotheses/scientific-method material), and the narrator says this "makes a lot of sense out of the things that followed." It also positions the whole Chautauqua as an attempt, after the sixties' assault on reason, to understand Phaedrus's discoveries as possibly "a solution for it all." The closing thought about Chris — how much he knows, how much he remembers — sets up the father-son memory thread as they enter Bozeman.
