# 5

## In one paragraph
Chapter 5 carries the party out of the prairie and into the High Plains: gas at Hague, an ugly road south, a first "Western town" at Herreid, lunch in Mobridge, the crossing of the Missouri, and reservation land beyond — then a miserable, exhausted camp near Lemmon that ends in Chris's tantrum and a night of half-sleep. The Chautauqua takes its next major step through the story of the beer-can shim: the narrator once offered to fix the slipping handlebars of John's expensive BMW with a strip cut from a beer can, and John, offended, refused — an impasse the narrator traces to a "dimensional" difference in how the two men see. The narrator names the distinction that will organize the whole book: he was seeing what the shim *meant* (underlying form), John was seeing what the shim *was* (immediate appearance) — two realities, one artistic and one scientific, that "don't match and they don't fit." The chapter's darker second half opens the family layer: Chris's recurring stomach pains have been diagnosed as "the beginning symptoms of mental illness," the narrator has stopped the psychiatrists because "they're not kin," and Goethe's poem of the father, the child, and the ghost surfaces unbidden. The chapter closes with a dream-vision in which the figure of Phaedrus appears in fog — and seems to be calling Chris.

## Core claims
- Pirsig (through the narrator) claims that the impasse over the beer-can shim was not about the shim but about two ways of looking: "I was seeing what the shim meant. He was seeing what the shim was" — underlying form versus immediate appearance, and he says it is important for the Chautauqua to make this difference clear.
- He claims John "isn't so interested in what things mean as in what they are," and that John approaches everything "immediately and intuitively, grooving on it," the way he approaches drumming — without rational premeditation.
- He claims that, judged by underlying form, the beer-can shim was objectively excellent: beer-can aluminum is "soft and sticky," doesn't oxidize further because of its thin oxide layer, and "any true German mechanic… would have concluded that this particular solution to this particular technical problem was perfect."
- He claims that judged by immediate appearance the same shim is "depressing" — "Who likes to think of a beautiful precision machine fixed with an old hunk of junk?" — which is why John was offended.
- He claims John "really does care about technology" but is rebuffed by it in his dimension; after botching things repeatedly without rational premeditation, he "puts a blanket curse on that whole nuts-and-bolts scene" and cannot believe there is anything for which grooving is not the way to go.
- He claims this dimensional difference "underlay much of the cultural changes of the sixties," produced the "generation gap" and the names "beat" and "hip," and is "here to stay" — a serious way of looking at things that "looks incompatible with reason and order and responsibility but actually is not." "Now we are down to the root of things."
- He claims "what we have here is a conflict of visions of reality": the world as immediately seen is reality, and the world revealed by scientific discovery is also reality — "two realities, one of immediate artistic appearance and one of underlying scientific explanation, and they don't match and they don't fit and they don't really have much of anything to do with one another."
- He claims John's anger the day his engine wouldn't start was because the failure "was an intrusion on his reality" that threatened his whole life style — the same sort of anger scientific people have had about abstract art.
- He claims that following little discrepancies long enough "sometimes open[s] up into huge revelations," and that in mechanical hang-ups you sit, stare, search randomly for new information, and "after a while the unseen factors start to emerge."
- On Chris, he claims "causes and effects don't seem to fit. Causes and effects are a result of thought. I would think mental illness comes before thought" — while admitting it doesn't make much sense even to him.
- He claims the psychiatrists "can't have real kindness toward him, they're not his kin," reflecting that "kind" was long ago something you were born into and is now often "just a faked-up attitude half the time, like teachers the first day of class."
- Of the ghost in Goethe's poem he says the story ends "in failure…death of the child. The ghost wins," then insists "here life is the end and ghosts have no meaning. I believe that" — while admitting "I'm not sure of much of anything these days. Maybe that's why I talk so much."

## Concepts
- **What things mean vs. what things are (underlying form vs. immediate appearance)** — The narrator's core distinction, arrived at through the shim episode: "I was going at it in terms of underlying form. He was going at it in terms of immediate appearance. I was seeing what the shim meant. He was seeing what the shim was." He says John looks, sees, talks, and thinks "from a completely different dimension" — a difference so huge it went unnoticed: "Some things you don't see because they're so huge."
- **Two realities / conflict of visions of reality** — "Two realities, one of immediate artistic appearance and one of underlying scientific explanation," each of which is reality regardless of the other, "and they don't match and they don't fit." People in John's dimension will have to do more than ignore the scientific one — "John will discover this if his points burn out."
- **The groovy dimension** — The narrator's name for John's mode: responding immediately and intuitively, without rational premeditation, "the way he thinks about drumming — which is to say he doesn't really think about it at all. He just does it. Is with it." From inside it, the narrator's mechanical talk is "square" — "all just parts and relationships and analyses and syntheses… it isn't really here."
- **Shim** — Defined in-scene for John: "a thin, flat strip of metal. You just slip it around the handlebar under the collar there and it will open up the collar to where you can tighten it again. You use shims like that to make adjustments in all kinds of machines." Beer-can aluminum, the narrator says, is the "best shim stock in the world."
- **Valve clatter ("nickels-and-dimes sound")** — The old engine's normal sound, "as if there were a lot of loose change flying around inside." Once you learn to expect it, "you automatically hear any difference. If you don't hear any, that's good" — trained listening as diagnosis.
- **Hang-up** — A mechanical-work impasse: "You just sit and stare and think, and search randomly for new information, and go away and come back again, and after a while the unseen factors start to emerge." The narrator applies the same method to the human impasse with John.
- **Generation gap / "beat" and "hip"** — Cultural results, the narrator says, of the dimensional difference between grooving and rational form — a difference "still in the process of reshaping our whole national outlook," not a fad.
- **Kin / kindness** — An old word that surfaces when the narrator explains why he stopped the psychiatrists: "they're not kin… same root… kindness, too… they can't have real kindness toward him." Long ago kinship was "something you were born into and couldn't help"; now "kind" is often a faked-up attitude. Consoling words, likewise, "are more for strangers, for hospitals, not kin."
- **"Punishing"** — Sylvia's child-psychology term for Chris's walking off into the dark; the narrator rejects the term ("a context I dislike. Let's just say he's being a complete bastard").
- **The ghost** — Introduced here through Goethe's two-hundred-year-old poem: a father riding at night with his son, who insists he sees a ghost the father explains away as fog and rustling leaves; it ends in "death of the child. The ghost wins." The narrator disavows it for "here" — then meets a ghost-figure of his own in the dream.
- **Phaedrus** — The figure who appears in the fog of the narrator's dream: recognized but deliberately unacknowledged, "knowing that to recognize it by any gesture or action is to give it a reality which it must not have." Named directly: "Evil spirit. Insane. From a world without life or death." He seems to be calling Chris.
- **Western town / reservation land** — Two readings of landscape by underlying form: Herreid's broad streets, dust, and random layout mean "Nobody is concerned anymore about tidily conserving space. The land isn't valuable anymore. We are in a Western town." Across the Missouri, the unbroken sod's "natural tidiness" — "Not kept up, just never messed up in the first place" — means reservation land.
- **Chautauqua** — The ongoing talk-series frame, invoked explicitly: the meaning/being difference in John's way of seeing is "important for the Chautauqua that I make this difference clear."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Hague gas stop** — Oil change and chain lubrication; Chris watches "with some impatience. Not a good sign," and complains his eyes hurt from the wind; the narrator later buys him plastic goggles and a thermometer for the saddlebags. No one — attendant or hardware man — knows a Missouri crossing in the ninety-mile Bismarck–Mobridge stretch, so they route south to Mobridge; the far bank is "all Indian reservation."
- **The road to Herreid** — Choppy, narrow concrete, head wind, sun in the eyes, and semis on roller-coaster hills whose shock waves scared the narrator the first time; now he holds tight and braces: "No danger. Just a shock wave that hits you."
- **Herreid as a Western town** — Resting in a park, the narrator gradually decodes the broad streets, dust pallor, weedy empty lots, and spread-out sheds: land no longer valuable, therefore a Western town — a small demonstration of reading underlying form from appearance.
- **Mobridge and the Missouri crossing** — Hamburgers and malteds at an A & W, then the river "banked by grass hills that hardly get any water at all"; Chris shows no interest in it. They clunk across the bridge, watching the water through the girders.
- **Reservation land** — Beyond the river, fences gone, John's motorcycle "like an ant" on the green sweep; the narrator contrasts the land's natural tidiness with the "chewed-up, scruffy look" abandoned land would have. He also notes the risk: "There's no friendly motorcycle mechanic on the other side of those rocks… If anything goes wrong now we're in real trouble."
- **The echo test** — Coasting to listen to the idle, the narrator hears something "funny," and takes a while to figure out it's not the engine but an echo off the bluff; he has Chris listen — "No comment from him."
- **Listening to valve clatter** — The narrator once tried to interest John in the engine's normal "nickels-and-dimes" sound; "All he heard was noise and all he saw was the machine and me with greasy tools in my hands." The failure seeds the chapter's central analysis.
- **The beer-can shim (the chapter's central parable)** — John's handlebars slipped; the narrator warned against the adjustable wrench (chrome damage, rust spots) and got out metric sockets and box-ends, then found the collar ends pinched shut — tightening could never fix it. He proposed shimming with beer-can aluminum, gleefully holding up the can ("best shim stock in the world") to save John a trip, time, and money. John got "noticeably haughty," dodged with excuses, and the repair was abandoned; "As far as I know those handlebars are still loose," and they have had no conversations about motorcycle maintenance since. The narrator's technical verdict: the offense was proposing to fix "his new eighteen-hundred-dollar BMW, the pride of a half-century of German mechanical finesse, with a piece of old beer can!" — though by underlying form the solution was perfect.
- **The Krupp's-private-shim fantasy** — The narrator imagines cutting the shim secretly, removing the printing, and presenting it as the last one "specially imported from Germany… from the private stock of Baron Alfred Krupp, who had to sell it at a great sacrifice. Then he would have gone gaga over it." The fantasy gratifies, then wears off as "just being vindictive" — evidence that the difference is about appearance, not metal.
- **John the drummer** — John is a musician making "a pretty fair income" drumming with groups all over town; the beer can hit him "the way he would respond to someone dragging the beat" — "a big thud… He didn't want any part of it." The narrator uses this to explain John's whole intuitive mode.
- **John's engine that wouldn't start** — Recalled as the day John got upset: the failure was "an intrusion on his reality… It just blew a hole right through his whole groovy way of looking at things"; compared to scientific people's anger at abstract art.
- **The drunk woman at the grocery store** — At an isolated store on the desolate road, a badly drunk woman buying beer for a man in a car can't choose a brand, spots the riders, and returns "again and again, offering [John] a dollar for a ride"; John puts her off graciously; the narrator's jokes fall flat "and just add to the depression."
- **The Lemmon camp disaster** — The predicted second-or-third-day slump (the narrator warned Sylvia of it back in Minnesota) arrives on schedule: exhaustion, a windy High Plains semidesert campsite by a reservoir, gear dumped in a bad spot, Chris refusing to move it. The narrator's fatigue-fogged search — riding the cycle over to use its headlight to find the flashlight to find the machete, only to realize the machete is in plain sight and Chris has the flashlight — is a concrete portrait of exhausted, stupid non-seeing. Then: fire that won't cook the steaks in the wind, a rock windbreak built blind, crossed headlight beams lighting the scene, Chris's firecracker BANG, steak dumped on the tarp ("I'm not angry that he spilled it, I'm angry that now the tarp's going to be greasy the rest of the trip"), and Chris's escalating scene ("I don't like this camping at all" — Sylvia: "It was your idea") which the narrator deliberately refuses to engage: "You take his bait and he'll feed you another one… until you finally hit him, which is what he really wants." Chris walks off into the dark and the explosion is avoided.
- **Chris's stomach pains — the medical history** — Examined "a half-dozen times"; once so bad they suspected appendicitis, on a vacation up north taken right after the narrator finished "an engineering proposal for a five-million-dollar contract" — six hundred pages in one week, "about ready to kill three different people." Chris was screaming, untouchable; rushed to a hospital; "they found nothing." It recurred; this spring it was diagnosed as "the beginning symptoms of mental illness." Sylvia reveals she and John "wondered why you brought him instead of your wife."
- **Stopping the psychiatrists** — The narrator halted treatment with no rational reason he can name, "just a mental block of my own… like a door slammed shut," finally surfacing the word: "they're not kin" — and following the etymology to kindness.
- **The Goethe poem retold** — Prompted by "mein Kind — my child," the narrator recites and then paraphrases the poem ("Wer reitet so spät durch Nacht und Wind? Es ist der Vater mit seinem Kind"): the father explains the ghost away as fog and rustling leaves, rides harder and harder, and it ends in the child's death — "The ghost wins." Sylvia looks at him, startled.
- **The failed goodnight** — Chris, back late, chatters about a great big sandpile and asks, "What was it like when you were a kid?" The narrator snaps "Go to sleep, Chris!" — then lies awake hearing him cry: "A few words of consolation might have helped there. He was trying to be friendly… I don't know what he needs, or what's sought."
- **The dream of Phaedrus** — In mosquito-plagued semisleep under a gibbous moon, the narrator dreams of moonlight, a fog bank, riding a horse with Chris toward an ocean; in the fog a figure appears that vanishes when looked at directly. He refuses to acknowledge it — recognition would "give it a reality which it must not have" — but knows it: Phaedrus. "Evil spirit. Insane. From a world without life or death." Panic held down, hair crawling: "he is calling Chris, is that it?…Yes?…"

## Thread
This chapter converts Chapter 1's felt unease about John and Sylvia's hatred of technology into the book's first fully articulated analytic result: the meaning/being, underlying-form/immediate-appearance split, framed as "two realities" that don't fit — the narrator declares "now we are down to the root of things," setting the target the coming Chautauqua chapters must reconcile (a way of looking that "looks incompatible with reason and order and responsibility but actually is not"). Simultaneously it opens the book's second, personal thread: Chris's diagnosed incipient mental illness, the narrator's refusal of the psychiatrists ("not kin"), and the Goethe ghost-poem all converge in the closing dream where Phaedrus — named as an evil, insane spirit — appears to be calling Chris. The chapter thus binds the philosophical inquiry to the father-son stakes: the "ghost" language earlier applied to rationality now attaches to Phaedrus himself, and the question of who or what Phaedrus is becomes urgent for both layers. Narratively, it moves the party across the Missouri into the High Plains and reservation land, and delivers the predicted second/third-day slump in group spirits.

## Connections
