# Introduction to the Twenty-fifth Anniversary Edition

## In one paragraph
Writing twenty-five years after publication, Robert M. Pirsig sets aside the book's famous success — millions of copies in twenty-three languages, called in the press "the most widely read philosophy book, ever" — to confess and correct its two failures. The minor failure is an etymological mistake: Phaedrus does not mean "wolf" in Greek, as the book claims, but "brilliant" or "radiant." The major failure is that the intended ending was not made sufficiently clear: readers took it as a "Hollywood ending" in which the narrator triumphs over a villainous ghost, when in fact it is "an honorable Phaedrus who triumphs over the narrator that has been maligning him all the time." Drawing on a lesson Allen Tate taught him about Henry James's The Turn of the Screw, Pirsig explains how the first-person form traps the reader inside a narrator whose account cannot be trusted. He then re-reads his own book as a fight between two minds in one body — a socially conforming narrator and an intellectually driven Phaedrus — and notes that this edition marks Phaedrus's voice in sans-serif type to make the true ending clearer.

## Core claims
- Pirsig says that rather than recount a success everyone knows, "it would be of higher quality now to write about the book's failures, and maybe help correct them" — there are two, a minor one and a major one.
- The minor failure, Pirsig admits, is that Phaedrus does not mean "wolf" in Greek; the character Plato likened to a wolf was Lycias (whose name resembles the Greek lykos, "wolf"), while Phaedrus actually means "brilliant" or "radiant." He calls himself lucky: "It could just as easily have meant something much worse."
- The major failure, Pirsig argues, is that the intended ending was insufficiently clear and so obscured the fundamental meaning of the book: it is not the narrator who triumphs over a villainous Phaedrus, but an honorable Phaedrus who triumphs over a narrator who has been maligning him all along.
- Critics who called the ending a "Hollywood ending" that undermines the book's artistic integrity "are right," Pirsig concedes — but not because a Hollywood ending was intended; a much different ending was intended and missed.
- Following Allen Tate, Pirsig claims the first person is the most difficult narrative form because the writer is imprisoned inside the narrator's head — "But so is the reader!" — and that this imprisonment is the form's strength: the reader cannot see past what the narrator sees.
- Applying this to his own book, Pirsig says the only reason readers know the ghost Phaedrus is "evil" is that the narrator tells them so; in the dreams, each mind is in fact pursuing the other in order to destroy it.
- Pirsig describes the book's situation as "a divided personality... two minds fighting for the same body, a condition that inspired the original meaning of 'schizophrenia,'" the two minds holding different values about what is important in life.
- The narrator, in Pirsig's account, is dominated by social values: he never tells his story except in ways calculated to make the reader like him, shares private thoughts with the reader but not with John, Sylvia, Chris, or the DeWeeses, and stays within society's normal boundaries because he has seen what happened to Phaedrus — "No more shock treatment for him."
- The narrator's one confession, Pirsig says, is that he is a heretic congratulated by everyone for having saved his soul "but who knows secretly that all he has saved is his skin."
- Only two others know or sense this: Chris, going to pieces with confusion and grief looking for the father he loves and can't find; and Phaedrus, who "knows completely what the narrator is up to and despises him for it."
- In Phaedrus's view, Pirsig writes, the narrator is a sellout and a coward who has abandoned truth for popularity and acceptance by his psychiatrists, family, employers, and social acquaintances.
- Phaedrus, by contrast, was dominated by intellectual values: he didn't care who liked him, single-mindedly pursued a truth he felt was of staggering importance to the world, and the world, not understanding, "was trying to kill him for his trouble" — he was socially destroyed, silenced, but the residue of what he knew lingered in the narrator's brain, and that residue was the source of the conflict.
- At the end, Pirsig maintains, it is Chris's agony that releases Phaedrus: when Chris asks "Were you really insane" and the answer is "No," it is Phaedrus, not the narrator, who answers; Chris realizes he is talking to his long-lost father again, the dissembling narrator vanishes, and "They have won it."
- The real Phaedrus, Pirsig insists, "is not a villainous ghost but rather a mild-mannered hyperintellectual"; for more on him he recommends the sequel Lila ("properly understood by very few") and the website www.moq.org.

## Concepts
- **first-person-narrator** — Per Allen Tate as Pirsig recounts him: the most difficult form, because the writer is locked inside the narrator's head and can't get out — no "meanwhile, back at the ranch" transitions. Its strength is that the reader is imprisoned there too: "what the governess sees is all the reader ever sees," which lets a writer hide a villainous narrator in plain sight.
- **divided-personality / schizophrenia (original meaning)** — Pirsig's description of the book's core condition: "two minds fighting for the same body, a condition that inspired the original meaning of 'schizophrenia.'" The two minds hold different values as to what is important in life.
- **social-values** — What dominates the narrator: the drive not to be isolated from the reader or from surrounding society; telling his story only in ways calculated to be liked; maintaining a careful position within society's normal boundaries after seeing what happened to Phaedrus.
- **intellectual-values** — What dominated Phaedrus: indifference to being liked, and single-minded pursuit of a truth he felt was of staggering importance to the world, at the cost of being socially destroyed.
- **hollywood-ending** — The critics' label for the book's ending, which they said undermined its artistic integrity. Pirsig grants they are right that the ending fails, but says no Hollywood ending was intended — a much different ending was intended that was not sufficiently clear.
- **phaedrus-wolf-error** — The book's minor failure: the claim that Phaedrus means "wolf" in Greek. It arose from a real 1960 University of Chicago episode (see below); in fact Plato likened Lycias to the wolf (Greek lykos), and Phaedrus means "brilliant" or "radiant."
- **sans-serif-voice** — The typographic device introduced in this edition: Phaedrus's voice is set in sans-serif type so the intended ending — Phaedrus's triumph — is made clearer.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The book's success** — Rave reviews, millions of copies sold in twenty-three languages, and a press description as "the most widely read philosophy book, ever" (attributed in a footnote to The London Telegraph and BBC radio). Pirsig says he had those dreams while writing in the early seventies but suppressed them publicly for fear they would be read as megalomania and a regression to his former mental illness; now that they are reality, he no longer has to worry.
- **The University of Chicago "wolf" episode (1960)** — The origin of the naming error, an actual experience that appears in Part IV. A philosophy professor — whose real name Pirsig recalls as either Lamm or Lamb — mentioned that Plato liked names suggesting his characters' personalities and that in the dialogue Phaedrus a likeness was made to a wolf, looking at Pirsig in a way that indicated the title fit him: an outsider more interested in attacking what was taught than learning from it. Pirsig's "hyperactive mind" seized on this as his definitive relationship to the school and it worked its way into the book — but the wolf was Lycias, not Phaedrus. Readers have pointed out the mistake to him many times.
- **Allen Tate's seminar on The Turn of the Screw (early 1950s)** — A creative writing seminar on winter afternoons at the University of Minnesota, taught by Allen Tate, "a distinguished poet and literary critic." For many sessions the subject was Henry James's The Turn of the Screw, in which a governess tries to shield her two protégés from a ghostly presence, fails, and they are killed. Pirsig was convinced it was a straightforward ghost story; Tate said no — the governess is the villainess, and "It is not the ghost who kills the children but the governess's hysterical belief that a ghost exists." Pirsig reread it and saw Tate was right ("You can interpret it either way"). Tate's explanation — James achieved this through the first-person narrator — is the key Pirsig now hands the reader for interpreting his own book.
- **The dream pursuits** — During the story Phaedrus appears in the narrator's dreams in such a way that the reader begins to see the pursuit is mutual: the narrator hunts Phaedrus to destroy him, and Phaedrus hunts the narrator for the same purpose. "Who will win?"
- **The corrected reading of the ending** — When Chris asks "Were you really insane," the "No" is spoken by Phaedrus, not the narrator; Chris's "I knew it" marks his understanding that, for the first time on the whole trip, he is talking to his long-lost father again. The tension is gone, the dissembling narrator has vanished, and Phaedrus says, "It's going to get better now... You can sort of tell these things." Pirsig says it is Chris's agony that releases Phaedrus.
- **The sans-serif edition change** — In this twenty-fifth anniversary edition, Phaedrus's voice is set in sans-serif type to make the intended ending clearer.
- **Recommendations for the "real Phaedrus"** — Pirsig points readers to Lila, "a sequel that has been properly understood by very few," and to www.moq.org, "a group that is among those few that understand it."

## Thread
This Introduction, written twenty-five years after the fact, is Pirsig's own interpretive key to everything that follows: it instructs the reader, before the trip even begins, not to trust the narrator's account of Phaedrus. It reframes the entire book from a story of a sane man haunted by an insane former self into a contest between a socially conforming narrator and an honorable, intellectually driven Phaedrus — a contest Phaedrus wins. It plants the terms the narrative will embody (the divided personality, social vs. intellectual values, the ghost, the shock treatment, the University of Chicago episode of Part IV) and flags the mechanics — first-person imprisonment, the sans-serif voice — by which the reader should track which mind is speaking. It follows nothing (the dedication and epigraph aside) and sets up everything, especially the ending's exchange between Chris and his father.
