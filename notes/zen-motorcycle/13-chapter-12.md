# Chapter 12

## In one paragraph
Robert M. Pirsig has the narrator pause the Chautauqua's philosophical drive to fill in the story: lunch with a happy John and Sylvia at Cooke City, a ride through Yellowstone Park, and a night in Gardiner, all shadowed by anxiety about tomorrow's visit to the DeWeeses, who knew Phaedrus and will presume the narrator is the man he once was. The narrator explains his habitual remoteness — his mind runs on things like the a priori continuity of a motorcycle, which cannot be dropped into lunchtime talk — and declines to "develop the characters" of his friends, because they are friends, not characters. He then reconstructs Phaedrus's baffling, mutually puzzled friendship with DeWeese, an abstract impressionist who teaches fine arts at the college, through remembered fragments: the string "sculpture," the failing-students conversation, the light-switch repair. Finally he restores a missing ten-year span of Phaedrus's life: the study of Oriental philosophy at Benares Hindu University, the doctrines absorbed there as "latent images," the Hiroshima classroom exchange that made him quit India, and the comfortable middle-class decade in which he gave up the pursuit of the ghost of reason before something drew him to the Montana mountains.

## Core claims
- The narrator says he is a Chautauqua orator, not a novelist: John, Sylvia and Chris are friends, not characters, and much of what they know of one another is simply not relevant to the Chautauqua — "That's the way it should be with friends."
- The narrator claims his apparent remoteness is a problem of our time: the range of human knowledge is now so great that everyone is a specialist, the distance between specializations so great that whoever wanders freely among them "almost has to forego closeness with the people around him." The lunchtime here-and-now stuff is a specialty too.
- The narrator states that Chris reads his moods before he himself is aware of them — Chris's worried look reveals the narrator's anger; Chris's running and jumping reveals his good mood.
- The narrator admits he has been impersonating Phaedrus with people who knew him, and that glossing over the awkward moments has each time expanded his knowledge of Phaedrus — "which over the years has supplied the bulk of the information I've been presenting here."
- The book says that for Phaedrus, failure to understand something created tremendous interest — hence his high regard for DeWeese, whose responses seemed "all haywire" and who appeared to have access to "a huge terrain of hidden understanding."
- Pirsig relates Phaedrus's theory that every teacher tends to grade up students who resemble him the most — and Phaedrus's puzzlement that the students he most identified with were all failing; DeWeese's laughter at this was a "supertruth laugh": "The best students always are flunking. Every good teacher knows that."
- The narrator says DeWeese, unlike the technology-hating Sutherlands, was so far removed from technology he felt no menace from it — he was actually "a technology buff, a patron of the technologies."
- In the light-switch argument, the book presents DeWeese's challenge to obviousness — "You have to have some familiarity." / "Then it's not obvious, is it?" — as the strange perspective that made him unanswerable and that Phaedrus only thought he understood at the very end of his Bozeman stay.
- The narrator reports that Phaedrus despised Yellowstone Park: an enormous museum of manicured exhibits chained off from injury, whose atmosphere made people "polite and cozy and fakey to each other" — such a difference from the surrounding high country.
- Pirsig claims Phaedrus became aware in India that doctrinal differences among Hinduism, Buddhism and Taoism matter far less than those among Christianity, Islam and Judaism: holy wars are not fought over them "because verbalized statements about reality are never presumed to be reality itself."
- The book states the Oriental position that logic presumes a separation of subject from object, therefore logic is not final wisdom; the illusion of that separation is best removed by eliminating physical, mental and emotional activity, through disciplines such as dhyana.
- The narrator judges it "creditable" that Phaedrus never took up meditation: in his entire time in India "sense" meant logical consistency, and he could find no honest way to abandon that belief.
- Pirsig says that within Indian philosophy the professor's answer (that the Hiroshima and Nagasaki bombs were illusory) may have been correct, but for Phaedrus and anyone concerned with mass destruction it was hopelessly inadequate — so he left the classroom, left India, and gave up.
- The narrator insists it is "extremely important to understand" that Phaedrus gave up the pursuit of the ghost of reason — and that because he gave up, the surface of life became comfortable for him, marred only by an occasional glimpse of inner emptiness in his short stories.
- The narrator guesses (his wife seems not to know) that inner feelings of failure, and the hope of getting back on the track, are what started Phaedrus into the mountains — and that abandoning his inner goals had made him age more quickly.

## Concepts
- **Chautauqua** — the narrator's ongoing traveling talk; here explicitly contrasted with novel-writing: it excuses him from "developing the characters" of his friends and confines the telling to what is relevant to the inquiry.
- **A priori continuity of a motorcycle** — the narrator's example of what he is "always thinking about": the a priori presumption of the continuity of a motorcycle from second to second, and how we talk and think about it — unstatable at lunch "without benefit of the entire edifice of the Chautauqua."
- **Specialization** — the narrator's diagnosis of his remoteness as a problem of our time: knowledge is so vast that all are specialists, and one who wanders freely among specializations must nearly forego closeness with those around him; even lunchtime here-and-now talk is a specialty.
- **Impersonation of Phaedrus** — the narrator's practice, with people who knew Phaedrus, of pretending to be the person they remember; each successful gloss over awkward moments has rewarded him with more knowledge of Phaedrus, the bulk of what the book presents.
- **Supertruth laugh** — the narrator's later reading of DeWeese's laughter at Phaedrus's failing-students insight: a kind of laughter that "destroys tensions produced by impossible situations," acknowledging a truth every good teacher knows (the best students always are flunking).
- **Technology buff / patron of the technologies** — the narrator's description of DeWeese: so far removed from technology he felt it no menace, not understanding it but knowing what he liked and always enjoying learning more — like an art patron talking to a painter.
- **Tat tvam asi ("Thou art that")** — the Sanskrit doctrine, highly valued in all the Oriental religions per the book, asserting that everything you think you are and everything you think you perceive are undivided; to realize this lack of division fully is to become enlightened.
- **Subject–object separation and logic** — the Oriental doctrine as the book states it: logic presumes a separation of subject from object; therefore logic is not final wisdom; the illusion of separation is best removed by eliminating physical, mental and emotional activity.
- **Dhyana / Chan / Zen** — one of the most important disciplines for removing the illusion of subject–object separation: the Sanskrit dhyana, "mispronounced in Chinese as 'Chan' and again mispronounced in Japanese as 'Zen.'"
- **Ghost of reason** — the object of Phaedrus's abandoned pursuit ("the ghost of reason"); the narrator stresses that his giving it up is extremely important to understand, and that giving up made life's surface comfortable.
- **Latent image** — the narrator's figure for what India left in Phaedrus: no occult secrets, only exposures that "acquired a kind of latent image that appeared in conjunction with many other latent images later on."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Lunch at Cooke City** — John and Sylvia look and sound happier than in years after the high country; the group whacks into hot beef sandwiches while the narrator, happy but reserved, mostly eats and listens; huge pines and park-bound cars visible through the picture window, low clouds threatening rain.
- **Chris as mood-mirror** — the narrator sometimes discovers he is angry only from a worried look on Chris's face, or that he is in a good mood from Chris's running and jumping; at lunch Chris nervously fields John's question (about the DeWeeses) that was directed at his father — used to show Chris understands the remoteness better than the Sutherlands.
- **The WWI spy movie** — the narrator remembers a film in which a spy studies a captured, identical-looking German officer through a one-way mirror for months, then impersonates him before the officer's old friends; he says he has "some of the same feeling" about meeting DeWeese, who will presume he is the person he once knew.
- **DeWeese introduced** — asked about tomorrow's hosts, the narrator says DeWeese "teaches fine arts at the college there, an abstract impressionist"; asked how he came to know him he evasively answers that he doesn't remember, only that DeWeese and his wife were friends of Phaedrus's friends; the Sutherlands wonder what "an engineering writer like myself" would have in common with an abstract painter, and the narrator has no answer.
- **Contrasting photographs** — photographs of Phaedrus in the Bozeman period show alienation and aggression (a department member half-jokingly called it a "subversive" look); photographs of DeWeese from the same period show a passive, almost serene face with a mild questioning expression.
- **The string "sculpture"** — Phaedrus reglued loose edge veneer on a dining-room table by wrapping a whole ball of string around it; when DeWeese asked, Phaedrus joked, "That's my latest sculpture… Don't you think it kind of builds?" — and DeWeese, amazed and serious, studied it and asked "Where did you learn all this?" Shows the crossed signals: DeWeese takes jokes seriously and laughs at what is serious.
- **The failing-students conversation** — walking home under trees, Phaedrus, upset about failing students, theorizes that teachers grade up students who resemble them (neat penmanship, big words), yet the students he most identifies with are all failing; DeWeese breaks up laughing, leaving Phaedrus miffed; the narrator later reads it as a supertruth laugh Phaedrus could have used, "because at this time he was taking things way too seriously."
- **The light-switch repair** — a switch in DeWeese's studio fails; DeWeese believes the trouble is in the wire near the bulb (the light died immediately on toggling; a bad switch, he felt, would show a time lapse). Phaedrus silently buys a switch across the street, installs it in minutes, and it works. Asked how he knew, he says the light worked intermittently when he jiggled the switch; the argument escalates — "It's obvious." / "Well then, why didn't I see it?" / "You have to have some familiarity." / "Then it's not obvious, is it?" — the unanswerable perspective that convinced Phaedrus DeWeese was concealing something, which Phaedrus thought he finally saw only at the very end of his Bozeman stay.
- **Yellowstone entrance** — the group pays a man in a Smokey Bear hat for a one-day pass; an elderly tourist in shorts, street stockings and shoes films them while his identically-legged wife watches approvingly; waves are exchanged — "a moment that will be preserved on film for years."
- **Phaedrus and the park** — Phaedrus despised Yellowstone without knowing exactly why: the guided-tour attitude of the rangers angered him, the "Bronx Zoo attitudes" of tourists disgusted him more; it seemed an enormous museum of manicured, chained-off exhibits making people polite, cozy and fakey; living within a hundred miles of it, he visited only once or twice.
- **The missing ten years / Benares** — between Immanuel Kant and Bozeman, Phaedrus lived a long time in India studying Oriental philosophy at Benares Hindu University: he listened to philosophers, visited religious persons, absorbed and thought; his letters show enormous confusion of contradictions and exceptions; he entered and left India an empirical scientist, "not much wiser," but exposed — carrying latent images.
- **The Hiroshima classroom exchange** — with the professor of philosophy blithely expounding the illusory nature of the world "for what seemed the fiftieth time," Phaedrus coldly asked whether the atomic bombs dropped on Hiroshima and Nagasaki were illusory; the professor smiled and said yes; Phaedrus left the classroom, left India and gave up.
- **The comfortable decade** — back in his Midwest, Phaedrus took a practical degree in journalism, married, lived in Nevada and Mexico, did odd jobs, worked as a journalist, science writer and industrial-advertising writer, fathered two children, bought a farm, a riding horse and two cars, and started to put on middle-aged weight — the comfortable surface of a life that had given up, punctured only by glimpses of inner emptiness in his short stories.
- **Night in Gardiner** — exiting the park at Gardiner (dry country: grass and sage in twilight), the group checks into a motel across a bridge over a boulder-rushing river; the narrator notices the cabins' quality — double-hung sash-weighted windows, doors that click shut without looseness, perfectly mitered moldings, "nothing arty… just well done," evidently by one person — and points it out to Chris; the elderly builder confirms he made all the cabins himself and, pleased at being noticed, his wife invites everyone to sit; they talk unhurriedly about the town at the park's oldest entrance (used before automobiles), Sylvia puts her hand on John's arm, and the woman "who knows all fragrances" identifies honeysuckle on the night wind — a quiet scene of noticed quality answering Phaedrus's fakey park.

- "They're friends, not characters, and as Sylvia herself once said, 'I don't like being an object!'" — the narrator refusing to novelize his companions.
- "The range of human knowledge today is so great that we're all specialists and the distance between specializations has become so great that anyone who seeks to wander freely among them almost has to forego closeness with the people around him." — the diagnosis of his remoteness.
- "The reward each time has been an expansion of knowledge about Phaedrus that has greatly aided further impersonation, and which over the years has supplied the bulk of the information I've been presenting here." — the narrator on meeting people who knew Phaedrus.
- "For Phaedrus, failure to understand something created tremendous interest and DeWeese's attitudes were fascinating." — the ground of the friendship.
- "It was a kind of laughter that destroys tensions produced by impossible situations and Phaedrus could have used some of it because at this time he was taking things way too seriously." — the supertruth laugh.
- "'You have to have some familiarity.' / 'Then it's not obvious, is it?'" — the climax of the light-switch argument, DeWeese's unanswerable perspective.
- "It seemed an enormous museum with exhibits carefully manicured to give the illusion of reality, but nicely chained off so that children would not injure them." — Phaedrus on Yellowstone Park.

## Thread
This chapter is Part II's biographical hinge. It follows Chapter 11's account of Phaedrus's collision with Kant and the a priori (echoed in the narrator's lunchtime musing on the motorcycle's continuity), and closes the gap the narrative had skipped: the ten Indian years at Benares, where Phaedrus met the subject–object doctrine, Tat tvam asi, and dhyana/Zen as "latent images" that the book says will appear in conjunction with later ones — the Eastern half of the book's title finally entering the record, and entering as something Phaedrus honestly could not yet accept. It marks the great pause in the pursuit of the ghost of reason ("He had given up") that makes his later Bozeman resurgence intelligible, and it sets up the DeWeese visit — introducing DeWeese's unanswerable perspective on "obviousness" as a concealed understanding Phaedrus only glimpsed at the end of his stay, a thread the coming chapters will have to pull. On the trip level, the impersonation anxiety (the spy movie) sharpens the narrator/Phaedrus split, and the Gardiner cabin-builder — unpretentious, careful, "just well done" — quietly plants the book's concern with quality of work against the fakey museum-park.
