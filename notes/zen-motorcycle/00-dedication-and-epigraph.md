# Dedication

## In one paragraph
This front-matter unit consists of two brief items: a dedication and an epigraph. The dedication reads simply "For my family," offering the book to the author's family without further elaboration. The epigraph is a three-line address to a figure named Phaedrus, asking whether we need anyone to tell us what is good and what is not good. It plants, before any narrative begins, the question of the good — and the suggestion that the answer may not require an external authority. It also introduces the name Phaedrus, addressed here as the recipient of the question.

## Core claims
- The epigraph poses, as a rhetorical question, the claim that we do not need to ask anyone else to tell us what is good and what is not good — the question "Need we ask anyone to tell us these things?" implies the knowledge is already available to us.
- By dedicating the book "For my family," the author presents the work as offered to his family.

## Concepts
- **the good** — Not defined here; the epigraph raises "what is good, / And what is not good" as a question addressed to Phaedrus, and implies that recognizing it may need no outside teacher ("Need we ask anyone to tell us these things?"). This is the book's opening framing of the question of goodness.
- **dedication** — The book's offering line: "For my family."
- **epigraph** — The quoted opening question addressed to Phaedrus about the good, placed before the text proper.

None — this unit contains no narrative events, maintenance episodes, or concrete cases; it is a two-part front-matter page (a one-line dedication and a three-line epigraph).

## Key quotes
- "For my family" — the entire dedication.
- "And what is good, Phaedrus, / And what is not good— / Need we ask anyone to tell us these things?" — the complete epigraph, addressing Phaedrus with the book's founding question about the good.

## Thread
This unit precedes the entire book and frames it: before the road trip or any argument begins, the reader is handed the question of what is good and the hint that its answer is not something to be obtained from an authority. The name Phaedrus, addressed here, is the name the book will later attach to the narrator's pre-breakdown former self, so the epigraph quietly introduces the book's central hidden character. The question of the good anticipates the book's central inquiry into Quality — the sustained attempt (and refusal) to define what "good" means.
