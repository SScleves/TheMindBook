# Author's Note

## In one paragraph
In this brief closing note, Robert M. Pirsig states that the book is based on actual occurrences and must be regarded in essence as fact, even though much has been changed for rhetorical purposes. He explicitly disclaims any connection to the established body of factual information on orthodox Zen Buddhist practice. He also disclaims, with dry humor, the book's factual reliability on the subject of motorcycles. The note is a single short paragraph serving as a final framing device for the whole work.

## Core claims
- Robert M. Pirsig states that what the reader has just read is based on actual occurrences and "must be regarded in its essence as fact."
- He claims that much has nonetheless been changed for rhetorical purposes.
- He states that the book should in no way be associated with the factual body of information relating to orthodox Zen Buddhist practice.
- He adds, self-deprecatingly, that the book is "not very factual on motorcycles either."

## Concepts
- **factual basis** — Pirsig's claim that the narrative is grounded in actual occurrences and should be taken as fact "in its essence," despite alterations.
- **rhetorical alteration** — Pirsig's acknowledgment that much of the material has been changed for rhetorical purposes, distinguishing literal accuracy from essential truthfulness.
- **Zen Buddhist practice disclaimer** — Pirsig's explicit statement that the book's content should not be associated with or taken as factual information about orthodox Zen Buddhist practice, despite the book's title.

## Key quotes
- "WHAT FOLLOWS IS BASED ON ACTUAL OCCURRENCES." — the note's opening assertion of factual grounding (rendered in the source in full capitals).
