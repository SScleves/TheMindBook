# A Model of Reality and Time Incrementing Time in Simulations

## In one paragraph
Thomas Campbell opens a six-chapter arc (using the notations "delta-t" and "DELTA-t") by building, from ordinary computer-simulation practice, a model of how time can be generated as discrete increments rather than experienced as a continuous given. He walks through how an iterative dynamic simulation (like calculating an artillery round's trajectory) advances by fixed time-steps, how nested subroutines within a larger simulation (a war containing battles containing battalions containing artillery shells) can each run on their own increment size, and how the fastest-required subroutine sets the "fundamental quantum" (metronome) for everything nested inside it. He then names the crucial payoff: delta-t is the small increment that drives the outer, controlling loop of our reality within OS and relates to NPMR, while DELTA-t is the larger increment that accumulates our PMR "real-time," itself computed only once every so many delta-t ticks of The Big Computer. From there he pivots to a philosophical argument: today's science, lacking any concept of the "loom" (the nonphysical machinery producing space-time), is left believing space-time spontaneously weaves itself into being, whereas Campbell holds that consciousness is the fundamental substance and space-time/time itself is merely a derived measurement construct.

## Core claims
- Campbell argues that ordinary iterative dynamic simulations (e.g., calculating an artillery round's or thrown ball's position over time) progress not continuously but in discrete jumps of a chosen increment, "delta-t," and that making delta-t small enough lets simulation time approximate continuous motion.
- He distinguishes "simulation time" (the cumulative sum of delta-t increments, driving the computed dynamics) from "real-time" (what a wall clock in the computer room measures) — the two can diverge wildly in either direction depending on equation complexity, delta-t size, and computer speed.
- He argues that a simulation can be paused for any amount of real-time between increments and resumed without disturbing the simulation's internal results.
- Using a simulated-war example, Campbell claims that large simulations naturally nest smaller simulations (war → battles → battalions → artillery trajectories), each level an interdependent subroutine, and that interdependent loops must iterate sequentially while independent loops can run in parallel.
- He claims the practical size of delta-t is set by the required accuracy and by the fastest element in the model — illustrated by a war sim needing 0.00001-second ticks for precise munitions but only 0.1-second ticks for a man's movement.
- He argues that a low-fidelity subroutine (e.g., a man-model) can be incremented far less often than the master clock (once every 10,000 ticks) without loss of relevance, because sub-quantum changes (blinks, twitches) are inconsequential to the larger simulation.
- He generalizes: whichever subroutine requires the smallest time increment becomes the "metronome" — the fundamental quantum of time — for the entire nested system, and the master/outermost loop is defined as the one with the highest fundamental frequency (smallest time quantum).
- Campbell claims that from inside any given local reality (loop), one can only perceive events whose effects are significant over one or more of that loop's own time quanta — faster (smaller-quantum) loops are invisible and incomprehensible from within a slower loop.
- He explicitly maps this simulation model onto AUM, NPMR, and PMR: delta-t is defined as the increment of the outer, controlling loop of our immediate reality within OS, used to calculate probable realities and related to NPMR time; DELTA-t is the larger increment that accumulates PMR's apparent real-time, itself driven by a lower-level simulation ticking only once every so many delta-t ticks of The Big Computer.
- He states directly that this implies you, the reader, and the entire universe are simulated beings and objects, and asserts this framing will prove "illuminating, consistent, and useful."
- He reiterates (referring back to Chapter 31, Book 1) that time is quantized — progressing in discrete increments — and that our time is an artificial construct created by AUM to define the space-time part of itself.
- Campbell argues time is not a fundamental property of "the thread that is woven to produce the fabric of reality" but a measurement construct — more related to the weaving machine's action (the loom) than to the thread (space-time fabric) itself.
- He claims mainstream science implicitly assumes space-time spontaneously self-creates from nothing (a "self-woven, 4D fabric containing all past, present, and future events") because it cannot perceive the higher-dimensional "loom, mill, and energy" that (per Campbell) actually produce it — and that this leaves scientists dependent on an unacknowledged mystical premise.
- He claims theists have offered "God does it" as a stand-in answer for 10,000 years while this gap in understanding persists.
- He dates this impasse to "the last forty years since general relativity and quantum mechanics dropped the first shoe" by telling us "our physical reality was delusional," notes Einstein tried to lead us out of that wilderness with Unified Field Theory but "could not find his way through the impenetrable cultural belief fog," and concludes that "belief blindness is as absolute as any blindness."
- Campbell argues Einstein was correctly looking for the nonphysical footprints of a higher-dimensional structure in his Unified Field Theory (treating mass as higher-intensity field strength), but erred by looking for continuous fields within the space-time construct rather than grasping that reality is digital/quantized and that space-time is a local, virtual phenomenon derived from a more fundamental digital energy field: consciousness.
- He claims consciousness — not space-time — is the fundamental field, and space-time is derived from a conscious intent to constrain individuated subsets of consciousness to the specific experience-base called PMR.
- He argues that a deep understanding of the Big Picture requires no academic (scientific/mathematical) prerequisites, unlike little-picture physics, and encourages readers to trust their own experience and inner knowing rather than waiting for scientists to lead.
- Campbell closes with an image: sentient entities inside a simulation's inner subroutines ("local AI Guys") cannot perceive outside their own time-loop level and so are oblivious to (or deny) the bigger picture, living in a limited universe and paying homage to "Loop Gods" who occasionally feed them fresh input data.

## Concepts
- **delta-t** — the small time increment that, in Campbell's model, drives the outer/controlling loop of our immediate reality within OS; it is used to calculate probable realities, is referred to as "simulation time," and is related to time in NPMR.
- **DELTA-t** — the larger time increment, defined as the increment that accumulates our PMR time (our apparent real-time); from a larger perspective it drives a lower-level simulation with a larger time increment, itself incremented only once every so many ticks of the overall simulation clock within The Big Computer.
- **simulation time** — the cumulative sum over delta-t increments in a computational simulation; the internal "clock" of the simulation, as opposed to real-time.
- **real-time** — what is measured by an external clock (e.g., "the clock on the computer room wall") while a simulation runs; can be much longer or shorter than the simulation time it produces.
- **iterative dynamic simulation** — a computational process (Campbell's example: calculating the position of a fired artillery round or thrown ball) that starts from equations of motion and initial conditions and advances position by looping forward one delta-t at a time.
- **fundamental quantum of simulation-time** — the smallest time increment used by the fastest/most demanding element of a nested simulation; it sets the tick rate ("metronome") for the entire system, since the subroutine needing the smallest increment determines the master loop's frequency.
- **nested subroutines / levels** — Campbell's structural claim that a large simulation (e.g., a simulated war) contains smaller simulations within it (battles within the war, battalions within battles, artillery trajectories within battalions), each an interdependent, iterative subset of code.
- **master loop / simulation driver** — the outermost loop in a nested simulation, defined as the one requiring the smallest time increment (highest fundamental frequency/sampling rate); it is "the guy in charge."
- **local reality** — the perceptual/experiential frame of a given loop or subroutine within a nested simulation; within it, one can only perceive effects significant over one or more of that loop's own time quanta.
- **time quanta** — the discrete unit of time proper to a given loop or local reality (e.g., one-tenth second for the man-model); entities within a loop experience real-time as accumulating in these units.
- **AUM** — referenced (via Chapter 31, Book 1) as having a "fundamental frequency and time quantum"; the source/creator whose artificial construct of time defines the space-time part of itself. (Not redefined in full here; used as an established prior term.)
- **NPMR** (Non-Physical Matter Reality) — referenced as having its own, differently sized time quanta compared to PMR; delta-t is described as related to time in NPMR.
- **PMR** (Physical Matter Reality) — referenced as having its own time quanta, distinct from NPMR's; DELTA-t accumulates "our PMR time, our apparent real-time."
- **OS (Our System)** — the system within which delta-t is defined as driving the outer, controlling loop of "our immediate reality."
- **The Big Computer** — the overarching computational substrate within which DELTA-t drives a lower-level simulation, itself ticking only once every so many ticks of the overall simulation clock.
- **fractal-like time loops** — Campbell's characterization of the nested, self-similar structure of "loops within loops within loops" across scales, forming what he calls a "generalized dynamic consciousness-evolution fractal ecosystem."
- **quantized time** — the claim (attributed back to Chapter 31, Book 1) that time progresses via discrete increments rather than continuously, and that our time is an artificial construct created by AUM.
- **digital reality** — Campbell's framing of reality as fundamentally digital/discrete (quantized in space and time) rather than continuous, contrasted with the "continuous fields" Einstein sought.
- **loom-and-weaving analogy** — Campbell's metaphor in which time is not a property of "the thread" (the fabric of reality/space-time) but of "the machine that does the weaving" (the loom); each cycle of the loom is one time increment, producing the seemingly continuous fabric of PMR 3D space-time experience.
- **consciousness as fundamental** — Campbell's claim that consciousness is the fundamental substance/energy/organization underlying existence, and that space-time is derived from a conscious intent to constrain individuated subsets of consciousness to a specific experience-base (PMR), rather than space-time itself being fundamental.
- **Unified Field Theory** — Einstein's project, described by Campbell as correctly seeking the nonphysical footprints of a higher-dimensional structure (mass as higher field-strength intensity) but failing because Einstein looked for continuous fields within space-time instead of grasping the digital, consciousness-derived nature of reality.
- **belief blindness** — Campbell's term for the "impenetrable cultural belief fog" that kept Einstein from completing his Unified Field Theory and has kept science stuck for the forty years since general relativity and quantum mechanics "told us in no uncertain terms that our physical reality was delusional"; stated flatly as being "as absolute as any blindness."
- **Loop Gods** — Campbell's image for how sentient beings inside an inner subroutine/time-loop (unable to perceive beyond their own loop) regard the higher-level process that occasionally supplies them fresh input data and to whom they "offer up the results of their efforts."
- **local AI Guys** — Campbell's term for sentient entities existing within each subroutine/time-loop level of a nested simulation, who cannot perceive outside their own loop level and are consequently oblivious to (or deny) the bigger picture.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Artillery-round / thrown-ball trajectory simulation** — Campbell's baseline example of an iterative dynamic simulation: starting from equations of motion and t=0 initial conditions, the loop computes position at t=delta-t, then t=2·delta-t, t=3·delta-t, etc. He notes that with complex equations, small delta-t, and a slow computer, it could take "several hours of real-time" to simulate "only a few seconds" of trajectory (a few hundred feet); conversely, simple equations on a fast computer with larger delta-t could simulate a full minute-long trajectory in "a few tenths of a second" of real-time — illustrating that simulation time and real-time are independent of each other.
- **Simulated war with four nested levels** — a system simulation of an entire war contains a subroutine for a specific battle, which contains a subroutine for a specific armor battalion, which contains a subroutine for an individual artillery shell fired by that battalion: four nested, interdependent levels (1-overall war, 2-battles, 3-battalions, 4-artillery trajectories) iterating sequentially to produce the simulated war, with independent loops able to run in parallel.
- **Delta-t sizing example: spear-throwing man vs. modern munitions** — Campbell sets a hypothetical war simulation's fundamental delta-t at 0.00001 seconds (needed for centimeter-level munitions accuracy) and asks whether this modern simulator could still handle an ancient/slow war whose only fast element is "a man running through the bushes with a spear" (requiring no finer than 0.1-second resolution). His answer: yes — the coarse man-movement-model is simply incremented once every 10,000 (10^4) ticks of the fine fundamental clock, since modeling the man's motion at 0.00001-second resolution (blinks, burps, hair motion, twitches) would be "completely inconsequential to the war."
- **Multi-model war simulation with staggered increments** — Campbell assembles a full example: man-model subroutines incremented every 10^4 ticks (0.1 sec each); tank-model subroutines incremented every 10^3 ticks (0.01 sec each); aircraft-model subroutines incremented every 10^2 ticks (0.001 sec each); missile subroutines incremented every third tick; and a nuclear-damage-propagation subroutine evaluated every single tick. The subroutine needing the finest increment (the nuclear-damage routine, at every tick) sets the master/outermost loop's fundamental frequency for the whole simulation.
- **Sentient man-model thought experiment** — Campbell extends the man-model example by imagining that if the "low fidelity simulated men" inside the man-model loop became sentient and learned to program, they could, from their own local one-tenth-second time quantum, decide to simulate the (comparatively slow) growth of their own hair and toenails — illustrating how a local reality's sense of "real-time" and what's worth modeling is relative to its own loop.
- **Cross-reference to Chapter 31, Book 1** — Campbell explicitly invokes his earlier discussion of AUM's fundamental frequency/time quantum and the differing time quanta of NPMR versus PMR as the payoff the simulation analogy has been building toward, and again invokes that chapter's claim that time is quantized and an artificial AUM-created construct.
- **Einstein's Unified Field Theory** — cited as a historical case of a scientist correctly intuiting that mass might be "nothing more than higher intensity field strength" (nonphysical footprints of a deeper structure) but failing because he sought continuous fields within space-time rather than recognizing reality's digital/quantized, consciousness-derived nature; Campbell adds that even had Einstein reached this understanding in the 1950s, his peers would likely have dismissed him and ended his career "in ridicule."

## Thread
This chapter opens a six-chapter arc (explicitly flagged at the start) that will lean on the delta-t/DELTA-t notation "extensively," building on Chapter 31 of Book 1 (AUM's fundamental frequency/time quantum, and NPMR vs. PMR time quanta) which is referenced twice. It takes the computer-simulation groundwork laid earlier in the trilogy's engagement with digital/virtual-reality metaphors and formalizes it into a specific mechanism — nested loops with staggered increments, capped by a master metronome — that Campbell will use to explain how PMR's "real-time" is generated as a derivative, lower-fidelity layer running atop a finer NPMR/OS-level delta-t within The Big Computer. The chapter pivots from the technical model into the book's broader polemic against mainstream physics (via the loom/weaving metaphor and the critique of Einstein's Unified Field Theory) and toward its central metaphysical claim that consciousness, not space-time, is fundamental — setting up subsequent chapters to elaborate on quantized time, the DELTA-t loop, and how "living history" and choice-driven growth are implemented within this digital reality.
