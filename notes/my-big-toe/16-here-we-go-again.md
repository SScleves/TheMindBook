# Ch 16 — Here We Go Again!

## In one paragraph
Campbell closes the autobiographical arc of Section 1 by recounting his return, in the early 1970s, to NPMR — the nonphysical door that had been "slammed shut" twenty years earlier — this time with a scientist's mission to understand it all. He reports that by the end of the 1970s he was exploring independently of Bob (Monroe) and the lab, having learned that paranormal "powers" are mere artifacts of the path, not its destination, and that the real center of the bigger picture is the quality of one's consciousness and the evolution of one's being. He insists nothing magical or exotic produced his knowledge — only hard work, dedication, good science, and being in the right place at the right time — and he normalizes his story by noting that many children have unusual dream experiences and many people have nonphysical guides. He describes his double life: an unremarkable physicist in missile-defense R&D by day, a guide to a small group developing their consciousness quality outside the workplace. The chapter's purpose is to let the reader judge the source of the trilogy's information, while conceding that the real evidence and key to understanding lie only within the reader's own experience.

## Core claims
- Campbell claims that by the early 1970s the door to NPMR reopened for him after being shut for 20 years, and that he re-entered it "with a mission to understand it all — from a scientist's perspective this time."
- Campbell argues that paranormal phenomena or "powers" are "only an artifact of a path well taken, not the objective or destination"; becoming too enamored of them can distract you and "retard or prevent your further development."
- Campbell claims the bigger picture is centered on the quality of your consciousness and the evolution of your being — and that his experience could be scientifically explained.
- Campbell claims the rate of learning accelerates: "The more you know, the faster you learn." Up to 1980 he was "on the launch pad"; then things took off and the pace never slowed, and his interest and dedication have never waned, with "more data points" still pouring in today.
- Campbell argues his story is not exceptionally strange: many children aged five to seven have unusual dream experiences (he simply later remembered his and understood them in a larger context), and many people have nonphysical guides or advisors — in our culture often framed within a religious paradigm.
- Campbell claims his knowledge came from hard work, focused interest, dedication, and "good science" applied to an opportunity — explicitly not magic, a bump on the head, aliens, a near-death experience, or mystical secrets from a Tibetan monastery. His good fortune ("or good planning") was being in the right place at the right time with the right credentials, interests, and attitude.
- Campbell claims he "essentially worked my way to where I am today" and has no regrets, feeling "extremely lucky to have found the path" he is on.
- Campbell claims "strange" is a relative word: anything not mundane must necessarily seem strange to those who have not experienced it.
- Campbell reports living harmoniously in both worlds: working as a physicist in missile-defense R&D (where colleagues would find him not "the slightest bit strange") while, away from work, guiding a small group of students in raising the quality of their consciousness; focusing his real work in the nonphysical made keeping a low profile in PMR easy.
- Campbell says he was "strongly encouraged" to share the results and conclusions of his explorations — that is what My Big TOE is; knowing his history should help readers view the unusual concepts ahead with broader perspective.
- Campbell acknowledges the reader's "dilemma of analysis (is this guy nuts, or what?)" — he faced the same dilemma himself and fully understands the reader's position.
- Campbell claims there is no easy way to judge the quality of the trilogy's information without knowing him personally; nevertheless he hopes his account "helps a little," and that readers with their own experience "will find familiarity" in what he has to say; he wishes the reader well — "Good luck, I hope you make out as well as I did, or at least learn something useful" — before concluding that "the evidence, as well as the key to understanding, lies within your own experience — and nowhere else."

## Concepts
- **NPMR (nonphysical-matter reality)** — the nonphysical reality Campbell explores; here, the realm whose "door" was slammed shut for 20 years and then thrown wide open again in the early 1970s, which he re-entered with a scientist's mission to understand. (Acronym used, not expanded, in this chapter.)
- **PMR (physical-matter reality)** — the ordinary physical world; Campbell maintains "a low profile in PMR" because most of his real work is focused in the nonphysical. (Acronym used, not expanded, in this chapter.)
- **paranormal phenomena / "powers"** — paranormal events encountered along the path of consciousness development; Campbell defines them as an artifact of a path well taken, not the objective or destination, warning that fascination with them can retard or prevent further development.
- **quality of consciousness** — what the bigger picture centers on, alongside the evolution of one's being; also what Campbell helps his small group of students increase. Campbell treats raising it as the actual point of the work, as opposed to chasing phenomena.
- **evolution of your being** — paired with quality of consciousness as the true focus of the bigger picture; the personal-development goal that paranormal distractions can impede.
- **nonphysical guides (advisors)** — nonphysical beings many people have as guides or advisors; Campbell notes that in our culture this sometimes occurs within a religious paradigm, normalizing his own guided experience.
- **accelerating rate of learning** — Campbell's observation that learning compounds: "The more you know, the faster you learn"; his own pre-1980 period was preparation ("on the launch pad") before rapid takeoff.
- **strangeness as relative** — Campbell's point that "strange" is a relative word: anything not mundane must necessarily seem strange to those who have not experienced it.
- **personal experience as evidence** — Campbell's epistemic stance closing Section 1: the evidence for, and key to understanding, the trilogy's claims lies within the reader's own experience and nowhere else.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The reopened door to NPMR (early 1970s)** — the door slammed shut ~20 years earlier "was thrown wide open again"; Campbell returns with a mission to understand NPMR from a scientist's perspective. Shows the transition from childhood experience to deliberate scientific exploration.
- **Bob and the lab vs. independent work (late 1970s)** — by the end of the 70s, "Bob and the lab were doing their thing," and Campbell was working independently. Marks his separation from the lab research (Bob is referred to only by first name here) into his own program.
- **The launch-pad timeline** — up to 1980: getting ready, learning, training; then "things really took off," the pace never slowed, and "more data points continue to pour in today." Illustrates the accelerating-learning claim with his own trajectory.
- **Childhood dreams (age five to seven)** — Campbell normalizes his early experiences: many children that age have unusual dream experiences; his distinction was later remembering his and understanding them within a larger context.
- **Nonphysical guides in a religious paradigm** — many people have nonphysical guides or advisors; in our culture this sometimes occurs within a religious frame. Used to show his experience is a common human pattern, not unique.
- **The anti-origin-story list** — no magic, no bump on the head, no aliens from outer space, no near-death experience, and no "deep mystical secrets hidden in a golden urn buried under the rubble of an ancient monastery in Tibet" — only good science and dedicated effort applied to an opportunity. A rhetorical inventory that strips away exotic explanations for his knowledge.
- **The double life** — by day, a physicist in an engineering services company specializing in missile defense research and development, indistinguishable from any colleague; outside work, guide to a small group of students and others interested in increasing the quality of their consciousness. Demonstrates his claim of working harmoniously with whatever environment he is in and keeping a low profile in PMR.
- **The "dilemma of analysis"** — Campbell puts the reader's skeptical question in their mouth ("is this guy nuts, or what?") and says he once faced the same dilemma, positioning himself alongside the skeptical reader.

## Thread
This is the final chapter of Section 1 ("Delusion or Knowledge") and the coda of Campbell's autobiographical credibility case: it follows the story of his childhood experiences, his training and lab work with Bob (Monroe), and the years when the nonphysical "door" was shut, and brings the narrative up to the present — an outwardly ordinary missile-defense physicist whose real work is in the nonphysical. Its job is to answer the "is this guy nuts?" question head-on, to reframe his history as ordinary human experience plus good science and hard work, and to state the ground rules for what follows: paranormal phenomena are side effects, consciousness quality is the point, and the reader's own experience is the only real evidence. It hands off to Section 2 ("Mysticism Demystified," ch. 17–34), where Campbell begins building the conceptual content the trilogy was written to share.
