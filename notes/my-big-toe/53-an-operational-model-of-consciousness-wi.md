# Ch 53 — An Operational Model of Consciousness Will the Real AI Guy Please Stand Up!

## In one paragraph
Campbell argues that artificial intelligence — personified throughout as "AI Guy" — is not a far-out theoretical problem but a nearly-solved practical one, held back mainly by the cost and limitations of current hardware and software rather than by missing concepts. Using Moore's Law extrapolations, he projects that within decades cheap computers will have more than enough number-crunching power to let AI Guy evolve "an affordable, intelligent, self-aware consciousness." He then challenges the reader's reflex to label such consciousness "artificial," attributing that insistence to fear, ego, and belief protecting our sense of superiority. The real point of the chapter is an analogy of scale: if humans, with "outrageously limited and extremely primitive computers," stand on the threshold of creating silicon-based consciousness, imagine what individuated consciousness AUM could develop within TBC — reality-cell based digital mind-beings like us. The chapter closes with an extended satirical sales pitch delivered to the "ladies and gentlemen of NPMR_N," marketing the human body as a "Physical User Interface" (PUI) for playing the space-time virtual-reality "Game of Life" and racking up consciousness quality points.

## Core claims
- Campbell claims AI technology itself "is not the issue": we already know how to build AI Guys that learn from experience, modify (design, implement, and evolve) portions of their own rule-set, and even generate other AI things to help them do their job — the barriers are technical and economical, not theoretical.
- Campbell argues that the evolution of AI is primarily constrained by the lack of sufficient inexpensive computer power, and that simple versions of AI Guy's interactive learning attributes already exist in computer games, universities, and industry expert-system products.
- Campbell invokes Moore's Law (noting it is "actually only an observation and not a physical law") to compute that by 2020 computers will be about 10,322 times faster than in 2000, and by 2040 about 106.5 million times faster — roughly eight orders of magnitude every forty years — and adds that many believe Moore's Law understates the coming acceleration once post-silicon technologies arrive.
- Campbell predicts silicon technology will likely hit its limits before 2020, but that promising new technologies half a decade to a decade away will make Moore's-Law growth "seem slow and quaintly poky by comparison."
- Campbell asserts that it will not be long before a common desktop provides AI Guy what he needs to evolve an affordable, intelligent, self-aware consciousness — i.e., that "some computer simulation dude could have an evolving consciousness."
- Campbell claims the insistence on calling such consciousness "artificial" is driven by fear, ego, and belief — a way of maintaining our sense of superiority — and that calling AI Guy an "artificial being" is "the pot calling the kettle black."
- Campbell suggests AI Guy should be thought of as merely another type of sentient (interactive, capable of learning from experience) being with its own function, purpose, style, and personality, and notes it is "probably good for us" that AI Guy will remain physically dependent however mentally brilliant.
- Campbell speculates that the first job of super AI Guy will be to help design better, more intelligent AI systems, recursively.
- Campbell's central argument-by-analogy: if we are near creating silicon-based consciousness with primitive machines, AUM could develop far more complex individuated consciousness within TBC — "reality-cell based digital mind-beings like you, perhaps" — and AUM might just as fairly call our derived consciousness "artificial."
- Campbell claims AUM might produce several artificial consciousness models to interact within several larger simulations — and that PMR might be one of them.
- Through the sales-pitch satire, Campbell claims the human body is a simplified user interface (the PUI) that hides the "prodigious technical complexity" of the underlying consciousness technology; that a "few power users" find the PUI limits their access to the underlying consciousness's full potential; that reincarnation works like a lifetime service contract in which the "accumulated quality score file" is transferred from an old PUI to a new one; and that the two sexes and the choice between human and animal ("furrier") bodies are trade-offs in how fast one can gain or lose quality-of-consciousness points in the Game of Life.

## Concepts
- **AI Guy** — Campbell's personification of artificial intelligence: a sentient (interactive, capable of learning from experience) being with its own function, purpose, style, and personality, who can learn from experience, modify portions of his own rule-set, and create other AI things; currently held back only by lack of computer power. (Campbell notes he is "appropriately sensitive to female AI implementations.")
- **AI (artificial intelligence)** — technology, already partially realized in games, universities, and expert-system products, for producing entities that learn from experience and self-modify; Campbell says its realization problems are "more technical and economical than theoretical."
- **Artificial consciousness** — the label Campbell offers skeptics for an evolving machine consciousness, then undermines: since we make up the definition, the "artificial" qualifier serves fear, ego, and belief — our need to feel superior — rather than accurate description.
- **Evolving consciousness** — the self-aware, learning consciousness AI Guy could develop given enough cheap computing power; Campbell refers readers back to Chapter 27 (Book 1) and the first part of Chapter 7 (Book 2) for what constitutes it.
- **Moore's Law** — the observation (not a physical law, Campbell stresses), named after former Intel chief Gordon Moore, that computer power doubles every year and a half at no appreciable increase in production cost; Campbell uses it to extrapolate a factor of ~10,322 by 2020 and ~106.5 million by 2040 relative to 2000.
- **Rule-set** — the set of governing rules an AI (and by analogy, a reality) runs on; advanced AI Guys can design, implement, and evolve portions of their own.
- **AUM** — (Absolute Unbounded Manifold) the being whose implementation of an "AI-thing" could turn out to be similar to our consciousness; AUM might call our derived consciousness and intelligence "artificial" and might run several such models in several larger simulations.
- **TBC** — named here (not expanded in this chapter) as the substrate within which AUM develops complex individuated consciousness; the venue for "reality-cell based digital mind-beings like you."
- **Reality cell** — the digital unit out of which AUM's mind-beings are constructed ("reality-cell based digital mind-beings").
- **PMR (Physical Matter Reality)** — our reality; in this chapter Campbell frames it as possibly one of AUM's several larger simulations populated by artificial consciousness models.
- **NPMR_N (Nonphysical Matter Reality, subscript N)** — the nonphysical reality whose inhabitants are the audience of the sales pitch — the beings who "sign up" for a body and enter the space-time experience.
- **PUI (Physical User Interface)** — the human body, satirically defined as a simplified user interface hiding the complicated details of how consciousness technology actually works; it lets the common user make simple choices and experience the organic macro-level of the space-time rule-set, "crashes occasionally" (needs sleep), comes in two basic models (the sexes), and is replaced free under a lifetime service contract (reincarnation) with the quality score file carried over.
- **Space-time / space-time rule-set** — in the pitch, the celebrated "new technology" that makes the virtual miracle of carbon-based experience possible; the PUI puts "the power of space-time at your fingertips."
- **Carbon-based life / carbon-based technology** — the "simplified carbon based artificial intelligence" starter model offered to NPMR_N residents; the experiential game of carbon-based life.
- **Quality points / quality of consciousness score** — the score accumulated through the life-game-experience, measuring the evolution of one's consciousness; human PUIs guarantee plenty of challenging opportunities to score (and lose) quality points, while "furrier" PUIs make both harder.
- **Game of Life** — "the space-time physical experience virtual reality game": incarnate existence framed as a game whose object is to rack up quality points, i.e., maximize the evolution of consciousness.
- **Silicon-based consciousness** — the consciousness humans are on the threshold of developing with their primitive computers; contrasted with AUM's reality-cell based digital consciousness.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The AI Guy personification** — a running character standing in for artificial intelligence generally; Campbell says with the right programming AI Guy "can easily develop a personality that is much better than your boss's," and that his day is coming sooner than expected. Used to make AI concrete and to set up the artificial-vs-real consciousness challenge.
- **Present-day AI examples** — computer games kids play today employing simple interactive-learning attributes of AI Guy; more sophisticated implementations in universities and industries doing AI research or building AI and AI-expert-system products, kept in the lab by cost and hardware/software limits. Shows AI is already partially here.
- **Moore's Law extrapolation (the numbers)** — applying Moore's Law: by 2020, computers 2^(20/1.5) ≈ 10,322 times faster than in 2000, making a $2,000 desktop hundreds of times faster than today's multi-million-dollar supercomputers; by 2040, ~106.5 million times faster than 2000, an increase of eight orders of magnitude (10^8) every forty years. Campbell caveats that extrapolation "is not an exact science" but concludes cheap power for self-aware AI is coming. Also: silicon likely maxes out before 2020, with successor technologies making Moore's-Law growth look "quaintly poky."
- **Recursive AI design loop** — the thought experiment that the first task for super AI Guy will be helping us design better AI systems "that will help us design better more intelligent AI systems that will help us design...." — an open-ended bootstrap.
- **The Jake dialogue** — a skit with an incredulous reader ("Hey Jake, do you think this guy is nuts or has he been hanging out on the fringe too long?"); Campbell tells Jake to review Chapter 27 of Book 1 and the first part of Chapter 7 of Book 2, then probes whether relabeling it "artificial consciousness" only comforts our ego. Used to expose fear, ego, and belief behind the "artificial" distinction — "the pot calling the kettle black."
- **The scale-up analogy (the chapter's core model)** — if humans with "outrageously limited and extremely primitive computers" can approach silicon-based consciousness, then AUM within TBC could develop complex individuated consciousness: reality-cell based digital mind-beings — us. AUM's AI-thing could resemble our consciousness ("except much less arrogant no doubt"), AUM could fairly call it "artificial," and PMR might be one of several simulations running such models.
- **The PUI sales pitch (extended satire, ~half the chapter)** — a carnival-barker infomercial addressed to the "ladies and gentlemen of NPMR_N," selling the human body as a Physical User Interface for the space-time virtual-reality game. Moving parts: the body as simplified user interface hiding technical complexity (nobody wants "to get into the details of the intestinal tract and experience being eliminated? Yuck!"); "power users" (mystics, implicitly) who complain the PUI limits access to the underlying consciousness's full potential; crashes and obsessive loops fixed overnight by the "on-site support team" (sleep); space-time hyped on "Think-Net" and the cover of "Mind magazine" ("Finally ... space-time for the rest of us!"); a lifetime on-site service contract with free replacement PUIs and automatic download/upload of the accumulated quality score file (reincarnation and continuity of consciousness quality); two basic models of carbon-based PUI (the sexes) guaranteeing "challenging opportunities to score ... quality points"; and the "furrier PUIs" (animal bodies — cute, tail-wagging) with fewer features, less commitment, lower scoring ceiling but lower risk of losing points — a trade-off for those not going all out. What Campbell says it shows: incarnate human life is an entry-level consciousness-evolution package in a larger virtual-reality game.

## Thread
This chapter sits in Section 4, "Solving the Mystery," where Campbell is assembling his operational model of consciousness as digital and computable. Its job is to make the idea of evolved digital consciousness feel near-term and plausible: he grounds it in real AI research and Moore's-Law hardware projections, then flips the analogy — if we can nearly do it with primitive silicon, AUM has already done it with us inside TBC. It explicitly builds on Chapter 27 of Book 1 and the first part of Chapter 7 of Book 2 (both cited to the skeptical reader "Jake" as prerequisites on what constitutes evolving consciousness), and it dramatizes, via the PUI sales pitch, the trilogy's core picture: PMR as a virtual-reality learning game entered from NPMR_N to raise one's quality of consciousness, with the body as interface and reincarnation as continuity of the quality score. This sets up the following chapters' more formal treatment of how consciousness, space-time, and the larger reality system operate.
