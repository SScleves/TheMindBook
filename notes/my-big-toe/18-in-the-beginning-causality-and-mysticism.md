# Ch 18 — In the Beginning...Causality and Mysticism

## In one paragraph
Campbell opens Section 2 by attacking the problem of beginnings head-on: any account of a beginning invites the endless question "What was before that?" He argues that objective causality traps us in a dilemma — if everything must have a cause, a beginning is a logical impossibility; if not everything needs a cause, we leap straight to mystical beginnings — so causality must violate its own logic to get started. His resolution is that causality is system specific (local, not universal): the logic of a causal system cannot address that system's own beginning, which lies outside it, in the next higher level of causality. From within a system its beginning necessarily looks mystical, but the mysticism can be removed by gaining the perspective of the superset that contains the system. Campbell insists he is not discarding scientific causality — he lives and works by it — but is marking the boundary of its meaningful application, and he promises that by the end of the trilogy the veil of mysticism will be logically removed from our beginnings. He closes with a reflection on mystics and belief traps: someone with a much bigger picture always appears mystical to those with smaller ones, and "the quality of your being expresses the correctness of your understanding."

## Core claims
- Campbell argues that the question "What was before that?" arises from our sense of objective causality — the assumption that everything must be preceded by its cause.
- Campbell claims that if everything must have a cause, then a beginning is by definition a logical impossibility: "By the logic of causality, beginnings are illogical." Because we do exist, the logic of causality requires the initial existence from which we are derived to "erupt spontaneously from nothing" — objective causality must violate its own logic in order to get started.
- Campbell argues the alternative — no beginning, an infinite and perpetual existence — is itself a mystical assertion "that comes from nowhere and goes nowhere," offering no possibility of answers or clues; it is a trivial solution that leaves no foundation upon which to build a reality.
- Thus, Campbell claims, both invoking objective causality and denying it logically end in a mystical beginning — but this apparent inevitability depends on the reality in which the beginning takes place and the reality from which one views it.
- Campbell's central claim: causality is system specific. The logic of causality (e.g., the logic of PMR physics) holds only within a given causal system; it can say nothing about the beginnings of its own system, because those beginnings lie outside the system, beyond the reach of its own causal logic.
- Campbell claims beginnings belong to the next higher level of causality; a system's beginning only appears mystical from a point of view inside that system. Mysticism can be removed from our own beginning by obtaining the perspective of the superset to which we belong.
- Campbell explicitly does not toss out objective causality: it "has been, and remains, the philosophical foundation of our science," motivating the questions "How does this work?" and "What caused that?" He is a scientist and lives and works by it; he is only pointing out its logical limitations and the boundary of its meaningful application.
- Campbell uses "mystical" as a general word for the unknowable: given that the subject is the creation of our reality, "necessarily undefined" and "unknowable" quickly morph into "mystical" in many minds.
- Campbell claims that the erroneous belief in a universal causality (as opposed to a local causality) is used repetitively to make anyone who rationally tackles beginnings look ignorant — the iterated "What was before that?" forces a confession of ignorance at the foundation of the discourse, which is then used to dismiss the whole argument as foolish conjecture.
- Campbell claims our physical space-time causality is local and simply does not apply to "what was before that"; the two mystical alternatives (no beginning, or popping out of nothing) are neither scientifically nor logically productive and provide no rational foundation for a scientific Big Picture Theory Of Everything.
- Campbell announces that this section will describe a major paradigm shift offering another alternative — necessarily mystical from the PMR point of view, but not belief-based, that makes good sense and is scientifically and logically productive.
- Campbell claims that without realizing that the causal logic which gives us science also limits our understanding of the larger reality, our perspective is trapped in a conceptual prison — a belief trap — of our own making.
- Campbell claims that a person whose picture of reality is significantly bigger than his neighbor's will always appear to be a mystic to the more limited viewpoint, no matter how rational, complete, or scientific his understanding actually is.
- Campbell's practical test: if the apparent mystic is good, loving, wise, productive, successful, and capable, try to understand what he understands; if he is arrogant, condescending, manipulating, proselytizing, or asking for donations, keep your door locked and avoid him.
- Campbell claims "the quality of your being expresses the correctness of your understanding," and closes by asking the reader to think about what the quality of their own being says about the correctness of their understanding.

## Concepts
- **objective causality** — Campbell's term for the assumption that everything must be preceded by its cause; the philosophical foundation of our science. He argues it must violate its own logic to get started, since it requires the initial existence from which we are derived to erupt spontaneously from nothing.
- **local causality (system-specific causality)** — Campbell's claim that causality holds only within a given causal system: the logic of causality (e.g., PMR physics) applies inside its system but "can say nothing about the beginnings of its own system because those beginnings lie outside that system." Our physical space-time causality is local and does not apply to "what was before that."
- **universal causality** — the erroneous belief (per Campbell) that causality applies without bound to everything, including its own system's origins; used repetitively to make rational discussion of beginnings look like conjecture built on ignorance.
- **mystical / mysticism** — Campbell's general word for the unknowable: "the terms 'necessarily undefined' and 'unknowable' quickly morph into 'mystical' in the minds of many — thus I use the word 'mystical' to generally describe the unknowable." A system's beginning necessarily appears mystical from inside that system.
- **PMR** — Physical Matter Reality (acronym used here as the reality whose physics exemplifies a local causal logic); Campbell treats PMR as a subsystem contained within a super system, and the paradigm shift he announces is "necessarily mystical from the PMR point of view."
- **hierarchy of causal systems** — Campbell's model of nested causal systems, "each being a subset of the next"; beginnings belong to the next higher level of causality, so mysticism about our beginning can be removed by obtaining the perspective of the superset to which we belong.
- **beginnings** — the problem this chapter addresses: any beginning invites "What was before that?"; by causal logic a beginning is either a logical impossibility (if everything needs a cause) or a spontaneous eruption from nothing (if it doesn't), both of which read as mystical from within the system.
- **belief trap (conceptual prison)** — the state of a mind that fails to realize that the causal logic which gives us science also limits our understanding of the larger reality; its perspective and capacity to understand are "trapped in a conceptual prison (a belief trap) of our own making."
- **Big Picture Theory Of Everything** — the scientific, rational account of all reality that Campbell is building; he argues neither mystical alternative (no beginning / popping out of nothing) provides a rational foundation for one.
- **paradigm shift** — the major shift Campbell says this section describes: an alternative account of beginnings that is necessarily mystical from the PMR point of view yet not belief-based, makes good sense, and is scientifically and logically productive.
- **the mystic (relative bigness of picture)** — Campbell's definition by perspective: "A mystic appears to be animated by unknowable interactions that lie beyond rational understanding"; anyone with a significantly bigger picture than yours appears mystical to you, regardless of how rational or scientific their understanding is.
- **quality of being** — Campbell's criterion for evaluating understanding: "The quality of your being expresses the correctness of your understanding" — goodness, love, wisdom, productivity, and capability signal correct understanding; arrogance, manipulation, and proselytizing signal the opposite.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The causality dilemma (thought experiment).** Campbell poses the question "Must everything have a cause?" and works both branches: answering "no" leaps immediately to mystical beginnings; answering "yes" makes a beginning a logical impossibility, forcing the initial existence to erupt spontaneously from nothing. Shows that objective causality violates its own logic at its start, so both branches end in mysticism.
- **The iterated "What was before that?" interrogation.** Campbell describes how this question, asked repeatedly and iteratively, inevitably ends with "a confession of complete ignorance existing at the foundation of an otherwise rational discourse," which critics then use to dismiss any rational treatment of beginnings as foolish or unsubstantiated conjecture. Shows how belief in universal causality is weaponized against Big Picture theorizing.
- **The "no beginning" alternative.** Campbell examines the option that existence is infinite and perpetual and rejects it as "an unbounded mysticism" that "comes from nowhere and goes nowhere" — a trivial solution leaving no foundation upon which to build a reality. Shows that denying causality is just as mystical as affirming it.
- **The hierarchy of nested causal systems (model).** Moving parts: a stack of causal systems, "each being a subset of the next"; each system's causal logic holds only inside it; each system's beginning lies outside it, at the next higher level of causality; PMR is one such subsystem inside a super system. What it shows: a beginning only appears mystical from inside its system, and the mysticism dissolves from the superset's perspective — "raise science... to the next higher level of causality... and the ever-present mysticism will recede to the outer edges of your newly acquired knowledge."
- **The neighbor-as-mystic analogy.** If your picture of reality is significantly bigger than your neighbor's, your neighbor may see you as a mystic — you will always appear mystical to a greatly limited viewpoint, however rational, complete, or scientific your understanding is. Campbell adds a discernment test: if the apparent mystic is good, loving, wise, productive, successful, and capable, the neighbor should try to understand what he understands; if he appears arrogant, condescending, manipulating, or begins proselytizing and asking for donations, the neighbor "would do well to keep his door locked and avoid you."
- **The dog analogy.** "Perhaps your dog thinks that you are a powerful irrational mystic." Shows that mysticism is relative to the observer's capacity to understand — the same relation between you and your dog holds between a Big Picture understanding and a limited one.
- **Closing self-examination exercise.** Campbell ends by turning the quality-of-being criterion on the reader: "What does the quality of your being say about the correctness of your understanding?"

- "The quality of your being expresses the correctness of your understanding." — the chapter's closing criterion.

## Thread
This is the first working chapter of Section 2 ("Mysticism Demystified"), following the section's opening in Chapter 17. It sets the logical ground for the whole cosmogony to come: by establishing that causality is local rather than universal, Campbell licenses himself to describe our reality's beginning from the perspective of a higher-level causal system without either committing to belief-based mysticism or violating scientific logic. The chapter explicitly announces "a major paradigm shift described within this section" — the alternative account of beginnings that is mystical only from the PMR point of view — which the following chapters of Section 2 (the origins of consciousness and the larger reality) then deliver. It also seeds two recurring motifs: the belief trap as the enemy of understanding, and the quality of one's being as the measure of the correctness of one's understanding. The trilogy-long promise that "the veil of mysticism will be logically removed from our beginnings" frames everything that follows.
