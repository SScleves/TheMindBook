# A Model of Reality and Time: Using the Delta-t Loop to Project (Simulate) Everything Significant That Could Possibly Happen in OS

## In one paragraph
Thomas Campbell generalizes the delta-t loop model introduced in the previous chapter: instead of TBC (The Big Computer) computing and storing only the single most probable future state at each iteration m, Campbell proposes that TBC tracks and stores *every significant possible* future state, each tagged with an expectation value (probability of actualization). This produces a geometrically expanding "family tree" of alternate reality state vectors (illustrated in Figure 5-3), with parent states OSi spawning generations of child states OSi', OSi'', and so on, through M generations for every increment of DELTA-t (real PMR time). Only one state per generation is actualized by the collective free will of OS's sentient beings and becomes part of the historical thread; all the rest become either "unactualized future possibilities" (still reachable) or "unactualized past possibilities" (permanently foreclosed, but not deleted — TBC saves everything). Campbell uses this expanded model to explain parallel/possible realities, the mechanics of what-if analysis available to sufficiently aware beings, and his notion of "dimension" as TBC's way of hosting each of these alternate branches as a live, computationally active, modifiable reality.

## Core claims
- Thomas Campbell argues that AUM's Big Computer (TBC) does not merely calculate the single most probable future for OS at each simulation step; it calculates and stores *all* significant possible future states, each with an associated expectation (probability) value.
- He defines a "significant state" as any unique, viable, meaningful configuration of OS — even an unlikely one — generated by permuting and combining all free will choices, object changes, and energy state changes, then discarding redundant or insignificant results; only states above some small arbitrary probability threshold are kept.
- Campbell claims this generalized delta-t loop produces a geometrically expanding array of state vectors: each state generated at iteration m spawns its own full set of significant child states at iteration m+1, continuing through M generations per actual DELTA-t increment.
- He argues that at each actual DELTA-t, exactly one (and only one) of the multiple generation-1 OSi states is actualized by the collective free will of OS's sentient beings, while the vast remainder become unactualized — either still-possible futures or now-impossible pasts.
- Campbell asserts TBC "saves and stores everything" — every state vector, its genealogy, and its relative likelihood — and that unactualized past states are not deleted but merely "dormant," remaining as complete and capable of generating further branches as any actualized state.
- He claims dormant/unactualized states will not spontaneously change, but a time-traveling sentient being can alter one and thereby modify it and all its descendants; if the alteration matches an already-computed possibility no new calculation is needed, otherwise a new branch is generated.
- Campbell argues this architecture enables "what-if" analysis, which he says is used by sentient beings between PMR incarnations (and by their NPMRN guides) to understand the consequences of past choices and work through personal belief systems as part of planning future experiences.
- He claims the whole massive initial calculation (all significant future states through M generations) need be done in full only once, at "the beginning of time for OS," after which TBC need only compute the newest generation of children and make minor corrective adjustments.
- Campbell states that "dimension" is to TBC roughly what a line on a page, or a saved computer file (potentially containing sub-folders), is to us — each uniquely significant state vector gets spun off into its own computationally live dimension/folder within TBC.
- He asserts that although TBC's software is exceptionally clever (a product of evolution optimizing within AUM's consciousness), it need not be perfect; the model requires only finite, functionally adequate, statistically meaningful processes, never infinite or perfect ones — and if an actualized state occasionally doesn't match a previously generated OSi, it is simply added to the set.

## Concepts
- **AUM** — the encompassing consciousness (referred to by the author as "AUM's consciousness") within which TBC operates and whose "consciousness experiments" are optimized by this whole modeling process; described as "apparently infinite (but actually finite)."
- **TBC (The Big Computer)** — the computational substrate/process, evolved within AUM's consciousness, that runs the delta-t loop, generates and stores all significant state vectors, computes their expectation values, and "has a good memory," saving both actualized and unactualized states indefinitely.
- **NPMRN** — the non-physical matrix reality (referenced here as the domain in which "travelers" can access parallel possible realities and in which the generalized delta-t loop, called the "NPMRN delta-t loop," runs); also the reality from which sufficiently aware beings or their guides run what-if analysis.
- **PMR** — physical matter reality; DELTA-t is described as its "fundamental time quantum," and "actualized" states are actualized "from our perspective — the perspective of OS," i.e., from the PMR-centric view.
- **OS (Our System)** — the specific reality system (our universe) whose past, present, and possible futures are being modeled; described as "one of those local realities" within the larger multidimensional system.
- **DELTA-t** — the real/actual increment of PMR time; one "present moment" of OS is created per DELTA-t, and the whole M-generation calculation of possible futures is performed between successive actual DELTA-t increments while "real-time is standing still in OS."
- **delta-t (simulation time) / the delta-t loop** — the internal simulation process, iterated through M consecutive sub-steps (indexed by m) between each actual DELTA-t, that computes possible/probable future states; in this chapter it is "generalized" or "expanded" so that it tracks all significant possible states at each m, not merely the most probable one.
- **Iteration index m / M** — m is the step counter (m = 1, 2, 3, ... M) within a single delta-t loop run; M is "an arbitrarily large but finite integer" representing how many generations of possible states are projected into the simulated future per actual DELTA-t.
- **State vector** — a unique configuration of OS (represented by a circle in Figure 5-3) capturing a particular arrangement of sentient free will choices, objects, position, and energy; every circle in the figure is "a unique state vector of OS."
- **Significant state** — the author's defined term for a state vector that is a "unique, viable, meaningful configuration of OS," generated by permuting/combining all free will choices and object/energy changes and then stripping out redundant, insignificant, "unproductive, degenerate, duplicative, repetitive, meaningless, and useless" results; retained only if its actualization probability exceeds some small arbitrary threshold.
- **Expectation value** — the probability/likelihood measure assigned to each significant possible state vector, indicating its chance of being actualized; Campbell notes this value "does not automatically come integrated with the experience of the reality" — accessing it requires a separate, precise intent/question.
- **Probable reality surface** — (carried over from the previous chapter, per the author) the thread formed by tracing, at each generation m, only the single most-likely-to-be-actualized state; described here as a "dashed thread" through a much smaller subset of the full possibility database, representing OS's most probable future.
- **OSi subscript notation** — Campbell's notational device: OSi denotes the i-th alternate state vector at a given generation (i = 1, 2, 3, ...), covering as many unique arrangements as there are unique combinations of sentient choices and other variables; OSi' and OSi'' denote second- and third-generation children of a given branch.
- **Parent/child state vectors (family tree of states)** — the structure whereby each first-generation OSi state spawns its own complete set of second-generation child states OSi', which in turn spawn third-generation states, and so on through M generations, producing "geometrically expanding" branching described as a family tree.
- **Actualization** — the process by which, at each actual DELTA-t, exactly one state vector per branch is selected (via collective free will choices and changes in objects/energy) to become "our next present moment" and join the historical thread; "actualized" means "what actually happened or actually took place from our perspective."
- **Unactualized future possibilities** — descendant state vectors of the just-actualized state; still retain "a finite chance of being actualized at some time in the future."
- **Unactualized past possibilities** — descendants of all the non-actualized sibling states; per the author these "can now never be actualized by free will choices within OS," yet are not deleted, only rendered "dormant."
- **Dormant states** — the author's term clarifying that unactualized past states are not "dead," just inactive; they remain "as alive and vital as any" saved state vector and retain full capacity to generate new descendant states if their defining choice-set is altered (e.g., by a time-traveling sentient being).
- **What-if analysis** — a capability the author says this architecture enables: exploring the exhaustive database of alternate/dormant possibilities to assess the impact of choices, used (per the author) by sentient beings between PMR incarnations, and by their guides, as part of planning and learning from past experience.
- **Dimension (per TBC)** — Campbell's redefinition of "dimension" as TBC's basic unit of hosting a unique reality-system state vector, analogized to a line on a page, a line on a computer screen ("press enter to get a new one"), or best of all a saved computer file (potentially containing sub-files/folders) — each uniquely significant state vector gets its own computationally live dimension/folder within TBC, capable of being modified.
- **PMRK** — referenced in passing as one of "the various PMRK," i.e., the general class of physical matter reality systems of which OS is a particular instance; each is a "diverging, branching, set of uniquely dimensioned potential worlds."
- **EBC (Even Bigger Computer)** — mentioned as a concept the author says will be revisited later (Topic 3, Chapter 11) in connection with multiple PMRK, implying a still-larger computational context beyond TBC/OS.
- **Redundant-state collapse** — the author's claim that as the process runs, redundant states among the unactualized-past-possibility set are collapsed, and entire branches of the family tree may cease expanding once no further significant unique possibilities remain — part of how TBC keeps the system from requiring infinite storage/computation.
- **Figure 5-3** — the chapter's illustrative diagram ("Generating the Possible States of OS"), showing a worked example with M = 3: at m = 1 there are three significant states (OS1, OS2, OS3, with OS3 double-bordered as most probable); at m = 2 each spawns three more (solid black); at m = 3 each of those spawns three more (medium gray); at m = 4, three more each (light gray) — used purely to make the geometric branching easy to visualize, since the real number of significant states per iteration is "very large" and not known in advance.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Figure 5-3 worked example (M = 3 for visualization)**: the chapter's central illustrative device. At m = 1, TBC generates three significant possible states of OS — OS1, OS2, OS3 — with OS3 double-bordered as the one determined most probable and eventually actualized. At m = 2, each of the three m = 1 states (including the two unactualized ones, OS1 and OS2) generates its own three "solid black" child states; the text specifically notes that only one state generated by OS3 becomes most probable and is actualized (shown double-bordered), and that travelers in NPMRN "sometimes" get into the never-to-be-actualized branches attached to OS1 or OS2 and can fail to realize these are merely possible, not probable. At m = 3, each solid-black state generates three "medium gray" states; at m = 4, each of those generates three "light gray" states. Campbell uses this small, artificially limited example (real M and the state count per iteration would be far larger) to make the recursive parent→child branching structure — and the fact that only one path per branch gets actualized — concrete and graphically followable.
- **The time-traveler thought experiment**: Campbell poses a hypothetical objection in his own voice ("I can hear you wondering... why would an unactualized state do this?") and answers it with a scenario: a sentient being travels back through history to an old, dormant (unactualized) state vector and alters something significant in it. If the alteration matches a possibility TBC had already computed for that branch, no new calculation is required; if not, a wholly new branch of possibilities is generated. This is offered as the mechanism explaining how "dead" past-possibility states can, in principle, become computationally active again.
- **The what-if analysis / between-lives planning practice**: Campbell describes (without naming a specific individual case) a generic practice in which sentient beings, between PMR incarnations, use the exhaustive database of possible/dormant states to run what-if analysis on the consequences of past choices — explicitly framed as help in overcoming "personal belief systems" and as a normal part of planning the next PMR experience, sometimes self-directed and sometimes guided by others in NPMRN.
- **The "dimension" analogies**: Campbell offers a sequence of everyday analogies to explain what a TBC "dimension" is — a line on a sheet of paper, a text line on a computer screen where pressing enter creates a new one, and (his preferred analogy) a saved computer file that may itself contain sub-files or folders, each folder representing "an executing piece of the overall simulation." He explicitly flags the first two analogies as imperfect before offering the file/folder one as better.
