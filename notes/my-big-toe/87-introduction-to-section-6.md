# Introduction to Section 6

## In one paragraph
This short introduction opens the book's final section by turning from explaining the model to asking what the reader will now do with it. Thomas Campbell argues that real learning starts only when a person can appreciate their own ignorance from within a larger point of view, and that no authority — parent, book, class, or guru — can hand over a "quality answer" ready-made. He poses the central leftover question of the whole trilogy, "What now?", and warns that reaching real conclusions requires the harder work of building sound logical/experiential bridges across one's personal "chasm of ignorance and ingrained belief" rather than simply jumping to conclusions. He then offers, with heavy irony, six possible reader responses to the book in reverse order of difficulty, ending with the one he actually recommends: a lifelong, skeptical but open-minded pursuit of Big Truth. He closes by affirming that free will and the consequences of one's choices — not which of the six options is chosen — are what ultimately matter.

## Core claims
- Thomas Campbell claims that "a major advance in Big Picture understanding" can be secured simply by appreciating one's ignorance from a larger perspective, even without being able to convert that ignorance into explicit knowledge.
- He claims learning begins only when a person can appreciate their ignorance enough to ask a question and genuinely care about the quality of the answer — and that believing you already know the right answer makes learning impossible.
- He states that "we are not children" and that simply asking the question is not enough, then claims there is no external authority ("Mom, or Dad, or organization") able to supply an easy answer that is also a quality answer, and defines a quality answer as one that is "part of your personal solution without also being part of your personal problem."
- He frames "What now?" — what the reader will do with the ideas of My Big TOE — as one of the biggest questions left open by the book, and says it is also his own open question, inviting readers to share their answers at his website.
- He argues that reaching significant conclusions about the book's content requires either building logically and scientifically sound bridges from carefully evaluated personal experience, or jumping to conclusions across a "chasm of ignorance and ingrained belief" — and that bridge-building, while harder, is necessary to progress beyond mere "tail chasing."
- He uses the image of puppies and kittens chasing their own tails as funny, but argues that a human spending an entire life in the same "self-referential endless loop of non-productive tail chasing" is, from the Big Picture perspective, more sad than funny.
- He offers six reader responses to the book, in reverse order of difficulty, presented satirically: (1) discard the ideas entirely; (2) get briefly excited then let the ideas drift away; (3) treat the book as merely something to get a t-shirt for; (4) write an angry letter to the publisher demanding the book be withdrawn; (5) seek someone else (book, class, seminar, guru) to develop the ideas further before eventually sliding back to option 2; (6) commit serious, lifelong, skeptical-but-open-minded effort to pursuing Big Truth wherever it leads.
- He claims all six choices (and countless others) are acceptable, because what matters is moving in a direction that is forward relative to one's own current perspective and accumulated experience.
- He asserts "you make choices, and the choices you make, make you," naming free will as "the driver," and describes reaping what you sow, getting what you deserve, and deserving what you get as simply "how the game is played — lose or win."
- He claims all individuals are equally important and valuable and that all choices are valid steps on one's path of being, whether or not the person understands what they are doing.
- He states his advice and hope have nothing to do with which of the six choices a reader settles on — whichever choice reflects who the reader is today can eventually lead them where they need to go — but hopes the choice is made after careful consideration and is continually reevaluated as life's evidence accumulates.

## Concepts
- **Big Picture** — Campbell's term (used throughout the trilogy) for the larger, more complete point of view from which ordinary ignorance and knowledge can be reappraised; here, simply "appreciating your ignorance" within this larger perspective already counts as a major advance in understanding.
- **appreciating your ignorance** — the described starting condition for learning: recognizing what one does not know from within a bigger frame of reference, distinct from having converted that ignorance into actual knowledge; a precondition for asking a genuine question.
- **quality answer** — an answer defined as one that becomes "part of your personal solution without also being part of your personal problem"; contrasted with an "easy answer," which no external authority can supply.
- **the "What now?" question** — the question Campbell identifies as the most obvious big question left hanging after reading My Big TOE: what, if anything, the reader will do with the book's ideas and concepts; he says it is his own question too, and invites readers to answer it at his website.
- **chasm of ignorance and ingrained belief** — Campbell's image for the gap a reader must cross to move from encountering the book's ideas to reaching significant personal conclusions; its width and shape depend on the breadth, depth, and quality of the individual's own experience data.
- **building bridges vs. jumping to conclusions** — two contrasting methods of crossing that chasm: building logically and scientifically sound bridges from carefully evaluated experience (harder, but necessary for real progress) versus simply jumping to conclusions (easier, but equated with unproductive "tail chasing").
- **tail-chasing analogy** — the comparison of a person endlessly and self-referentially chasing conclusions without evaluated-experience "bridges" to puppies or kittens chasing their own tails; funny in animals, but "more sad than funny" when a human dedicates an entire life to it, as seen from the Big Picture.
- **the six suggestions** — Campbell's satirical list, in reverse order of difficulty, of what a reader might do with the book's ideas: (1) discard the ideas as useless, even repurposing the physical books; (2) let initial excitement fade and forget the Big Picture; (3) engage only superficially, e.g., to get an official Big TOE t-shirt from the website; (4) send an indignant letter to the publisher demanding the book be withdrawn, addressed to the author's (joking) home in Antarctica; (5) seek external help (book, class, seminar, guru) to develop one's Big Picture understanding, with a likely eventual slide back to option 2; (6) commit serious, lifelong, skeptical-but-open-minded personal effort to pursuing "Big Truth," described as becoming "a pudding-head on the path to true love."
- **free will as the driver** — Campbell's assertion that free will drives the process by which "you make choices, and the choices you make, make you"; choices and their consequences (reaping what you sow, getting/deserving what you get) constitute "how the game is played."
- **pudding-head on the path to true love** — Campbell's whimsical phrase for the character of someone who commits to option six: a lifelong, humble, skeptical yet open-minded seeker of Big Truth.
- **my-big-toe.com** — the author's website, mentioned as the place where readers can share their answer to "What now?" and where an "official Big TOE t-shirt" can be procured (referenced under suggestion 3).

## Experiments, examples & case studies
This is a short, reflective introduction rather than an experiential or evidential unit, so it contains no field cases, informants, or techniques. Its concrete material is entirely rhetorical/illustrative:
- The **puppies-and-kittens tail-chasing image** is used as a concrete, everyday picture to illustrate the danger of self-referential, non-productive reasoning loops when a person jumps to conclusions instead of building experiential bridges.
- The **six-item satirical list of reader responses** functions as a worked-through set of concrete "case studies" of possible reactions to the book, each described with specific comic detail — e.g., using the books "to warm yourself this winter," giving them to "some mushy-brained acquaintance," collecting "17 shiny gold stars" and "empty aspirin bottles" as proof of having finished the book, writing "a fiery letter full of indignation and well-placed exclamation marks" to the publisher and having it forwarded to "the author at his home in Antarctica," and seeking a "book, class, seminar, guru" for further development.
- The reference to **my-big-toe.com** and the "official Big TOE t-shirt" is a concrete, specific (if tongue-in-cheek) real-world pointer for reader action.

## Thread
As the introduction to Section 6 ("The End is Always the Beginning"), this unit pivots the trilogy from exposition of the model to a direct challenge about personal application, setting up the section's role as a closing reflection rather than further model-building. It reprises the book's recurring emphasis (carried over from earlier sections) on evaluated personal experience over received answers, and on free will and choices-with-consequences as central to the reader's own evolution — themes the book has built throughout via its discussions of consciousness, reality, and personal growth. It hands off to whatever follows in Section 6 by framing the remaining chapters as being about what a reader does next, rather than what the model itself contains.
