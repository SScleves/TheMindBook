# Ch 38 — Does the Big Dude Have an Attitude? Do We? Speculate! Speculate! Dance to the Music!

## In one paragraph
Campbell asks how AUM (the larger consciousness in his model) regards us — and answers that the question is irrelevant, unanswerable, and driven by ego. Because AUM has limits, there could logically be other AUMs (evolution "tends to explore all the possibilities"), but from our tiny perspective AUM appears infinite in all ways, so such speculation is practically pointless and nearly pointless philosophically. Campbell argues that everything that can directly affect us is contained within a highly limited set, so AUM's attitude, intelligence, caring, or opinion of us contributes nothing to understanding Our System or the mechanics of existence. He diagnoses the urge to ask as anthropomorphism plus inflated human self-importance, fed by "feel-good" belief systems that manipulate needy egos with emotional hooks. Through the stomach-cell and Petri-dish analogies he models how a higher-level being plausibly relates to its lower-level components — caring, but not individually attentive, and deliberately non-meddling. The chapter closes on his practical stance: this life's initial conditions were custom designed (with your input and approval) for your growth; focus on what you can grasp, let go of the rest, and keep your humor.

## Core claims
- Campbell claims that since AUM has limits and is aware of them, it must set priorities, make judgments, pace itself, focus on what is most significant, and use its limited resources wisely for maximum self-improvement.
- Campbell allows that if AUM is finite there could be other AUMs ("Sure, why not? Evolution tends to explore all the possibilities"), but insists it makes no difference to us or our reality: from our tiny perspective AUM appears infinite in all ways.
- Campbell claims questions about multiple AUMs are totally pointless practically and almost pointless philosophically — fertile ground for amusement ("the philosophy boogie") but not to be taken seriously. Once you have gone far beyond your ability to comprehend what you are saying, either find the humor in it or be quiet.
- Campbell argues that accepting that your reality is dramatically affected by systems and phenomena beyond your capability to understand and control is the first step toward wisdom; differentiating these unknowables from what is within your potential to comprehend is the second step.
- Campbell warns that cramming unlived, unexperienced ontological conceptual structures into your intellect squanders enthusiasm, makes you believe you know more than you do, and yields no significant increase in spiritual quality.
- Campbell claims that absolutely everything that can possibly affect us directly is fully contained within a highly limited set; what is external to AUM affecting us indirectly is beyond our theoretical knowing.
- Campbell holds that speculation about how focused AUM is on us, how intelligent it is, how much it cares, or whether it is emotional or computer-like is totally superfluous: our PMR could be insignificant or AUM's main focus, one of many experiments in consciousness or a unique implementation of the consciousness cycle — AUM's viewpoint makes no difference to understanding the larger reality. Only an ego cares about relative comparisons, hierarchy, and pecking order.
- Campbell argues that human anthropomorphic tendencies and egocentric species-pride make him suspicious of any logic that puts humans too close to the center of Big Picture significance; belief in the supreme importance of humankind is "the ultimate source of much mischief" and the first step toward believing some humans are superior to others.
- Campbell claims cultural, personal, and religious belief systems tend to be "feel-good" systems (hence their popularity) that use emotional hooks — alternating feel-bad (ignorance, guilt, inadequacy) with feel-good (importance, security, salvation, forgiveness, superiority) in "one-two punches," the good cop/bad cop routine — to capture and manipulate needy egos, and that such systems "rule the land, often subtly without being noticed."
- Campbell claims emotional hooks are used specifically to capture and manipulate needy egos — those who feel fear, feel lost and powerless, feel inadequate, helpless, and insecure, or feel as if they are not doing what they should be doing; feel-good beliefs cover up these fears and help you feel strong, in charge, and powerful, feel as if you belong, are accepted, and understood, and feel loved, safe, and cared for.
- Campbell says he deliberately belittles our culturally ingrained self-satisfying importance throughout the trilogy, using blunt humor (never cynicism) as his weapon against this conceptual error.
- Campbell anticipates that a few readers, if caught on a bad day, may have difficulty with the relative humble position we most likely occupy in the big scheme of things, and may feel emotionally upset or angered by what they wrongly interpret as an implied lack of personal importance.
- Campbell claims that, like bacteria, we are important in our own way and should be able to feel good without feeling superior; being a small part of something very large is only a downer if erroneous belief and ego make it one.
- Campbell describes himself as by nature a cheerful, happy, upbeat type who thinks people, reality, and life are a blast — an exciting ride, a wonderful opportunity; he urges the reader to find the humor, read and smile, let go, and periodically "open up the top of your head" so fresh ideas can blow away the musty odor of old paradigms and force traditions and the status quo to periodically re-justify their value, since "nothing encourages humor more than honest introspection."
- Campbell claims the initial conditions of your PMR existence were custom designed for you to optimize your growth potential — with your input and approval; learning and joy go together, not-learning and misery go together, and focused effort in the PMR learning lab can dramatically raise your joy-to-misery ratio, with growth spilling over into others' lives.
- Campbell claims we inevitably recast anything sentient and important to us in our own image — we can only project our own quality onto others — and "a wrong answer, for some reason, always seems far superior to admitting ignorance," so be on guard.
- Campbell claims every being is interested in and cares about what it interacts with at its own local level; everything else is invisible, mystical (hopelessly beyond its knowing), or apparently irrelevant. When focus expands, it expands upward; lower levels are taken for granted unless they do something unusual.
- Campbell suggests consciousness evolution is most likely a more immediate, up-close-and-personal experience for AUM than biological evolution is for us — and we are part of AUM's personal evolutionary process, hence clearly significant as full-fledged members of the consciousness cycle.
- Campbell concludes that AUM's attitudes and feelings toward OS, PMR, humanity, or any human are beyond the scope of even the biggest Big TOE — beyond our comprehension and irrelevant to everything of practical or theoretical significance. "AUM does what AUM does for its own reasons and we do what we do for our own reasons." Answering such ego questions with belief and pseudo-knowledge is a giant step backward that erects barriers to individual progress.

## Concepts
- **AUM** — the limited-but-vast consciousness Campbell's model rests on; in this chapter, a being that, being aware of its limits, must prioritize and spend its finite resources wisely for maximum self-improvement, and which "does what AUM does for its own reasons." From our tiny perspective it "in all ways, appears to be infinite."
- **Multiple AUMs** — Campbell's own speculative extension: if AUM is finite, there could be others, since evolution explores all possibilities; he immediately dismisses the question as making no difference to us or our reality.
- **PMR** — physical-matter reality; our everyday reality frame. Campbell calls it a "learning lab" whose initial conditions for each existence are custom designed (with your input and approval) to optimize growth.
- **OS (Our System)** — Campbell's term for our system of reality (used here alongside PMR as things whose standing in AUM's eyes is unknowable and irrelevant).
- **Big TOE / Theory Of Everything / Reality Model** — Campbell's overarching model; he stipulates that AUM's interactions with anything other than itself, and AUM's attitudes toward us, are "unnecessary to (and totally beyond)" it — everything that can directly affect us fits in a highly limited set.
- **Consciousness cycle** — the evolutionary consciousness process of which we are "full-fledged members"; our PMR might be one of many experiments in consciousness or a unique implementation of this cycle.
- **Consciousness evolution vs. biological evolution** — Campbell's comparison: AUM's relation to consciousness evolution is likely more immediate and personal than our relation to biological evolution; we are part of AUM's personal evolutionary process.
- **Unknowables** — systems and phenomena that dramatically affect your reality but lie beyond your capability to understand and control; accepting them is step one of wisdom, distinguishing them from the comprehensible is step two.
- **Feel-good belief systems** — cultural, personal, and religious belief systems that boost ego and self-importance (making you feel important, safe, valued, superior); popular precisely because of this, and blocking evaluation of a bigger picture that refuses to emotionally manipulate. The "feel bad stick serves as the foil to the feel good carrot."
- **Emotional hooks** — the manipulation mechanism of belief systems: feel-bad (guilt, inadequacy, fear) interspersed with feel-good (salvation, acceptance, superiority) in one-two punches aimed at needy egos — Campbell's "good cop, bad cop routine." They target those who feel fear, lost, powerless, inadequate, helpless, or insecure, and cover these up by making you feel strong, in charge, powerful, that you belong, are accepted and understood, and are loved, safe, and cared for. Beliefs without emotional hooks are, to the addicted, "like cigarettes without nicotine."
- **Ego** — the part of us that needs relative comparisons, hierarchy, and pecking order; the only thing that can care how AUM regards us, and (with erroneous belief) the only thing that can turn smallness into a downer. Ego issues are "counterproductive to both science and self-improvement."
- **Anthropomorphism / self-importance** — the tendency to recast anything sentient and important to us in our own image, judging others by projecting our own quality onto them; combined with species-pride, it clouds our vision and seeds wars, atrocities, and the idea that some humans are superior to others.
- **Pseudo-knowledge** — belief-based answers to ego questions (like "how does AUM regard us?"); adopting them is a giant step backward that creates barriers to individual progress.
- **PMR learning lab / custom-designed initial conditions** — Campbell's framing of a lifetime: the starting conditions of your PMR existence are designed, with your input and approval, to optimize growth; effort and attention there raise the joy-to-misery ratio.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The stomach-cell analogy (Campbell's model of AUM's regard for us)** — Campbell asks "Do I care about the cells in my stomach?" Sure — sometimes more than others — but he never deals with them individually or in small groups "unless I get an ulcer or a perforation." Moving parts: (1) each stomach cell "dearly cares" about the pH of the secreted hydrochloric acid and its regulating feedback mechanisms — its own local level; (2) the cells don't care directly about the style of his haircut, but indirectly a new haircut could reduce stress, help him "find a steady job and a girlfriend who can cook," and thus please the stomach — even though the stomach cannot understand haircuts or the complex chain connecting haircuts, stress, jobs, girlfriends, and stomach acid. What it shows, per Campbell: everything is important and related; a higher level cares about lower levels without individual attention; lower levels neither perceive nor need to perceive the higher level's concerns; and stomach cells don't "feel slighted" at not being the center of the body — nor should we.
- **The Petri-dish bacteria experiment** — even when experimenting with bacteria in a Petri dish, "we leave them alone to do whatever they do. We don't name each bacterium and take them out to lunch on their birthdays — even if the experiment is important." Meddling, unless carefully restricted and precisely controlled, would invalidate the experiment — and then "the experimenter would have to flush the little fellows down the drain and start over. Uh oh!" Campbell uses this to model why a higher consciousness running an important experiment would plausibly be hands-off, and to slyly hint at the stakes of a spoiled experiment.
- **"Like bacteria, we are important in our own way"** — Campbell anticipates that a few readers, caught on a bad day, may struggle with the relatively humble position we likely occupy and may feel emotionally upset or angered, wrongly interpreting it as an implied lack of personal importance; his answer is the explicit scaling of the analogy to humanity: small parts of something very large can feel good without feeling superior; only belief systems and ego make smallness a downer.
- **The cheerful-upbeat stance** — Campbell describes himself as by nature a cheerful, happy, upbeat type who finds people, reality, and life "a blast — an exciting ride, a wonderful opportunity," and urges the reader to find the humor, read and smile, let go, and periodically "open up the top of your head" so fresh ideas can blow away the musty odor of old paradigms and force traditions and the status quo to re-justify their value, since "nothing encourages humor more than honest introspection."
- **The "Dr. Feelgood" / belief-pills skit** — Campbell's mock dialogue: "Wow, that sure sounds good to me! Where can I get a few of those feel good belief-pills? Not for myself, of course, but I have this friend who has a bonafide medical need…" followed by "Say, what's the long-term price for these happy hallucinations? Are you sure this is legal?" He casts adherents of feel-good beliefs as "needy individuals who are addicted to the medications of Dr. Feelgood," for whom hook-free beliefs are "like cigarettes without nicotine." Shows his method of using blunt humor to expose belief-system addiction.
- **The good cop/bad cop routine** — his model of emotive manipulation's mechanics: feel-bad (ignorance, guilt, inadequacy) precedes and is interspersed with feel-good (importance, salvation, redemption, forgiveness, superiority) in "one-two punches" appealing to the needy ego; the carrot-and-stick pair by which belief systems "rule the land."
- **The "philosophy boogie"** — Campbell's image for speculation about multiple AUMs and AUM's attitude: dance it for fun with your intellect, but "don't take yourself or your brilliant choreography too seriously"; past the limits of comprehension, find the humor or be quiet.
- **Wars and atrocities from superiority** — Campbell's historical-scale observation (no specific events named): many wars, atrocities, and "general acts of meanness" spring from a sense of superiority; the belief that humans are supremely important leads to the belief that some humans outrank others — a "slippery slope that is both steep and long."

## Thread
This chapter sits early in Book 2's Section 3 ("Man in the Loop"), where Campbell turns from deriving AUM and the consciousness fractal to situating humanity within it. It follows directly from the preceding treatment of AUM's limits ("As previously pointed out, if AUM has limits…"), and its job is boundary-setting: it fences off an entire class of questions — multiple AUMs, AUM's attitude, intelligence, and feelings toward OS/PMR/humanity — as ego-driven, unknowable, and unnecessary to the Big TOE, since everything that can directly affect us lies within a limited, comprehensible set. It simultaneously inoculates the reader against feel-good belief systems (explaining why he uses blunt humor to deflate human self-importance throughout the trilogy) and models the correct scale-relation via stomach cells and Petri-dish bacteria: cared about, part of the larger being's evolution, but not individually coddled. This prepares the ground for the rest of Section 3's account of the individual's actual role and responsibilities — growth in the PMR learning lab under custom-designed initial conditions — rather than speculation about status in AUM's eyes.
