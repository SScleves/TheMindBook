# Ch 17 — Introduction to Section 2

## In one paragraph
Campbell opens Section 2 ("Mysticism Demystified") by redefining the word "mystical": it is not a fixed category of nonsense but a moving boundary relative to a culture's or an individual's knowledge and beliefs — much of yesterday's mysticism is today's science, and much of today's will be tomorrow's. He argues that Western science itself rests on a mystical premise (the belief that everything unknown must lie inside the PMR data-set and obey objective causality), and traces a "farce" in which that faith is transmuted into an unquestioned foundation, science becomes the sole judge of truth, and philosophy is marginalized. He then states the mission of Section 2: to develop the basic concepts needed to support a rational Big Picture Theory Of Everything (Big TOE) and to push the reader's "mystical edge" back until the residual unknown has no practical significance. Using the extended analogy of bacteria in the human intestine, he argues that PMR may be a local reality — a subset of something larger — and that a comprehensive TOE must cover everything objective, subjective, normal, and paranormal. He deliberately withholds the "surprise ending," insisting that transcending ingrained belief systems (not intellectual capacity) is the prerequisite for "getting it," which is why a trilogy rather than a technical paper is required, and he asks for open-minded skepticism and patience with Section 2's abstract, initially unlit "tunnels."

## Core claims
- Campbell claims "mystical" is a relative term: whatever appears to lie beyond our potential ability to explain within PMR (physical-matter reality) gets labeled mystical; much considered mystical a thousand years ago is science today, and much of today's mysticism will be understood by future science.
- Campbell argues that what appears mystical from the Western view reflects a "double dose of ignorance": cultural beliefs plus the limited understanding of contemporary science.
- For individuals, Campbell says what lies beyond personal understanding may be (1) interpreted as mystical, (2) construed by belief into something that suits the individual's needs, (3) regarded as temporary ignorance of something theoretically knowable, or (4) regarded as permanent ignorance of something theoretically unknowable — and any conclusion about what lies beyond one's objective reach is necessarily belief-based (unless the conclusion is that there can be no conclusions).
- Campbell defines belief as "a conclusion based upon a mystical premise," and argues scientists' faith that the unknown must fit the PMR data-set and ordinary objective causality is simply "a more accepted form of mysticism" — so the objective causality of Western materialism springs from a mystical assumption, and faith becomes science.
- Campbell claims this illogical transformation chain-reacts: science becomes truth (or its sole judge) and is given the job of defining reality; ideas bifurcate into science/truth vs. philosophy/conjecture; philosophy is marginalized as producing "nothing but useless arguments"; science ends up imprisoned by its ancient faith-based paradigms while philosophy vainly mimics science's illusion of objectivity — a "preposterously convoluted French farce" with a surprise ending he will not reveal until Section 6.
- Campbell argues he must put off the ending because seeing the Big Picture requires transcending ingrained belief systems, not just intellect — which is why a trilogy, not a technical paper, is required; academic papers only communicate value inside accepted belief systems, and "digging out details" is a totally different mental process from discovering Big Picture paradigms.
- Campbell claims: "You will never be able to see the Big Picture as long as you focus on individual politically correct pixels."
- The goal of Section 2, per Campbell: develop the basic concepts to support a rational theory of existence, and push the reader's "mystical edge" (where knowledge meets ignorance) back until the residual unknown has no potential significance or practical interest.
- Campbell argues a TOE that does not reach beyond PMR is only a "little TOE" confined to PMR and limited to a local causality; "everything," practically defined for a given awareness, means everything knowable/important/meaningful/significant that can directly interact with that awareness — so what counts as a complete Big TOE is relative to perspective.
- Via the intestinal-bacteria analogy, Campbell claims there may be a natural practical limit to human knowledge, that we may be a very small part of a much greater reality, and that Homo sapiens should not be surprised to be in the bacteria's situation.
- Campbell argues that allowing the possibility that PMR is a local reality (a subset of something larger) is the first step toward comprehending a bigger picture; the little-picture concept is fully contained within the more general bigger-picture concept as a logical superset.
- Campbell notes it is as easy to understand the limitations of a bacterium's perspective as it is difficult to understand the limitations of our own — we cannot be aware of what is beyond our awareness — but we can stay open to learning new things and, in the process, expand the scope of our awareness, and therefore our reality, to its outer limits.
- Campbell states his criterion for an improved reality paradigm: if the larger paradigm gives a better, more concise understanding of the available data and produces valuable new knowledge, approaches, and processes, it is a more accurate, productive, and truer representation of the whole — it "broadens and deepens the available solution space" practically and usefully.
- Campbell claims My Big TOE will attempt to encompass everything known and knowable; what lies beyond it will remain practically unknowable (forever mystical), though both the mystical and the "beyond mystical" will be explored via logical extrapolation.
- Campbell insists there is "no catch, no megalomania, no hypothetical wackiness, no goofy beliefs — just straightforward science that better describes the measured data," and that his Big TOE is primarily derived from carefully evaluated scientific data rather than theoretical conjecture.
- Campbell claims a correct Big TOE must cover everything objective, subjective, normal, and paranormal — mind and matter, consciousness and concrete, personal as well as scientific facts — and that it is the reader's job to assess how well it explains their experience; a little TOE or incorrect Big TOE will fail to support all the data.
- Campbell claims the correct Big TOE will necessarily be strange ("it will need to be strange or it could not possibly be a correct Big TOE"); something less strange would be easier to sell but would not be correct.
- Campbell argues egos trick people into believing they are much less ignorant than they are — citing scientists at the end of the nineteenth century lamenting that everything important had already been discovered, a claim now laughable — and that modern physics demonstrates "there is nothing as outrageous or strange as the truth."
- Campbell asks the reader to combine intuition with intellect (while applying the data of their own experience), warns that Section 2 is abstract with "precious little light" at the end of its tunnels initially, and asks the reader to delay final judgments until concepts are developed and applied in later sections — the method to the madness apparent by the end of Book 3.
- Campbell claims appreciating and understanding the Big Picture is always the first step in focusing and directing the effective investment of the resources you have at your command; the point and purpose of the work is to offer an expanded view of reality and of the reader's relationship to that reality, useful and helpful in a direct, practical way, delivering "a reality model that provides a unique perspective which can be profitably applied to your professional and personal life."
- Campbell states his purpose: his discovery must lead to the reader's discovery, in personal as well as objective terms; even if the books only cause readers to reassess their beliefs, concepts, knowledge, and attitudes, his effort will have been worthwhile.

## Concepts
- **mystical** — In Campbell's usage, whatever process, phenomenon, or conceptualization appears to lie beyond our potential ability to explain within the context of PMR. It is relative: relative to a culture's accumulated knowledge and beliefs, and, for an individual, relative to that person's understanding, knowledge, and ignorance.
- **PMR (physical-matter reality)** — Campbell's acronym for the physical world; the reality frame Western science assumes to contain everything. He argues PMR may be a "local reality," a subset of something larger.
- **NPMR (nonphysical-matter reality)** — Campbell's acronym for nonphysical-matter reality; used here as the counterpart to PMR when describing what an individual may know little or much about.
- **belief** — Campbell defines belief as "a conclusion based upon a mystical premise." Both culturally given and personally derived beliefs are held with a certainty that, he says, only "deep bone-level ignorance" could sustain.
- **Big TOE (Big Picture Theory Of Everything)** — A comprehensive theory of existence that reaches beyond PMR and accounts for everything objective, subjective, normal, and paranormal — mind and matter, consciousness and concrete, all true data and facts of existence, personal and scientific, in one theory.
- **little TOE (Little picture Theory Of Everything)** — A TOE that does not reach beyond PMR; confined to PMR and limited to a local causality.
- **Tiny TOE** — Campbell's illustrative term for a theory of everything from a radically limited perspective (his example: intestinal bacteria); it describes only "everything that is potentially knowable" by that awareness, and seems complete from within while missing causes on which its reality profoundly depends.
- **"everything" (practical definition)** — For a given awareness: everything knowable, important, meaningful, or significant that can directly interact with that awareness. Hence what constitutes a complete Big TOE is relative to perspective, knowledge, and limitations.
- **mystical edge** — Campbell's term for the boundary "where knowledge meets ignorance." Section 2 aims to push the reader's mystical edge back until the residual unknown has no potential significance or practical interest.
- **local reality** — PMR considered as a subset of a much greater reality; admitting this "outside theoretical possibility" is Campbell's first step toward comprehending a bigger picture.
- **superset paradigm / improved reality paradigm** — The bigger picture forms a logical superset fully containing the little-picture concept; an improved reality paradigm is one that "broadens and deepens the available solution space relative to existing data and problems in a way that is practical and useful," and is thereby a more accurate, truer representation of the whole.
- **objective causality / Western materialism** — The scientific article of faith that the unknown must be contained within the PMR data-set and follow ordinary objective causality; Campbell argues this is mysticism transmuted into an unquestioned philosophical foundation ("Faith becomes science").
- **open-minded skepticism** — The stance Campbell asks of the reader: neither belief nor dismissal; "the only approach that retains the possibility of success for either of us."
- **Big Picture** — Campbell's term for the whole, top-level view of reality; seeing it requires transcending ingrained belief systems, not just intellectual capacity, and cannot be achieved by focusing on individual details ("pixels").
- **Big Truth** — Mentioned as what the reader's own explorations will bring to bear on the trilogy's conclusions as understanding accrues over the next four sections.
- **pseudo-knowledge** — Listed among the human limitations (with beliefs, preconceived notions, attitudes, fears, desires, needs, and cultural biases) that constrain us beyond the mere capacity of our minds.
- **beyond mystical** — What lies past even the mystical for a given awareness (as bread-making lies beyond the bacteria's mystical stomach-source of food); Campbell says both the mystical and the beyond mystical will be explored via "logical extrapolations that reach well beyond our capacity to comprehend."
- **reality model** — Campbell's term for what My Big TOE offers the reader: "a reality model that provides a unique perspective which can be profitably applied to your professional and personal life," following from appreciating and understanding the Big Picture as the first step in directing the resources one has at their command.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The intestinal-bacteria Tiny TOE analogy (the chapter's central model)** — Campbell asks the reader to imagine a Tiny TOE built by bacteria living in the human intestine. Moving parts: (1) the bacteria's TOE need only describe what is potentially knowable by the bacteria, so it ignores the sun's nuclear fusion and the atmosphere's composition even though all earthly life depends on them; (2) it would seem complete from inside while omitting the money in your bank account, the light bulb in your refrigerator, or the car in your garage — items that can nonetheless indirectly and profoundly affect the intestinal environment; (3) to the bacteria, digested food descending from the stomach seems mystical, while the economic, social, and physical processes behind growing wheat and making and marketing bread are "beyond mystical" — forever beyond the largest reality even "the most brilliant intestinal bacteria" could comprehend. Campbell's punchline: "Do not be too surprised to find Homo sapiens in a similar situation." The analogy shows why PMR may be a local reality with invisible causal mechanisms ordering it from beyond.
- **The French-farce narrative of science and philosophy** — Campbell dramatizes Western intellectual history as a farce in acts: mystical assumption becomes scientific foundation → faith becomes science → science becomes truth/sole judge of truth and defines reality → ideas bifurcate (science/truth vs. philosophy/conjecture) → philosophy is marginalized as producing "useless arguments" → science, unable to escape its faith-based paradigms, is imprisoned by its core beliefs and struggles with "self-induced myopia" while philosophy vainly mimics its illusion of objectivity. He teases a "surprise ending" withheld until the end of Section 6.
- **The pixels analogy** — Detail-digging science vs. Big Picture discovery: "You will never be able to see the Big Picture as long as you focus on individual politically correct pixels." Used to argue that academic papers and incremental detail-work cannot generate Big Picture paradigms.
- **Late-nineteenth-century scientists** — Historical reference: scientists toward the end of the nineteenth century "often lamented the obvious fact that everything important (in science and technology) had been discovered" — a claim laughable a century later. Campbell uses it to show egos trick us into underestimating our ignorance, and cites modern physics as demonstrating that nothing is as outrageous or strange as the truth.
- **The Gordian knot metaphor** — My Big TOE proposes to "dissolve the Gordian knot of limiting belief" that condemns science and philosophy to "picking the bones of the past."
- **The imagined skeptic's outburst** — Campbell voices the anticipated objection ("Encompass everything knowable? Derive physics from metaphysics? Explain the paranormal scientifically? No way!… Is this guy nuts or what?") and answers it: no catch, no megalomania, just science that better describes the measured data — with open-minded skepticism as the only viable reader stance.
- **The "abstract tunnels" caution** — Campbell's closing model of Section 2 itself: many abstract conceptual tunnels with "precious little light" at their ends initially; conceptual building blocks must be defined before Big TOE construction can begin, with validation accruing across the next four sections and full sense by the end of Book 3.

## Key quotes
- "Much of what was considered mystical a thousand years ago is considered science today and much of what is considered mystical today will be clearly understood by a future science." — the opening relativization of "mystical."
- "Belief is a conclusion based upon a mystical premise." — Campbell's core definition, applied to scientists' faith in PMR causality as much as to religion.
- "Voila! Faith becomes science, or at least an integral part of the scientific attitude." — the climax of his argument that Western materialism rests on mystical assumption.
- "You will never be able to see the Big Picture as long as you focus on individual politically correct pixels." — why detail-focused science cannot discover Big Picture paradigms.
- "Seeing the Big Picture requires more than intellectual capacity. It also requires transcending ingrained belief systems." — why he withholds the ending and why a trilogy is needed.
- "A TOE that does not reach beyond PMR is only a little TOE (Little picture Theory Of Everything) confined to PMR and limited to a local causality." — the Big/little TOE distinction.
- "Do not be too surprised to find Homo sapiens in a similar situation." — the payoff of the intestinal-bacteria analogy.
- "Allowing the outside theoretical possibility that our beloved PMR may be a local reality (a subset of something larger) is the first step toward comprehending a bigger picture." — the chapter's pivotal move.
- "The biggest picture must cover everything — everything objective, everything subjective, everything normal, and everything paranormal." — the completeness criterion for a correct Big TOE.
- "Oh yes, it will need to be strange or it could not possibly be a correct Big TOE." — Campbell's warning about strangeness and his own credibility being on "thin ice."
- "Until then, open minded skepticism is the only approach that retains the possibility of success for either of us." — the reader stance he requires.
- "To be optimally effective, my discovery must lead to your discovery." — the closing statement of purpose.

## Thread
This chapter is the hinge from Section 1 into Section 2. Following Section 1's groundwork, it reframes "mystical" as a relative boundary of knowledge, indicts Western science's own hidden mysticism, and announces Section 2's job: laying the abstract conceptual foundation — the "building blocks" — for a rational Big TOE, before those concepts are linked up and applied in later sections. It explicitly sets up the epistemology chapter to come ("In the chapter after next we will thoroughly explore" the difference between knowledge and belief) and the chapter before it, which will "take a look at our collective beginning" ("It is always a good idea to start at the beginning"). It also plants the withheld "surprise ending" that Campbell promises will play out by the end of Section 6, and warns that full validation only accrues by the end of Book 3.
