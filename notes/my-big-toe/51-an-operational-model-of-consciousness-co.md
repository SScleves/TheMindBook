# Ch 51 — An Operational Model of Consciousness Computers, Simulations, Artificial Intelligence, and Us

## In one paragraph
Campbell opens this chapter by pulling together the trilogy's earlier result — that we, and the reality (OS, Our System) we appear to live in, are simulated entities running in TBC (The Big Computer), which is itself a portion of the mind or consciousness of the evolving AUM — and announces that he will now carry the idea further by comparing advanced simulations and their simulated entities with the operational nature of our local reality. He argues that, operationally, humans are not very different from the entities we simulate in our own computers: the differences are mostly in the quality and richness of sensory input data, the extensive use of parallel processing and feedback loops, and the capacity of our dedicated processing hardware (brain and central nervous system). The one seemingly decisive difference he identifies is free will — our capacity to make choices that reflect and define the quality of our evolving consciousness — along with the apparent human monopoly on emotions and a soul. Campbell then undercuts that very distinction: he calls the belief that humanity exclusively possesses a nonphysical part (soul) a human conceit, and previews that even the soul has a simulation analog, so the distinction is perhaps not as important as it first appears.

## Core claims
- Campbell claims (recapping previous chapters) that we and the reality (OS) we appear to exist within are simulated entities in TBC, and that TBC represents a portion of the mind or consciousness of the evolving AUM.
- Campbell argues that, in many ways, we are not that different from the entities we simulate in our computers — there are many operational similarities between humans and digital creations.
- Campbell holds that the few operational differences are mostly matters of degree: the quality and richness of the input data from our primary five sensors, the extensive use of parallel processing and feedback loops, and the capacity of our dedicated processing equipment (brain and central nervous system).
- Campbell notes we are functionally similar to some of our digital creations even though we are of radically dissimilar substance, motivation, limitations, and construction.
- Campbell states that the single most significant apparent difference between us and what we might create in a computer is our free will to make choices that reflect and define the quality of our evolving consciousness — a nonphysical component and a will free to make decisions, express intent and complex motivation, and evolve itself.
- Campbell observes that humans also see themselves as special because they appear to have emotions and a soul, which simulated entities in digital computers "obviously" do not.
- Campbell argues that the belief that humanity exclusively possesses a nonphysical part (soul) is a human conceit that leads many people to place themselves above all other ("lesser") life-forms, and doubly so above digital creations.
- Campbell previews that even the soul — this great source of human pride that seemingly sets us apart from any simulation, no matter how sophisticated — has a simulation analog, and is perhaps not as important a distinction as it initially appears.

## Concepts
- **TBC (The Big Computer)** — In Campbell's model, the computer-like process within which we and our reality are simulated; it represents a portion of the mind or consciousness of the evolving AUM.
- **AUM** — Campbell's evolving fundamental consciousness; TBC is a portion of its mind or consciousness. (The acronym itself is not expanded in this chapter; it is used as the established name of the evolving consciousness from earlier chapters.)
- **OS (Our System)** — The reality we appear to exist within; Campbell glosses it as "the reality (OS)" and treats it, together with us, as a simulated construct inside TBC.
- **simulated entities** — Entities generated within a simulation; Campbell applies the term both to the creatures we simulate in our computers and to us, as simulated entities in TBC.
- **operational model of consciousness** — The chapter's framing (from its title): describing consciousness by how it operates, comparing the attributes of advanced simulations and their simulated entities with the operational nature of our local reality.
- **free will** — In this chapter, the seemingly single most significant difference between us and our digital creations: the freedom to make choices that reflect and define the quality of our evolving consciousness; a will free to make decisions, express intent and complex motivation, and evolve itself.
- **intent** — Something a free will expresses, alongside complex motivation and decisions; Campbell lists it among the capacities that appear to make us superior to digitally simulated entities.
- **soul / nonphysical component** — The nonphysical part humans believe they exclusively possess; Campbell calls the belief in its exclusivity a human conceit and claims the soul has a simulation analog.
- **human conceit** — Campbell's label for the belief that humanity alone possesses a nonphysical part (soul), which leads many humans to rank themselves above all other ("lesser") life-forms and doubly above digital creations.
- **primary five sensors** — Campbell's operational term for our five senses, framed as the input-data channels whose quality and richness partly distinguish us from computer-simulated entities.
- **parallel processing and feedback loops** — Computational features whose extensive use in humans Campbell lists among the few operational differences between us and simulated entities.
- **dedicated processing equipment** — Campbell's term for the brain and central nervous system, framed as the hardware whose capacity differentiates us from our digital creations.
- **evolving consciousness** — The quality that our free-will choices reflect and define; consciousness in Campbell's model is something that evolves through choice.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The humans-as-simulated-entities-in-TBC model (recap and extension).** Campbell restates the model built in previous chapters — we and our reality (OS) are simulated entities in TBC, a portion of the evolving AUM's mind — and announces the chapter's project: comparing the attributes of advanced simulations and their simulated entities with the operational nature of our local reality. Moving parts named here: input data from the primary five sensors, parallel processing, feedback loops, and dedicated processing equipment (brain and central nervous system).
- **The human vs. computer-simulated-entity comparison.** Campbell's central analogy: humans compared point by point with the entities we simulate in our computers. He says operationally there are many similarities; the differences are mostly in richness of sensory input, parallel processing/feedback, and processing capacity; and substance, motivation, limitations, and construction are radically dissimilar even while function is similar. What it shows, per Campbell: we are functionally much closer to our digital creations than we assume.
- **The free-will/soul objection and its promised deflation.** Campbell stages the natural objection — we are unique and fundamentally superior because we possess a nonphysical component, free will, emotions, and a soul, which digital entities "obviously" lack — and then previews that even the soul has a simulation analog, so the distinction may not hold. This sets up the argument the rest of the chapter (and the following material) is meant to deliver.

## Key quotes
- "We have earlier developed the concept that we, and the reality (OS) we appear to exist within, are simulated entities in TBC, which represents a portion of the mind or consciousness of the evolving AUM." — the chapter's opening recap of the model being extended.
- "In many ways, we are not that different from the entities that we simulate in our computers." — the chapter's central comparative claim.

- "It would seem that the single most significant difference between us and what we might create within a computer is our free will to make choices that reflect and define the quality of our evolving consciousness." — free will as the apparent key differentiator.

- "…even this great source of human pride and distinction that seemingly would set us apart from any simulation, no matter how sophisticated, has its simulation analog and is perhaps not as important a distinction as it initially appears." — the chapter's forward-looking thesis about the soul.

## Thread
This chapter opens the operational-model arc of Section 4 ("Solving the Mystery"). It explicitly picks up "the concept that we, and the reality (OS) we appear to exist within, are simulated entities in TBC" from previous chapters, and carries that idea further by discussing the attributes of advanced simulations as an operational mirror of our local reality. Its job in the argument is to erode the assumed gulf between humans and digitally simulated entities: it grants the apparent differentiators (free will, emotions, soul), reframes the soul's exclusivity as human conceit, and promises that the soul itself has a simulation analog — setting up the subsequent development of consciousness as something that can be modeled operationally, in computational terms.
