# More Good Company

## In one paragraph
This closing chapter of the trilogy widens the "good company" of chapter 91 beyond top scientists and philosophers to include Western thinkers, artists, writers, political leaders, and educators — deliberately restricted to Western, non-dogmatic sources for pragmatic reasons of audience. Thomas Campbell groups a large collection of quotations (mostly from Einstein, but also Jung, Emerson, Kierkegaard, Twain, Freud, and many others) under seven headed themes — belief and self-imposed limitation, mysticism, growth, wisdom, fear/ego/delusion, truth/science/logic, and education/learning — using each set to show that the trilogy's ideas are not fringe but echo widely respected Western voices. The chapter then turns from quotation to direct address, with Campbell explicitly stating what he as author has and has not done for the reader ("I have done what I can do. The ball is in your court"), reiterating that growth, wisdom, and discovery of Big Truth cannot be transferred and must be personally experienced. It closes with a meditation on responsibility, choice, and courage, a final challenge (in charge and driving, or dragged kicking and screaming?), and a farewell — "Uncle Tom" signing off and pointing back toward NPMR exploration — followed by the book's website and ordering information, marking the literal end of the trilogy's text.

## Core claims
- Campbell argues that many of the trilogy's core attitudes are not radical outliers but are already expressed, in different language, by widely respected Western scientists, artists, and thinkers — "once differences in personal expression are accounted for," fundamental truth looks the same from any direction.
- He claims the West is not actually "spiritually bankrupt" but simply lacks a widely acceptable cultural mode for expressing spiritual values; the spiritual values that are culturally present have been reduced to "effete slogans" attached to dominant religious dogma.
- He argues religious values are largely distinct from, and often have little to do with, spiritual values — true in both East and West.
- He diagnoses a widespread Western (and especially scientific) belief trap he calls "arrogance by assumed omniscience": the assumption that what one's peer group has not directly experienced must be delusion, and that the delusional always believe themselves non-delusional.
- He cites the historical mockery of the young Einstein's relativity theory by established scientists as a case of this same trap in action.
- He restates that a successful Big TOE (as opposed to a "little PMR-only TOE") logically requires at least one assumption that looks "mystical" only from the limited vantage of PMR; his own Big TOE rests on exactly two assumptions — the existence of primordial consciousness (AUO) and the well-understood Fundamental Process of evolution — from which all of reality, including PMR, is said to flow.
- He claims personal growth (raising the quality of one's consciousness, i.e., lowering its entropy) is the entire point of existence, and frames this as a "law of being": grow or die, since stability is found only in growth and stasis is the first sign of decay.
- He argues wisdom cannot be transferred — it can only come from one's own experience, never from a book, guru, or chemical, and only the wise can recognize wisdom in others ("it takes one to know one").
- He argues that fear (the opposite of love) is the root of most personal problems and the chief inhibitor of raised consciousness quality, operating through an alliance of ego and intellect that constructs self-protective belief systems ("personal fantasy") to deny fear.
- He claims scientific methodology should not be abandoned but must "rise above" the superstition and belief that currently limits its vision, so that legitimate inquiry can extend into NPMR and consciousness rather than remaining trapped in PMR-only causality.
- He argues true education is generated from the inside out (self-discovery) and is fundamentally different from training or the "force-feeding [of] factual information," which he says is what most modern schooling has become.
- He states directly that he (the author) can only point, model, and provoke — he cannot cause the reader to integrate Big Truth, have the necessary experiences, or actualize potential; all of that is "entirely up to you."
- He claims that time/change in PMR does not diminish personal responsibility for growth, and that every choice, whether or not one is aware of participating in a "consciousness-quality learning lab," moves one along the Big Picture.
- He warns (referencing "anti-rats," defined earlier in Book 2, Chapter 2) that slackers and stragglers who let growth opportunities slip by are their primary prey.
- He asserts that Big Truth is universal, never changes, and is not hidden from a chosen few — it stares everyone in the face daily, but conscious awareness is dimmed by belief to the point that people wall themselves off from the experience needed to see it.
- He frames the reader's only real choice as how to make their next journey: in charge and driving, along for the ride, locked in the trunk, or dragged kicking and screaming at the end of a rope.
- He closes by extending an open invitation to future NPMR exploration, framing himself ("Uncle Tom") as an ongoing host/guide even after the book itself ends.

## Concepts
- **Big TOE** — the author's own "Theory Of Everything," built (he says) from just two assumptions: primordial consciousness (AUO) and the Fundamental Process of evolution; contrasted with a "little PMR-only TOE" that lacks a big-picture, seemingly mystical premise.
- **AUO** — the seemingly mystical assumption underlying the Big TOE: the existence of primordial consciousness, appearing mystical only from the limited perspective of PMR.
- **AUM** — referenced as the source consciousness one is a "chip from the old ... block" of; growth is what "links us to AUM" and gives existence positive direction, purpose, and meaning.
- **Fundamental Process** — the well-known, understood process of evolution that, combined with AUO, is claimed to generate all of reality, including PMR, in the Big TOE model.
- **PMR** — physical matter reality; described here as a "virtual physical reality" experienced by individuated consciousness, and as a "little picture" that limits available solution space when treated as the whole of reality.
- **NPMR** — non-physical matter reality; framed as the "next higher level of causality," which personal exploration of consciousness is said to access; the destination Campbell points toward at the book's close.
- **Big Truth / Big Picture** — the universal, unchanging truth/perspective the trilogy pursues; said to be undiscovered by most not because it is hidden but because belief dims and limits conscious awareness of it.
- **Quality of consciousness / entropy reduction** — the author's metric for personal growth: raising consciousness quality is equated with lowering its entropy; framed as the central point and purpose of existence.
- **"Arrogance by assumed omniscience"** — Campbell's label for the belief trap in which a person or group assumes that what falls outside their own or their peers' direct experience cannot be real, treating this common-sense confidence as a substitute for actual investigation.
- **Conceptual center** — Campbell's term (used across the trilogy) for the mainstream of cultural/scientific belief that defends the status quo and treats common belief as if it were common knowledge.
- **Belief trap** — a self-imposed limitation created and defended by belief, which insulates the believer from additional knowledge or a larger truth; illustrated throughout the chapter's first quote-set.
- **Path of Knowledge** — the pursuit of Big Truth via a "long-term vigorous, open minded, and skeptical commitment"; the pursuit the reader is invited to continue after the book ends.
- **Individuated consciousness** — each person as a distinct unit of consciousness that creates a "local reality" with both shared (objective, PMR-rule-based) and personal (subjective, NPMR-rule-based) components.
- **Digital(ly based) consciousness** — mentioned as one of the claims the "conceptual center" mockingly rejects as "an obvious impossibility," listed alongside the ideas that no reality exists beyond PMR and that we are not individuated consciousness experiencing a virtual physical reality.
- **Anti-rats** — referenced (defined earlier, in Book 2, Chapter 2) as entities that primarily prey on "slackers and stragglers" who let growth opportunities pass unexplored.
- **AUMosaurus** — a whimsical coinage for an immense, herd-like aspect of AUM ("running with the herds of AUMosaurus thundering across the mental plains") used to illustrate limits that may be permanently beyond human capacity to grasp.
- **Boot-camp phase** — the early, more effortful stage of spiritual growth/learning, after which the author says growth should become "joyful and fun" rather than grim work.
- **Education vs. training** — Campbell's distinction between real education (learning generated from the inside out, a "being process") and training/force-fed factual information, which he says Western schooling has increasingly substituted for genuine education.
- **Fear-ego-intellect alliance** — the dynamic (elaborated earlier in Book 2, Chapter 8, and recapped here) in which fear and ignorance drive ego and intellect into a "mutual support alliance" that denies fear by constructing a defensive personal belief system, ultimately enslaving the self to maintaining that deceit.
- **Western-culture restriction** — Campbell's explicit editorial choice in this and the prior chapter to quote only Western, non-dogma-associated figures, justified as pragmatic communication strategy for his intended (Western) readership rather than a claim that non-Western wisdom is inferior.
- **The Great Experiment in Consciousness** — Campbell's name for the larger enterprise humanity is part of; invoked here in his warning that mankind's growing destructive capacity (atomic energy plus future genetic engineering and psychotropic pharmaceuticals) raises the stakes of understanding the Big Picture "before it prematurely terminates this portion of" the Experiment.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
The chapter's "concrete material" is its own curated set of quotations, each functioning as a case that a well-known Western figure independently expressed an idea consonant with the Big TOE. Campbell groups them under six headings with his own connecting commentary:

- **Belief and Self-Imposed Limitations.** Opens with the historical anecdote of early-1900s scientists mocking the young Einstein's "obviously incorrect" theory of relativity out of an inflated sense of their own omniscience — offered as a real-world instance of "arrogance by assumed omniscience." Quotes: Einstein ("He who joyfully marches in rank and file has already earned my contempt..."; "Common sense is the collection of prejudices acquired by age eighteen"); Al Lee ("People see the world not as it is, but as they are"); Ralph Waldo Emerson ("People see only what they are prepared to see"); Carl Jung ("If one does not understand a person, one tends to regard him as a fool"); Herbert Agar, *A Time for Greatness* (1942) ("The truth which makes men free is for the most part the truth which men prefer not to hear"); J. K. Galbraith ("The conventional view serves to protect us from the painful job of thinking"); Winston Churchill ("Men occasionally stumble over the truth..."); Jeremy W. Hayward (on beliefs penetrating to the "psychosomatic" level); Gerald G. Jampolsky (on belief systems distorting perception of "the world we see that seems so insane"); John C. Lilly ("Whatever one believes to be true either is true or becomes true in one's mind"); Peter McWilliams ("When we argue for our limitations, we get to keep them"); Michel Eyquem de Montaigne ("Nothing is so firmly believed as that which we least know"); Miguel de Unamuno (on the true meaning of "skeptic"); Sigmund Freud (on illusions saving us pain until they collide with reality); Clarence Darrow ("The pursuit of truth will set you free..."); Kierkegaard (on freedom of speech compensating for unused freedom of thought); Susan Taylor ("Thoughts have power; thoughts are energy..."); Goethe ("There is nothing more frightful than ignorance in action"); Demosthenes ("Nothing is easier than self-deceit..."); Dr. Robert Anthony ("If you don't change your beliefs, your life will be like this forever...").
- **Mysticism, Ideas from the Edge.** Campbell first voices, in quotation-marked pastiche, the dismissive "conceptual center" position that mysticism is a pacifier for "the dumb, the uneducated, the emotionally needy, and the gullible," then contrasts it with real quotes: Einstein ("The most beautiful thing we can experience is the mysterious..."; "There are only two ways to live your life. One is as though nothing is a miracle..."; "The important thing is not to stop questioning..."; and his remark on the "grand aim of all science" being to cover the most facts from the fewest axioms — which Campbell explicitly links to his own two-assumption Big TOE as an instance of "elegant simplicity"); Wallace Stevens (on meditations on "the text of life" being part of reality's structure); Ralph Waldo Emerson ("The foundations of a person are not in matter but in spirit").
- **The Process and Importance of Growth.** Frames "grow or die" as a law of being. Quotes: Kierkegaard ("The self is only that which is in the process of becoming"); Mark Twain ("What is the most rigorous law of our being? Growth..."); C. R. Lawton ("Unless you by to do something beyond what you have mastered, you will never grow" — verbatim as printed); Oliver Wendell Holmes ("One's mind, once stretched by a new idea, never regains its original dimensions"; also "What lies behind us and what lies before us are tiny matters compared to what lies within us"); Anne Morrow Lindbergh ("Only in growth, reform, and change... is true security to be found"); Einstein, linked by Campbell to the atomic age raising the urgency of humanity's spiritual growth ("The release of atomic energy has not created a new problem...") — Campbell adds that mankind has since devised further ways to destroy life (genetic engineering, psychotropic pharmaceuticals, the Information Age), raising the stakes for grasping the Big Picture before "The Great Experiment in Consciousness" is prematurely terminated.
- **Wisdom.** Recaps the trilogy's earlier claim that wisdom is non-transferable and derived only from experience, never from books, chemicals, or gurus. Quotes: Sandra Carey ("Never mistake knowledge for wisdom..."); Walter Lippman ("It requires wisdom to understand wisdom: the music is nothing if the audience is deaf"); Horace ("Wisdom is not wisdom when it is derived from books alone"); Einstein ("Try not to become a man of success, but rather try to become a man of value"); Michel de Montaigne ("We can be knowledgeable with other men's knowledge, but we cannot he wise with other men's wisdom" — verbatim as printed).
- **Fear, Ego, and Delusion.** Recaps the Book 2, Chapter 8 discussion of fear/ego/intellect/ignorance/delusion producing self-inflicted problems. Quotes: Emerson ("Fear defeats more people than any other one thing in the world"); Carl Jung (on the "inflated consciousness" being egocentric, unable to learn, and self-doomed); Einstein ("To understand the world, one must not be worrying about one's self").
- **Truth, Science, and Logic.** Argues scientific methodology must rise above (not be abandoned to) superstition/belief to extend into study of consciousness/NPMR. Quotes: five from Einstein — on the laws of mathematics and certainty vs. reality; on not making the intellect "our god"; on significant problems requiring a new level of thinking; "Not everything that can be counted counts..."; and on genuine religiosity coming through "rational knowledge" rather than fear of life/death and blind faith. Also John Dewey, *The Quest for Certainty* ("Every great advance in science has issued from a new audacity of imagination"); Charles Sanders Pierce (on the "sincere desire to find out the truth" as vital to science; source spells the name "Pierce"); Victor Daniels ("We must learn to tailor our concepts to fit reality, instead of trying to stuff reality into our concepts").
- **Education, Learning and Personal Growth.** Uses the "stuffing tissue in a bra" analogy for education-from-outside producing only "an educated falsie," and criticizes modern schooling for substituting force-fed facts and "functional literacy" for real education. Quotes: Eleanor Roosevelt ("One's philosophy is not best expressed in words; it's expressed in the choices one makes..."); Galileo ("You cannot teach a man anything; you can only help him find it within himself"); Einstein ("Education is what remains after one has forgotten everything he learned in school"; also "Great spirits have always found violent opposition from mediocrities..."); Oliver Wendell Holmes (on sailing toward "the port of heaven" against or with the wind, but never drifting or anchoring); Goethe ("Knowing is not enough; we must apply. Willing is not enough; we must do"; also "Everybody wants to be somebody; nobody wants to grow"); Bertrand Russell ("What we need is not the will to believe, but the wish to find out"); Richard Rohr (on finding courage to go down into oneself); Ralph Waldo Emerson ("All life is an experiment"); Robert Green Ingersoll ("Happiness is not a reward — it is a consequence...").
- **Closing meditation quotes.** Isadora Duncan, *My Life* ("What one has not experienced, one will never understand in print") — used to reinforce that Big Truth must be personally experienced, not just read. William Blake ("Truth can never be told so as to be understood, and not be believed") — used to argue the reader must move beyond mere belief to personal understanding.
- **The author's litany ("I have done what I can do...").** A repeated rhetorical structure ("I have shown you... but I cannot cause you to...") that walks through everything Campbell says he has provided (the Big Picture, potential, sights along the tour, a model of reality, pointed-out limitations, Big Truths, an explanation of spirituality/consciousness/love/paranormal interconnection, encouragement) paired each time with what only the reader can supply (integration, actualization, personal experience, courage, freedom, choices).
- **Final direct-address device.** The four-option challenge to the reader about how they will make their next journey (driving, passenger, locked in the trunk, or dragged kicking and screaming at the end of a rope), followed by the farewell "Bon voyage, amigo!" and the "Uncle Tom" sign-off inviting future exploration of NPMR, ending in the book's real-world website (http://www.My-Big-TOE.com, http://www.lightningstrikebooks.com) and a phone-order number (1-800-901-2122) — the trilogy's literal closing material.

## Thread
This chapter is the direct continuation of chapter 91's "good company" device, widening it from elite scientific/philosophical figures to a broader Western cultural roster, and it functions as the trilogy's rhetorical send-off. Structurally it closes Section 6 ("The End is Always the Beginning") and Book 3 by shifting from argument-building to direct address: after having built the Big TOE model across Sections 1-5, Campbell uses this chapter to (1) show the model's resonance with mainstream Western wisdom via quotation, and (2) explicitly hand responsibility for further growth, experience, and discovery back to the reader, since — consistent with the trilogy's repeated claim that wisdom/Big Truth cannot be transferred — no amount of further exposition can substitute for personal experience of NPMR and one's own consciousness. It references and depends on earlier material (the AUO/Fundamental Process framing from Section 2, the fear-ego-intellect discussion from Book 2 Chapter 8, and the "anti-rats" from Book 2 Chapter 2), tying the whole trilogy together at its close, and ends the book's actual text with real-world publisher/ordering information.
