# Ch 33 — Infinity Gets Too Big for Real Britches

## In one paragraph
Campbell's stated goal for this chapter is to make clear "the concept of, and necessity for, a finite AUM" — that AUM (the Absolute Unbounded Oneness / larger consciousness system of his model) does not need to be infinite, or anywhere close to it, to generate all the reality we can comprehend. He builds the case with scale arithmetic: stacking nested universes (each the size of an electron relative to the next) shows that even absurdly huge numbers like 10^400 or 10^(100 billion) remain "relatively infinitesimal" compared to true infinity, so "infinity is far too big for real things." He argues that actual infinity drags in logical inconsistencies (infinite processes needing infinite time and energy, "always unavailable to anything real"), so AUM need only be *relatively* or *apparently* infinite — "infinity" should be read as a metaphor, not a mathematical abstraction attached to a real noun. The same drill applied to time (seconds since the Big Bang vs. DELTA-t quanta) shows that tracking all time everywhere is trivial for a finite AUM and TBC with gazillions of spare capacity. Campbell then draws the epistemic moral: we comprehend only an infinitesimal fraction of the possibilities of existence; other reality systems with other rule-sets teem with unimaginable life-forms; and letting your beliefs and ignorance define the limits of possible reality is "bad science" that walls you into a conceptual prison.

## Core claims
- Campbell claims AUM is "a real thing, an apparently infinite something, but actually quite finite" — being infinite "is not a prerequisite for the position."
- Campbell argues true infinity is unnecessary and problematic for anything real: "the concept of infinity brings with it all kinds of logical inconsistencies such as infinite processes that require infinite time and infinite energy which are always unavailable to anything real." Relative/apparent infinity is sufficient.
- Campbell instructs the reader to treat "infinity" as a metaphor: "'Infinite' makes a handy mathematical concept but a very misleading adjective in front of a real, extant, noun."
- Campbell claims relative infinity is all that matters practically: if something is "relatively infinite to us, that is good enough to account for everything we can know" (his atom-in-a-cell comparison).
- Campbell asserts that even staggeringly large numbers (10^400, 10^(100 billion), 10^(trillion trillion)) are "not a tiny miniscule fraction of the way to infinity" — one can always raise any number to an exponent equal to itself.
- On time: fewer than 10^18 seconds have elapsed since the Big Bang, consuming only about 10^62 PMR DELTA-t time-quanta (one DELTA-t = 10^-44 s) — "a trivial number for an apparently infinite AUM to track."
- Campbell claims AUM maintains "a huge excess capacity that allows it to do, think, create, and be much, much more than we can imagine"; AUM and TBC have "multiple gazillions of margin (extra capacity) left over to grow into and play with."
- Campbell argues we exist in, and can comprehend, "only a tiny, tiny, minute, infinitesimal fraction of the possibilities for existence"; scaled-up and scaled-down universes could coexist with us within our finite 3D space-time.
- Such scaled universes would not evolve carbon-based humans (our matter suits our scale), but "weird stuff" with enough complexity, memory, and processing power could evolve intelligent life-forms there — "because Mother Nature is one aggressive babe!"
- Campbell claims our contemporary objective knowledge-base sets the limits of imagination: "all we can conjure up are various versions of ourselves because that is all we know"; little picture science can hypothesize only a minute fraction of the possibilities of creation.
- Campbell claims that "in a digital consciousness reality, anything that can be conceived is possible," and anything rational (rule-based) with enough potential and complexity will eventually evolve to a lower-entropy system of systems.
- Campbell asserts "we are not alone — reality is teeming with intelligent life-forms"; humans and the nonphysical entities of OS are "like one small school of fish in a gigantic ocean of sentient life," and learning to move awareness between dimensions reveals that diversity.
- Campbell claims the existence of other reality systems makes no practical difference "until one or more of these realities interact with your reality"; exploring them is interesting but does little to reduce your entropy, and "activities that do not contribute significantly to the positive evolution of your consciousness are not important."
- Campbell claims the Fundamental Process of evolution combined with the self-organizing potential of sufficiently complex digital systems "generates all the reality that is within our capacity to comprehend"; our universe is "only one special-case application of that process."
- Campbell claims the recursive, self-generating progression that spawns individuated sentient awareness resembles fractal generation, and that "in both consciousness and fractal systems, each part of the pattern carries the blueprint of the whole."
- Every reality evolves its own compatible systems and ecosystems; the space-time rule-set is appropriate only for a tiny subset of reality, and other rule-sets define consistent causalities "unimaginable to the residents of PMR."
- Campbell claims profitability is the criterion for existence: "If any system or subsystem is profitable (can lead to self-initiated entropy reduction), it probably exists."
- Campbell condemns belief-limited science: "Letting your ignorance define the limits of an acceptable reality is bad science; unfortunately, it is also the norm. That is why young scientists make most of the dramatic breakthroughs." Believing you know when you do not cuts you off from ever knowing.
- Campbell claims human cultures are "little more than communities of shared belief where common belief-blindness leads to erroneous conclusions that are universally held as obvious truth."
- Campbell rejects the scientists' assumption that alien life must resemble earth life as "a product of little picture thinking" whose arrogance "creates the walls of its own conceptual prison."
- Campbell claims PMR time appears continuous only because our smallest measurable times are vastly huge compared to one time quantum — like the imperceptible granularity of glass or steel from the macro view.
- Campbell notes that "a digitally simulated interactive virtual reality has few intrinsic limitations."

## Concepts
- **AUM** — Campbell's Absolute Unbounded Oneness, the aware consciousness that generates reality; here defined as "a real thing, an apparently infinite something, but actually quite finite," with huge excess capacity — "a Big Dude with a lot to do and keep track of," for whom being infinite is not a job requirement.
- **relative infinity / apparent infinity** — Campbell's replacement for actual infinity when talking about real things: something so large relative to us that it "is good enough to account for everything we can know." AUM need only be relatively, apparently infinite.
- **infinity (as metaphor)** — Campbell's ruling that "infinity" is a handy mathematical abstraction but "a very misleading adjective in front of a real, extant, noun"; in the reader's mind it should function as metaphor, not defined mathematical concept.
- **PMR** — Physical Matter Reality, our physical universe; a real thing, one reality among many; "the PMR learning lab" where the reader's current task is reducing entropy. Its matter and rule-set suit only our scale and niche.
- **NPMR / NPMR-N** — Nonphysical Matter Reality, and the specific subset NPMR-N; Campbell uses them as nested layers of existence: possibilities within our 3D space-time, in other 3D space-times, outside 3D space-time but within NPMR-N, and outside NPMR-N but within NPMR — each layer further beyond our comprehension.
- **OS (Our System)** — the reality system containing our PMR and its associated nonphysical entities; "only one of many evolving reality systems," a fact Campbell says has no practical importance until other realities interact with ours.
- **TBC (The Big Computer)** — the computational engine paired with AUM; like AUM it is real and finite, with "multiple gazillions of margin (extra capacity)" beyond everything tasked to it.
- **DELTA-t (time quantum)** — the fundamental increment of PMR time, one DELTA-t = 10^-44 s (numbers "resurrected" from Chapter 31); small enough to make nanoseconds appear absolutely continuous, yet the total count since the Big Bang (~10^62) is trivial for AUM to track.
- **granularity of time** — Campbell's claim that PMR time is quantized but appears continuous from the macro view, exactly as a tabletop of discrete particles appears solid because its granularity is imperceptible at our measuring scale.
- **digital consciousness** — the substrate of Campbell's reality model; "in a digital consciousness reality, anything that can be conceived is possible," and sufficiently complex digital systems self-organize toward lower entropy.
- **Fundamental Process of evolution** — the simple evolutionary process which, combined with self-organizing digital consciousness, "generates all the reality that is within our capacity to comprehend" and spawns individuated sentient awareness through recursive progression; it "seems to work everywhere."
- **entropy (in consciousness)** — Campbell's quality measure: evolution moves rule-based systems toward "more highly organized (lower entropy)" states; the reader's task is "reducing your entropy within the PMR learning lab"; profitability = capacity for "self-initiated entropy reduction."
- **rule-set** — the governing "physics" of a given reality; each dimension of existence (separate reality) functions under its own rule-set defining its own consistent causality; the space-time rule-set covers only a tiny subset of reality.
- **little picture / big picture** — Campbell's contrast between belief-bounded PMR-only thinking ("little picture science," "little picture thinking") and the wider view; exploring the Big Picture requires appreciating your limitations and applying open-minded skepticism.
- **open minded skepticism** — the only approach Campbell endorses for exploring the Big Picture "that makes sense and produces results"; the alternative is painting yourself into a corner "with broad strokes of belief and arrogance."
- **belief-blindness** — the condition in which knowledge outside your core beliefs becomes "entirely invisible to your self-limited vision"; human cultures are communities of shared belief where common belief-blindness yields errors held as obvious truth.
- **consciousness ecosystem** — the shared, teeming environment of sentient life-forms across dimensions; moving your awareness between dimensions reveals "the diversity of life-forms that share our consciousness ecosystem."
- **nested universes** — Campbell's scale construct: universes each standing to the next as an electron stands to our universe (a size-ratio of 10^100 per step), used to show how modest even a billion such nestings (10^(100 billion)) is next to infinity.
- **fractal similarity of consciousness** — Campbell's observation that consciousness generation resembles how "a simple geometric relationship generates an intricate fractal pattern," with each part of the pattern carrying "the blueprint of the whole."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Atom-in-a-cell scale analogy** — In PMR biology, "large biological cells must seem infinitely big to atoms." Campbell translates this: if our entire universe were one atom in an apparently infinite cell of something bigger, we "couldn't care less"; relative infinity is all that ever matters to the small.
- **The nested-universes thought experiment** — From sub-atomic particle to universe spans about 100 orders of magnitude (which he writes as 10^100). Stack four such universes (each an electron to the next) and the colossus is "only 400 orders of magnitude bigger than one of our own tiny electrons" — 10^400, and "everyone knows that 10 and 400 are not big numbers." A billion nested universes needs only 10^(100 billion). Even 10^(trillion trillion), or a number using every zero you could write in your lifetime, is not "a tiny miniscule fraction of the way to infinity" — you could always raise it to an exponent equal to itself. Shows infinity is "unnecessarily big... when you are talking about a real thing."
- **Time-since-the-Big-Bang count (numbers resurrected from Chapter 31)** — Fewer than 10^18 seconds since the Big Bang "went kaaaboom!"; only ~10^62 DELTA-t quanta (one DELTA-t = 10^-44 s); fewer than 10^27 nanoseconds, 10^36 nano-nano seconds (n-ns), 10^45 n-n-ns. Shows a ten n-n-n-n-ns-long DELTA-t makes PMR nanoseconds seem "absolutely continuous," while the total tally remains "a trivial number for an apparently infinite AUM to track."
- **Deep-sea thermal vents** — Life exists around high-temperature vents, under huge pressure, in total darkness at the ocean bottom: "Not as we are used to perhaps, but life just the same." Campbell's evidence that nature "tends to leave few empty places" and evolves something to populate whatever can be populated — so unimaginable realms may be populated too.
- **The set of possible sentient life-forms** — AUM is one member of that set, we are another; "bacteria and flat worms are two more." Evolution fits life-forms to their environment; an environment beyond our imagination will evolve forms and functions beyond our imagination.
- **Palm trees on mountain tops** — "Palm trees do not grow on high mountain tops for similar reasons": every reality evolves its own compatible systems and ecosystems; our critters, planet, and universe inhabit only the sliver of reality "appropriate for our kind and our purpose."
- **The tabletop granularity analogy** — Glass, steel, or wood appears solid and continuous though made of discrete particles with vast empty space between them; you'd need to measure at atomic scale to notice. Time is the same: continuous-seeming at macro view, quantized underneath.
- **The fractal analogy** — The recursive, multi-leveled, self-generating progression by which evolution plus self-organizing digital consciousness spawns thinking beings is "reminiscent of how a simple geometric relationship generates an intricate fractal pattern"; in both, each part carries the blueprint of the whole.
- **School of fish in a gigantic ocean** — Humans and the nonphysical entities of OS are "like one small school of fish in a gigantic ocean of sentient life" — his image for how minute our slice of the consciousness ecosystem is.
- **The wall / conceptual prison image** — Knowledge outside your core beliefs is invisible; "to view what lies beyond, you must first tear down the wall or at least some portion of it." The scientists' assumption that alien life must resemble earth's "creates the walls of its own conceptual prison."
- **Young scientists observation** — Because letting ignorance define acceptable reality is the norm, "young scientists make most of the dramatic breakthroughs" — offered as evidence of how belief calcifies vision.

## Thread
This chapter closes a load-bearing gap in Section 2's demystified mysticism: having built AUM, TBC, and the digital-consciousness reality in the preceding chapters, Campbell must show the model does not require an actual infinity (with its "logical inconsistencies") — a finite but apparently infinite AUM suffices, keeping the whole construct "real." It explicitly resurrects the DELTA-t and Big Bang numbers "made up in Chapter 31," extending the quantized-time argument from space-scale to time-scale. Along the way it widens the ontology — nested universes, other rule-sets, teeming sentient life beyond OS — while immediately disciplining the reader back to the practical task: none of it matters to reducing your entropy in the PMR learning lab. It also rehearses the recurring epistemic sermon (open-minded skepticism vs. belief-blindness) that frames the whole trilogy, and Campbell himself points forward to Chapters 9 and 10 of Book 3, "where parallel probable universes are discussed," as the place to revisit these capacity arguments.
