# Ch 62 — The Fundamentals of Experience and the Space-Time Rule-Set

## In one paragraph
Campbell works through what it means to say space-time is "made of" consciousness, rejecting the construction-material image (bricks and mortar) in favor of "constructs of consciousness" — deformations or specific organizations within a continuum, like lumps in a sheet, produced by placing constraints (rule-sets) on subsets of AUM's apparently infinite digital consciousness. He argues the common cultural instinct that PMR is caused by consciousness rather than the reverse is exactly backward, using a thought experiment about a newly formed, lifeless earth to expose the hidden assumption, and traces that same reversed assumption to the roots of scientific determinism. He then reframes creating PMR as simply imposing a rule-set that produces the experience of PMR physics, without AUM and TBC needing to manufacture actual mass or motion. The chapter closes by returning to the AI Guy thought experiment to explain how two levels of rule-set — a lower-level defining what an agent can do locally, and a higher-level defining its goals, values, and direction — are both necessary for functional, non-dysfunctional experience, illustrated by HAL from *2001: A Space Odyssey* as a cautionary case of an intelligence cut loose from its top-level purpose.

## Core claims
- Campbell claims space-time is a mental construct within the "apparently infinite consciousness of AUM," and that calling it "made out of" consciousness is misleading because that phrasing imports a PMR-trained, brick-and-mortar sense of construction materials as component parts.
- He argues space-time is not built out of chunks of consciousness but is "a specific configuration of consciousness" — realities (and individuated beings, including you) are "a limited implementation of consciousness developed to serve a particular evolutionary purpose (occupy an available niche) within the greater consciousness ecosystem," not literally made of consciousness the way a house is made of wood.
- Campbell distinguishes "constructs of consciousness" (deformations of, or specific organization within, a continuum — "lumps in the sheet") from "constructed of consciousness" (building something separate and more complex out of something more basic and different in kind); he says both individuated conscious beings and PMR are the former.
- He states this deformation/organization is effected "by placing constraints (including rule-sets) upon subsets of an apparently infinite digital consciousness, thus bounding them into individual existence relative to the whole."
- Campbell acknowledges the widespread intuitive notion that consciousness (or "soul," or "the mind of God") is the fundamental substance from which everything else is made, calling it good poetic metaphor but cautioning that poetic/ambiguous language becomes a "belief trap" if it confuses one's sense of reality.
- Campbell claims that the notion of spirit, mind, or consciousness being more fundamental than material objects "falls directly and naturally out of the intuition of millions of people — without the intervention and encouragement of religious dogma" — a common idea most people intuitively grasp but cannot rationalize; because "any answer is better than no answer" is a typically human trait, many have turned to religious or scientific belief to ease the discomfort and anxiety of not knowing how consciousness, purpose, and the larger reality interconnect.
- He argues the deeper puzzle — how PMR (rocky planets, school buses, atomic bombs, tapeworms, spouses, children) could be "made from" consciousness — is a dead-end question; the better question is how all of it could be "part of a larger consciousness construct," which he says Chapter 34 of the book resolves.
- Campbell claims that from a PMR-culture point of view his assertion (that we are mind, and PMR a virtual artifact of experience rather than an actual reality) looks "unsupportable, delusional, wacko, and just plain dumb," but only because that viewpoint is embedded in scientific/Western belief traps.
- He claims what "does compute" is that our interaction with space-time, bodies, and matter is "a constrained experience of consciousness" — PMR experience takes place within consciousness; saying "PMR is created by consciousness" is logically equivalent but more confusing because it wrongly implies manufacturing actual solid objects rather than manufacturing only the experience of them.
- Campbell reiterates his earlier division of an individuated consciousness's perceptions into internal (personal mind) and external (environment, other beings) categories, and argues that without physical experience there is no physical world for that being — PMR "disappears from our perception and thus ceases to be a part of our personal reality" — citing an entity in a sensory deprivation chamber as retaining full internal awareness despite losing external contact.
- He claims there is both a personal and a shared reality, and that — contrary to common wisdom — the personal reality is "the big primary one" while the shared reality is "the little virtual one," a 180-degree reversal that makes this paradigm shift hard for culturally "bass-ackwards" calibrated people to accept.
- Campbell distinguishes his claim from a "tree falls in the woods" semantic puzzle: losing all physical senses makes the world "undeniably disappear for you — but only for you," not for anyone else.
- He grants that people who believe in PMR's fundamental realness are "absolutely correct from their point of view" that the planet would keep existing even if every sentient being lost its senses — but says this rests on a hidden assumption that PMR is fundamental and causes our consciousness, rather than the other way around; as Campbell puts it, such people "think they are physical body-machines (digital or analog) experiencing a virtual consciousness instead of a consciousness experiencing a virtual body."
- Campbell illustrates the reversed-causality assumption with a personified, newly molten earth complaining about having to "evolve the people," worried they'll believe that if they all blinked simultaneously it would disappear — a comic dramatization of the belief he is attacking.
- He argues determinism is "birthed from this same erroneous paradigm": if physical experience is assumed to define the one universal reality, then consciousness and free will become theoretically impossible, and he says science is "securely stuck in that same belief trap."
- Campbell claims the truth is exactly opposite: in a virtual reality like PMR, what you experience is "a rule-based, derived, or computed reality, not a fundamental reality," while "consciousness is the one universal reality" in the "real world of fundamental existence."
- He instructs the reader to invert the cultural assumption: instead of seeing us as derived from PMR, see PMR as derived from our experience — just as we create the rule-set that defines AI Guy's experience, AUM and TBC can create our experience within TBC, and thereby create PMR, "without breaking a sweat," without needing to create actual mass or motion, only the experience of mass and motion.
- Campbell claims AUM and TBC need only provide a rule-set that "contains or subsumes our PMR physics (known and unknown)," and our experience under that rule-set will be an experience of PMR; he notes he uses "physics" throughout the trilogy in the broadest sense, to mean all science.
- Turning to AI Guy, Campbell recalls (from "near the end of Chapter 22" of the book) that rule-sets operate on two levels: (1) conditionals and algorithms defining what AI Guy can do at the lower, local, immediate level, and (2) additional higher-level constraints defining profitability and giving Big Picture progress a clear direction — the top-level rules that determine goals, values, and purpose.
- He argues that if AI Guy had few limits on self-modifying his lower-level rule-set, and could also freely redefine his own values via his higher-level rule-set, he would become "the sole master of his purpose, accountable to only himself," risking laziness, indolence, mania, depression, paranoia, cynicism, schizophrenia, or "running open loop" — losing up/down, right/wrong, good/bad, success/failure without top-level purpose and direction.
- Campbell compares such an ungoverned AI Guy to HAL from *2001: A Space Odyssey*, and warns "the same thing can happen to you" if you lose or weaken your connection to your own purpose, whether through ignorance or pharmacological, psychological, or biochemical influence; he adds that our own top-level rule-set is not very constraining, so we should heed its few guiding rules or risk ending up "like Hal — or worse."
- He extends the point with a boat analogy: a boat needs a rudder/motor/paddle/sail and a destination — one without the other is equally dysfunctional; "little picture tunnel vision" of this kind is common enough in the culture to be considered normal, even desirable, and fear often distorts our innate sense of core direction and purpose to serve immediate ego needs.
- Campbell's prescribed remedy: "1) Repair the rudder, 2) set a destination, and 3) fire up and engage the engine," continually re-engineering all three toward optimal long-term profitability, so that as entropy decreases the purpose animating the journey grows bigger than the little picture that birthed it.
- He closes by acknowledging we cannot modify all the rule-sets that define our existence — we are stuck with PMR's actual physics while extant in PMR, and with a spiritual need to improve consciousness quality — but reminds the reader that as "chips from the old AUM block" we have license and capacity to understand the Big Picture, be an active, aware participant in our evolution, and be a "power-user" of, as well as an experiencer within, our larger reality, viewable through "the Big Picture window of our Big TOE."

## Concepts
- **AUM** — the "apparently infinite consciousness" within which space-time is a mental construct; the source whose subsets are bounded by constraints/rule-sets into individuated existence; also the entity (with TBC) that creates PMR by imposing a rule-set on our consciousness.
- **PMR (physical-matter reality)** — our physical/space-time reality; described here as a "constrained experience of consciousness," a rule-based, derived, computed reality (not a fundamental one) that arises when AUM/TBC impose a rule-set subsuming PMR physics on our consciousness.
- **TBC** — paired with AUM as the agent/mechanism that "can create our experience," providing the rule-set that produces PMR experience without needing to manufacture actual mass or motion.
- **Space-time rule-set** — the specific configuration of constraints on consciousness that produces the mental construct of space-time; the chapter's titular subject, functioning as the "rule-based, derived, or computed reality" we call PMR.
- **Consciousness ecosystem** — Campbell's framing for the greater context within which realities and individuated beings are "a limited implementation of consciousness developed to serve a particular evolutionary purpose (occupy an available niche)."
- **Constructs of consciousness vs. constructed of consciousness** — Campbell's key semantic distinction: "constructs of" means deformations/specific organization within a continuum (lumps in a sheet — process-wise, not building a separate thing from a different, more basic material), while "constructed of" wrongly implies building something more complex out of something more basic and different in nature; both PMR and individuated beings are constructs of, not constructed of, consciousness.
- **Lumps in the sheet** — Campbell's continuum analogy for how individuated beings and PMR relate to consciousness: they are deformations or organized regions within one continuous "sheet," not separate things built from sheet-material.
- **Rule-set** — a set of constraints imposed on consciousness (or on an agent's behavior) that bounds it into individual existence or defines its possible experience/interactions; discussed here at the level of space-time physics and, later, at the two levels governing AI Guy.
- **Constraints** — the mechanism (including rule-sets) by which subsets of AUM's infinite digital consciousness are deformed/organized and thereby "bound... into individual existence relative to the whole."
- **Individuated existence** — the bounded, separate existence of a being or reality produced by constraints placed on the wider consciousness continuum.
- **Belief traps** — Campbell's caution that poetic substitutions for "consciousness" (e.g., "soul," "the mind of God") become traps if they confuse one's sense of reality with ambiguous, abstract symbolic language.
- **Personal reality vs. shared reality** — Campbell's distinction between an individual's own internal perceptual reality (which he says is "the big primary one") and the reality shared with others (which he says is "the little virtual one") — a reversal of common cultural assumption.
- **Sensory deprivation chamber** — used as the concrete case establishing that losing external/environmental perception does not eliminate an entity's internal awareness or its potential; only external (shared) reality is lost.
- **Determinism** — the doctrine Campbell says is "birthed" from the erroneous assumption that physical experience defines the one universal reality; once that assumption is made, consciousness and free will "appear to be theoretically impossible," and he says science is "securely stuck" defending it.
- **Free will** — named as the casualty of the determinist paradigm; implicitly restored once the causal arrow is reversed (consciousness as fundamental, PMR as derived/experiential).
- **Virtual reality** — Campbell's characterization of what PMR actually is: a reality in which "what you experience is a rule-based, derived, or computed reality, not a fundamental reality"; creating a virtual reality means "creating experience," not manufacturing mass or motion.
- **Two-level rule-sets** — the model (recalled from "near the end of Chapter 22" of the book) in which an agent like AI Guy operates under (1) a lower-level rule-set of conditionals/algorithms defining what he can do locally, and (2) a higher-level rule-set of constraints defining profitability, goals, values, and purpose, which together let him gain and learn from experience.
- **Higher-level rule-set** — the top-level constraints that define an agent's profitability criteria, goals, values, and purpose, giving "the arrow of Big Picture progress a clear direction"; described as "not particularly constraining" for humans, but essential to heed.
- **Lower-level rule-set** — the local conditionals and algorithms defining what an agent (e.g., AI Guy) can do at the immediate level of awareness; may be modified "within narrow limits" by the agent itself to optimize learning efficiency.
- **Open loop** — an electronics-engineering term Campbell borrows to describe an agent (AI Guy, or a person) operating without the direction of a higher-level, purpose-giving rule-set — "he would not know up from down, right from wrong, good from bad, success from failure."
- **Entropy** — invoked in the boat analogy's close: "as your entropy decreases, the purpose that animates your journey will eventually grow to be bigger than the little picture that gave it birth," linking rudder/destination/engine action to the trilogy's recurring entropy-reduction goal.
- **Big Picture** — the larger, purpose-giving frame of reference an agent needs (via the higher-level rule-set) to avoid "little picture tunnel vision"; something the reader is urged to actively understand and engage with.
- **Big TOE** — the book's own titular "theory of everything," here figured as a "window" through which we can view the larger reality even when we are not directly experiencing it.
- **Chips from the old AUM block** — Campbell's phrase for individuated beings' status as part and parcel of AUM's original consciousness, giving them "the license and capacity to soar" and to understand the Big Picture.
- **Power-user** — Campbell's term for the aspirational, active relationship one can have to the larger reality: not merely an experiencer within it, but an aware, capable participant/user of it.
- **Boat-without-a-rudder analogy** — Campbell's image for dysfunction: a boat without steering/propulsion (rudder, motor, paddle, sail) is dysfunctional, but so is a boat with propulsion and no destination; both a mechanism for progress and a direction/purpose are required.
- **Profitability** — the criterion defined by the higher-level rule-set, giving "the arrow of Big Picture progress a clear direction"; the standard toward which the reader is told to "continually re-engineer" rudder, destination, and engine.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The house-of-wood vs. tree-is-wood contrast** — Campbell contrasts "houses can be made of wood" (construction, component parts) with "trees...simply are wood" (constitutive identity) to make vivid the difference between "constructed of" and "constructs of" consciousness; he says beings and PMR are the tree case, not the house case.
- **The newly molten earth thought experiment** — Campbell pictures the earth as "a newly spun-off blob of molten minerals and elements" with no sentient beings anywhere, PMR "humming along nicely," used to grant that PMR-realists are "absolutely correct from their point of view" that PMR doesn't logically depend on anyone's existence — while exposing the hidden assumption behind that view (that PMR is fundamental and causes consciousness, not vice versa).
- **The complaining earth's monologue** — a comic first-person aside ("Oh no! Not the people!... Give them a cognitive inch, and before long they will believe that if they all happen to blink at the same time I would simply disappear... I wish I could quit this job of being physical reality") satirizing the anxiety of a "fundamental PMR" that fears people discovering the truth about causality.
- **The sensory deprivation chamber case** — cited to show that an entity cut off from external/environmental perception "retains the full awareness and the full potential of its internal environment," supporting the claim that personal (internal) reality is primary and shared (external) reality is derivative/virtual.
- **The "tree falls in the woods" distinction** — Campbell explicitly denies his argument is that familiar semantic puzzle: he insists that if you lose all physical senses, the world genuinely disappears "for you" — but importantly, only for you, not for others — clarifying the personal/shared reality claim against a likely misreading.
- **AI Guy's two-level rule-set (recalled)** — Campbell revisits his running AI Guy thought experiment (introduced, per the text, "near the end of Chapter 22" of the book) to illustrate lower-level (local conditionals/algorithms) versus higher-level (profitability/goals/values/purpose) rule-sets, and how AI Guy learns from experience within and via limited self-modification of the lower-level rule-set.
- **The ungoverned-AI-Guy scenario** — a hypothetical in which AI Guy faces few limits on modifying both his lower- and higher-level rule-sets: Campbell walks through the possible failure modes — becoming "sole master of his purpose, accountable to only himself," turning lazy/unfocused/indolent, or slipping into mania, depression, paranoia, cynicism, or schizophrenia, "running open loop."
- **HAL from *2001: A Space Odyssey*** — invoked as the recognizable cultural case of "nefarious renegade computers turning on their human creators," which Campbell uses to warn the reader that losing connection to one's own top-level purpose (through ignorance, drugs, psychological or biochemical influence) risks the same fate — "or worse" — for a person, not just a fictional AI.
- **The rudderless/destinationless boat** — a paired image: boats without rudder/motor/paddle/sail are dysfunctional, but boats with propulsion and no destination are equally useless; used to argue that "little picture tunnel vision" (common enough in the culture to seem normal or even desirable) results from fear distorting our innate sense of direction to serve immediate ego needs.
- **The three-step corrective action** — Campbell's concrete prescription: "1) Repair the rudder, 2) set a destination, and 3) fire up and engage the engine," to be continually re-engineered in real time toward optimal long-term profitability.
- **"Room with a view" closing gag** — Campbell ends by having the narrator taunt AI Guy ("Hey, AI Guy! Look at us; we got a room with a view! Naaah nah-nah naaah nah!"), a playful capstone asserting humans' capacity to view the larger reality through the "Big Picture window" of the Big TOE — a vantage AI Guy, as a bounded construct, may lack.

## Thread
Positioned in Section 4 ("Solving the Mystery"), this chapter follows directly from Chapter 61's claim that PMR is a logically required design solution (a space-time rule-set for hybrid, clean-slate beings) and now works out what "experience" itself is and how a rule-based virtual reality can feel solid and physical without being fundamentally physical. It reverses the intuitive PMR-is-fundamental/consciousness-is-derived causal arrow, ties that reversal to a diagnosis of scientific determinism, and reframes "creating PMR" as merely imposing a rule-set — setting up the trilogy's next move (explicitly deferred to Chapter 63) of explaining exactly how that imposition works ("space-time starts with a bang"). The chapter also folds in the AI Guy two-level rule-set material (first developed, per the text, "near the end of Chapter 22" of the book) to give a functional, cautionary account — via HAL — of what happens to an intelligence that loses its higher-level, purpose-giving rule-set, linking the abstract metaphysics back to practical guidance about purpose, direction, and entropy-reduction in the reader's own life.
