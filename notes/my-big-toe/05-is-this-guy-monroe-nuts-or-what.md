# Ch 5 — Is This Guy Monroe Nuts, or What?

## In one paragraph
This short autobiographical chapter carries Campbell from graduate school (almost twenty-seven, in school continuously since age five) into his first job — applying classical physics and mathematics to electromechanical and electromagnetic systems simulation, as a civilian physicist in a conservative military organization in 1972 — and stages the pivotal encounter that will lead him to Robert Monroe. Campbell reports that his meditation practice had matured to the point where the mantra itself became unnecessary — any two-syllable "-ing" nonsense word worked, proving to him the technique was "nothing but science and technique," not magic. He could now drop instantly into deep meditation as "disembodied point consciousness adrift in the void," even at his desk, and used the state to find solutions to work problems. His boss, Bill Yost, hands him Monroe's *Journeys Out Of The Body*; Campbell reads it as an intriguing but unverifiable claim — a wild concept he can neither confirm nor deny, and therefore initially dismisses as irrelevant. The chapter ends with Yost inviting him to go meet Monroe in person, which Campbell accepts precisely to judge for himself whether Monroe is "crazy or sane, honest or a hustler, delusional or rational."

## Core claims
- Campbell claims that his assigned "super-secret" mantra had no special power: his own "research and experimentation" showed that any two-syllable nonsense word ending in "ing" (a resonant sound) worked just as well.
- Campbell argues that meditation is a method, not mysticism: a technique for "controlling thoughts by filling the otherwise active mind with fluff" — "nothing mystical or magical here… no bananas or hankies were required."
- Campbell claims the mantra eventually became an obstacle — it "seemed to get in the way and slow me down" — so he dropped it, and could thereafter enter and exit the meditative state instantly.
- Campbell claims that in this state he was "disembodied point consciousness adrift in the void," gone completely from ordinary reality "with no residual awareness of their reality," and that he used this state practically at work to find solutions to problems.
- Campbell describes Monroe's *Journeys Out Of The Body* as a diary-style, "this is what happened to me" account in which Monroe claimed hard evidence for the reality of the out-of-body experience, presented "matter-of-factly with no theory or belief system attached."
- Campbell characterizes the out-of-body claim as "a wild concept — a more or less independent reality reachable only through the mind."
- Campbell positions himself as open-minded but not gullible: prior experience with "functional altered states of consciousness" made him more open than the average scientist, but "I knew what was real to me, and my measurement data (experience) included none of that."
- Campbell's initial epistemic verdict on Monroe: with no way to judge the veracity of the claims, the book "could never be logically confirmed or denied — and therefore was irrelevant." (This stance is what the invitation to meet Monroe overturns.)
- Yost's challenge — "consider what it would mean if it were true… What does it logically imply if it is true — if the evidence is real and not made up?" — frames the question the rest of the trilogy pursues.

## Concepts
- **mantra** — In Campbell's usage, a two-syllable nonsense word repeated to occupy the mind during meditation. He reports discovering that any resonant "-ing"-ending nonsense word works as well as the "given super-secret mantra," and that the mantra is ultimately dispensable once the state is familiar.
- **meditation** — For Campbell here, a learned, non-mystical technique of "controlling thoughts by filling the otherwise active mind with fluff"; with practice it becomes a state one can enter and leave "in an instant," useful even for problem-solving at work.
- **altered states of consciousness** — States beyond ordinary waking awareness; Campbell says his prior experience with "functional altered states of consciousness" made him more open-minded than the average scientific type when reading Monroe.
- **point consciousness** — Campbell's description of his deep meditative condition: "disembodied point consciousness adrift in the void," entirely removed from the physical world with no residual awareness of it.
- **the void** — The contentless space in which Campbell's point consciousness drifts during deep meditation; to observers he appeared like "a dead body" or deep in thought.
- **out-of-body experience** — The subject of Monroe's *Journeys Out Of The Body*: the claim, backed (per Monroe) by "hard evidence," that awareness can leave the body — implying "a more or less independent reality reachable only through the mind."
- **evidence** — The hinge of the chapter: Monroe claims "hard evidence" of the OBE's realness; Campbell counts only his own "measurement data (experience)" as real to him, and Yost's key question is what it logically implies "if the evidence is real and not made up."
- **open-minded skepticism** — Not named as a term but enacted: Campbell is "probably more open minded than the average scientific type, but not gullible," refusing both to condemn Monroe as rubbish and to believe him, pending a way to judge veracity.
- **reality** — Used here in Campbell's personal-empiricist sense: what is real to him is what appears in his own experiential "measurement data"; Monroe's account, if true, would mean "a new aspect of reality opens up that I have never before considered."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Dropping the mantra (self-experiment)** — Campbell's "little research and experimentation" showing any two-syllable nonsense word ending in "ing" worked as well as his assigned secret mantra; he concludes the mantra has no intrinsic magic and eventually abandons it entirely as an impediment. Campbell says this shows meditation is science and technique, not mysticism.
- **Meditating at his desk** — Campbell would meditate at work to "find solutions, and return without anyone suspecting"; colleagues who tried to engage him found it "as if engaging a dead body," earning him a reputation for eccentricity — "unusual powers of concentration or an unusual ability to sleep while sitting up — nobody could tell which." Shows the state's depth (total disengagement from physical reality) and its practical utility.
- **The book toss** — Bill Yost, Campbell's boss ("smart — no frills, no BS, no hidden agenda, no tact, — an engineer through and through"), tosses him *Journeys Out Of The Body* by Robert A. Monroe with only "Read it… and tell me what you think." Campbell notes: "When your boss says, 'Read it,' you just read it, you do not ask why."
- ***Journeys Out Of The Body* (Monroe's book)** — Described as configured like a diary; Monroe "claimed to have collected hard evidence in support of the reality of the out-of-body experience," laid out matter-of-factly with no theory or belief system attached. It presents the "wild concept" of an independent reality reachable only through mind.
- **The debrief conversation with Yost** — Campbell's rapid-fire rhetorical verdict ("Is this guy (Monroe) nuts; is he trying to sell books to the gullible; or is he for real?") and his fear that his openness was "a political mistake" in a conservative 1972 military workplace where he was "a longhaired, wild-eyed kid-physicist." Yost agrees it is wild but presses the conditional: what would it logically imply if true? The conversation ends unresolved.
- **The invitation** — Weeks later Yost invites Campbell to join a group from work driving "about forty-five minutes" out of town to meet Monroe that Friday. Campbell accepts: "I would really like to meet this guy and see if he is crazy or sane, honest or a hustler, delusional or rational." Yost: "Me too… me too!"

## Key quotes
- "There was nothing mystical or magical here, only a method of controlling thoughts by filling the otherwise active mind with fluff, nothing but science and technique — no bananas or hankies were required." — Campbell demystifying his own meditation practice after discovering any resonant nonsense word works as a mantra.
- "That I was disembodied point consciousness adrift in the void — gone completely from their world with no residual awareness of their reality — was my secret." — how deep his desk-side meditations went while colleagues assumed he was thinking.
- "It was a wild concept — a more or less independent reality reachable only through the mind." — Campbell's first reaction to the central claim of Monroe's *Journeys*.
- "I was probably more open minded than the average scientific type, but not gullible. I knew what was real to me, and my measurement data (experience) included none of that." — his epistemic starting position toward the OBE claim.
- "Is this guy (Monroe) nuts; is he trying to sell books to the gullible; or is he for real?" — the title question, put rhetorically to Yost after reading the book.
- "If his story is for real, if you take him at face value, a new aspect of reality opens up that I have never before considered. That would be a definite Wow!" — Campbell registering the stakes if Monroe is genuine.
- "Think about that... What does it logically imply if it is true — if the evidence is real and not made up?" — Yost's challenge, the conditional question that frames Campbell's whole subsequent investigation.
- "…something that could never be logically confirmed or denied — and therefore was irrelevant." — Campbell's initial dismissal of the book before the invitation to meet Monroe.
- "I would really like to meet this guy and see if he is crazy or sane, honest or a hustler, delusional or rational." — Campbell accepting the trip to see Monroe, setting up the next chapters.

## Thread
Within Section 1, "Delusion or Knowledge," this chapter is the turning point of Campbell's autobiographical setup: it follows his student-era Transcendental Meditation training and mastery of altered states (chapters 3–4) and converts a private mental discipline into the beginning of a research trajectory. It establishes his credentials as a working physicist and calibrated skeptic, demystifies meditation technique, and introduces the two people who redirect his life — Bill Yost, the straight-shooting engineer boss, and (at one remove) Robert Monroe. The chapter deliberately ends on a cliffhanger — the drive out to meet Monroe — setting up the direct engagement with Monroe and the out-of-body research that the following chapters recount, and planting Yost's question ("what does it logically imply if it is true?") as the seed of the entire Big TOE project.

## Connections
- Ch 3–4: the meditation practice whose maturation is reported here (mantra learned, then discarded) begins in the preceding autobiographical chapters.
- Ch 6 onward (Section 1): the meeting with Monroe teed up here, and the ensuing laboratory/OBE experiences, continue the "Delusion or Knowledge" narrative and supply the experiential "measurement data" Campbell demands in this chapter.
- Ch 17+ (Section 2, "Mysticism Demystified"): this chapter's "science and technique, no bananas or hankies" stance toward meditation prefigures the trilogy's programmatic demystification of mystical practice.
