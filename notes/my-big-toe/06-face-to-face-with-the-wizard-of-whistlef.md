# Ch 6 — Face to Face with the Wizard of Whistlefield

## In one paragraph
Campbell narrates the Friday-evening trip in which he and eleven co-workers drive to Whistlefield, Robert Monroe's five-hundred-acre estate, to meet "Mr. Out-of-Body" in person. Campbell sizes Monroe up on the spot — wealthy, sane, conservative, an engineer's personality, "genuinely interested in real science" rather than money or fame — which dismantles Campbell's working hypothesis that Monroe might be a lunatic or a huckster duping the gullible. Monroe recounts that his out-of-body experiences "just started happening" about fifteen years earlier, that professionals found him rock-solid sane, and that his evidential data consists mostly of remote viewing under controlled circumstances with a parapsychologist. The chapter's pivot is Monroe's open challenge for "hard-core science and engineering types" to help him do science acceptable to other scientists: Campbell (a physicist) and Dennis Mennerich (an electrical engineer) volunteer, striking a deal to trade legitimate scientific research for Monroe's teaching of altered states. Along the way Campbell paints his own younger self — hyper-rational, emotionally detached, a "perpetual outsider" musing on fear-driven "cultural insanity" — and closes with the comic ordeal of reaching Monroe by phone and the first small hints (Monroe's foreknowledge that Dennis could come Thursday) of Monroe "knowing things paranormally." Campbell flags this evening as the moment his life "was about to take a sharp turn."

## Core claims
- Campbell claims his first face-to-face assessment showed Monroe was "no poor raving lunatic": the classy estate and Monroe's success as a businessman and cable-company CEO made "duping the gullible for their money" an unlikely motivation — though Campbell reserved final judgment until meeting the man.
- Campbell claims Monroe's out-of-body experiences began spontaneously ("It just happened... about fifteen years ago for no apparent reason"), that Monroe initially feared he was going nuts, and that psychologists, psychiatrists, and a parapsychologist all found him "rock-solid sane."
- Campbell claims Monroe had "inadvertently stumbled into an altered state of consciousness that gave him access to a larger reality, producing some amazing evidential data under controlled circumstances with the parapsychologist."
- Campbell claims Monroe wanted legitimacy, not recognition, money, or fame — he "desperately wanted to legitimize what had spontaneously happened to him," to "remove the stigma of nutty and replace it with the approval of an accepting science," and was willing to put his money where his mouth was.
- Campbell argues that scientists, "contrary to their own press reports, are mostly just sheep of a different sort": all credibility flows from peer review, and notable peers would treat altered states and out-of-body as "intellectual leprosy" — which is why established scientists would never join Monroe's fringe lab and why Monroe craved respectability.
- Campbell claims that much culturally normal behavior is "cultural insanity" — absurdly illogical attitudes and actions ranging from benign ("makeup urgency") to terribly destructive — and tentatively concludes its key motivators are fear-based and emotionally driven.
- Campbell characterizes his younger self as a born "perpetual outsider" who could not relate to fear or emotion, and argues the outsider's view is more objective and impartial — an advantage for a scientist, for whom "nothing was more important than logical clarity and objectivity"; he also concedes (in retrospect) that graduate school had skewed his notion of normalcy and that the emotional people "were the ones who were actually normal."
- Campbell claims the deal struck was an exchange: he and Dennis would help Monroe do "legitimate scientific research" in return for Monroe teaching them what he knew about out-of-body and altered states of consciousness.
- Campbell claims that Monroe habitually knew things paranormally — the first instance being Monroe's confident insistence that Dennis would be free the coming Thursday, which turned out true only because Dennis's conflict vanished "just this morning"; Campbell says Monroe "was always a step ahead and usually right. Unlike me, he did not need to wait for the facts."
- Campbell claims he kept an exit ready — if Monroe turned out not to be interested in real science, he would "quickly and politely bow out" — establishing his skeptical-scientist posture toward what follows.
- Campbell claims this evening changed his life forever: "Strange and stranger (all carefully scientific of course) was about to become as common as air."

## Concepts
- **out-of-body (OOB) experience** — The phenomenon Monroe is famous for; in this chapter it is the state in which one goes somewhere "without taking your body along, as it were." Monroe's began spontaneously about fifteen years before this meeting, worried him at first, and became something he experimented with, recorded, and eventually controlled.
- **remote viewing** — Defined in Monroe's own words when Campbell asks: "Obtaining information paranormally by going somewhere in the out-of-body state to collect the target information — without taking your body along, as it were." Monroe says his evidential data was "remote viewing sorts of things for the most part."
- **altered states of consciousness** — The broader research subject: non-ordinary states such as Monroe's, into which he "inadvertently stumbled." Monroe's nascent facility was to be a lab "dedicated to the study of altered states of consciousness," and studying altered states is what Campbell had wanted to do "for a year or more, but did not know how to start."
- **larger reality** — Campbell's phrase for what Monroe's altered state gave him access to; the source of the "amazing evidential data" produced under controlled circumstances with the parapsychologist. (Used here without further elaboration; it becomes a load-bearing term later in the trilogy.)
- **evidential data** — Data supporting the reality of the paranormal experience; Campbell asks Monroe directly what evidential data he has, and Monroe answers that most of it is in his book — remote-viewing results obtained under controlled circumstances.
- **cultural insanity** — Campbell's coinage for "those absurdly illogical attitudes and actions that our culture considers normal." Some (like "makeup urgency") are entirely benign; "others range from mildly dysfunctional to terribly destructive." Campbell's tentative conclusion: its key motivators are fear-based and emotionally driven.
- **peer review as sheep behavior / intellectual leprosy** — Campbell's claim that "all credibility flows from peer review" and that scientists are "mostly just sheep of a different sort," so notable peers would treat altered states and OOB "as intellectual leprosy"; this "brick wall of closed-mindedness" is what Monroe had run into and why established scientists would not staff his lab.
- **the outsider's view** — Campbell's self-description: "born, it seemed, a perpetual outsider," different but "not better or worse"; he argues "Outsiders have a more objective and impartial view," which suits a scientist whose highest values are logical clarity and objectivity.
- **knowing things paranormally** — Monroe's habit, first glimpsed here in his certainty that Dennis could come Thursday; Campbell says he "would eventually get used to Monroe being distracted and knowing things paranormally."
- **Whistlefield** — Monroe's estate: "five-hundred acres of lakes, forests and fields," a large country manor on a hill, half a dozen horses, a barn, two small lakes; the "Wizard of Whistlefield" of the chapter title is Monroe himself.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The Friday caravan to Whistlefield** — Twelve co-workers (from an organization of about five hundred, mostly "very conservative... professional technical types") pile into three cars to meet Monroe. Campbell uses their nervous jabbering, fear of the dogs, and make-up fumbling as live specimens of emotionally driven behavior, and their unexpected open-mindedness forces him to hypothesize "bias errors in my analysis algorithms" and plan to debug his assumptions — an early display of him treating his own mind as an instrument to be calibrated.
- **The Spock self-portrait** — Campbell says he thought "Spock was normal while the rest of the Enterprise crew were hopeless, eternally lucky, mush-heads," and only later realized the emotional people were the normal ones — used to show how graduate school and isolation among hard-core scientists had "nudged my vision of normalcy a tad off center."
- **The dogs test** — Two Dalmatians and a German shepherd charge the cars; everyone stays inside until "the friendliness of the dogs could be verified," while Campbell pops out immediately because the dogs were "obviously friendly." He uses the episode to contrast rational reading of evidence with fear-driven caution ("neither brave nor foolish"), and notes he felt more centered among "rational beings that knew what they were about."
- **"Makeup urgency"** — All three women reflexively fumble for fresh make-up before meeting Monroe; Campbell's standing example of a benign form of cultural insanity, "blatantly irrational behavior" that he observes with amusement and careful mental notes.
- **First reading of Monroe** — Monroe steps out momentarily tentative, "like a captive alien or a strange animal at the zoo," then greets everyone "like an experienced politician." He is not the Col. Sanders Southern gentleman Campbell half expected but "more like the dogs than the house"; Campbell privately casts him as "Santa Monroe... a jolly old elf," and Monroe — with a knowing impish grin — demands "What are you smiling at?", giving Campbell the momentary feeling Monroe had read his mind.
- **The direct interrogation** — Campbell's rapid-fire questions ("How and when did you first go out-of-body?" / "How did you react?" / "What sort of evidential data do you have?" / "What exactly is remote viewing?") elicit Monroe's origin story: spontaneous onset ~15 years prior, initial fear of insanity, clean bills of mental health from psychologists, psychiatrists, and a parapsychologist, controlled remote-viewing evidence, and encouragement to pursue, record, and control the experiences.
- **The deal on the deck** — At the future lab, Monroe issues his challenge for "hard-core science and engineering types" with good credentials to help him "do real and proper science that would be acceptable to other scientists." Campbell's hand shoots up ("a reflexive act conditioned by over twenty consecutive years as a student"); he offers: teach me altered states and I'll help you do legitimate scientific research. Dennis Mennerich, an electrical engineer with a master's degree, makes the same offer. After a long pause and a credentials check, Monroe: "OK... You've got a deal!" Campbell notes Monroe would have preferred established scientists but that reputations to protect keep such people "far away from the safety of the crowd."
- **The telephone saga** — Weeks of no answer at the scrawled number, guessing whether a digit was a three or an eight, an anonymous answering machine, an unlisted number, an unreturned message — then a live "hello" and a Monroe who apparently does not remember Campbell at all ("What? Physicist? What kind of physicist are you?"). Campbell uses it to show Monroe's distracted manner and to set up the question "is his mind a little loose?" — answered in retrospect: "His mind was not loose, he was always a step ahead and usually right."
- **Monroe's foreknowledge about Dennis** — Monroe insists both men "just come on" Thursday at 7 with no confirmation needed. Dennis reveals that until that very morning he had a conflict: "Had you told me this yesterday or anytime last week, I would have told you that I couldn't have made it... But just this morning, things changed." Campbell presents this as the first datum of Monroe "knowing things paranormally."
- **The motorcycle arrangement** — Campbell plans to take his "big four cylinder Honda"; Dennis, who barely knows him, agrees to ride pillion with his own helmet — a closing beat Campbell reads as courage ("Maybe only once," he chuckles), and the setup for the Thursday lab visit.

## Thread
This chapter is the hinge of Campbell's autobiographical setup in Section 1 ("Delusion or Knowledge"): the moment the skeptical young physicist stops reading about Monroe and meets him, tests him against the lunatic/huckster hypotheses, and binds himself into the knowledge-for-research exchange that will generate the trilogy's experiential data. It follows from the preceding chapters' account of who Campbell was and how he came to be heading to Monroe's (the invitation this chapter fulfills), and it sets up the lab work, training, and "carefully scientific" strange experiences of the chapters immediately following — beginning with the Thursday-evening lab meeting arranged here. It also plants two seeds the trilogy will grow: the "larger reality" accessible through altered states, and the critique of peer-review-bound science as sheep-like, which underwrites Campbell's later insistence on first-person open-minded skepticism over institutional credentialism. The Monroe-knows-things-paranormally beat (Dennis's Thursday) is the first of the small evidential anomalies Campbell will accumulate.
