# The Politics of Reality

## In one paragraph
Thomas Campbell argues that each person only partially defines their own "local reality" through their senses and interpretations, and that groups who share a similar local reality tend to declare it the one true reality — a move he calls "the politics of reality." He contends that logic and rationality are far weaker tools for navigating life's most important decisions than people like to believe, because the space-time rule-set (PMR) is deliberately designed with uncertainty so that choice, free will, and intent — not deductive logic — drive personal growth and lowering of entropy. Most people's sense of being rational, he says, is a self-serving illusion produced by ego and belief traps, used to justify wants, needs, dominance, and comfort rather than to find truth. The chapter closes with an extended satirical monologue voicing the "democratic," majority-rules dismissal of nonphysical reality (AUM) and psi phenomena, illustrating how fear, conformity, and selective attention to evidence enforce a socially sanctioned version of "official reality."

## Core claims
- Each individual "partially defines his or her own local reality," and shared local realities among people of common experience get elevated by ego into a claimed single true reality — everyone else being "dumb, confused, or delusional."
- Local reality is bounded by the senses and their interpretation; what falls outside is dismissed as nonexistent, mystical, or nonphysical — illustrated by the thought experiment of a species that was always blind and deaf.
- Logic can only operate where there is enough data, and Campbell argues most of life's significant decisions (who to marry, how to grow one's consciousness) must be made without enough data for a definitive logical conclusion.
- This "beyond-logical, uncertain state of affairs is by design": if all interaction in PMR were deductive/logical, choice and free will would become moot and existence would be "automatic and machine-like within a closed solution set."
- PMR physical law (the space-time rule-set) governs objective causality (cannonballs), but Campbell says it was never meant to govern personal learning, interaction, and relationship the same way.
- People's sense of being rational is mostly a "feel-good delusion of the ego" that manufactures a sense of competence and personal security, not an accurate self-description.
- Randomness/pseudo-randomness, uncertainty, and what he calls the "psi uncertainty principle" are necessary parts of the space-time rule-set; psi effects (divination, mind reading, telepathy, remote viewing, precognition, psychokinesis) would be detrimental to growth for a "low quality, high entropy consciousness" while being largely irrelevant to growth for a "low entropy, high quality consciousness" — hence their natural limitation.
- Irrationality and illogic are "the norm, not the exception," and the belief that oneself (or one's in-group) is the rational exception is itself a common delusion.
- Simple analytical thinking is often used to masquerade as intelligence, supporting "a superior claim to correctness" that serves dominance, wants, needs, and desires rather than truth.
- The appearance of an orderly, rational life narrative is a delusion that lowers anxiety and provides "a coherent media or frame of reference" reflecting the quality of one's being — the drive to rationalize choices is "a necessary part of what makes us work."
- Society overvalues objective, well-behaved, easily understood aspects of life (career, status, material success) while devaluing subjective growth — "focusing on the chaff and throwing away the wheat" — leaving most people in an endless "soap opera" until death.
- PMR is "not intrinsically important" to a person's purpose except as a structured learning experience; solving logic problems is only a "supporting, enhancing, enabling structure," not the main goal, which is the iterative expression of intent and feedback that lowers entropy over time ("pull yourself up by your bootstraps").
- Humanity's worst crimes — war, holy wars, genocide, racism, ethnic cleansing — are, Campbell argues, motivated by ego in service of belief, and people will rationalize almost anything to preserve a comfortable, closed concept of reality; this is "the politics of reality."
- Maintaining the shared delusion of being logical, rational people in an objective physical world requires "selectively ignoring the facts of objective and subjective experience" to enforce official, authority-sanctioned reality (scientific, cultural, political, or religious); "fear, control, and conformity make ignorance their friend."
- The chapter's closing satire argues that popular rejection of nonphysical reality (AUM) and psi phenomena functions like a "vote" — majority opinion and appeals to "smart people," lack of lab-testable "hard physical evidence," and fear of being thought weird are used to dismiss the book's claims without genuine examination.

## Concepts
- **Local reality** — the personal, partial slice of reality each individual constructs, defined and limited by their senses and their interpretation of the data those senses collect; people of similar sensors/interpretation share similar local realities.
- **Politics of reality** — Campbell's term for the social process by which a group with a common local reality, driven by ego, fear, belief traps, and a "small picture," insists their shared version is the only correct reality and enforces it via cultural, scientific, political, or religious authority.
- **Space-time rule-set (PMR physical law)** — the objective, causal rule system (referred to elsewhere in the book as PMR, the physical matter reality) that provides "basic order and causality" for the physical world, analogous to the physics that governs cannonballs, but which Campbell says was never meant to govern personal relationships and learning the same way.
- **PMR (physical virtual reality set)** — described here as a "theatrical set (a playground with rules)" and a "virtual learning lab" that is not intrinsically important to a person's purpose except that it provides structured learning experience; likened in importance to one's house or car rather than to one's children or parents (which stand for the subjective/intuitive dimension).
- **Psi uncertainty principle** — the principle, referenced as part of the space-time rule-set's design, that keeps psi phenomena uncertain/limited by design rather than freely available, tied to the necessity of randomness and uncertainty in PMR.
- **Psi effects** — the class of phenomena named as divination, mind reading, telepathy, remote viewing, precognition, and psychokinesis; Campbell argues these are naturally limited because unrestricted access would be detrimental to growth for a low-quality, high-entropy consciousness while being largely irrelevant to a low-entropy, high-quality consciousness.
- **Ego** — the self-protective aspect of identity that, per Campbell, drives people to define "local truth" as whatever feels good and boosts self-esteem, to justify actions/beliefs, and to manufacture an illusion of personal rationality and superiority over others.
- **Belief trap** — a self-justifying, self-referential system of belief that produces a false sense of being rational and logical; described as a source of ego security and, at scale, a driver of humanity's worst atrocities.
- **Quality of consciousness** — the developmental measure Campbell says experience is meant to grow, as opposed to "getting the right answers"; distinguished from mere intellectual/logical competence.
- **Entropy (low/high, in consciousness terms)** — used as a metric of consciousness quality; a "low entropy, high quality" consciousness is largely unaffected by psi-effect availability, whereas a "low quality, high entropy" consciousness would be harmed by it; personal growth is framed as an increment-by-increment lowering of entropy ("pull yourself up by your bootstraps").
- **Free will / intent** — the capacity for genuine, non-deterministic choice that Campbell says would become moot if PMR interactions were purely deductive/logical; the space-time rule-set's built-in uncertainty exists, he argues, precisely to preserve room for free will and intent-driven (rather than causal-logic-driven) choices.
- **Rationality as illusion** — Campbell's claim that most people's sense of being rational and logical is, for the most part, a "feel-good delusion of the ego" rather than an accurate account of how decisions are actually made.
- **AUM** — referenced here only in passing as "this foreign sounding AUM gobbledygook," voiced through the mouth of the skeptical majority-opinion monologue as an example of nonphysical-reality terminology that mainstream opinion dismisses without examination (not defined in this chapter; treated as prior-established terminology).
- **My Big TOE** — invoked by the author as itself being built on and using logical process/science as a foundation, even while arguing that logic alone cannot reach the deepest truths ("It is the foundation of My Big TOE").
- **Soap opera (of ordinary life)** — Campbell's metaphor for the typical human life pattern: continuous drama with no resolution until death, resulting from over-investing effort in objective/material success and under-investing in subjective personal-quality growth.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The blind-and-deaf-humanity thought experiment**: Campbell asks the reader to imagine every human on the planet had always been blind and deaf (with survival needs like food and beer assumed handled), then to imagine what culture, political boundaries, and civilization would look like, how such a species would interpret its interaction with "critters" and with each other, and how it would deal with "sunburn, snow, tornados, fire, tigers, birds, fleas, bumblebees and good-feeling babes." He uses this to show how much of what we take as "physical reality" would vanish into nonexistence, mysticism, or the nonphysical for a population whose sensory bandwidth was different from ours — illustrating that local reality is sense-and-interpretation-dependent, not absolute.
- **The "blind person with exceptional hearing" analogy**: arguing that intellect alone cannot optimize a life, Campbell says trying to optimize your life by primarily applying your intellect (what most intellectuals do) "is like a blind person with exceptional hearing trying to drive an automobile or fly an airplane" — the wrong sense entirely for the job at hand, however well developed.
- **The "mind-reading boss/spouse/mother-in-law/telemarketer" thought experiment**: readers are asked to imagine their boss, spouse, mother-in-law, children, or telemarketers having direct access to their mind, to make vivid why "great power in the hands of an irrational ego or manipulating intent is always frightening and usually destructive" — used as an intuitive argument for why psi abilities (like telepathy) are naturally limited for everyone's mutual protection.
- **The "little boys wanting to be as strong as full-grown men" analogy**: used immediately after the psi-effects discussion to argue that just as it would be reckless for young boys to have adult-level strength, "evolution is not that careless" about handing out psi power to consciousnesses not ready for it.
- **The pre-schoolers rationality thought experiment**: Campbell asks whether a "gaggle of pre-schoolers" is rational, logical, or highly interactive, and challenges the reader to give "a good, thoughtful answer" about whether adult "rationality" is really a fundamental difference in interactive consciousness or merely superficial (better at math, better planning) or ego-driven self-justification.
- **The extended satirical "vote" monologue**: a multi-paragraph, quoted internal-monologue voice (rhetorical "Right?" repeated) walks through the standard skeptical dismissal of nonphysical reality — appealing to what "smart people" believe, the absence of "hard physical evidence," a comparison to historical medicine ("doctors were bleeding people with leeches to cure them"), fear of being thought "weird," and a closing image of tossing the book "in the fireplace and go watch TV." Campbell uses this fabricated dialogue as a case study in how majority opinion, ego, and fear jointly perform "the politics of reality" against ideas like AUM and psi phenomena, without those ideas needing to be actually examined or refuted.

## Key quotes
- "Each individual partially defines his or her own local reality." — opening claim framing the whole chapter's argument.
- "This position concerning the accepted notion of reality becomes just another cultural, scientific, and religious belief system. Be careful: It is an easy trap to fall into." — warning against mistaking shared local reality for absolute truth.
- "The point of experience is growing the quality of your consciousness, not getting the right answers." — Campbell's reframing of what logic/experience are actually for.
- "This beyond-logical, uncertain state of affairs is by design." — asserting that PMR's uncertainty is purposeful, not a flaw.
- "Your appearance (to yourself and others) of rationality and logical process is, for the most part, an illusion, a feel-good delusion of the ego..." — core claim about the nature of perceived rationality.
- "It can be a wonderfully productive tool — I live and work by it every day. Science is based upon it — it is the foundation of My Big TOE." — Campbell's caveat that he is not dismissing logic outright.
- "Such is the power of fear, belief traps, a closed mind, and a small picture combined. This is the politics of reality." — the chapter's title-line thesis statement.
- "Fear, control, and conformity make ignorance their friend." — on how official/sanctioned reality is maintained.
- "If you really want to know how real reality really is, all you need to do is take a vote." — opening the satirical closing section on majority-rules epistemology.
- "Where is the hard physical evidence? I am not buying this consciousness mumbo jumbo until I can put my hands on some good samples of nonphysical energy to study in the lab." — voice of the skeptical monologue Campbell constructs and critiques.

## Thread
This unit sits late in Book 2's Section 4 ("Solving the Mystery," ch50-72), after the trilogy has laid out the space-time rule-set, PMR, entropy/quality-of-consciousness, and psi phenomena as technical concepts; here Campbell turns those concepts toward a social/psychological critique of why people collectively reject nonphysical reality and psi. It builds directly on prior material about the psi uncertainty principle and low/high entropy consciousness (cited explicitly as "things we learned in previous sections") and extends the book's recurring theme that PMR is a designed learning environment rather than the whole of reality. The chapter's satirical closing monologue about "voting" on reality anticipates and pre-empts the kind of mainstream, evidence-demanding skepticism the book expects from readers, setting up continued arguments (later in Section 4 and into Book 3) about subjective/intuitive growth versus objective/logical process.
