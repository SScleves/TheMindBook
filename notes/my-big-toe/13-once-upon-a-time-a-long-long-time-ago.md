# Ch 13 — Once Upon a Time, a Long, Long Time Ago

## In one paragraph
Campbell corrects a claim he often makes — that he learned what he has learned "from a cold start" — by confessing "one little white lie": once he became familiar with the out-of-body experience and NPMR as an adult, childhood memories returned showing he had done all of this before. He recounts how, between ages five and eight, "friendly nonphysical beings" deliberately got him out-of-body, taught him techniques, mentored him nightly, and then without warning subjected him to a high-stakes standardized test administered by a panel of judges to determine "the quality of my being." He argues along the way that out-of-body experience is common and mundane — most young children have fully conscious OOBEs and most adults have non-conscious ones — but cultural limits on our concepts of consciousness cause us to reject and forget these natural abilities. The chapter's climax is corroboration: twenty-three years later, Campbell and Bob discover they were independently given the identical test, question for question, convincing Campbell that a standardized examination exists in NPMR. The chapter thus offers autobiographical evidence that Campbell's adult explorations were a resumption, not a beginning, of training in the nonphysical.

## Core claims
- Campbell claims anyone can learn what he has learned — he began as a "bone-headed physicist" with "no particular natural talent" — though he admits his own start was not as cold as he first believed.
- Campbell claims that between the ages of five and eight, friendly nonphysical beings purposefully (not as a random prank) helped him get out-of-body, taught him techniques to do it at will, and mentored him almost every night.
- Campbell asserts that most children, particularly those younger than seven, have spontaneous, fully conscious out-of-body experiences; parents dismiss these as meaningless dreams and the children forget them.
- Campbell asserts that most adults also have spontaneous out-of-body voyages, but not fully conscious ones, so they "do not qualify as experiences."
- Campbell argues that out-of-body phenomena are "much more mundane and common than strange"; OOBE only seems strange because our limited concepts of consciousness and reality force us to reject mental functions "naturally available to our species" and to misunderstand what the mind does during sleep.
- Campbell claims his mentors blacked him out during the out-of-body transition "to make the process more comfortable," and later concludes the violent vibrations he experienced when staying conscious were not necessary — "They simply did not want me to become too independent."
- Campbell claims that while out-of-body as a child he was an adult, and returned to being a kid when back in his body; his mental space retained its adult nature in the altered state.
- Campbell claims he was subjected, without forewarning, to a battery of exams in NPMR designed to determine "the quality of my being" — how evolved an entity he was — with progressively harder multiple-choice situations testing ego, desire, fear, helping-others versus personal path, and how well he "truly understood love."
- Campbell claims the test was administered by a panel of judges who could slip "back in time" to inspect his mind for his true motivations, that advocates vouched for him, and that "serious hardball politics" and competitive hostility existed between factions of judges.
- Campbell claims the test is standardized: twenty-three years later he and Bob reconstructed the identical sequence of test problems from their separate experiences, and he has since met several others whose NPMR events were "identical in form, function, and content."
- Campbell claims that after the first big test he was put in regular nightly training classes for most of a year — learning to control his mind, manipulate nonphysical energy, make the right choices for the right reasons, think and act fast, and break old PMR conceptual habits — then the training stopped and did not resume for some twenty years.
- Campbell claims that in a test setting, knowledge is "far more valuable than goods," and that getting one question wrong ends the test.
- Campbell claims he has been "hauled in front of panels of judges" at least five times since, because of his "unpredictable quirky human nature," and has thus far prevailed.

## Concepts
- **OOBE (out-of-body experience)** — Being consciously aware and active outside the physical body. Campbell treats it as a common, natural human phenomenon: fully conscious and spontaneous in most young children, present but non-conscious in most adults (and therefore not qualifying as an "experience"). It seems strange only because of self-imposed conceptual limitations on consciousness and reality.
- **NPMR (nonphysical-matter reality)** — The nonphysical reality in which Campbell's out-of-body activity, training sessions, tests, and encounters with nonphysical beings take place; he speaks of becoming "effective and efficient in NPMR" and of others experiencing identical event-sequences "while in NPMR."
- **PMR (physical-matter reality)** — Ordinary physical reality; Campbell describes training himself to "break old PMR conceptual habits" that limit effectiveness in NPMR, and "popping back into physical reality" in his bed when the transition failed.
- **Nonphysical beings / mentors** — The "friendly nonphysical beings" who purposefully got the child Campbell out-of-body, taught him transition techniques, blacked him out during transitions for comfort (and, he later concluded, to keep him from becoming too independent), and "loved to teach."
- **The standardized test** — A fixed, standardized battery of exam-situations in NPMR that determines "the quality of my being" — how evolved an entity the subject is. It presents progressively harder multiple-choice situations (treasure vs. knowledge, sexual enticements testing ego and desire, helping others vs. personal path, fear, understanding of love); one wrong answer ends it; the series must restart from the beginning even after a question is revealed. Campbell infers it is standardized because he and Bob received the identical sequence.
- **Panel of judges** — The entities who administer and evaluate the exam. Campbell describes them as able to slip back in time and inspect his mind for true motivations, split into hostile competing factions, and subject to appeal to "a higher level judge."
- **Advocates** — Beings who took Campbell's side before the judges, vouching for his readiness; he says they had evidently been working with him for a long time to prepare him, with their "plans, careers, and reputations" hanging in the balance.
- **Training sessions** — Regular nightly classes in NPMR (nearly a year in the 1950s, resumed in adulthood) in which Campbell was run through situations, given jobs, and tested: learning mind control, manipulation of nonphysical energy, right choices for right reasons, speed, focus, and control.
- **Inner-space** — Campbell's term for the arena of these nonphysical adventures ("my adventures in inner-space took an unexpected turn").
- **Altered state** — The state in which Campbell's mental space "retained its adult nature" even while he was a child in PMR.
- **Love** — Presented as a test subject: the judges "probed how well I truly understood love," placing it among the qualities that measure how evolved an entity is.
- **Ego / desire / fear** — Qualities the test deliberately targeted ("problems that tested ego and desire with sexual enticements... They played to my emotions and ego, tried to instill fear").

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The white-lie correction** — Campbell's standing claim ("if a bone-headed physicist like me could do it, anybody could... from a cold start") is qualified: after adult familiarity with OOBE and NPMR, old memories "returned clear as crystal," showing his start was not cold. Shows, for Campbell, that memory of nonphysical experience returns once one has "the knowledge and perspective to understand" it.
- **Spock into the Twilight Zone** — Campbell's image for his own slow, tedious journey: "Dragging Spock from the deck of the Enterprise into the Twilight Zone, with logic fully intact and uncompromised." Meant to show one can enter this territory without abandoning logic.
- **The hedge glide (age 5-8)** — First remembered OOBE: floating a foot or two above the yard after slipping out through his second-story bedroom wall, unable to steer or stop, bracing for a crash into a thick hedge — then gliding straight through it "without interacting with it." Shows the nonphysical body's non-interaction with physical objects, and the playful, non-threatening character of childhood OOBEs.
- **The crawling analogy** — We are "born with two good legs, but never learned to walk because the ability to crawl precedes the ability to walk," and society "stigmatizes non-crawling non-conformists as wackos." Campbell's analogy for how natural mental abilities (like conscious OOBE) are abandoned under social pressure.
- **The conscious-transition experiment** — Campbell insisted on staying conscious through the out-of-body transition instead of being blacked out. Result: his body "became plastic," shaken by violent, growing oscillations "like a loose canvas awning flapping in a strong wind," repeatedly throwing him back into physical reality until he conceded "OK, you do it" — and was "almost instantly awake in the out-of-body state." His later interpretation: "I had been had" — the violent oscillations were unnecessary; the mentors did not want him too independent.
- **The kid-becomes-adult observation** — Out-of-body, the child Campbell was an adult; back in body, a kid again — evidence, for Campbell, that the being in the altered state is not defined by the physical age of the body.
- **The first test question and the gangster joke** — Asked "Would you rather have this treasure (I got a picture) or learn something new?", Campbell — knowing knowledge was obviously more valuable — joked "Just gimmee the loot... I can always learn something new on my own" in his "best gangster voice." Result: "BZZZZZT!! Wrong!" and instant transport before a panel of judges. Shows the test's seriousness and the literal grading of choices.
- **The judges' hearing** — Exclamations ("He failed the first question!", "He is not ready!", "Send him back!"); judges slipping back in time to inspect his true motivations; advocates approaching the bench; a 2-3 split among five judges ("Failed was failed — send him home" vs. "Continue the test, let's see what he can do"); appeal to a higher-level judge; a second chance granted amid "serious hardball politics" and hostility "thick as tar" between factions. Campbell likens his own bewilderment to "Alice at the Queen's castle."
- **The retake** — The test restarted with a precise replay of question one, from which Campbell surmised it was a fixed, standardized series. Subsequent situations tested ego and desire (including "bizarre" sexual enticements), choices between helping others and pursuing one's personal path, fear, emotions, and understanding of love, until a problem left him clueless; he guessed and the test ended.
- **The Bob corroboration (twenty-three years later)** — At Bob's house, Bob commented on Campbell's previous night's NPMR training exercise (having watched from the audience, including Campbell's end-zone-style "show-off display," which Campbell compares to football players dancing in the end zone). Bob then described a difficult test he had recently been given; after Bob's third test description, Campbell's childhood memories flooded back and he described tests four and five, leaving Bob speechless. They alternated describing the rest, discovering they had "bombed out at the same place but with different answers." Campbell's conclusion: "Without a doubt, that was a standardized test." He has since met several others with identical NPMR event-sequences.
- **The year of nightly training (early 1950s)** — After the first big test, nightly training classes for most of a year: situations, jobs, further testing; learning mind control, nonphysical-energy manipulation, right choices for right reasons, fast thinking and acting, following directions, breaking PMR habits — until he felt "competent and strong, like an athlete ready to walk into the arena." Then, one night, no more training; it would not resume for some twenty years.
- **The rap sheet** — At least five later hearings before panels of judges "because of my unpredictable quirky human nature"; Campbell has "prevailed" each time and jokes he must "have a rap sheet a mile long."

## Thread
This chapter sits inside Book 1's autobiographical arc (Section 1, "Delusion or Knowledge"), where Campbell establishes his credentials-by-experience before presenting the theory. It retroactively reframes the adult story told in surrounding chapters — his training and OOBE explorations with Bob and the resumption of "sentience within the larger reality" — as phase two of a process that began in childhood: mentorship, a standardized evaluative test, and a year of nightly training that ended abruptly and resumed some twenty years later. It supplies two argumentative supports Campbell leans on later: (1) OOBE is a common, natural, culturally suppressed human capacity (so the reader's skepticism is a learned limitation), and (2) independent corroboration — the identical test sequence experienced separately by Bob and by "several others" — as evidence that NPMR events are shared and objective-like rather than private fantasy. It also seeds the picture of NPMR as an organized place with mentors, advocates, judges, politics, standardized procedures, and purposes concerning the evolution of beings, which the trilogy's later model-building elaborates.
