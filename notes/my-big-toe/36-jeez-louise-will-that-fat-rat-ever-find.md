# Ch 36 — Jeez Louise, Will That Fat Rat Ever Find the Cheese?

## In one paragraph
Campbell opens Book 2's "Man in the Loop" section by deflating human self-importance and reframing physical reality (PMR) as a purpose-built learning lab for consciousness. He argues that our limited 3D awareness is not a defect but the design feature: it makes our interactions transparent and gives immediate feedback, so that every choice reveals and shapes the quality of our consciousness. Right and wrong are redefined "operationally" — by whether an intent lowers or raises the average entropy of the consciousness system — and most pain, he claims, is self-inflicted by wrong choices, though a random external component also exists. The chapter's governing image is the rat in a pudding-maze: we taste our way through life, getting shocks for wrong choices and cheese for right ones, with no shortcuts, no excuses, and no way to quit. Campbell then extends the picture: humans are not central (we are bacteria in a petri dish, bit players, not stars), individual consciousness matters directly in NPMR in a way individuals never do in biological evolution, and AUM has deliberately made the experiment open-ended by adding "anti-rats" who scatter poisoned cheese — the origin of the good-versus-evil struggle that free will requires.

## Core claims
- Campbell argues that human "intelligence" and "cleverness" are self-servingly defined as the ability to influence, manipulate, control, and dominate others — which is why our technologies and social systems evolve around war, defense, trade, and communications, and why we have created no fewer than five independent ways to destroy all life (nuclear bombs, global warming, toxic waste, pollution, and excessive non-ecological global resource consumption).
- Campbell claims limited awareness is the point of PMR: it guarantees that our interactions accurately and immediately reflect the quality of our being, producing a simple, straightforward interactive virtual environment with immediate feedback and consequences — "an optimal nursery for budding consciousness."
- To a less limited consciousness, Campbell says, humans are exceedingly simple and transparent: our motivations demonstrate our consciousness quality in everything we do, say, think, and feel; we cannot hide what we are.
- Campbell claims we create our reality through the implementation of our intent-choices and how we interpret our perceptions; the results are usually precisely what we need and deserve to stimulate productive growth. Wrong choices produce pain; right choices produce spiritual growth.
- Campbell distinguishes right and wrong "in the evolutionary sense": absolute right and wrong are operationally defined by their effect on the average entropy of the system — "right" intents improve the quality of your being, "wrong" ones stimulate no growth and may lose previously earned quality. Social/cultural right and wrong is relative, customary, and often a diluted, distorted shadow of the absolute, twisted by cultural, religious, personal, and scientific belief systems.
- Campbell claims most pain is self-inflicted (wrong choices at its source); only a small part comes from external randomness — yet we prefer to believe the opposite because an external enemy is easier to accept and defeat than an internal one. (He defers the origin of randomness to Chapter 11 of Book 2 and the next two Sections.)
- Campbell asserts that PMR is one of many learning labs configured within NPMR_N — some using versions of space-time, some not — each a local reality whose inhabitants see themselves as physical and everything else as nonphysical. All reality systems are virtual; there is no actual distinction between real and virtual. Everything is consciousness: "a vast system of self-modifying digital organization evolving toward lower entropy."
- Campbell claims feedback is immediate and obvious but can be ignored or misinterpreted when ego makes too big a fuss; attentiveness is essential, and there are "absolutely no shortcuts, no end runs, no acceptable excuses, and no way to quit."
- Campbell rejects the anthropomorphic God ("little old man with the long white beard" you can hustle favors from) as a product of little-picture culture and a fearful, needy mind; no one hands out judgments, and not even AUM is likely paying attention to any individual human bacterium in the PMR petri dish. Humanity is not central to the Big Picture; no reality or dimension is more central than another.
- Campbell claims feelings of superiority (species, race, culture, religion, nation, profession, etc.) are errors of perception born of ignorance, belief, and ego; they have generated destructive belief systems, damaged biosphere-earth, retarded consciousness growth, and lead to destructive results for all — including the ones feeling superior.
- Campbell contrasts biological with consciousness evolution: in biological systems the continuous immortal group, not the discontinuous mortal individual, is the primary beneficiary of evolution (an individual's impact is nearly infinitesimal); in a consciousness system such as NPMR_N, individual immortal conscious entities contribute their lowered entropy directly to the whole, so a single entity can be individually very important. As each individuated consciousness evolves, AUM evolves.
- Campbell claims quality runs the gamut from high-entropy, fear-based, self-serving ego to the unlimited, low-entropy unconditional love exhibited by AUM-consciousness, and your personal significance to the whole rises dramatically as you move toward the latter. An evolved consciousness can outgrow its petri dish and "join the laboratory staff."
- Campbell asserts you have no choice about playing: you exist, cannot stop existing, and are evolving toward the positive or negative pole every day; neutrality is impossible, denial and ignorance affect nothing, and physical death simply initiates a new maze — learning is cumulative (the consciousness cycle).
- Campbell claims failure modes are two: not understanding that reality is a choice-maze driving consciousness evolution (so you never try), or having taste buds so twisted by beliefs, self-absorption, attachments, needs, and fears (ego) that you cannot tell electric shocks from fine white-cheddar cheese.
- Campbell claims evolution is impersonal and shows no compassion for failures or slow learners ("Goodbye dodo birds, adios dinosaurs, so long trilobites"); your concept of "fair" is a function of ego and belief and mostly irrelevant. An evolving system is either unprofitable, profitable, or astable.
- Campbell claims evolution is also patient, and every rat gets all the help it can profitably use from nonphysical friends who care about individual progress.
- Campbell argues that a universal happy ending (all rats eventually make it — ubiquitous love, peace, and light) would make the experiment too narrow, scientifically shallow, and of dubious value to AUM; so poisoned cheese and clever distractions are scattered by a competing group of anti-rats, adding the struggle of good versus evil. Free will requires that negative intent be a possibility; without free will there can be no experiment, and there is no point in experiments whose outcome is predetermined. Maximum free will enables maximum potential for both success and failure.
- Campbell claims everyone eventually chooses sides between the Good Guys and Bad Guys — no choice becomes an active choice, leading by circumstance toward one direction or the other.

## Concepts
- **AUM** — Campbell's larger consciousness system, the "non-anthropomorphic AUM thought-system-thing"; the whole of which individuated consciousnesses are evolving parts. AUM "invented (evolved) the concept of limited virtual physical realities," runs progressively harder experiments like any scientist, and itself evolves as its individuated parts evolve. Its consciousness exhibits unlimited, low-entropy unconditional love.
- **PMR (physical-matter reality)** — our 3D space-time reality, described as a virtual learning lab, an "optimal nursery for budding consciousness," a "particular biome" of the larger eco-mind-system, and a petri dish in which humans are the bacteria.
- **NPMR / NPMR_N** — the nonphysical (larger) reality; NPMR_N contains many subsets configured as learning labs, some employing versions of space-time, others not; each is a local reality whose inhabitants see themselves as physical. Within it, consciousness forms a vast interdependent evolving ecosystem.
- **Our System** — Campbell's term for our local reality, whose limits a "simplistic view" confuses with the larger reality of AUM (e.g., assuming all rats must eventually make it).
- **Individuated consciousness** — a unit of consciousness (also called a being, entity, or soul) with free will and "the potential of AUM," which grows up (improves its quality) by exercising free will intent within an interactive virtual system with feedback.
- **Limited awareness** — the bounded 3D, "what you see is what you get" perspective; not a flaw but the mechanism that makes interactions transparent and feedback immediate, creating efficient learning opportunities.
- **Little picture / Big Picture** — the little picture is the belief-blind, ego-driven local perspective in which trivial personal soap operas seem vital; the Big Picture is the larger-reality perspective from which those concerns are often completely inconsequential and from which one may eventually participate directly.
- **Quality of consciousness** — the attribute PMR exists to grow; demonstrated in everything one does, says, thinks, and feels; equivalent to spiritual development; measured by entropy (lower entropy = higher quality).
- **Entropy** — Campbell's metric of consciousness quality: everything is consciousness "evolving toward lower entropy"; absolute right and wrong are defined by their effect on the average entropy of the system; quality ranges from high-entropy fear-based ego to low-entropy unconditional love.
- **Fundamental Process (of evolution)** — the impersonal evolutionary process, here focused on improving consciousness quality; deaf to reasons and excuses, showing no compassion for failures, yet infinitely patient; Campbell exhorts the reader to "Implement the Fundamental Process!"
- **Absolute vs. relative right and wrong** — absolute right and wrong are "operationally defined to optimize the growth (evolution) of the quality of our consciousness"; relative (social/cultural) right and wrong is customary, a diluted, distorted shadow of the absolute, often twisted by belief systems (cultural, religious, personal, and scientific).
- **Self-inflicted pain** — internally caused pain rooted in wrong choices, which makes up the bulk of daily pain and suffering; contrasted with the rarer externally caused pain reflecting randomness.
- **Randomness** — the source of genuinely external pain ("sometimes the ball takes a bounce and hits you squarely between the eyes"); its origin and nature deferred to Chapter 11 and the next two Sections.
- **Learning lab** — Campbell's designation for PMR and other NPMR_N subsets: places where individuated consciousness grows by exercising free will intent within a virtual system of direct interactive experience and feedback.
- **Virtual reality** — the status of all reality systems: "All reality systems are virtual; hence there is no actual distinction between real and virtual."
- **Digital consciousness** — consciousness as "a vast system of self-modifying digital organization evolving toward lower entropy"; humans are "sentient chunks of individuated digital consciousness."
- **Consciousness ecosystem / eco-mind-system** — consciousness within NPMR_N pictured as a vast interdependent evolving ecosystem "designed to evolve consciousness quality"; unlike biological ecosystems it is driven not by survival and propagation but by self-improvement (entropy lowering). PMR is one biome of it.
- **Petri dish** — Campbell's image for a local reality: humans are bacteria in the PMR petri dish; an evolved consciousness can outgrow its assigned dish "and one day join the laboratory staff."
- **Rat maze / pudding-maze** — the chapter's central model of life in PMR: a "wonderfully complex pudding-maze" we must taste our way through; wrong choices bring electric shocks (misery), right choices bring cheese (happy, productive, rewarding lives). "Success is simultaneously its own criteria, evaluation, and reward."
- **Anti-rats / poisoned cheese** — a competing group that scatters bits of poisoned cheese and clever distractions and confusions through the maze, added to make the experiment open-ended; they introduce the struggle between good and evil, positive and negative intent, so it is no longer certain all rats make it.
- **Free will** — the individuated consciousness's capacity of choice; it requires that negative intent be possible, and without it "there can be no experiment"; maximum free will enables maximum potential for both success and failure. (Derivation deferred to Chapter 11.)
- **Ego** — the cluster of needs, wants, desires, fears, ignorance, and beliefs (lack of consciousness quality) that drives control and dominance, distorts feedback, twists the taste buds, and generates feelings of superiority; "only a fat-ugly ego could care a flip about the relative importance of earth-based humans."
- **Unconditional love** — the low-entropy end of the quality spectrum, "exhibited by AUM-consciousness"; opposite of the severely limited, high-entropy, fear-based, self-serving ego.
- **Consciousness cycle** — the continuing process one participates in and drives: physical death "initiates a process that eventually creates a new maze for you to explore — your learning is cumulative."
- **Evolutionary profitability** — an evolving system's status: unprofitable (say goodbye if not turned around in time), profitable (progress is being made), or astable (hanging around waiting to see which way it will go). In consciousness evolution, personal profitability contributes directly to the whole.
- **Nonphysical friends** — helpers each rat has: it gets "all the help it can profitably use from its nonphysical friends who do care and understand the significance of individual progress."
- **Good Guys / Bad Guys** — the two sides of the positive/negative-intent struggle everyone eventually joins; no choice becomes an active choice.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The rat-in-a-pudding-maze model (central)** — Life in PMR is a maze where wrong turns meet immediate electric shock and right choices yield cheese; the maze is made of pudding, so "to succeed, we must taste our way through it." Moving parts: rat (individuated consciousness), maze (custom-designed life circumstances), shocks (self-inflicted pain from wrong choices), cheese (happy, productive, rewarding lives from right choices for right reasons), taste (discernment of quality). Shows evolution where success is its own criterion, evaluation, and reward. Campbell warns: "Don't ask for an easier maze — you won't get one."
- **The failed/confused rat** — A rat that sees no point in exploring and cannot tell shocks from cheese faces "a long and difficult training experience"; shocks become "no big deal! 'That's life,'" and its world may seem random, existential, nihilistic, mystical, or ruled by an unfathomable, jealous, vengeful, or demanding god. Collections of such rats develop cynicism, self-pity, anger, victim-hood, resignation, escapism, recreational drug use, and obsession with sex and the symbols of power (vicarious violence and domination through entertainment, competition and sport; conspicuous consumption; macho-vehicles and aggressive driving; ownership of weapons) — all strategies against fear-generated anxiety. Shows what losing the sense of direction (black and white dissolving into meaningless gray) looks like.
- **Anti-rats and poisoned cheese (the upgraded experiment)** — Campbell rejects the "simplistic view" that since some rats figure it out and are encouraged to help the rest, all rats eventually make it ("a heavenly vision of ubiquitous love, peace, and light... in Ratville"): that experiment would be too narrow, shallow, and of dubious value to AUM. So poisoned cheese and clever distractions are scattered by competing anti-rats, adding good vs. evil to the struggle of ignorance/fear/ego vs. knowledge/wisdom/love and making the outcome genuinely open. Explicitly NOT "an adolescent AUM putting two big spiders in a glass jar to see what happens" — free will requires the possibility of negative intent. Evolution may simply want to know "whether rats or anti-rats are the natural winners."
- **The petri-dish / bacteria analogy** — Humans are bacteria in the PMR petri dish: not individually attended to by AUM, just as what we do that affects bacteria "is usually not intended to be a personal affront or reward to any individual bacterium" — evolution's impersonality. Yet "a brilliant bacterium must remain in its petri dish," whereas an evolved consciousness can outgrow its dish and join the laboratory staff.
- **Five ways humanity can destroy all life** — nuclear bombs, global warming, toxic waste, pollution, and excessive non-ecological global resource consumption; offered ironically as proof of our "creativity and awesome intelligence" and evidence that cleverness-as-domination is the wrong metric.
- **Biological vs. consciousness evolution contrast (model)** — In biological evolution the immortal group is the beneficiary and an individual's impact is nearly infinitesimal (progress accrues through many tiny aligned contributions); in NPMR_N, immortal individual entities contribute lowered entropy directly to the whole, making one entity potentially very significant. Explains how you can be one of a gazillion in a petri dish yet individually important.
- **Consciousness-ecosystem / biome metaphor** — NPMR_N as an interdependent evolving ecosystem (evolving to lower its entropy, not for survival/propagation) with PMR as one biome; any part feeling superior to another is "shooting oneself in the foot over and over again."
- **Children and pets** — Young children and "your favorite furry critter" illustrate how a little-picture perspective produces transparent, straightforward interactions and reactions.
- **The white-bearded old man** — The anthropomorphized God "you can hustle favors from by believing the correct things and performing the correct rituals" — a product of little-picture culture and a fearful, needy mind; nobody is calling plays from the sidelines.
- **Forrest Gump** — cited (spelled "Forest Gump") as "the famous cinematic philosopher-genius" for "Shit happens," illustrating the random external component of pain (bad bounces, crumbled cookies).
- **Fish learning to walk** — Improving consciousness quality is "much easier than a fish learning to walk on land and breathe air."
- **Dodos, dinosaurs, trilobites** — evolution's discarded failures/slow learners, showing that it weeps no tears and that "fair" is an ego construct.
- **Bad weather at a picnic / Mother Nature** — unreasonable-seeming growth conditions are as impersonal as bad picnic weather from "mean old Mother Nature."
- **Refrigerator-door question** — "What do your motivations and actions say about the quality of your consciousness?" — to be pasted "on the refrigerator door of your mind" and asked often, because "one day not that far off, you will need to deal with the answer."
- **Team recruitment close** — "Go rats go!" and the invitation to join the Good Guys (or "just wear the t-shirt"), with the Bad Guys' "benefits package" stinking; the trilogy itself offered as a possible "useful hint or secret map" toward "personal cheese."

## Key quotes
- "Our species has always intuitively known that a little knowledge is a dangerous thing, we just never appreciated how dangerous or how little." — opening deflation of human cleverness.
- "Limited awareness guarantees that our interaction within PMR accurately and immediately reflects the quality of our being." — why the little picture is a feature, not a bug.
- "In order to have a meaningful opportunity to do it right, you must also have the opportunity to do it wrong. From the perspective of the Big Picture, physical reality provides an optimal nursery for budding consciousness." — PMR's purpose.
- "Absolute right and wrong (intents, motivations, choices, and actions) are defined and differentiated by the effect they have on the average entropy of the system." — Campbell's operational ethics.
- "All reality systems are virtual; hence there is no actual distinction between real and virtual. Everything is consciousness, a vast system of self-modifying digital organization evolving toward lower entropy." — the ontological core of the chapter.
- "We are in a wonderfully complex pudding-maze; to succeed, we must taste our way through it." — the title image.
- "There are absolutely no shortcuts, no end runs, no acceptable excuses, and no way to quit." — the non-negotiable terms of the learning lab.
- "You should be proud to be a bit player, not disappointed because you are not the star." — humanity's non-centrality.
- "Though even a brilliant bacterium must remain in its petri dish as a part of its group, an evolved consciousness can outgrow the culture within its originally assigned petri dish and one day join the laboratory staff!" — individual potential in consciousness evolution.
- "Unfortunately, evolution shows little compassion and weeps no tears for its failures or slow learners. Goodbye dodo birds, adios dinosaurs, so long trilobites." — the impersonality of the Fundamental Process.
- "Free will requires that negative intent be a possibility, and without free will there can be no experiment. There is no point in doing experiments if the outcome is predetermined." — why evil exists in the maze.
- "It is my best hope that some hard working rat will find a useful hint or secret map somewhere in this trilogy that will make a difference for him or her in the pursuit of their personal cheese." — the chapter's (and trilogy's) stated aim.

## Thread
This is the second chapter of Book 2 ("Man in the Loop"), following the trilogy's turn from Book 1's assault on belief and its mysticism groundwork toward placing the human being inside the consciousness-evolution machinery. It converts the AUM/consciousness metaphysics already introduced (AUM, PMR, NPMR_N, entropy-as-quality) into an account of what our life is for: PMR as a purpose-built, feedback-rich learning lab in which limited awareness, free-will choice, and mostly self-inflicted pain drive the evolution of individual consciousness quality. It also plants the moral architecture of what follows: absolute right/wrong defined by system entropy, the impersonality and patience of the Fundamental Process, humanity's non-centrality, and — crucially — the introduction of anti-rats/negative intent, setting up the good-vs-evil dynamics of the larger reality. Campbell explicitly defers two loads to later chapters: the origin and nature of randomness and the derivation of free will (both to Chapter 11 of Book 2 and the next two Sections), and the explanation of why a low-entropy consciousness has no sense of superiority (an aside near the end of Chapter 20 of Book 2).
