# How to Influence and Lead?

## In one paragraph
This chapter is a leadership-and-influence primer built as a set of numbered tip-lists. William Cooper opens by defining the leader's core job (bring out the best in subordinates while completing tasks) and gives three habits for doing this: expecting the best, encouraging small wins, and correcting flaws gently. He then lists three qualities of a "great leader" (self-awareness of style, valuing creativity, giving recognition), moves into confident body language for leaders, then into three named influence/persuasion tactics — Reasoning, Inspire, and Ask. A section on leading different personality types introduces Myers-Briggs self-assessment and team-composition awareness, followed by short case-study vignettes (teacher, coach, promotional website) illustrating case-study-based teaching and marketing. The chapter closes by pivoting from "bad leader" traits (poor integrity, inflexibility) straight into a description of cult leaders as built from narcissistic and charismatic traits — implicitly framing charisma and command of followers as leadership qualities that can curdle into something dark.

## Core claims
- William Cooper claims that setting low expectations makes subordinates lazy and produces underperformance, while high, realistic expectations let a team "strive to achieve them."
- He claims progress should be praised even in small increments because "goals aren't reached in a day," and that this builds team momentum and focus.
- He claims a good leader addresses personal defects/limitations by setting clear standards and correcting "in a kind and gentle manner" rather than dwelling on the flaw itself.
- He claims a strong leader needs intelligence, adaptability, and empathy, and lists self-knowledge of leadership style, valuing creativity ("intellectual motivation," which he calls a defining quality of transformational leadership), and giving rewards/recognition as concrete practices. (Source citation [76] is attached earlier, to the "guidelines shown below" — i.e., the Expect the Best / Encourage / Positive Change list — not to this section.)
- He claims body language is an under-attended but fundamental leadership skill, and that specific cues (sitting upright, eye contact, open gestures, fingertips together while resting hands) signal attentiveness.
- He claims maintaining eye contact "creates trust," while looking away or staring around erodes earned faith and undercuts what a leader says.
- He claims body language must be balanced: too-open displays (e.g., power poses) can be perceived as arrogance, while too-closed displays (e.g., keeping the head down) read as doubtful and invite questioning of a leader's decisions.
- He claims (citing source [77]) that persuasion improves leadership, and describes three tactics — Reasoning, Inspire, and Ask — as distinct levers depending on audience and goal.
- On Reasoning, he claims it works via the word "because," should prioritize quality of reasons over quantity (else "challengers may look down on your weaknesses"), and requires accounting for even trivial details.
- On Inspire, he claims it works through emotion — enthusiasm, active language, confident tone, and painting a picture of an accomplished future — and that you "don't need to be someone famous" to use it.
- On Ask, he claims it is effective "for individuals of high power," works by withholding solutions and asking the other person how to resolve the problem, is used by journalists, lawyers, and vehicle salespeople, and that overusing "leading questions" specifically "can backfire."
- He claims Myers-Briggs testing is used in some industries as a self-recognition tool and a "friendly conversation starter," and is "the first step toward being able to work with others and to lead teams."
- He claims a team should not be built of one personality type, and that a leader must know each team member's traits like puzzle pieces to get "wonderful" results.
- He claims a leader should accept feedback despite it being uncomfortable, since "you are not infallible."
- He claims the two defining bad-leader traits are poor integrity (breaking one's word, causing employees to "act poorly" in turn) and lack of flexibility (sticking to one leadership style regardless of situation or employee).
- He claims cult leaders combine fringe beliefs, a propensity for violence, and charisma, and specifically identifies narcissism and charisma as the "two common traits" of cult leaders.
- He claims narcissistic cult leaders see themselves as superior from a young age, are ego-driven and demanding, cast themselves as "the hero in their own story," and treat others "like pawns in a shady chess game."
- He claims charismatic cult leaders are defined by style of talk, dress, or treatment of followers, are able to "convince anyone of anything," and can "command attention."

## Concepts
- **Expecting the best** — a leadership habit of setting high, realistic expectations and giving associates challenges they can rise to, on the premise that people live up to (or down to) the expectations set for them.
- **Encouragement** — empowering the team by praising even slight achievements or progress, since large goals take time and small wins build motivation and momentum.
- **Encouraging positive change** — responding to a subordinate's personal defects not by dwelling on them but by setting clear standards and correcting kindly and gently, so insecurity doesn't block their work.
- **Leadership style self-assessment** — knowing one's own strengths, improvable areas, and people skills, and judging whether those qualities help or hinder the team.
- **Valuing creativity** — encouraging the team to voice and act on creative ideas and giving them new challenges; the author labels the underlying quality "intellectual motivation" and calls it a defining feature of transformational leadership.
- **Transformational leadership** — named only in passing as the leadership style that intellectual motivation (valuing creativity) is said to define.
- **Rewards and recognition** — recognizing and awarding people so they feel appreciated, on the premise that "happy workers will always give their best."
- **Confident/attentive body language** — a cluster of nonverbal signals (sitting upright, maintaining eye contact, open gesturing, fingertips together while hands rest) that communicate observance and attentiveness to a team.
- **Eye contact for trust** — looking associates directly in the eyes while listening, said to build trust; its absence ("staring around") distracts others and erodes earned faith.
- **Open vs. closed body language balance** — the principle that a leader must avoid both extremes: too much openness reads as arrogance, too much closure (e.g., a lowered head) reads as doubt, so the leader should relax and consciously control the message their body sends.
- **Power poses** — an example of "too open" body language: poses meant to project confidence that can instead be perceived as arrogant.
- **Reasoning (influence tactic)** — persuading by explaining facts and demonstrating them with confidence, characterized by the word "because"; the author advises using quality reasons over quantity and accounting for even trivial details.
- **Inspire (influence tactic)** — persuading through emotion: enthusiasm, active/involving language, confident tone, and vivid pictures of an accomplished future, aimed at showing each person how they can contribute.
- **Ask (influence tactic)** — persuading high-power individuals by withholding solutions and instead asking them how to resolve the problem, letting them "persuade themselves" to work with you; attributed as a technique used by journalists, lawyers, and vehicle salespeople.
- **Leading questions** — a sub-technique within "Ask" that the author warns can backfire if overused, urging the leader to evaluate the honesty of their queries.
- **Myers-Briggs personality test** — a personality-typing test the author says some industries require, useful for self-recognition and as a conversation starter, and framed as a first step toward leading teams.
- **Recognizing your team** — knowing every team member's individual traits (since a team of one personality type is undesirable) so their differences fit together like puzzle pieces.
- **Accepting feedback** — a leader's willingness to receive and act on team notes about their own missteps, framed against the premise that no leader is infallible.
- **Case study learning** — a teaching method in which students watch or read about a specific case, then work individually or in groups toward a solution, guided by outlines, a clear introduction, context ("like a movie preview or book summary"), and a challenge relevant to their lives.
- **Poor integrity** — a bad-leader trait defined as failing to keep one's word; the author claims employees mirror a leader's unacceptable character by acting poorly themselves.
- **Lack of flexibility** — a bad-leader trait: sticking to one leadership style regardless of situation, versus a good leader's willingness to adapt style to different employees' motivations.
- **Cult leaders** — described as combining fringe beliefs, a propensity for violence, and charisma; the author narrows this to two common traits, narcissism and charisma.
- **Narcissistic trait (of cult leaders)** — a pattern of believing oneself superior from childhood, being demanding and ego-driven, self-casting as "the hero," and treating others as instrumental pawns.
- **Charismatic trait (of cult leaders)** — a complex quality spanning talking style, dress, and treatment of followers, marked by an ability to convince anyone of anything and to command attention.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Case Study: Teacher** — a classroom scenario where the teacher shows students a video of a specific case, then has them work in groups or individually toward a solution, using a provided guideline. The author lists supporting project types (journaling, interviews, student discussion) and four setup tips: provide outlines proportional to students' skill level; give a clear introduction so students can express different learning styles; give context (compared explicitly to "a movie preview or a book summary"); and pick a challenge relevant to students' lives that is complex enough to yield varied solutions. This illustrates case-study learning as a leadership/teaching tool.
- **Case Study: Coach** — two vignettes under coaching: (1) "Stunning Characters," where coaching engagements focus on an administrator's weaker leadership skills, potentially exposing character flaws or blind spots, framed as a positive platform for changing one's style; (2) "Growing Business," where coaching helps CEOs and entrepreneurs and small-business owners grow their companies despite challenges. These illustrate coaching as an influence/leadership-development mechanism.
- **Case Study: Promotion** — a marketing-oriented pair of tips: (1) maintain a dedicated, easy-to-find case-studies page (labeled "case studies" or "success studies") with clear challenge/process/outcome structure; (2) place case studies on the homepage itself (e.g., client testimonials, calls to action) to show visitors evidence of happy clients. This illustrates using case studies as a persuasive/promotional tool rather than a teaching one.

## Thread
This chapter shifts the book's register from individual manipulation tactics toward organizational leadership, packaging influence as a managerial skill set (expectation-setting, encouragement, body language, the Reasoning/Inspire/Ask tactics, personality-type awareness). It builds directly on earlier persuasion material by re-applying general influence tactics to a workplace/team context, and it reuses "case study" as a recurring format (teaching, coaching, marketing) the book leans on elsewhere. Its final turn — from "what makes a bad leader" straight into cult leaders defined by narcissism and charisma — sets up a bridge to the book's darker material on the Dark Triad and manipulative personality types, implying that the same charisma prized in leaders is also the engine of cult control when combined with narcissism and fringe belief.
