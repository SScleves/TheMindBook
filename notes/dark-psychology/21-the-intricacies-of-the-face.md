# The Intricacies of the Face

## In one paragraph
William Cooper opens Chapter 21 by arguing that face-to-face analysis is the easiest form of behavioral reading because the face and head give off the clearest nonverbal cues. He walks through the literal anatomy of the head and face (bones, nerves, blood supply) before turning to the practical skill: decoding "standard facial signals" tied to universal emotions, micro-expressions, and the specific behavior of eyebrows, eyes, and mouth. The bulk of the chapter is then organized as a long catalogue of "common signs" for named emotional/relational states — happiness, sadness, anxiety, fear, anger, envy/jealousy, disinterest, interest, boredom, searching for relief, stress, disgust, shame, pity, and calmness — each broken into a short numbered list of tells or symptoms. The chapter closes with a section on emotions and appearance, a short note on trustworthiness, and a return to pure anatomy (chin, mouth, nose, eyes) that describes the eye's visible structures in detail. Overall this chapter functions as a reference catalogue rather than a narrative, teaching the reader to read faces and to recognize emotional/psychological states in themselves and others.

## Core claims
- William Cooper claims analyzing human behavior is "much easier" in person because the face and head display the easiest-to-follow nonverbal cues.
- He claims that reading someone's facial features can give access to their "innermost thoughts," and that this skill is valuable both professionally (e.g., watching reactions to a pitch in real time) and personally.
- He cautions that knowing what someone feels does not mean knowing why — a closed-off posture could stem from an unrelated bad day or unrelated bad news, not from anything about you.
- He claims there are "universal emotions" — worldwide facial expressions that cross cultural borders — and that even blind people display similar facial expressions for these emotions, which he lists as surprise, disgust, anger, fear, contempt, sadness, and happiness.
- He claims micro-expressions are emotions that appear and vanish almost instantly, that they are picked up subconsciously, and that the subconscious will register threatening behavior in others even when the conscious mind "would rather not know."
- He attributes specific meanings to eyebrow position (raised/arched = surprise, lowered/knit = anger, inner corners drawn up = sadness), eye behavior (wide open = surprise, intense stare = anger, crow's-feet crinkles = happiness, blinking = fear/romantic feeling, rapid blinking = dishonesty or stress), and mouth behavior (dropped jaw = surprise, open-mouth gasp = fear, one-sided raise = hatred, corners up/down = happiness/its opposite, lip and cheek biting = anxiety, pursed lips = distaste, covering the mouth = keeping a secret).
- For each of fourteen named emotional/relational states, he lists concrete behavioral or physiological "signs," several of which he backs with cited (but not further sourced beyond a note number) statistics.
- He argues appearance and self-image are tied to emotional health, offering advice (reject conventional beauty ideals, live in the moment, allow negative emotion, smile) as a route to being perceived as, and becoming, happier and more approachable.
- He asserts trustworthy people are "always surrounded by loyal friends" and that honest individuals are "in short supply."
- He closes with anatomical description, noting the chin is "an important element of our face but does not (itself) contain the ability to emote," while the eyes are called "one of the most expressive parts of the human face."

## Concepts
- **the head** — the anatomical structure containing the skull and attached bone/cartilage (including the lower jaw), connected to the spinal column by the cervical vertebra/atlas, protecting the brain; weighs 5–11 pounds (2.3–5.0kg); supplied with blood via internal and external carotid arteries plus vertebral arteries to the scalp, and innervated by twelve pairs of cranial nerves. The author also frames the head's organs (eyes, ears, nose, tongue) as the body's "processing center": data is collected through these orifices and interpreted by the brain, with humans able to process it quickly thanks to this central nerve cluster.
- **the face** — the front portion of the head, consisting of the eyes, nose, and mouth (with the mouth bordered by fleshy tissue around the oral cavity); described by the author as a "distinctive feature" that aids identification of individuals and reflects family resemblance while remaining unique to each person. He adds that humans have "short and retracted faces underneath a huge braincase," that studying re-created faces of ancient ancestors helps us understand our connection to them, and that the face is "a window to deeply complex creatures" — we memorize others' faces to recognize them later, and use our own to signal elation or repulsion, with emotions radiating from us "like a ripple through a disturbed lake."
- **cutaneous innervation of the head** — the author's term for how face/scalp sensation is delivered, given via branches of the trigeminal nerve and broken into five named nerve pathways.
- **maxillary nerve** — one of the five listed pathways providing cutaneous (skin) sensation to the head.
- **cervical plexus** — one of the five listed pathways providing cutaneous sensation to the head.
- **ophthalmic nerve** — one of the five listed pathways providing cutaneous sensation to the head.
- **dorsal rami of cervical nerves** — one of the five listed pathways providing cutaneous sensation to the head.
- **mandibular nerve** — one of the five listed pathways providing cutaneous sensation to the head.
- **facial skeleton** — the fourteen solo bones the author says form the face, supporting the respiratory, digestive, olfactory, and visual systems; he ties its evolutionary history to roughly 6 million years of hominin development, noting continued uncertainty in how the modern human face arose despite new fossil discoveries.
- **universal emotions** — the author's term (attributed to unspecified "research") for facial expressions that appear across cultures and even in blind individuals; listed as surprise, disgust, anger, fear, contempt, sadness, and happiness.
- **micro-expressions** — the author's term for emotional facial displays that appear and disappear almost instantly; described as instinctual, subconscious "blimps on your radar" that flag threatening behavior in others even without conscious awareness.
- **eyebrows (as emotional signal)** — facial region the author says people can't help but react with; raised/arched brows signal surprise, lowered/knit brows signal anger, and inner corners drawn up signal sadness.
- **eyes (as emotional signal)** — per the author, wide-open eyes signal surprise, an intense stare signals anger, crow's-feet crinkling signals happiness, blinking signals fear or romantic feeling, and rapid blinking signals dishonesty or stress.
- **mouth (as emotional signal)** — per the author, a dropped jaw signals surprise, an open-mouthed gasp signals fear, a one-sided raise signals hatred, upturned/downturned corners signal happiness/its opposite, lip-and-cheek biting signals anxiety, pursed lips signal distaste, and covering the mouth signals concealing a secret.
- **signs of happiness** — the author's four-part list for inviting/recognizing happiness: living with integrity (per one's own limits, beliefs, and values), living in the moment rather than dwelling on the past, showing daily gratitude, and finding satisfaction/meaning in one's work or activities.
- **signs of sadness** — the author's four listed indicators: changes in weight/appetite, changes in sleep pattern, use of drugs/alcohol to cope, and fatigue.
- **signs of anxiety** — the author's four listed indicators: excessive worry, agitation (sweaty hands, dry mouth, racing pulse, lightheadedness, depersonalization, shakiness — tied to the "fight or flight" response), restlessness (especially in children/teens), and fatigue.
- **signs of fear** — the author's four listed indicators: heartburn (from excess stomach acid), lack of sense of humor, skin rashes/hives (often on the back), and exhaustion.
- **signs of anger** — a ten-item list including calling others demeaning names, nitpicking self and others, impatience, past abuse-related trauma, irritability/short temper, blame-shifting and difficulty accepting responsibility, physical aggression toward objects/pets/people, others "walking on eggshells" around you, others avoiding raising complex issues with you, and others appearing scared/closed-off in your presence.
- **signs of envy or jealousy** — four listed indicators: false commitments/outward pleasantness masking backhanded criticism, failure to appreciate others' success (belittling progress), boastfulness about one's own accomplishments and "one-upping" others, and anxious/over-competitiveness including attempts to defame more accomplished people.
- **signs of disinterest** — four listed indicators (framed around romantic partners): loss of sexual desire, absence of future-oriented "fantasy"/planning together, going out often without the partner (beyond the normal ~two-year mark reclaiming of personal space), and a lonely feeling/craving for connection.
- **signs of interest** — four listed indicators: breaking away from one's friend group toward a new person, "falling in" (leaning toward someone during conversation), "seeking answers" (asking many questions to fill in gaps about the other person), and facial exploration (holding a steady gaze/eye contact on someone's face).
- **signs of boredom** — four listed indicators: spending most of one's time on the phone, sleeping a lot, loss of personal interest in previously loved things, and daydreaming.
- **signs of searching for relief** — a grouped list of cognitive symptoms (memory issues, poor concentration, poor judgment, fixation on adversity, anxiety, constant worry), physical symptoms (dizziness, chest pain, increased heart rate, loss of sex urge, increased illness, constipation, aches), behavioral symptoms (over/under-eating, over/under-sleeping, neglecting duty, drug/alcohol use), and emotional signs (unhappiness, agitation, anger, isolation, mental distress).
- **signs of stress** — three listed indicators: being overemotional (crying frequently), overworking (as an avoidance behavior), and being chronically moody.
- **signs of disgust** — three listed indicators: anger (from feeling unjustly treated), fear (worry over consequences), and sadness (frustration/being overwhelmed by circumstances).
- **signs of shame** — three listed indicators: wanting to disappear/withdraw from people, reacting with anger and blame-shifting, and turning to addiction (drugs) for temporary relief from guilt.
- **signs of pity (self-pity)** — three listed indicators: struggling with everything (relationships, health, finances), distorted/warped perception of one's life as worse than it is, and depression.
- **signs of calmness** — three listed indicators: handling issues as they arise without anticipating problems, treating oneself kindly (not dwelling on past mistakes), and staying "always happy even when things are not okay" by giving oneself emotional space.
- **emotions and appearance** — the author's four-part guidance connecting emotional state to self-presentation: rejecting conventional/advertised beauty ideals, shifting focus off physical appearance and onto personal substance, allowing oneself to feel bad (embracing negative emotion as natural and growth-producing), and smiling (described as infectious and as making a person more approachable).
- **trustworthiness** — a short section asserting that trustworthy people are surrounded by loyal friends, that one will be "hurt from time to time" but the resulting experience is worthwhile, and that honest individuals are in short supply.
- **the chin** — anatomical description: a bony attachment to the face connected to the jaw, enabling chewing and talking movement; the author notes it does not itself carry emotional expression.
- **the mouth (anatomy)** — described as the shared entrance to the digestive and respiratory systems, lined with mucous membranes that are pink when healthy, with paler gums fitting closely around the teeth.
- **the nose** — described as the structure between the eyes serving as entrance to the respiratory tract, containing the olfactory organ, providing breathing air and the sense of smell.
- **the eyes (anatomy)** — described as slightly asymmetrical globes about an inch in diameter, called by the author "one of the most expressive parts of the human face."
- **iris** — per the author, the colored part of the eye.
- **cornea** — per the author, the clear dome covering the iris.
- **pupil** — per the author, the part of the eye that lets light inside.
- **sclera** — per the author, the white part of the eye.
- **conjunctiva** — per the author, a thick layer covering the front of the eye except for the cornea.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Re-created ancestor faces**: the author points to re-created faces of human ancestors as a way to understand our connection to them, arguing ancient civilizations pursued the same happiness and partnership we do today and that, despite dramatically changed communication, "we are the same at our core."
- **Blind individuals and universal emotions**: the author states that even blind people display facial expressions similar to sighted people for the seven universal emotions (surprise, disgust, anger, fear, contempt, sadness, happiness), offered as evidence these expressions are innate rather than learned by observation.
- **Professional pitch scenario**: the author gives the example of "watch[ing] others react to your professional pitches, in real-time" as a practical application of reading facial signals, framing facial-reading skill as something that lets a person "ace tough situations" by monitoring others' real-time reactions.
- **National Sleep Foundation study (cited, sadness section)**: the author cites a study attributed to "the national sleep foundation" claiming individuals with insomnia are "ten times more likely to be depressed" than those without the condition.
- **Anxiety and Depression Association of America study (cited, sadness section)**: the author cites a study claiming that "in every five people, there is one person that uses drugs to cope with depression."
- **Fatigue-depression statistic (cited, sadness section)**: the author cites unspecified "research" claiming 90% of individuals with depression also experience fatigue.
- **Anxiety-and-age statistic (cited, anxiety section)**: the author cites unspecified "studies" claiming people around age 65 are at high risk from anxiety disorders.
- **128-children restlessness study (cited, anxiety section)**: the author cites "a study done to 128 children with anxiety disorder" finding that 74% of them showed restlessness, used to support restlessness as a childhood/teen sign of anxiety.
- **Girls-dancing-together scenario (interest section)**: the author describes women beginning a night out dancing together as a group for "comradery and protection," and frames a woman breaking away from her group toward a new person as a sign of romantic interest and comfort.
- **Two-year-mark relationship scenario (disinterest section)**: the author describes a normal relationship milestone where partners begin reclaiming personal space and going out separately "around the two-year mark," presented as a benchmark for distinguishing normal independence from a genuine disinterest sign.

## Thread
This chapter narrows the book's broader treatment of nonverbal reading down to a single instrument: the face. It builds directly on the premise (stated at the top of the chapter) that in-person analysis is easier because facial/head cues are the "easiest to follow," positioning the face as a foundational data source for the deception-detection and behavioral-reading skills the book teaches elsewhere. Structurally, it moves from hard anatomy (bones, nerves, blood supply) to applied signal-reading (universal emotions, micro-expressions, eyebrows/eyes/mouth), then into an extended reference catalogue of fourteen named emotional/relational states with their behavioral tells — material a reader would use to diagnose emotional states in themselves or a counterpart during a live interaction. It closes by looping back to anatomy (chin, mouth, nose, eyes), bookending the applied material with the same anatomical register it opened with.
