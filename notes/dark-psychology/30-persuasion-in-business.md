# Persuasion in Business

## In one paragraph
This chapter applies persuasion techniques to three business contexts: sales, marketing, and customer service. In sales, William Cooper lists seven techniques (credited to a numbered source, "[81]") a salesperson can use to close deals, including finding common ground, displaying advantages, and using scarcity. In marketing, he restates a set of six persuasion principles (reciprocity, authority, social proof, commitment, liking, scarcity), and for two of them — liking and scarcity — poses the marketer's task as an open question about their own brand. In customer service, he shifts to specific verbal tactics — building rapport, using present-tense action language, keeping ideas comprehensible, and repeating ideas in varied phrasing — as tools for persuading customers who are already in a relationship with a company. The throughline is that persuasion is not a single move but a toolkit adapted to the stage of the business relationship (acquiring a customer, shaping brand perception, or retaining/servicing an existing one).

## Core claims
- William Cooper claims that understanding persuasion techniques gives salespeople "a lead over the market's competitors," not just the ability to close individual sales.
- He claims a salesperson "should never argue with their customers" and should instead find common ground, remain "optimistic, enthusiastic, quick, and polite," and "strive to be lovable."
- He claims salespeople should avoid offering discounts to display advantages, because doing so "makes you look desperate."
- He claims customer objections should be treated as an opportunity: a salesperson with "a good understanding of the goods" can turn criticism into an explanation of how the product eliminates the customer's pain points.
- He claims the commitment and consistency principle works by getting a customer to commit to something small first, after which "a bigger step could possibly be approved at a later date," because people want to act consistently with prior commitments.
- He claims the reciprocity principle means customers feel obliged to return a favor, so a salesperson who "goes the extra mile" earns future orders through a "feeling of mutual support."
- He claims people who lack enough information to decide on their own will follow others' example, which is why showing client logos, testimonials, and celebrity endorsements persuades prospects.
- He claims the scarcity principle (telling people they may lose access to a product or must wait longer for it) can drive quick sales, but the salesperson "should be sincere on time limits and quantity limitations."
- In marketing, he claims reciprocity works by making customers "feel happy about something," since marketing "strives to create good feelings... rather than pointing out negative issues."
- He claims authority persuades because "most people tend to follow credible leaders" — illustrated by preferring a 20-year real-estate professional with a reputable brand over an unknown individual ("Peter") selling a flat.
- He claims the social proof principle works because "people tend to do what others do," and it is easier to show people what others have done than to tell them what to do.
- He claims commitment in marketing means letting customers "have their say" in what a brand offers, which lets a business "snowball" its advertising once customers are invested — commitment being "the foundation of a good relationship."
- He claims liking persuades because "we are narcissistic creatures" who buy more from people who "complement and value us," provided the appreciation feels genuine.
- He claims scarcity in marketing works because "people love to buy products that others can't purchase," so the marketer's task is to make the brand "look and feel extremely rare and scarce."
- In customer service, he claims shared experience, needs, or frustration make a customer "more likely to agree with and be open to" a representative, and that rapport makes persuasion "unbelievably effective and rather simple" — provided the representative is genuine.
- He claims speaking in the present tense with action words persuades more effectively than speaking in the past tense, helping customers move from past disappointment "towards solutions here and now."
- He claims comprehensible plans, ideas, and approaches convince customers more, and that quickly bringing about understanding boosts persuasive power.
- He claims repetition is one of the most effective customer-service persuasion tools when the representative varies the phrasing so the customer is not annoyed by the repeated insistence.

## Concepts
- **Common ground** — a sales technique of identifying shared interests or areas of connection with a prospective customer, asking about their criteria, and being "genuinely aware" of them, paired with being optimistic, enthusiastic, quick, polite, and likeable, since "people purchase products from people they like."
- **Display the advantages** — a sales technique of showing the major benefits of a product or service in light of the customer's specific priorities and expectations, and showing why buying from the salesperson beats buying from competitors, without resorting to discounts.
- **Transform objections into strengths** — a sales technique of using deep product knowledge to answer customer complaints or criticism by explaining how the product's benefits eliminate the customer's pain points and satisfy their needs.
- **Commitment and consistency principle** — in sales, getting a customer to commit to something small (or take a small first action), after which they can be led to a bigger commitment later, relying on people's tendency to act consistently with earlier actions; in marketing, letting customers "have their say" in what the brand, product, or service offers, so the business can "snowball" its advertising once customers are committed — commitment being "the foundation of a good relationship."
- **Reciprocity principle** — the idea that people feel obliged to return a favor; in sales, a salesperson who does extra for a customer earns future business; in marketing, it means creating positive feelings in customers so they feel inclined to reciprocate.
- **Providing ample information and support** — a sales technique based on the claim that people without enough information to decide on their own will follow others, implemented by showing client logos, testimonials, and celebrity endorsements.
- **Celebrity endorsement** — named in the chapter as "a good example" of the providing-information-and-support tactic, i.e., using a celebrity's association with a product to supply social proof to undecided buyers.
- **Scarcity principle** — telling people they risk losing access to a product or must wait longer for it, used (in sales) to drive quick decisions, and (in marketing) framed as the challenge of making a brand feel rare and hard to obtain.
- **Authority principle** (marketing) — the claim that people tend to follow credible leaders or trusted brands, so buying from an authority (e.g., an experienced, branded professional) feels safer than buying from an unknown individual.
- **Social proof principle** (marketing) — the claim that people do what others do, so showing prospects what others have already done is more persuasive than instructing them directly.
- **Liking principle** (marketing) — the claim that people buy more from those they like, framed around a claimed human tendency toward narcissism (wanting to be complimented and valued), with genuineness as the key challenge for the marketer.
- **Rapport building** (customer service) — connecting with a customer over shared experiences, needs, or frustrations, or genuinely trying to see things from their perspective, making the customer more open to being persuaded.
- **Present-tense action words** — a customer-service language technique of framing statements in the present tense with active language (rather than the past tense) to help move the customer toward a solution "here and now."
- **Comprehensibility** — the claim that customers are persuaded more by plans, ideas, and approaches they can easily understand, so quickly generating understanding increases persuasive power.
- **Repetition technique** — repeating the same underlying idea or suggestion to a customer multiple times using different phrasing, so the message reinforces itself without becoming annoying through literal repetition.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The "Peter" example (authority)** — the author contrasts buying a flat from "a person named Peter" versus "a real estate professional who has over 20 years' experience with a reputable brand backing him up," to illustrate why people trust authority/credibility over an unbranded individual.
- **Client logos and testimonials (providing information and support)** — the author gives showing "the logos of current clients and their testimonials" and using celebrity endorsements as concrete ways to supply social proof to prospects who lack enough information to decide alone.
- **Closed online community invitation (liking)** — as an example of demonstrating genuine appreciation, the author suggests inviting customers "to a closed online community and ask their thoughts and suggestions on what you are doing."
- **Present-tense action-language examples** — to illustrate present-tense, action-oriented persuasion, the author gives three sample lines not tied to any named product: "Purchasing this item saves you money in the long run," "Putting your order in now will ensure that it's installed promptly," and "it's not hard to make room for the product in your home."
- **Coffee-maker repetition example** — to illustrate repetition without annoyance, the author uses the case of convincing a client that a coffee maker saves them time in the morning, restating that idea in different words as: "You'll have more time to get ready for work in the morning because the coffee maker automatically brews your coffee," noting this "restates the same idea, but without actually using the same words."
- **Citation marker "[81]"** — the sales-techniques list is explicitly attributed to a numbered outside source in the text, though the source itself is not named within the chapter.
