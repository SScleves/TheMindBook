# Personality Types

## In one paragraph
William Cooper surveys mainstream personality theory as background for reading people: he opens with competing academic definitions of "personality," then walks through what he calls the "four categories of personality" — introvert/extrovert, sensing/intuition, thinking/feeling, and judging/perceiving — drawing on Carl Jung's typology and naming thirteen of the type combinations (ISTJ, ISFJ, etc. — the eight sensing types in full, but only five of the eight intuitive types) with their popular nicknames and traits. Each dichotomy is illustrated with a short case study of fictional or generic people (Mike and Sarah; a lost stranger asking directions; Dan and Steve at a law office; Susan and Angie). The chapter then turns to the nature-versus-environment question, citing a Minnesota twin study, before closing with the four ancient "temperaments" (melancholic, choleric, phlegmatic, sanguine) rooted in humourism. The chapter functions as descriptive groundwork — a taxonomy of "normal" personality — rather than a manipulation technique itself, setting up later chapters that presumably build on how personality differences can be read and exploited.

## Core claims
- Cooper states that despite different academic definitions, all descriptions of personality share common ground: patterns of behavior and characteristics that predict what a person will do.
- He presents Jung's introvert/extrovert distinction as being about how each type "regains energy" — introverts via quiet, extroverts via people — not about shyness or self-esteem, which he calls a misconception.
- He claims a "recent study" found that when extroverts take risks and succeed, they get a dopamine boost, and that this is "precisely what extroverts crave."
- He states that sensing people make up the largest percentage of the population and that sensing individuals are "also likely to be female."
- He claims most people with phlegmatic temperament are introverts and mostly female.
- He claims men are "mostly considered" to have the choleric temperament because testosterone is dominant in them.
- On personality generally, he states it was long believed personality changes very little over a lifetime, but says a few psychologists have disproven this — small shifts do occur (e.g. an introvert becomes "a bit" more outgoing without turning into an extrovert, and vice versa).
- Separately, of temperament specifically (not personality in general), he asserts it is "formed from birth and remains the same throughout the life of a person," governed by how a person's brain stem processes things.
- Citing the Minnesota twin study, he concludes that "both genetics and the environment affect a person's personality."
- He claims identical twins in that study shared about 50% of traits versus about 20% for fraternal twins, taken as evidence that genetics play a role but do not fully determine personality.

## Concepts
- **Personality** — defined via three cited sources: Randy Larsen and David Buss (a static collection of patterns of thoughts, behaviors, and feelings that influences interaction with the environment, formed from genes and environment); Feist and Feist, 2009 (a unique characteristic and pattern of permanent traits giving behavior consistency and individuality); and Funder (all patterns of thoughts, emotions, and behavior plus every hidden psychological mechanism influencing those patterns).
- **Introvert** — a personality type (per Carl Jung, 1960) that regains energy in quiet environments; often mistaken for shyness or low self-esteem, but Cooper (citing Susan Cain) says introverts are typically warm, influential, capable of profound insight, and prone to expressing themselves through outlets like writing.
- **Extrovert** — the counterpart type that regains energy by being around people, is outgoing, seeks to be the center of attention, talks through problems rather than withdrawing, and is more willing to take risks.
- **Sensing** — one pole of how people process information (Jung); sensing people attend to their immediate environment and concrete sensory information (sight, sound, touch), are practical and active, and rely on past experience before deciding. Described as the majority trait and more common in females.
- **Intuition** — the opposing information-processing style; intuitive people look beyond present limits, question things, ask "what if," and are suited to deep thinking, innovation, and seeing "the bigger picture," but per the case study may be poor at concrete practical tasks like remembering directions ("daydreamers").
- **ISTJ (The Assessor)** — introversion, sensing, thinking, judging; responsible, reliable, quiet, serious, takes time to assess situations logically before concluding; often successful in business and personal affairs.
- **ISFJ (The Guardian)** — introversion, sensing, feeling, judging; cordial, quiet, responsible, trustworthy, meets deadlines, produces quality work, loyal, values tradition, likes neat/harmonious surroundings.
- **ISTP (The Expert)** — introversion, sensing, thinking, perceiving; flexible, tolerant, logical, does not involve emotion in thinking, focuses on critical facts/details before concluding.
- **ISFP (The Artist)** — introversion, sensing, feeling, perceiving; lives in the moment, goes with the flow, can seem hard to know but is friendly and kindhearted once known, creative, often drawn to the arts.
- **ESTJ (The Overseer)** — extroverted, sensing, thinking, judging; hardworking, diligent, strong-willed, sees challenges through to completion.
- **ESTP (The Persuader)** — extroverted, sensing, thinking, perceiving; gets people on board and makes things happen, lively, jovial, suited to harsh/critical situations, doesn't take criticism to heart.
- **ESFJ (The Supporter)** — extroverted, sensing, feeling, judging; friendly, good at following rules and caring for others, wants to be liked, strong desire to help/please, generally popular.
- **ESFP (The Entertainer)** — extroverted, sensing, feeling, perceiving; outgoing, loves fun, shares adventures, likable, attracts people through charm and warmth.
- **ENTJ (The Chief)** — extroverted, intuitive, thinking, judging; decisive, hardworking, enjoys debating and analyzing, goal-focused.
- **ENTP (The Originator)** — extroverted, intuitive, thinking, judging (as given in the text); innovative, sees possibilities everywhere, inventive.
- **ENFJ (The Mentor)** — extroverted, intuitive, feeling, judging; cares about others, wants to help them reach potential, considers multiple possibilities in challenges, seeks meaningful relational connection.
- **ENFP (The Advocate)** — extroverted, intuitive, feeling, perceiving; enthusiastic, charismatic leader, resourceful, goes the distance for what they believe.
- **INTJ (The Strategist)** — introverted, intuitive, thinking, judging; enjoys planning, strives for perfection, adaptable/flexible despite being a planner.
- **Thinking** — a mode of judging (Jung) based in logic ("the mind"); thinkers make firm decisions and don't reverse them, appear professional and focused at work, and may seem coldhearted though Cooper says this doesn't mean they lack emotion.
- **Feeling** — the opposing mode of judging, based in emotion ("the heart"); feelers weigh how choices affect others and social settings, are more social and likable at work, and are more open to people — which also makes them get hurt more easily.
- **Judging** — a preference for organization, order, structure, planning tasks to completion, and observing deadlines.
- **Perceiving** — a preference for keeping options open and staying flexible; perceivers enjoy the start of a task more than its completion and are curious explorers of their environment.
- **Nature vs. environment (personality stability)** — Cooper's discussion of whether personality is fixed or changeable; he states it was long believed personality changes little over a lifetime, but some psychologists have shown small shifts occur, e.g., an introvert can become "a bit" more outgoing without becoming an extrovert, and vice versa, due to life experiences.
- **Minnesota twin study** — a study (per the text, 1979–1999) of 350 twins raised apart, used as evidence that both genetics and environment shape personality.
- **Humourism** — the ancient Greek medical concept underlying the four temperaments; the idea that bodily fluids ("humors") are responsible for a person's temperament. Cooper says temperament is set from birth, stable for life, and rooted in how a person's brain stem processes things.
- **Melancholic temperament** — social yet thorough and accurate, respectful of tradition (e.g., traditional gender-role courtesies), well-suited to administration, management, and social work.
- **Choleric temperament** — focused, goal-oriented, logic-driven, seen as unfriendly and not fun by others, impatient, quick to anger, aggressive, self-centered, and neglectful of nurturing relationships; Cooper links it more to men via testosterone.
- **Phlegmatic temperament** — mostly introverted and, per Cooper, mostly female; rule-following, dislikes rule-breaking, prioritizes making others happy and puts others' needs first even at personal cost; high emotional intelligence and empathy, but prone to being taken advantage of.
- **Sanguine temperament** — adventurous, lively, cheerful, sociable, creative, easy at making friends; but struggles to complete or even remember tasks/responsibilities, talks more than listens, and will readily drop people who aren't "fun."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Mike and Sarah (introvert/extrovert case study)** — Two seniors at Millbrook High School who attended school together since first grade but rarely interacted. Mike is quiet and reserved, prefers staying home reading or working on projects, and avoids loud parties with unfamiliar people. Sarah is talkative, attends every party, and interacts with almost everyone, readily accepting weekend invitations. Illustrates the introvert/extrovert energy-source contrast in ordinary social behavior.
- **The lost stranger (sensing/intuition case study)** — A person walks an unfamiliar road from town "A" to town "B"; a stranger heading the opposite way asks for directions to a shop back toward town "A." A sensing-type person gives clear, accurate directions based on attention to the route just traveled. An intuitive-type person asked the same, even having used the road before, struggles to recall directions, illustrating intuitives as "daydreamers" who "hardly pay attention to reality."
- **Dan and Steve (thinking/feeling case study)** — Two coworkers at a Chicago law office. Their colleague Miriam, a litigator, shares that she is newly engaged. Dan congratulates her briefly and heads straight to his office (the thinker, prioritizing efficiency/logic over the social moment); Steve stays to listen fully because he wants to know more and feels it would be rude to leave (the feeler, prioritizing the relational/emotional impact).
- **Susan and Angie (judging/perceiving case study)** — Two college-friend coworkers newly employed at the same firm, given a three-day assignment with a presentation. Angie (the judger) immediately plans the work and finishes two days early. Susan (the perceiver), whose job allows remote flexibility, spends the first three days visiting friends and doing other things, only starting the assignment two days before the deadline after "energizing" herself. Illustrates the planning/structure vs. flexible/last-minute-energy contrast.
- **Minnesota twin study (nature vs. environment)** — Study of 350 twins who grew up apart, conducted between 1979 and 1999, including both identical and fraternal twins. Both types showed shared traits despite separation, but identical twins shared about 50% of traits versus about 20% for fraternal twins. Cooper cites this as evidence that both genetics and environment shape personality.

## Thread
This chapter is descriptive scaffolding rather than a manipulation "technique" chapter: it establishes a shared vocabulary of personality (Jungian dichotomies, the sixteen-type combinations, and the four ancient temperaments) that Cooper can presumably draw on in later chapters when discussing how to read, predict, or exploit different personality types in manipulation and persuasion contexts. It follows the book's broader pattern of grounding manipulation tactics in psychological theory (as seen with concepts like the Dark Triad elsewhere in the book) before showing how such theory is used tactically.
