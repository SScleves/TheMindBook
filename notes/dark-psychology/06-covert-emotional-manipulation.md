# 7 Powerful Covert Emotional Manipulation Techniques

## In one paragraph
This chapter is William Cooper's catalog of seven covert techniques for manipulating people through their emotions rather than their logic. He opens by widening the emotional palette beyond fear — already discussed earlier in the book — to greed and frugality, arguing that the manipulator must know their target for an appeal to "ring true." He then enumerates the seven tactics: emotional blackmail, intimidation, the bait and switch, the blame game, insinuation, triangulation, and the law of state transference. Each is defined and illustrated with everyday scenarios drawn from families, romantic relationships, the workplace, and politics. Throughout, Cooper frames the material defensively: recognizing when you are in a vulnerable position is presented as the way to "guard against unwelcome attempts."

## Core claims
- Cooper claims emotional manipulation works at various levels and through multiple emotions: fear is powerful, but greed ("think of all the money you could make") and frugality ("think of all the money you could save") work just as well when matched to the person.
- He argues the manipulator must know the person they are dealing with in order to make manipulation attempts ring true — targeting is prerequisite to effectiveness.
- He claims manipulators latch onto victims specifically because the victims are in a vulnerable position; recognizing one's own vulnerability is the defense the chapter recommends.
- Cooper says emotional blackmail relies on the subject's emotions rather than their logic; the target believes they are acting of their own free will, but in reality they are responding to the manipulator's scheming.
- He claims empathy and remorse are "the most effective" feelings people can feel and are often enough on their own to motivate the victim to behave as the manipulator needs.
- He argues intimidation is "nothing more than a threat": the manipulator need not act on it, but must be as convincing as possible, and it only works insofar as the target fears possible aggression.
- He warns intimidation can backfire: the manipulator must eventually make good on threats or the victim realizes they are "just talk and no action" and rids themselves of the manipulator.
- Cooper says intimidation is predicated on fear, exploited either through overt physical threats or psychological tactics such as blackmail.
- He claims guilt is "one of the most powerful manipulation techniques known to humankind," usable by making people feel inferior for help they once received or inadequate for a condition they possess.
- He argues able manipulators in the workplace can produce compelling evidence against others to clear their own name, and may preemptively condemn others to confuse them.
- He claims insinuation works by delivering a deliberately crafted, multi-interpretation "compliment" that hurts and sows doubt while giving the manipulator deniability ("it was just a joke").
- He says triangulation introduces a third party into the relationship to confuse the victim about the connection and intensify their attachment to the manipulator; the third party may become the manipulator's next target.
- Cooper states the law of state transference: the person holding the balance of power in a situation can shift their emotional state onto the person they are communicating with — a powerful concept in the hands of a dark persuader.
- He claims matching the target's state establishes a connection "at a deep, subconscious level," and that persuasion success or failure turns on subconscious cues.

## Concepts
- **covert emotional manipulation** — the chapter's subject: exerting undetected influence over a target by triggering and exploiting emotions (fear, greed, frugality, guilt, empathy, remorse) rather than engaging their logic; effective only when tailored to what the specific person cares about.
- **emotional blackmail** — occurs when the manipulator has some type of control over the victim and uses it to extort them; distinct from ordinary blackmail (holding damning information) but "just as damaging." The manipulator may inspire empathy and remorse for themselves, keeping the target under constant risk of emotional pain and suffering so they comply while believing they act freely.
- **intimidation** — "nothing more than a threat": the manipulator finds out what the victim fears most and attacks from that angle, without necessarily acting on the threat, but being as convincing as possible. Predicated on fear; achieved through overt physical threats or psychological tactics such as blackmail. Backfires if the manipulator never delivers.
- **the bait and switch** — the manipulator dangles something others want, then takes it away, laying down rules and conditions the victim must meet for access — while never intending to let the victim actually get it, stringing them along until exhaustion. Less sophisticated manipulators give a "taste" to entice continued compliance. Also: pretending to be someone you are not (e.g., romantically interested) until you extract the benefit, then reverting to your true self.
- **the blame game** — using guilt to manipulate: making people feel inferior for help they received ("remember when you needed my help? Now, I need your help") or inadequate for a condition they possess ("things would be different if you weren't sick"); in the workplace, shifting blame for failures onto others, sometimes with fabricated compelling evidence.
- **guilt card / shame card** — the two moral levers within the blame game: manipulators "play the 'guilt card' or can play the 'shame card' by using morals," including claiming a moral high ground ("I am an expert in what I do") to deflect blame preemptively.
- **insinuation** — a devious technique of undetected mental influence: the "innocent" manipulator makes a deliberately crafted, insinuating statement — usually framed as a veiled "compliment" — designed to have multiple possible interpretations that cause hurt and doubt. If the target takes offense, the manipulator insists it was a joke or an unintentional compliment, leaving the remark to "operate on" the victim and intensify anger and instability.
- **triangulation** — an often-used controller tactic in which a partnership is formed between the victim, the manipulator, and a third party (an old lover, someone from the gym) to confuse the victim about the connection and produce an intense attachment to the manipulator; vague negative comparisons with the third party are "much worse," and the manipulator may view the third party as their next target.
- **the law of state transference** — the idea that the person with the balance of power in a situation can shift their emotional state to the individual they are communicating with; described as a powerful concept when used by a dark persuader.
- **state** — "a term relating to a person's general mood"; a strong congruent state is when a person's thoughts, words, and acts are aligned.
- **state match** — the first phase of state transference: the influencer forces their own state to suit the target's natural state (e.g., matching a sad, slow-talking subject), establishing rapport at a deep, subconscious level.
- **hook point** — the moment when, after the influencer subtly changes their own state (e.g., slightly intensifying tone of voice), the target follows along; conformity at this test shows the hook point is reached, after which the influencer can move the subject into whatever state — positive or negative — best suits their purposes.
- **rapport** — the deep, subconscious connection produced by state matching; Cooper calls state matching "another means of establishing rapport."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Greed and frugality openers** — For an ambitious, greedy person, "think of all the money you could make" drives them "over the edge"; for an overly frugal person, "think of all the money you could save" hits their sweet spot. Shows that the manipulator must know the target for the appeal to ring true.
- **The ex-addict blackmail scenario** — The manipulator knows the victim is insecure about a past of addiction and one or two stints in rehab, and that the victim wants to turn over a new leaf. The manipulator threatens to bring up that past every time they want something. Cooper uses it to answer "why doesn't the victim just get away?": the manipulator latches on precisely because the victim is in a vulnerable position.
- **The spider-fearing sibling** — One sibling is afraid of spiders; another uses that fear to make them do the dishes when it isn't their turn. Cooper says this "may seem childish" but is a powerful tool when used correctly — with the catch that the manipulator must eventually make good on threats or the "jig may be up."
- **Easy-money bait and switch** — A manipulator senses someone is desperate to make money and manipulates them with the promise of easy money or a steady income; the victim goes along only to be defrauded at some point. Shows the tactic preys on emotional needs and is not limited to romance.
- **Fake romantic interest** — A person pretends to be romantically interested in a target desperate for love and affection, gets close enough to extract the benefit they seek, then "pulls the bait and switch" and reverts to their true self.
- **The production-line supervisor** — A supervisor makes a mistake that costs the company money. Questioned by upper management, he pins the blame on line workers by claiming a moral high ground ("I am an expert in what I do" — implicitly the workers are not), then shames and guilts them: "you guys let me down after everything I've done for you." Cooper presents this as a rather overt attempt to make others take blame for something they may not be responsible for.
- **Politicians as blame-gamers** — Politicians who find someone else to blame for their country's problems present themselves as saviors and heroes with a solution for everything, yet "when you drill down on their track record, they really don't achieve much of anything."
- **The "prostitute" insinuation** — A partner smiles and says, "What do you know? As a prostitute, you can make a lot of money!" Questioned, he says it was intended as a joke — but Cooper says the victim will tell themselves what it means "for a long time to come," wondering why he cares about prostitution, what he feels about them, and whether his praise of them as a lover was sincere. Such remarks intensify anger, potential disagreements, and partnership instability, while the manipulator keeps the cover of an "unintentional compliment."
- **Triangulation third parties** — The manipulator brings up an old lover the victim keeps thinking about, or "someone she always finds unexpectedly in the case of gyms," creating vague negative comparisons; when confronted, the manipulator says the victim's "real problem is your depression and your low self-worth." Cooper notes the manipulator may strategically view the other man as their next target.
- **State-transference walkthrough** — The influencer first matches a subject who is feeling sad and talking slower than usual, connecting at a deep subconscious level; then subtly intensifies their tone of voice to test compliance. If the target keeps pace, the hook point is reached, and the influencer shifts the subject to whatever state — "nice and positive or angry and negative" — best suits their purposes. Cooper says this illustrates the effect of subconscious cues on persuasion's success or failure.

## Thread
This chapter builds directly on the book's earlier treatment of fear as a manipulation lever ("We have already discussed how fear is a very powerful emotion"), generalizing from fear to a full toolkit of emotional levers and packaging them as seven named tactics. It advances the book's technique-manual arc: after foundational material on emotions and vulnerability, Cooper here supplies the concrete covert repertoire — blackmail, intimidation, bait and switch, blame, insinuation, triangulation, state transference — always paired with the defensive framing that recognizing one's own vulnerable position is the guard against these attempts. The closing technique, the law of state transference with its state-matching, hook point, and subconscious rapport, points forward to the book's later material on rapport-based and subconscious influence methods (NLP, conversational hypnosis).
