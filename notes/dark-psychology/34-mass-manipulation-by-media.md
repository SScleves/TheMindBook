# Ten Strategies of Mass Manipulation by Media

## In one paragraph
This short chapter presents a list, which William Cooper attributes to Noam Chomsky, of ten strategies that governments and other power centers allegedly use through the media to control and manipulate the population. The chapter runs through the list one by one, from distraction and gradual introduction of unpopular measures through to the claim that "the system" now understands individuals better than they understand themselves. Along the way it introduces the "boiled frog principle" as the mechanism behind gradual, invisible change, and the "Problem-Reaction-Solution" method as a way of manufacturing consent for new restrictions. The chapter is presented as a straightforward enumeration rather than a narrative, with each strategy given a short definition and, in a few cases, an illustrative example.

## Core claims
- The author states that Noam Chomsky, described as "the renowned critic and linguist" and "one of the classic voices of intellectual dissent in the last decade," compiled a list of the ten most common and effective strategies for mind control used by governments to manipulate the population through the media [85].
- The author claims that distraction works by continuously shifting public attention to trivial news so that decisions made by political and economic lobbies on vital issues pass into the background unnoticed.
- The author argues that unacceptable measures are made acceptable by introducing them gradually over consecutive years so the change becomes virtually imperceptible, citing the reduction in workers' rights as a real-world example of this.
- The author attributes the gradual strategy's underlying mechanism to the "Boiled Frog Principle," which he says is a metaphorical principle told by Chomsky to describe humans' poor ability to adapt to unpleasant and harmful situations without reacting.
- The author states that the boiled frog phenomenon itself actually dates back to research conducted by "John Hopkins University" in 1882, in which a frog dropped into boiling water jumps out, but a frog placed in cold water that is heated slowly stays in and boils.
- The author claims this boiled-frog dynamic is applied daily in modern society through techniques disguised as "well-being" (better living, health, more products and services, progress, technology) that in fact mask manipulation, and that when change is slow enough to become invisible it escapes consciousness and provokes no reaction, opposition, or revolt.
- The author describes "Problem-Reaction-Solution" as creating or letting a problem escalate (e.g., urban violence or organized terrorist attacks) so that the public itself demands the very security measures — at the cost of freedom — that the instigators wanted to impose.
- The author claims the "strategy of deferring" works by presenting an unpopular decision as "painful but necessary" and securing acceptance for its future application, since a future sacrifice is easier to get accepted than an immediate one, giving the public time to resign themselves to it.
- The author asserts that television messages, especially advertising, address the public as if they were children — using childlike words, arguments, gestures, and intonation — to overcome resistance and neutralize critical sense through suggestibility, and that politicians use the same tactic, sometimes presenting themselves as father figures.
- The author claims that messages from power deliberately target emotion to reach the unconscious, producing a "short circuit" of the rational part of the individual that neutralizes critical capacity and opens the door to implanting ideas, desires, fears, or induced behavior.
- The author states that keeping people ignorant — by not improving education quality and by widening the gap between private and public education — is a method of control, since ignorance prevents people from having their own tools of analysis and drowns curiosity for knowledge.
- The author claims that most fashions and trend brands are not spontaneous but induced and promoted by centers of power to create mass tastes, interests, and opinions, mostly around "ephemeral, unnecessary, and even ridiculous lifestyles."
- The author claims that reinforcing self-blame makes individuals believe their misfortune is due to their own insufficient intelligence or effort, so instead of rebelling against the system they blame themselves, producing a depressive state that inhibits action — and "without action, there is no revolution."
- The author asserts that over the past 60 years, advances in neuroscience and applied psychology have let "the system" come to know individuals better than they know themselves, giving the system more control over people than people have over themselves.

## Concepts
- **strategy-of-distraction** — Continuously shifting public attention to trivial or less important news so that decisions on much more vital issues, made by political and economic lobbies, pass into the background unnoticed.
- **gradual-strategy** — Getting an unacceptable measure accepted by introducing it little by little over consecutive years, so gradually that the change becomes virtually imperceptible and provokes no reaction that a single, sudden implementation would have caused.
- **boiled-frog-principle** — A metaphorical principle, per the author attributed to Chomsky, describing humans' poor ability to adapt to unpleasant and harmful situations without reacting when the change is introduced slowly enough to stay invisible to consciousness; illustrated by frogs jumping out of already-boiling water but staying in water heated slowly from cold until they boil.
- **problem-reaction-solution** — Also called "Create Problems and Offer Solutions"; a method of degenerating a public situation or artificially creating a problem to provoke a public "reaction" so that citizens themselves demand the "solution" the instigator wanted to impose (e.g., new security laws at the expense of freedom).
- **strategy-of-deferring** — Presenting an unpopular decision to citizens as "painful but necessary" and securing immediate acceptance for its application at a future date, since a future sacrifice is easier to get accepted than an immediate one, giving people time to resign themselves to it.
- **treat-people-like-children** — Television messages, especially advertising, address the public using words, arguments, gestures, and intonation as if speaking to a child, to overcome resistance by neutralizing critical sense and leveraging suggestibility; politicians use it too, sometimes posing as father figures.
- **taking-advantage-of-the-emotional-aspect** — Aiming messages at generating emotion to reach the unconscious, producing a "short circuit" on the rational part of the individual that neutralizes critical capacity and opens the unconscious to grafted ideas, desires, fears, or induced behavior.
- **keep-the-public-ignorant** — Exercising control by preventing people from having their own tools of analysis: not raising the quality of education, drowning out curiosity for knowledge, and promoting a strong discrepancy between the quality of private and public education.
- **making-the-public-complacent** — Inducing and promoting fashions and trend brands from a center of power to create waves of mass tastes, interests, and opinions, typically around ephemeral, unnecessary, or ridiculous lifestyles, so people are persuaded to be "in fashion."
- **reinforcing-self-blame** — Making individuals believe they alone are guilty of their own misfortune (due to insufficient intelligence or effort), so instead of rebelling against the system they blame themselves, producing a depressive state that inhibits action and thereby prevents revolt.
- **knowing-people-better-than-they-know-themselves** — The claim that advances in neuroscience and applied psychology over the past 60 years have let the dominant "system" come to understand individuals better than the individuals understand themselves, giving the system greater control over people than people have over themselves.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Reduction in workers' rights** — the author's example of the gradual strategy in action: measures or ways of working implemented in several companies over consecutive years have ended up making it normal that a worker has no guarantee of social protection, changes the author says would have caused a revolution if implemented all at once.
- **The boiled frog experiment** — the author states that research conducted by "John Hopkins University" in 1882 found that a frog dropped into a pot of already-boiling water inevitably jumps out to save itself, whereas a frog placed in a pot of cold water that is then heated slowly and steadily will inevitably end up boiled; the author uses this to ground the boiled-frog principle as more than a metaphor.
- **Urban violence / organized terrorist attacks** — the author's example of Problem-Reaction-Solution: letting urban violence escalate, or organizing terrorist attacks, so that citizens themselves demand that government enact new security laws at the expense of freedom.

## Thread
This chapter shifts the book's focus from interpersonal manipulation tactics (used by individuals such as narcissists, con artists, or persuaders in one-on-one or small-group settings) to manipulation at the societal scale, attributing to Chomsky a specific taxonomy of how governments and media allegedly manage entire populations. It functions as a capstone or bridge before the book's final material (a Bonus chapter and Conclusion), generalizing techniques covered elsewhere in the book — emotional exploitation, exploiting suggestibility, controlling information, inducing self-doubt — onto the scale of mass media and governance, suggesting the same manipulative logic scales from the individual target to the citizenry as a whole.
