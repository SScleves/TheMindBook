# How to Analyze Yourself?

## In one paragraph
This chapter turns the book's lens inward: William Cooper presents self-analysis as the route to self-awareness — understanding your personality, behaviors, and tendencies, including "the unsightly corners of your own mind that you would rather keep locked away." He argues that embracing one's whole being, "even the darkness," is what makes true contentment possible. The chapter is a strategy list organized into three broad movements — being aware of your feelings (noticing thoughts, journaling, checking perceptions, identifying feelings), scrutinizing your values (knowing, identifying, and planning core values via guided questions), and discovering yourself (writing and evaluating your life story, an exercise he links to "narrative therapy"). Cooper closes with practical caveats: self-analysis takes time, the person you are will keep changing, and physical factors like sleep must be tracked because sleep deprivation degrades thinking, mood, and judgment. The overall promise is that deliberate self-analysis lets you "make the rules and steer yourself toward realizing your goals."

## Core claims
- Cooper claims that self-awareness gives you an understanding of yourself, your personality, and your behaviors and tendencies, and that part of the process is accepting the parts of your mind you would rather keep locked away.
- He argues that true contentment is achieved by embracing our whole being, "even the darkness."
- The book says your thoughts are essential in defining who you are: they guide how you feel and your attitude and perceptions of situations, so you should stay in touch with your mind and detect harmful or self-punishing thoughts.
- Cooper claims perceptions can lead you astray into wrong conclusions about what occurred, so you should study the moves and beliefs behind your interpretations.
- He argues your feelings reveal who you are through how you react to situations and people, and that physical cues can help you understand how you feel.
- The book claims that knowing your values gives an overview of who you are at your core; values are beliefs you remain loyal to, usually based on morality, and they change as you learn more about yourself.
- Cooper asserts that "You have always been completely in control of the person you are" — you decide what you wish to embody, and daily routine merely "fogs up the lens" of that ability.
- He claims that writing your own life story and reviewing it (which he says "will be referred to as narrative therapy") highlights the moments you felt essential or worthy and shows how you see yourself and your life's path.
- The book says the genre you unconsciously give your story reveals self-perception: drama means you feel life is dramatic and intense, comedy means you think it has been full of fun, a love story indicates you are a romantic.
- Cooper claims self-analysis takes time and the person you are will change in the days to come.
- He argues lack of sleep encourages stress, raises anxiety above where it should be, and causes a list of harms (slow thinking, accidents, health challenges, depression, forgetfulness, lower libido, faster aging, weight fluctuation, impaired judgment).
- The chapter's closing claim: engaging in self-analysis will assist you in changing into the person you are meant to be — you can live by your own values, make the rules, and steer toward your goals.

## Concepts
- **self-awareness** — Cooper's framing term for the chapter: the understanding of yourself and your personality, plus knowledge of your behaviors and tendencies, achieved partly by accepting the "unsightly corners" of your own mind.
- **notice-your-thoughts** — The strategy of keeping in touch with your mind because thoughts define who you are and guide feelings, attitude, and perceptions; you must be able to tell whether thoughts are harmful, whether you are "pinning yourself down," and in which areas you are hard on yourself.
- **keep-a-journal** — A strategy for staying in tune with your patterns: emotions and reactions are documented, and reviewing the pages gives an objective perspective on your values and consistency.
- **be-conscious-of-your-perceptions** — Recognizing that your perceptions can lead you astray into wrong conclusions; the remedy is to study your moves and beliefs about what happened and write down what you saw, heard, or felt that made you interpret the situation as you did.
- **identify-your-feelings** — Analyzing your feelings and how you respond to different topics, interactions, tonal variations, facial appearances, and body language, so you can tell why you have certain feelings and what directed your choices; physical cues are allowed as aids.
- **know-your-values** — The awareness of what you value, which gives an overview of who you are at your core; Cooper notes the concept can be "intangible and unclear," beliefs are based on individual experiences, and values change as you get to know yourself.
- **identify-your-values** — Defining values as the beliefs you remain loyal to, usually based on morality (e.g., never steal, even when theft would benefit you significantly); done by writing answers to four guided questions about admired people, your future self, passions, and past good deeds.
- **plan-your-core-values** — Writing your identified values down to "create a map," then picking one or two at a time and forming a plan to better embody them — on the premise that you have always been completely in control of the person you are.
- **write-your-story** — Documenting the events that changed you and the beliefs you hold dear, allowing you to look back on your life "like a spectator."
- **evaluate-your-story** — Interrogating the written story with questions about recurring themes, genre, title, chapters, labels, and language used about yourself and others.
- **narrative-therapy** — Cooper's name for authoring your own account for review: it highlights moments in life when you felt essential or worthy and shows the way you see yourself and the path of your life up to where you are.
- **put-it-in-your-mind-that-it-takes-time** — The caveat that you can follow all the steps but must remember self-analysis needs time; it's vital to analyze yourself and put your ideas into action, and the person you are will change in the days to come.
- **track-your-sleeping** — Monitoring the hours you sleep each night because exhaustion encourages stress and raises anxiety; the amount of sleep needed varies per individual, and deprivation carries an eight-item list of harms.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The moody friend at lunch** — Cooper's worked example of perception gone wrong: you blame yourself that your friend was mad at you during lunch break and conclude you did something wrong. Being conscious of your interpretation of her mood helps you see why you reached that conclusion; you are then to write down what you saw, heard, or felt, and check whether outside reasons explain her mood. Shows that perceptions, not events, drive conclusions.
- **The "never steal" value** — Example of a core value: an idea you have "thrown meaning behind" and hold to even when theft would benefit you in a significant way. Illustrates values as morality-based beliefs you remain loyal to.
- **Four value-identification questions** — The exercise Cooper prescribes: (1) think of two people you admire — what qualities and beliefs make you admire them? (2) think of the person you hope to be and write down all the positive aspects of their character; (3) what are you passionate about? (4) what good thing have you done even when it would have been easier to walk away or take advantage?
- **Bravery and charity action prompts** — Concrete embodiment examples: if you look up to brave people, "right this moment, plan an activity that places you outside of your comfort zone"; if you want to be charitable, "call that homeless shelter, right this instant, and offer your services." Shows that value-planning means immediate action, not contemplation.
- **Story-evaluation question set** — Five questions to ask after writing your story: recurring themes (are you always saving people or always the one saved?), whether it is a love story, drama, comedy, or other genre; the story's title; the chapters it divides into; whether you have labeled yourself and others; and whether the words about yourself and others are positive.
- **Genre-interpretation examples** — Telling your story as a drama means you feel your life is dramatic and very intense; as a comedy, that your experience has been full of fun ("Or maybe it feels like a cosmic joke?"); a love story could indicate that you are a romantic.
- **Eight effects of sleep deprivation** — Cooper's list of what happens when you don't sleep: (1) you think and learn slowly; (2) increase in accidents; (3) many health challenges; (4) increase in depression and forgetfulness; (5) lower libido; (6) faster aging; (7) weight fluctuation; (8) impaired judgment.

## Thread
After many chapters cataloguing manipulative techniques and how to read and defend against other people, this chapter redirects the analytical toolkit onto the reader: the same observational habits (watching thoughts, feelings, perceptions, body-language responses) are applied to oneself. It fits the book's defensive-awareness frame — knowing your own values, harmful thought patterns, and vulnerabilities (including physical ones like sleep debt) is presented as groundwork for self-direction ("you can make the rules"), which complements earlier material on how manipulators exploit people who lack that self-knowledge. Its note that contentment requires embracing "even the darkness" echoes the book's recurring theme that dark traits exist in everyone, not just in predators.
