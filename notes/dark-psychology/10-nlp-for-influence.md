# Neuro-linguistic Programming (NLP) for Influence

## In one paragraph
This chapter returns to NLP — introduced earlier in the book as a way to fixate a message in people's minds — and examines it in depth as a manipulator's tool. Cooper sketches NLP's history (Bandler and Grinder modeling therapists Perls, Satir, and Erickson in 1970s California), lays out three core NLP presuppositions, and explains NLP modelling: since everyone perceives the world uniquely, practitioners instead offer people a "model" for replacing habits and thoughts. He then covers NLP as communication, citing Mehrabian's finding that words carry only 7% of a message versus 38% para-verbal and 55% non-verbal, and shows how manipulators exploit verbal tone and non-verbal control. The chapter's practical core is rapport-building — mirroring, pacing/calibration, and leading, plus the "four secret ingredients": smiling, eye contact, active listening, and genuine interest. It closes with the "pleasure" and "pain" leverages: persuading "towards pleasure" people with rewards and "away from pain" people with threats, with sample scripts for each.

## Core claims
- Cooper says NLP's underlying purpose is to fixate a message in people's minds; it is commonly used in education to help students remember content more efficiently.
- He argues NLP grew out of research on human cognition and learning showing humans are creatures of habit who cannot internalize something at first glance — people who can fixate content in one look are "truly unusual."
- In dark psychology, Cooper says, NLP is used to establish a message so that individuals "will not question the validity of the information," eventually taking it at face value, accepting the manipulator's intentions and bringing down their own barriers.
- NLP was born in 1970 when Richard Bandler and John Grinder studied the communication of excellent psychotherapists capable of producing effective, continuous change and healing.
- Cooper states that while there is no conclusive scientific research on NLP's validity, "empirical evidence has proven it to be rather effective," though results across fields have been mixed — supporting the notion that NLP is not cross-cutting but focused on fixating messages in the subconscious.
- A universal model of the world cannot be represented because everyone experiences the world differently; what can be modeled is how knowledge and experience are fixated in the mind.
- NLP has been proven effective, per Cooper, in coercing others to do things or embrace ideas through chants, slogans, songs, and visual imagery reinforcing a thought pattern — which is why it is popular in the "dark" domain: the message can be communicated so individuals don't perceive it directly but are subjected to it anyway.
- Corporate values recited through chants and slogans "disguised as motivational team-building practices" are, in Cooper's account, really indoctrination; uniforms exemplify how individuality is discouraged in favor of universalization of behavior, dress, and attitude, and non-conformers are cast aside.
- Citing Mehrabian's late-1960s research, Cooper claims words count for only 7% of getting a message across, para-verbal communication 38%, and non-verbal communication 55%.
- Words in themselves are meaningless against tone, pitch, speed, and volume; even a hateful comment can seem less damaging in a friendly tone, and beautiful words can be ruined by a somber delivery.
- Skilled manipulators tailor verbal communication to their need — knowing when to smile and sound cheerful to extract gain — and pay close attention to gestures and body language to avoid "blowing their cover," often holding a prop (cigarette, coffee, phone) to diffuse unconscious mannerisms.
- Rapport is a powerful manipulation tool: when one is in "relationship" with another subject, "his distrust and resistance disappear." People who like each other adapt to each other's expressive behavior.
- Psychopaths' famed charm, Cooper argues, is rapport used as a lure — a "hideous monster" that looks like the most attractive person imaginable is a far more effective trap — so one should always be wary of a friendly stranger, who "could be a master manipulator."
- The brain systematically leads us to choices that avoid pain and pursue pleasure, but individuals differ: some respond more to the "pleasure leverage" and others to the "pain leverage," and the persuader should match the pitch to the person's type.

## Concepts
- **Neuro-linguistic Programming (NLP)** — an approach born from research on human cognition and learning that evolved into a framework encompassing "everything that pertains to modifying human behavior through the use of sound and language"; behavior modification becomes possible without actively engaging the individual in a conscious activity leading to that change. Its underlying purpose is fixating a message in people's minds.
- **"The overall meaning of any communication is found in the response it gets, not the original intention of the communication"** — NLP core concept: what matters most is what listeners understood the intention to be and what response they give, not what communication the user puts forth.
- **"The map is not the territory"** — NLP core concept about the relationship between an object and how it is presented: the description differs from the thing described (hearing drinks described doesn't quench thirst). In psychotherapy, patients' descriptions of their lives are only a "map" of their life.
- **"Behaviour is inherently leaned towards adaption"** — NLP core concept: people change and are meant to adapt, and adaptation is easier than people would like to believe; it underlies NLP models that replace a harmful behavior (regular alcohol consumption) with an accepted one (drinking water regularly).
- **NLP modelling** — the effort to map how the human mind perceives the world; since perception is unique to each individual, practitioners instead offer people a "model" to follow, intended to help them learn something or replace existing habits and thoughts with new ones by "essentially talking themselves into" it. (Historically, "modeling" also meant Bandler and Grinder's analysis of star therapists.)
- **Mehrabian's proportions (7% / 38% / 55%)** — Albert Mehrabian's late-1960s research finding that in getting a message across, words/verbal content count for only 7%, para-verbal communication (tone, volume, voice rhythm, etc.) accounts for 38%, and non-verbal communication (especially body and facial mimicry) has an influence of 55%.
- **Verbal communication** — often misrepresented as just words and language; words are meaningless when faced with tone of voice, pitch, speed, and volume, contextual clues people recognize instinctively from an early age. Manipulators tailor it to their needs, playing with their voice to implant their true intentions.
- **Para-verbal communication** — tone, volume, voice rhythm, etc.; per Mehrabian, 38% of the message.
- **Non-verbal communication** — the bulk (55%) of human communication: how we dress, act, and gesticulate, all pointing toward our true intentions; one can betray one's words by acting in a way that contradicts the outward portrayal. Manipulators police their gestures and often carry a prop to diffuse unconscious mannerisms.
- **Rapport** — "the ability to enter someone else's world to build a 'bridge,'" created by mirroring the posture and language of the other person to create resemblance and get in tune with their deepest, most unconscious part; when in "relationship," the subject's distrust and resistance disappear. Its three elements are Mirroring, Pacing, and Leading.
- **Mirroring** — the technique of physical adaptation to the other subject: reflecting posture, gestures, breathing, facial expressions — adapting to everything that can be seen, "as a mirror." Also used in active listening (crossing your arms when they do).
- **Calibration / Pacing** — the complete correspondence of the language style (e.g., representation systems) and of the models (e.g., meta-programs) used by the subject you want to influence.
- **Guide (Leading)** — after adapting to the partner for a while and establishing a relationship, moving on to lead the interaction, changing the direction of communication and emotions; being in "Guide" means guiding someone based on the established relationship toward a specific goal or outcome.
- **The four secret ingredients (of a "magical relationship")** — Smile, Eye contact, Active listening, Genuine interest.
- **Smiling / genuine smile** — a well-placed smile connects and builds rapport, but a fake smile "can be spotted a mile away" (the smiler seems uncomfortable, insincere); to use smiling one must train the mind to smile with genuine feeling — "get into character" and tell yourself you genuinely care about the person.
- **Eye contact** — tricky: a deep, penetrating stare makes people defensive and may trigger a "flight or fight" response; positive eye contact comes at logical points in the interaction (with a smile and firm handshake on meeting, triggering a "safe" response). Reluctant or fearful eye contact from the counterpart signals discomfort — a cue to "pounce" and press one's objectives.
- **Active listening** — the most overlooked influence tactic: really listening gives the impression you care, creating the "narrow ties" essential to building trust capital; demonstrated by looking at the person and mirroring some mannerisms.
- **Echoing** — interjecting a comment repeating what a person said ("the weather is nice today" → "yes, the weather is really nice") to build rapport; agreement is irrelevant, you go along for rapport's sake.
- **Pleasure leverage / Pain leverage** — the two motivational levers: the brain systematically avoids pain and seeks pleasure, but people differ in which leverage they are more sensitive to.
- **People "towards pleasure"** — in NLP, people driven more by pleasure: they go towards things, focus on what they want to happen, are open to change, ask "what is there to gain?", are activated when there is something to conquer, and are motivated by awards; persuade them by describing the pleasures/advantages of complying.
- **People "away from pain"** — people motivated by the pain leverage: focused on avoiding problems and running from everything painful or annoying, they ask "what is the risk in this thing?", prefer to think about problems and how to avoid them, tend to see the flaws in things, are activated by a problem to solve, and are motivated by threats; persuade them by describing the pains and negative consequences of not complying.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **The founding of NLP (1970, California)** — Richard Bandler, a recent graduate of the University of Santa Cruz, and John Grinder, a professor there, studied the communication characteristics of excellent psychotherapists. They met Gestalt therapist Fritz Perls at the Esalen Center in California and modeled (analyzed) him; then analyzed Virginia Satir's family-therapy communication, drawing many language models from her empathic style; then, on the advice of anthropologist Gregory Bateson (a great friend of Bandler), analyzed Milton H. Erickson, a doctor known as one of the greatest experts in clinical hypnosis. The modeling of these three therapists produced two books: "The Structure of Magic" and "The Models of the Hypnotic Technique of Milton H. Erickson."
- **Mehrabian's communication research (late 1960s)** — American psychologist Prof. Albert Mehrabian's research on which aspects of communication get a message across; the "shocking" result: words 7%, para-verbal 38%, non-verbal 55%. Cooper uses it to justify the manipulator's focus on tone and body language.
- **Corporate values, chants, and uniforms** — companies list values and principles and have employees cite them through chants and slogans disguised as motivational team-building; Cooper says the true intent is indoctrinating employees to perform as expected. Workplace uniforms show how individuality is discouraged in favor of universalized behavior, dress, and attitude; non-conforming employees are "generally cast aside" — true of any social group.
- **The salesman's charm** — salesmen "automatically turn on the charm" to woo a customer, then revert to their real selves after the sale: an example of verbal communication tailored to the manipulator's need.
- **The prop trick** — manipulators keep a cigarette, cup of coffee, phone, or similar prop at hand to diffuse any unconscious mannerism that might blow their cover.
- **The attractive monster thought experiment** — Cooper asks the reader to compare a hideous monster (frightening, so you flee) with a monster that wants to eat you but looks like the most attractive person imaginable — a far more effective trap; this is how psychopathic charm works as a lure for unsuspecting victims.
- **Spotting genuine smiles (grocery store, bank, cashier, waiter)** — people at the grocery store or bank smile and say "good morning" simply because it's their job (fake); a sincere smile involves normal eye contact under the right circumstances — a cashier smiling while handing you your receipt, a waiter smiling while greeting you at your table.
- **Job-interview eye contact** — eye contact with a pleasant smile and firm handshake on meeting can trigger a "safe" response; in a job interview this relaxes the interviewer and makes them more open to what you say.
- **Weather echo script** — sample echoing exchange: they say "the weather is nice today," you reply "yes, the weather is really nice," regardless of whether you agree.
- **The sea invitation (matching the leverage)** — some people don't move at all when pitched with pleasure ("Come to the sea and we'll have fun and stay cool") but accept the pain-leverage version ("Come to the sea with us instead of staying at home to get bored and suffer the heat").
- **Scripts for "towards pleasure" people** — "If you want to make money, then you have to listen to me." / "If you want to win then you have to do what I say." / "If you want to be the best then you have to follow these directions."
- **Scripts for "away from pain" people** — "If you want to avoid a big problem, then you have to listen to me." / "If you want to be quiet, then you have to do what I tell you." / "If you don't want to make a mistake, then you must follow these directions."
