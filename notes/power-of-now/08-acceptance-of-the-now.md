# Acceptance of the Now

## In one paragraph
This chapter opens Part Three by teaching acceptance of the present moment as the gateway out of suffering. Eckhart Tolle argues that all forms are impermanent and move in cycles of success and failure, so clinging to any condition guarantees pain, while offering no resistance to life is "a state of grace, ease, and lightness." He reframes all negativity as resistance — a delusional strategy of the ego that never works — and offers practices for dissolving it: using negativity as a wake-up signal, becoming "transparent" to irritants, and accepting even one's own non-peace. From nonresistance he derives compassion (the deep bond with all creatures, sharpened by meditating on mortality) and then develops the chapter's central teaching: surrender, defined not as resignation or passivity but as unconditional inner acceptance of the Now that remains fully compatible with taking "positive action" to change one's life situation. The chapter closes by applying surrender to personal relationships — arguments, manipulation, and the nonreactive "no" — arguing that only through letting go of resistance does one discover true power and "essential invulnerability."

## Core claims
- Tolle claims life moves in cycles of success and failure; dissolution is needed for new growth, one cycle cannot exist without the other, and the down cycle is "absolutely essential for spiritual realization" — deep failure, loss, or the emptiness of success is what draws people to the spiritual dimension. A cycle can last for anything from a few hours to a few years, with small cycles nested within large ones.
- He claims "failure lies concealed in every success, and success in every failure": on the level of form everybody fails sooner or later, all forms are impermanent, and conditions the mind judges "good" either end, change, or undergo a polarity shift into "bad."
- He claims you can still be active, create, and enjoy forms without being identified with them — "they are not your life, only your life situation."
- He claims many illnesses are created by fighting the cycles of low energy that are vital for regeneration; the intelligence of the organism may create an illness as a self-protective measure to force you to stop.
- He claims happiness and unhappiness "are in fact one. Only the illusion of time separates them," and that when inner dependency on form is gone, outer conditions paradoxically tend to improve greatly while fear of loss disappears.
- He claims all inner resistance is experienced as negativity and all negativity is resistance — the two words are almost synonymous — and that the ego believes negativity can manipulate reality, when in fact it stops desirable conditions from arising and keeps undesirable ones in place; its only "useful" function is to strengthen the ego.
- He claims that once identified with a form of negativity, a person unconsciously does not want positive change, because it would threaten their identity as a depressed, angry, or hard-done-by person; they will then ignore, deny, or sabotage the positive in their life — "It is also insane."
- He claims changes in work, relationships, or surroundings are only cosmetic unless they arise from a change in your level of consciousness, which can only mean becoming more present.
- He claims that fully accepting anything — including your own non-peace — transmutes it into peace; "When you accept what is, every moment is the best moment. That is enlightenment."
- He claims that at the level of Being all suffering is recognized as an illusion (suffering is due to identification with form), and that miracles of healing sometimes occur by awakening Being-consciousness in others — if they are ready.
- He claims compassion is the awareness of a deep bond between yourself and all creatures, grounded in shared mortality: there is "total equality between you and every other creature."
- He claims that the quality of your consciousness at this moment is the main determinant of your future, so surrender is the most important thing you can do to bring about positive change; no truly positive action can arise out of an unsurrendered state of consciousness.
- He claims surrender is not defeat, passivity, or resignation but "yielding to rather than opposing the flow of life" — accepting only "the tiny segment called the Now," not the overall life situation, and then acting.
- He claims non-surrender hardens both the psychological form (ego shell, separateness, judgment, competition, fear — paranoia being only a slightly more acute form of this normal dysfunction) and the physical body (tension, contraction, restricted life energy); bodywork gives only temporary relief unless the resistance pattern is dissolved.
- He claims resistance is always unconscious: "You cannot be conscious and unhappy" — total presence dissolves negativity almost instantly, and unhappiness is kept alive by giving it time.
- He claims the spiritual dimension only becomes a living reality through surrender; the energy then emanated is of a "much higher vibrational frequency than the mind energy that still runs our world" and creates no suffering.
- He claims only an unconscious person can use or manipulate others, and equally only an unconscious person can be used and manipulated; fighting unconscious behavior in others makes you unconscious yourself.
- He claims that if you can never accept what is, you cannot accept anybody as they are, and making the Now a means to an end turns every person into a means to an end.
- He claims the ego believes resistance is strength, but "resistance is weakness and fear masquerading as strength"; resistance cuts you off from Being, the only place of true power, and only through becoming "vulnerable" can you discover your true and essential invulnerability.

## Concepts
- **Acceptance of the Now** — accepting the "isness" of this moment: narrowing attention to the present without mentally labeling or judging it, so there is no resistance and no emotional negativity. Acceptance of what is immediately frees you from mind identification and reconnects you with Being.
- **Surrender** — "the simple but profound wisdom of yielding to rather than opposing the flow of life"; since the Now is the only place the flow of life can be experienced, surrender is accepting the present moment unconditionally and without reservation, relinquishing inner resistance to what is. It is a purely inner phenomenon, fully compatible with outer action, plans, and goals.
- **Resistance** — saying "no" to what is through mental judgment and emotional negativity; it is the mind itself ("Resistance is the mind") and is always unconscious. It hardens both psyche and body.
- **Negativity** — the felt form of inner resistance, ranging from irritation and impatience to fierce anger, depression, resentment, and suicidal despair; in this context "all negativity is resistance" and the two words are almost synonymous.
- **The pain gap** — the gap between the demands or rigid expectations of your mind and what is; resistance becomes particularly pronounced when things "go wrong," which is precisely this gap.
- **Pain-body** — the emotional pain-body (a concept carried from earlier chapters) that resistance can trigger, so that even a minor situation produces intense negativity; Tolle says even the pain-body cannot survive for long in your presence.
- **Ego** — the mind-made self that believes negativity can manipulate reality, loves negativity because it strengthens it, exists in continuous resistance-mode, is "cunning," and plays counterfeit roles to cover up what it sees as weakness (which is really your power).
- **Being** — the unchanging, timeless, deathless dimension you abide in beyond the mind-made opposites; "your life, your very Being — which exists eternally in the timeless realm of the present," accessible only through surrender, and the only place of true power.
- **Life situation vs. life** — outer forms and circumstances are your life situation, not your life; your life is Being itself, unaffected by transient circumstances.
- **State of grace** — offering no resistance to life produces "a state of grace, ease, and lightness," no longer dependent on things being good or bad.
- **Joy of Being** — the vibrant peace found within on entering nonresistance; secondary-source happiness is only its pale reflection. Being takes you beyond the mind's polar opposites and frees you from dependency on form: "Even if everything were to collapse and crumble all around you, you would still feel a deep inner core of peace. You may not be happy, but you will be at peace."
- **Integrity** — as taught by plants and animals: "to be one, to be yourself, to be real."
- **Negativity as signal** — as long as negativity is there, use it as a signal reminding you to be more present: a voice saying "Attention. Here and Now. Wake up."
- **Transparency practice** — imagining yourself becoming transparent, without the solidity of a material body, so the external cause of a negative reaction passes right through you instead of hitting a "wall" of resistance inside.
- **Forgiveness** — letting a rude or hurtful remark pass through you with no resistance, "as if there is nobody there to get hurt anymore. That is forgiveness." It makes you invulnerable without preventing you from saying the behavior is unacceptable.
- **Non-peace / the miracle of surrender** — forgiving yourself for not being at peace; the moment you completely accept your non-peace it is transmuted into peace — "Anything you accept fully will get you there."
- **Enlightenment** — defined here as: "When you accept what is, every moment is the best moment. That is enlightenment."
- **True relationship / the screen** — as long as you are unaware of Being, the reality of other humans eludes you, because you have not found your own; your mind will only like or dislike their form (body and mind). True relationship becomes possible only when there is an awareness of Being. Coming from Being, you perceive another person's body and mind as "just a screen, as it were, behind which you can feel their true reality" — so when confronted with someone's suffering or unconscious behavior, you stay present and in touch with Being and look beyond the form to feel their "radiant and pure Being through your own."
- **Compassion** — "the awareness of a deep bond between yourself and all creatures," grounded in shared mortality and total equality; at its deepest level it becomes healing based not on doing but on being.
- **Die before you die** — one of the most powerful spiritual practices: meditating deeply on the mortality of physical forms, including your own, until all mind-forms die and only the divine presence you are remains, radiant and fully awake.
- **Light of the world** — the state of an emanation of pure consciousness whose vast peace dissolves what is not peace, breaking the karmic cycle of action and reaction and eliminating suffering "on the level of cause." Animals, trees, and flowers will feel your peace and respond to it: "You teach through being, through demonstrating the peace of God."
- **Karmic cycle** — the cycle of action and reaction broken when you no longer react to unconscious behavior and so give it no reality.
- **Resignation** — falsely accepting the overall situation ("Okay, I resign myself to being stuck in the mud"); explicitly not surrender, which accepts only the Now while acting to change the situation.
- **Isness** — the unlabeled, unjudged reality of this moment that you accept in surrender.
- **Positive action** — action taken from the surrendered, unlabeled Now; "far more effective than negative action, which arises out of anger, despair, or frustration."
- **Negative action** — action arising out of anger, despair, or frustration.
- **Surrendered action** — doing infused with Being: through nonresistance the quality of your consciousness, and therefore of whatever you do or create, is enhanced immeasurably, and "the results will then look after themselves."
- **Flashlight in the fog** — Tolle's visual analogy for surrender-plus-action: the fog is your life situation (past and future), the flashlight is your conscious presence, and the narrow clear space it cuts is the Now.
- **Mental movies** — mind-projections that continually cast you into the future and so lose the Now; to be avoided even while planning.
- **Masked resistance** — the attitude "I can't be bothered anymore" or "I just don't care anymore," which is tainted with hidden resentment and is not surrender at all.
- **Mind energy vs. spiritual energy** — the mind energy that "still runs our world" versus the higher-vibrational-frequency spiritual energy that comes into the world through surrender and creates no suffering for any life form.
- **Unconsciousness** — the state in which negativity, manipulation, reaction, and role-playing operate; resistance is always unconscious, and negativity "can only survive in your absence."
- **Nonreactive "no"** — a "high-quality 'no'" that comes not from reaction but from insight, from a clear realization of what is right or not right for you at that moment; free of all negativity, it creates no further suffering.
- **Yield to overcome** — the deep wisdom underlying Eastern martial arts: don't resist the opponent's force; cited as the model for nonreactive doing.
- **"Doing nothing" in presence** — real "doing nothing," implying inner nonresistance and intense alertness, is "a very powerful transformer and healer of situations and people," radically different from unconscious inactivity stemming from fear, inertia, or indecision. If action is required instead, you respond out of conscious presence rather than reacting from the conditioned mind; in that state your mind is free of concepts, including the concept of nonviolence — "So who can predict what you will do?"
- **Role-playing** — the counterfeit roles and false masks the ego plays to cover up "weakness"; until surrender, unconscious role-playing constitutes a large part of human interaction.
- **Polarity shift** — the change by which a condition the mind judged "good" turns "bad," suddenly or gradually (Tolle's examples: prosperity to empty consumerism, wedding to divorce).
- **Invulnerability** — the true and essential invulnerability discovered only through letting go of resistance and becoming "vulnerable."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Illness from fighting low cycles** — Tolle says many illnesses are created by fighting the cycles of low energy that are vital for regeneration: when mind-identification makes it impossible to accept a low cycle, "the intelligence of the organism may take over as a self-protective measure and create an illness in order to force you to stop" so regeneration can take place.
- **Polarity-shift examples** — illustrations of impermanence: "The prosperity of today becomes the empty consumerism of tomorrow. The happy wedding and honeymoon become the unhappy divorce or the unhappy coexistence." Separately, when a condition the mind has identified with simply disappears rather than turning bad, the mind cannot accept it and clings to the disappearing condition "almost as if a limb were being torn off your body."
- **Practice: let a plant or animal teach you** — watch any plant or animal and let it teach you acceptance of what is, surrender to the Now, Being, integrity ("to be one, to be yourself, to be real"), and "how to live and how to die, and how not to make living and dying into a problem."
- **Practice: negativity as an alarm clock** — whenever negativity arises (from an external factor, a thought, or nothing identifiable), look on it as a voice saying, "Attention. Here and Now. Wake up. Get out of your mind. Be present." Even the slightest irritation must be acknowledged, or unobserved reactions build up cumulatively. Then either drop it completely, or, if you cannot, accept that it is there and take your attention into the feeling.
- **Practice: the car alarm / transparency exercise** — Tolle's worked example: you sit quietly at home; a penetrating car alarm sounds across the street; irritation arises. The irritation has no purpose; the mind created it automatically and unconsciously out of the delusion that resistance dissolves the undesirable condition — the irritation is in fact "far more disturbing than the original cause." The practice: feel yourself becoming transparent, without the solidity of a material body, and let the noise pass right through you rather than hit a solid inner "wall." Practice with little things first — the car alarm, the dog barking, the children screaming, the traffic jam — because "the resistance mechanism is the same" whether it is a car alarm, a rude person, a flood, an earthquake, or the loss of all your possessions.
- **The rude person** — someone says something rude or designed to hurt; instead of unconscious reaction (attack, defense, or withdrawal) you let it pass through you: "It is as if there is nobody there to get hurt anymore. That is forgiveness." You may still tell the person the behavior is unacceptable, but they no longer control your inner state.
- **The chronic seeker (Q&A-style address)** — Tolle addresses someone still seeking outside, unable to leave the seeking mode ("Maybe the next workshop will have the answer, maybe that new technique"), telling them: "Don't look for peace. Don't look for any other state than the one you are in now" — then forgive yourself for not being at peace, and the complete acceptance of non-peace transmutes it into peace.
- **The deep-lake analogy** — beyond the mind-made opposites you become like a deep lake: the outer life situation is the surface, sometimes calm, sometimes windy and rough with the cycles and seasons, but "deep down… the lake is always undisturbed. You are the whole lake, not just the surface."
- **Mortality contemplation for compassion** — next time you say "I have nothing in common with this person," remember that in two years or seventy, "both of you will have become rotting corpses, then piles of dust, then nothing at all" — a sobering, humbling realization leaving little room for pride and revealing total equality between all creatures. Tolle insists this is not a negative thought but a fact.
- **Practice: "Die before you die"** — meditate deeply on the mortality of physical forms, including your own: your physical form dissolves, is no more; then all mind-forms and thoughts also die; "Yet you are still there — the divine presence that you are. Radiant, fully awake." Intended effect: realizing "Nothing that was real ever died, only names, forms, and illusions," and opening compassion as healing based on being rather than doing.
- **Stuck-in-the-mud example** — illustrating that surrender accepts only the Now, not the overall situation: if stuck in the mud you would not say "Okay, I resign myself to being stuck in the mud" — resignation is not surrender. Instead: recognize fully that you want out, narrow attention to the present without labeling it, then take action ("positive action") to get out, continuing to refrain from labeling the Now until you achieve the result.
- **The flashlight-in-the-fog analogy** — walking a path at night in thick fog with a powerful flashlight: the fog is your life situation (past and future), the flashlight your conscious presence, the clear space the Now. Used again later: "separate out this instant and surrender to what is. That's the flashlight cutting through the fog."
- **Bodywork example** — bodywork and certain forms of physical therapy can help restore the flow of life energy restricted by resistance, but give only temporary symptom relief unless surrender is practiced in everyday life, since the resistance pattern (the cause) has not been dissolved.
- **The lilies (Jesus)** — learn from nature how everything gets accomplished without dissatisfaction or unhappiness; Tolle cites Jesus: "Look at the lilies, how they grow; they neither toil nor spin."
- **Practice: the surrender-then-act sequence for an unsatisfactory situation** — (1) separate out this instant and surrender to what is; (2) ask "Is there anything I can do to change the situation, improve it, or remove myself from it?"; (3) if so, take appropriate action, focusing on the one thing you can do now (planning may be that one thing, but without running "mental movies" into the future); (4) if no action is possible and you cannot leave, use the situation to go more deeply into surrender, the Now, and Being — whereupon "change often comes about in strange ways" and inner obstacles like fear, guilt, or inertia dissolve in the light of conscious presence.
- **Practice: witnessing resistance (mind energy to spiritual energy)** — start by acknowledging that resistance is there; be there when it arises; observe how the mind creates it and labels the situation, yourself, or others; look at the thought process; feel the energy of the emotion. By witnessing it you see it serves no purpose; focusing all attention on the Now makes the unconscious resistance conscious, "and that is the end of it." Tolle poses the probing questions: "Would you choose unhappiness? … What is its purpose? Who is keeping it alive? … But do you want it to die? Have you truly had enough? Who would you be without it?"
- **The prison example** — if you cannot take action at all (Tolle's example: being in prison), two choices remain: resistance or surrender — "Bondage or inner freedom from external conditions. Suffering or inner peace."
- **Practice: surrender in an argument** — when in conflict with a partner or someone close: observe your defensiveness as your position is attacked, or the force of your own aggression; observe the attachment to your views and the mental-emotional energy behind the need to be right and make the other wrong (the energy of the egoic mind), making it conscious by feeling it fully. Then one day, mid-argument, realize you have a choice and drop your reaction "just to see what happens." Tolle warns against the counterfeit version — saying "Okay, you are right" with a face that says "I am above all this childish unconsciousness," which merely displaces resistance while the ego claims superiority; true surrender means letting go of the entire mental-emotional energy field that was fighting for power. The unmistakable sign of true surrender: suddenly feeling "very light, clear, and deeply at peace." Then, with your resistance no longer energizing the other's mental position, "true communication begins."
- **Practice: the nonreactive "no"** — surrender does not mean allowing yourself to be used by unconscious people; you can say "no" firmly and clearly or walk away while in complete inner nonresistance. Let the "no" come from insight into what is right for you at that moment, not from reaction — "a high-quality 'no'… free of all negativity" that creates no further suffering. If you cannot surrender, take action immediately: speak up, change the situation, or remove yourself — "Take responsibility for your life."
- **Eastern martial arts** — cited as the model for nonreactive doing: "don't resist the opponent's force. Yield to overcome."

- "All inner resistance is experienced as negativity in one form or another. All negativity is resistance." — the equation at the heart of the chapter.
- "Attention. Here and Now. Wake up. Get out of your mind. Be present." — the "voice" negativity should be heard as, turning it into a signal for presence.
- "The moment you completely accept your non-peace, your non-peace becomes transmuted into peace. Anything you accept fully will get you there… This is the miracle of surrender." — the answer to the seeker who cannot stop seeking.
- "When you accept what is, every moment is the best moment. That is enlightenment." — the chapter's definition of enlightenment.

- "Look at the lilies, how they grow; they neither toil nor spin." — Jesus, quoted by Tolle as nature's lesson in accomplishment without unhappiness.
- "Resistance is weakness and fear masquerading as strength." — the reversal of the ego's view of strength, leading to the closing claim that vulnerability reveals true invulnerability.

## Thread
This chapter opens Part Three (Acceptance and Surrender), turning the book's diagnostic teachings — mind identification, the pain-body, unconsciousness — into the culminating practice of surrender. It follows Part One's tools for accessing the Now (presence, watching the thinker, the inner body) and Part Two's application to relationships, and it gathers them under a single move: relinquishing inner resistance to what is. It reframes negativity (Part One's enemy) as a usable wake-up signal, extends relationship practice (Part Two) into surrender in arguments and the nonreactive "no," and grounds compassion and healing in Being rather than doing. Its careful distinctions — surrender vs. resignation, positive vs. negative action, real vs. masked surrender — answer the standing objection that acceptance means passivity, and set up the book's final chapter on the deeper reaches of surrender.
