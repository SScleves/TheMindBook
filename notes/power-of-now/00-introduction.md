# Introduction

## In one paragraph
In this brief Introduction, dated July 9, 2001, Eckhart Tolle explains why *Practicing the Power of Now* exists and how it should be used. He recounts that since *The Power of Now* was first published in 1997 it has been translated into fifteen languages and, he says, has affected "the collective consciousness of the planet" far beyond what he imagined, with readers writing daily to say their lives were changed. Many of those readers asked for the practical aspects of the teachings in a more accessible format for daily practice, and that request became the impetus for this book. Tolle describes the book's contents — exercises and practices plus shorter passages from the original work that serve as reminders and a daily primer — and introduces "meditative reading" as the intended way to engage the passages. He also frames the historical moment: a new state of consciousness is emerging as unprecedented numbers of people become ready to break out of collective mind-patterns of suffering. Finally, he offers the book as an introduction for readers who felt daunted or overwhelmed by *The Power of Now*.

## Core claims
- Tolle claims that *The Power of Now* (first published 1997) has had an impact on "the collective consciousness of the planet" far beyond anything he could have imagined, has been translated into fifteen languages, and prompts daily mail from readers worldwide saying their lives were changed by contact with its teaching.
- Tolle argues that although "the effects of the insanity of the egoic mind are still visible everywhere," something new is emerging: never before have so many people been ready to break out of collective mind-patterns that have kept humanity "in bondage to suffering since time immemorial."
- He claims a new state of consciousness is emerging — "We have suffered enough!" — and that it is emerging even now from within the reader as they hold the book and read of the possibility of the liberated life, in which one no longer inflicts suffering on oneself or others.
- He states that this book was created in response to readers' requests to have the practical aspects of the teachings presented in a more readily accessible format for daily practice.
- He says the book contains, besides exercises and practices, shorter passages from the original work that serve as reminders of its ideas and concepts and as a primer for incorporating them daily.
- Tolle claims that only words written or spoken "in a state of presence" have transformative power — the power to awaken presence in the reader — which is why a passage read meditatively can be re-read many times and feel fresh and new each time.
- He offers the book as an alternative entry point: for readers who felt daunted or overwhelmed by *The Power of Now*, it can serve as an introduction.

## Concepts
- **meditative reading** — Tolle's term for reading not primarily to gather new information but "to enter a different state of consciousness as you read." Because of this, the same passage can be re-read many times and feel fresh and new each time; such passages are best read slowly, pausing for quiet reflection or stillness.
- **presence** — the state in which, Tolle says, words must be written or spoken for them to carry transformative power; that power is defined as "the power to awaken presence in the reader." (Used here without fuller definition; central to the book's later chapters.)
- **egoic mind** — the source of the "insanity" whose effects Tolle says are "still visible everywhere"; used in the Introduction without further definition.
- **collective mind-patterns** — the shared mental patterns that, Tolle claims, have "kept humanity in bondage to suffering since time immemorial," and which unprecedented numbers of people are now ready to break out of.
- **collective consciousness** — the planet-wide consciousness Tolle says *The Power of Now* has already impacted; the medium in which the new emergence is happening.
- **new state of consciousness** — the emerging condition Tolle announces ("A new state of consciousness is emerging"), which he says is arising even at this moment from within the reader.
- **the liberated life** — the possibility the book speaks of: a life "in which you no longer inflict suffering on yourself or others."

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Reader mail as evidence of impact** — Tolle reports receiving mail "from around the globe every day" from readers saying their lives have been changed through contact with the teaching in *The Power of Now*; he cites this, along with translation into fifteen languages since 1997, as the sign of the book's impact and the impetus (via readers' requests for a practical format) for creating this volume.
- **Practice: meditative reading** — Instruction: read the shorter passages not to gather information but to enter a different state of consciousness; read slowly; pause many times to allow "a moment of quiet reflection, or stillness." Intended effect: the passage's transformative power awakens presence in the reader, which is why it feels fresh and new on every re-reading.
- **Practice: random opening** — Instruction: "you may just open the book at random and read a few lines" as an alternative way of using the book at other times. Intended effect (implied by the surrounding text): the same presence-awakening contact with the teaching without sequential study.
- **Usage note for daunted readers** — Tolle says that for readers who felt daunted or overwhelmed by *The Power of Now*, this book can serve as an introduction — positioning it as an on-ramp as well as a practice companion.

## Thread
This Introduction is the frame for everything that follows: it establishes that the book is a distilled, practice-oriented companion to *The Power of Now* — exercises, practices, and presence-charged excerpts — and prescribes how to read it (meditative reading, slowly, with pauses, or opened at random). It sets the book's stakes with the claim of an emerging new state of consciousness and the possibility of the liberated life, themes the rest of the book then develops. It follows from nothing within the book itself, but explicitly follows from the 1997 original and its readers' requests.

## Connections
- Sets up the rest of the book, which the Introduction describes as consisting of exercises, practices, and "shorter passages" from the original work; the reading method given here (meditative reading) applies to every subsequent unit.
- The terms named in passing here — egoic mind, presence, suffering, the liberated life — are used in the Introduction without fuller definition and left for the body of the book to elaborate.
