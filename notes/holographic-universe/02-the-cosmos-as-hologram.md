# Ch 2 — The Cosmos as Hologram

## In one paragraph
Talbot traces the intellectual biography of physicist David Bohm to show how the second pillar of the holographic model — a holographic *universe*, complementing Pribram's holographic *brain* — was built. Starting from quantum physics's strangest findings (particles without dimension, wave-particle shapeshifting, properties that seem to appear only under observation), Bohm was repeatedly struck by evidence of interconnectedness that mainstream physics ignored: the EPR twin-particle puzzle, the organism-like behavior of plasmas, and later the Aharonov-Bohm effect. Dissatisfied with Bohr's "complete" quantum theory, Bohm proposed a subquantum field, the quantum potential, whose nonlocal character dissolves separateness without breaking relativity's light-speed limit. The ink-in-glycerine device and then the hologram gave him the metaphor to crystallize everything: visible reality is an "explicate order" continually unfolding out of and enfolding back into a deeper "implicate order" — a dynamic "holomovement" in which everything, including consciousness, space, and an unimaginably vast sea of energy, is one unbroken whole. Aspect's 1982 experiment confirming nonlocal correlation between twin photons gives the model, in Talbot's words, tremendous support, and the chapter closes by fusing Bohm with Pribram: the brain is a hologram enfolded in a holographic universe.

## Core claims
- Talbot presents quantum physics as an "Alice-in-Wonderland realm": electrons literally possess no dimension, and all subatomic phenomena are "quanta" — somethings that are always somehow both wave and particle, the basic stuff of the universe.
- One interpretation of the evidence (held by physicists such as Nick Herbert, but explicitly *not* by Bohm) is that quanta only manifest as particles when they are being observed; unobserved, an electron is always a wave.
- Bohr's view is that if particles only come into existence in the presence of an observer, it is meaningless to speak of their properties as existing before observation — and that twin particles are not separate "things" but part of an indivisible system.
- Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen (1935) argued that Bohr must be wrong, because his view would require twin particles to communicate instantaneously — faster than light — which no "reasonable definition" of reality would permit (the EPR paradox).
- Bohm's view is that particles do exist without observers, that a subquantum level awaits discovery, and that a new field there — the quantum potential — explains quantum findings as well as Bohr's interpretation does, while pervading all space and not diminishing with distance.
- Bohm argues (in *Causality and Chance in Modern Physics*, 1957) that no theory can be complete because nature may be infinite, and that an effect can have an infinite number of causes; no cause-and-effect relation is ever really separate from the universe as a whole.
- Bohm's quantum potential implies the whole organizes the parts (not vice versa), and that at the subquantum level location ceases to exist — all points in space are equal to all other points ("nonlocality").
- Bohm's mature claim (in *Wholeness and the Implicate Order*, 1980) is that tangible everyday reality is a kind of illusion, like a holographic image; beneath it lies the implicate (enfolded) order, from which our explicate (unfolded) order is continuously projected — a "holomovement."
- Bohm argues that dividing reality into parts is always an arbitrary abstraction; everything is one unbroken thing, and our habit of fragmenting the world causes problems in science, in our lives, and in society, and may even lead to our extinction.
- Bohm holds that consciousness is a more subtle form of matter, present in various degrees of enfoldment in all matter; the observer *is* the observed, and even a rock is in some way alive.
- Bohm takes seriously the calculation that every cubic centimeter of empty space contains more energy than all the matter in the known universe: space is a plenum, not a vacuum, and our universe is only a ripple — a "pattern of excitation" — on an infinite sea of energy.
- The sea of energy is not all the implicate order enfolds: as the foundation that has given birth to everything, it contains at the very least every subatomic particle that has been or will be, and every possible configuration of matter, energy, life, and consciousness — from quasars to the brain of Shakespeare, from the double helix to the forces that control the sizes and shapes of galaxies. Bohm concedes there is no reason to believe the implicate order is the end of things; there may be undreamed-of orders beyond it, "infinite stages of further development."
- Talbot reports that Aspect's 1982 experiment does not prove Bohm's model but provides it with "tremendous support," since it is generally viewed as virtual proof that twin photons are nonlocally connected.
- Combined, Bohm and Pribram's theories say (per Talbot): our brains mathematically construct objective reality by interpreting frequencies that are projections from a deeper, dimension-transcending order — the brain is a hologram enfolded in a holographic universe.
- Pribram's view is that the objective world "out there" is a vast ocean of waves and frequencies which the brain converts into familiar solid objects; a china cup is both cup and interference pattern. Bohm goes further: we even construct space and time.

## Concepts
- **Quanta** — the plural of quantum; the single category of "somethings" that are always somehow both wave and particle, believed to be the basic stuff from which the entire universe is made. One electron is a quantum; the word is also synonymous with "wave particle."
- **Wave-particle duality** — the chameleonlike ability of an electron (and every subatomic particle, plus light, gamma rays, radio waves, X rays) to manifest as either a compact particle or a blurry wave spread out over space, and to shift back and forth.
- **Observer-created properties** — the interpretation (Bohr's, disputed by Bohm) that quanta coalesce into particles only when observed, so a particle's properties cannot be said to exist before observation.
- **Polarization** — the spatial orientation of a photon's wavelike aspect as it travels away from its point of origin; twin photons always measure to identical angles of polarization.
- **EPR paradox** — Einstein, Podolsky, and Rosen's 1935 argument that Bohr's view forces twin particles into instantaneous (faster-than-light) communication, which special relativity forbids; therefore Bohr had to be wrong.
- **Plasma** — a gas containing a high density of electrons and positive ions (positively charged atoms), in which electrons stop behaving like individuals and act as part of a larger interconnected whole.
- **Plasmons** — Bohm's name for the collective movements of oceans of electrons in metals, whose seemingly haphazard individual motions produce highly organized overall effects; their discovery established his reputation.
- **Quantum potential** — Bohm's proposed new field at the subquantum level: like gravity it pervades all of space, but unlike gravitational or magnetic fields its influence does not diminish with distance — subtle but equally powerful everywhere.
- **Nonlocality** — the property, implied by the quantum potential, that at the subquantum level location ceases to exist: all points in space become equal to all other points, and it is meaningless to speak of anything as separate from anything else. Bohm's holographic order later explains why: saying every part of a piece of holographic film contains all the information possessed by the whole is really just another way of saying the information is distributed nonlocally — so a universe organized on holographic principles would also be expected to have nonlocal properties.
- **Infinite causality** — Bohm's view that an effect can have an infinite number of causes (the Lincoln example), so no single cause-and-effect relationship is ever really separate from the universe as a whole.
- **Hidden (enfolded) order / degrees of order** — Bohm's realization that there are different degrees of order, perhaps endless hierarchies of it; things that appear disordered may have order of an "indefinitely high degree" (mathematicians cannot even prove randomness). Order can be manifest (explicit) or hidden (implicit), as with the ink drop in glycerine.
- **Implicate order** — the deeper, enfolded level of reality, a vast and more primary level that gives birth to all objects and appearances of our physical world the way holographic film gives birth to a hologram.
- **Explicate order** — our own level of existence, the unfolded and perceptible order; the hologram projected from the film.
- **Enfoldment and unfoldment** — the countless exchanges between the two orders; an electron is an ensemble enfolded throughout the whole of space, and its apparent motion or detection is a continuous series of unfoldments and enfoldments (like the ink drop unfolding from the glycerine). The constant, flowing exchange between the orders also explains how quanta shapeshift from one kind of particle to another: one particle (say an electron) enfolds back into the implicate order while another (a photon) unfolds and takes its place.
- **Holomovement** — Bohm's preferred term for the universe, since "hologram" suggests a static image and does not convey the dynamic, ever-active enfoldings and unfoldings that create our universe moment by moment.
- **Undivided wholeness** — because everything is made of the seamless holographic fabric of the implicate order, viewing the universe as composed of "parts" is as meaningless as viewing geysers in a fountain as separate from the water; ultimately even the implicate and explicate orders blend into each other.
- **Relatively independent subtotalities** — Bohm's term for what we usually call "things": aspects of the holomovement that possess unique qualities (like whirlpools in a river) yet cannot be truly divided from the whole.
- **Fragmentation** — our almost universal tendency to break the world into parts and ignore dynamic interconnectedness, which Bohm believes underlies problems in science, our lives, and society.
- **Consciousness as a more subtle form of matter** — Bohm's proposal that the basis of the mind-matter relationship lies in the implicate order; consciousness is present in various degrees of enfoldment in all matter, and something "mindlike" exists already in the electron.
- **Sea of energy / plenum** — the finding that every cubic centimeter of empty space contains more energy than all matter in the known universe; Bohm holds that space is full (a plenum, not a vacuum) and is the ground of all existence, our universe being a mere "pattern of excitation" on it.
- **Aharonov-Bohm effect** — the discovery that under the right circumstances an electron can "feel" the presence of a magnetic field in a region where there is zero probability of finding the electron; a nonlocal effect, still occasionally disputed despite numerous confirmations.
- **Space-time continuum** — Einstein's general-relativity insight that space and time are not separate but part of a larger whole; Bohm takes it "a giant step further": everything in the universe is part of a continuum.
- **Superstring theory** — cited by Bohm as an example of a physics theory that will probably not be testable for several decades, in defense against the charge that his own theory is untestable.
- **Phantom limb syndrome** — invoked from Pribram's work: the felt solidity of the world (fine china, beach sand) is "really just elaborate versions" of it — brain-constructed sensation.
- **Frequency domain** — Pribram's picture of what is "out there": a vast ocean of waves and frequencies that the brain converts into concrete-seeming objects.

## Experiments, examples & case studies
- **Bohm's dripless tea kettle (boyhood, Wilkes-Barre, Pennsylvania)** — as a boy Bohm invented a dripless tea kettle; his businessman father urged him to profit from it, but on learning the first step was a door-to-door test-market survey, Bohm's interest in business waned. Talbot uses it to show his early inventive curiosity, which turned to quantum physics at Pennsylvania State College in the 1930s.
- **The dimensionless electron** — physicists found that although an electron sometimes behaves as a compact particle, it literally possesses no dimension: trying to measure the width of an electron is an impossible task. Talbot's point: an electron "is simply not an object as we know it."
- **Electron fired at a television screen** — shooting an electron at the screen of a switched-off TV produces a tiny point of light where it strikes the phosphorescent coating; the single point of impact reveals the particlelike side of its nature.
- **The two-slit behavior of wavelike electrons** — an electron can dissolve into a blurry cloud of energy and behave as a wave: fired at a barrier with two slits, it can go through both slits simultaneously, and wavelike electrons colliding create interference patterns. Shows the wave side of the duality.
- **The "quantum bowling ball" thought illustration (fig. 7)** — Talbot's analogy for observation-dependence: a bowling ball rolled down a talcum-powdered lane traces a single line while watched, but during a blink it leaves "a broad wavy strip, like the undulating swath of a desert snake." This is comparable to what physicists found: quanta appear to coalesce into particles only when observed.
- **Nick Herbert's "quantum soup" and Midas analogy** — physicist Nick Herbert, a supporter of the observation-dependence interpretation, imagines that behind his back the world is "a radically ambiguous and ceaselessly flowing quantum soup" which his glance freezes back into ordinary reality; we are like King Midas, who never felt silk because everything he touched turned to gold — we never experience true quantum texture because "everything we touch turns to matter."
- **Positronium decay and twin photons** — the unstable positronium atom (an electron plus a positron, its antiparticle) self-annihilates and decays into two photons traveling in opposite directions; quantum physics says that no matter how far apart they travel, measurement will always find identical angles of polarization. (Talbot's footnote: positronium decay was not the actual process EPR used — it is chosen because it is easy to visualize.)
- **The EPR thought experiment (Einstein, Podolsky, Rosen, 1935)** — in the paper "Can Quantum-Mechanical Description of Physical Reality Be Considered Complete?" they argued: produce twin particles, let them separate a significant distance, measure both polarizations at precisely the same moment; if identical (as predicted) and if Bohr is right that properties don't exist until measured, the photons must be communicating instantaneously — impossible under special relativity. Therefore Bohr had to be wrong. Bohr's counter: twin particles are not independent things but an indivisible system, so no communication is needed. Most physicists sided with Bohr — partly because quantum theory was so successful at prediction, and because the experiment couldn't yet be technically performed.
- **Bohm's plasma work (Lawrence Berkeley Radiation Laboratory, before his 1943 Berkeley doctorate)** — Bohm's landmark work on plasmas found that electrons in a plasma stop behaving like individuals: their individual motions look random, yet vast numbers produce surprisingly well-organized effects, and the plasma constantly regenerates itself and encloses impurities in a wall the way an organism encases a foreign substance in a cyst. Bohm later remarked he frequently had the impression the electron sea was "alive." Talbot: a striking example of quantum interconnectedness.
- **Electrons in metals and the discovery of plasmons (Princeton, from 1947)** — as an assistant professor Bohm extended the Berkeley research to electrons in metals; again haphazard individual movements produced highly organized overall effects — entire oceans of particles each behaving as if it knew what untold trillions of others were doing. He named these collective movements plasmons; the discovery established his reputation.
- **The Bohm–Einstein conversations (Princeton)** — after three years teaching quantum theory, Bohm wrote a textbook to improve his understanding, sent copies to Bohr (no answer) and Einstein. Einstein initiated a six-month series of spirited conversations, saying he had never seen quantum theory presented so clearly, yet both agreed the theory predicted phenomena admirably but gave no real way of conceiving the basic structure of the world; Bohr's claim that the theory was complete grated on both. *Quantum Theory* (1951) was hailed as a classic — but Bohm no longer gave the subject his full allegiance.
- **The quantum potential proposal (published 1952)** — assuming particles exist without observers and that a subquantum level exists, Bohm found that simply proposing a new field there (the quantum potential, pervading all space, influence undiminished by distance) explained quantum findings as well as Bohr could. Reaction was mainly negative — dismissals out of hand and passionate attacks, virtually all resting on philosophical differences; Bohm's alternative was treated as "little more than heresy."
- **The bullet that killed Lincoln (from *Causality and Chance in Modern Physics*, 1957)** — asked what caused Lincoln's death, one might say the bullet in John Wilkes Booth's gun; but a complete list of causes would include everything leading to the gun's development, all factors that made Booth want to kill Lincoln, even every step of human evolution that produced a hand capable of holding a gun. Bohm's point: effects have infinitely many causes, and no cause-effect relation is separate from the whole. In the same book he argued no theory can be complete because nature may be infinite.
- **The ballet analogy for plasma electrons** — Bohm's explanation via the quantum potential of how plasma electrons (and other specialized states such as superconductivity) behave as wholes: the whole system undergoes "a co-ordinated movement more like a ballet dance than like a crowd of unorganized people" — closer to the organized unity of a living being than to parts of a machine assembled together.
- **The fish and the two television cameras (fig. 8)** — Bohm's analogy for twin-particle correlation without faster-than-light signaling: two cameras, one on an aquarium's front and one on its side, feed two monitors; a viewer who has never seen a fish or aquarium might think there are two fish that mysteriously communicate, since each turn of one is matched by the other. In fact, at the deeper level of reality (the aquarium) the two fish are one and the same. This, says Bohm, is precisely what happens with the two photons from positronium decay: apparent separateness masks a deeper unity, and since the quantum potential permeates all of space, all particles are nonlocally interconnected.
- **John Stewart Bell's proof (CERN, 1964)** — Bell, a theoretical physicist at CERN (a center for peaceful atomic research near Geneva), was impressed by Bohm's 1952 paper ("His idea was to complete quantum mechanics by saying there are certain variables in addition to those which everybody knew about"). Realizing Bohm's theory implied nonlocality, he wondered whether it could be tested; on a 1964 sabbatical he produced an elegant mathematical proof showing how. The catch: to exclude normal communication between the particles, all operations had to happen within a few thousand-millionths of a second — beyond available technology.
- **The Aharonov-Bohm effect (Bristol University, late 1950s)** — after his run-in with McCarthyism, Bohm became a research fellow at Bristol, where with research student Yakir Aharonov he found that under the right circumstances an electron can "feel" a magnetic field located in a region where there is zero probability of finding the electron. Many physicists initially disbelieved it, and despite confirmation in numerous experiments, papers occasionally still appear arguing it doesn't exist. Talbot casts Bohm as the voice noting the emperor has no clothes.
- **The ink-drop-in-glycerine device (seen on a BBC television program, 1960s)** — a jar with a large rotating cylinder, the narrow gap filled with glycerine, a drop of ink floating motionless in it. Turning the handle spreads the ink until it seems to disappear; turning it back makes the faint tracing collapse into a droplet again (fig. 9). Bohm: this struck him as very relevant to order — the spread-out ink had a "hidden" (nonmanifest) order revealed on reconstitution, showing that "new notions of order must be involved." It became his first model of enfolded versus manifest order.
- **The hologram as master metaphor (papers early 1970s; *Wholeness and the Implicate Order*, 1980)** — soon after the glycerine device Bohm hit on the hologram: interference patterns on holographic film also look disordered to the naked eye yet enfold a hidden order, just as a plasma's order is enfolded in seemingly random electron behavior. He became convinced the universe itself employs holographic principles — a giant, flowing hologram — and distilled the mature theory in the 1980 book.
- **The geyser/fountain image** — electrons and all particles are no more substantive or permanent than the form a geyser of water takes gushing from a fountain: sustained by constant influx from the implicate order, and when "destroyed" merely enfolded back into the deeper order. Also used later for wholeness: geysers are not separate from the water.
- **The jeweler and the gem** — Bohm's account of the observer's role: both wave and particle aspects are always enfolded in a quantum's ensemble; the way an observer interacts determines which aspect unfolds — no more mysterious than the way a jeweler manipulates a gem determining which facets become visible.
- **Hand, lamplight, dog** — Talbot's direct-address illustration of undivided wholeness: look at your hand, the light from your lamp, the dog at your feet — you are not merely made of the same things, "You are the same thing. One thing. Unbroken."
- **Eddies and whirlpools in a river** — Bohm's caution that wholeness does not mean undifferentiated mass: eddies look separate and have individual characteristics (size, rate, direction of rotation), but it is impossible to determine where a whirlpool ends and the river begins. Hence "relatively independent subtotalities" instead of "things."
- **Fragmentation in life and society** — Bohm's examples of harmful part-thinking: believing we can extract the earth's valuable parts without affecting the whole; treating parts of the body without concern for the whole; tackling crime, poverty, and drug addiction without addressing society as a whole. He argues this fragmenting habit may even lead to our extinction.
- **The mindlike electron and the living rock** — Bohm holds that consciousness is present in degrees of enfoldment in all matter (perhaps why plasmas seem lifelike); dividing the universe into living and nonliving is meaningless — even a rock is in some way alive, with life and intelligence present in matter, "energy," "space," "time," and "the fabric of the entire universe."
- **Andromeda in the thumbnail; Cleopatra meeting Caesar** — the flip side of enfoldment: as every portion of a hologram contains the whole image, every portion of the universe enfolds the whole. If we knew how to access it we could find the Andromeda galaxy in the thumbnail of our left hand, or Cleopatra meeting Caesar for the first time — the whole past and implications for the whole future are enfolded in each small region of space and time; every cell, leaf, raindrop, and dust mote enfolds the cosmos. Talbot ties this to Blake's "To see a World in a Grain of Sand."
- **The zero-point energy calculation** — per current physics, every region of space is awash with fields of waves of varying lengths, each with at least some energy; calculating the minimum energy a wave can possess shows every cubic centimeter of empty space contains more energy than the total energy of all matter in the known universe (the section title renders this as "the energy of a trillion atomic bombs in every cubic centimeter of space"). Some physicists dismiss the calculation as error; Bohm takes it as a glimpse of the implicate order's hidden vastness — physicists ignore it like fish unaware of the water they swim in.
- **The cooled crystal analogy** — a crystal cooled to absolute zero lets a stream of electrons pass through without scattering; raising the temperature makes flaws lose their "transparency" and scatter electrons. From an electron's viewpoint the flaws would look like pieces of "matter" floating in nothingness, but both flaws and "nothingness" are part of the same fabric — the deeper order of the crystal. Likewise matter does not exist independently of space: the universe is a ripple, a "pattern of excitation," on the cosmic sea of energy.
- **Interference patterns everywhere** — Talbot's first piece of experimental support for a holographic universe: even disregarding the implicate sea of energy, space is filled with light and other electromagnetic waves that constantly crisscross and interfere with one another, and since all particles are also waves, physical objects and everything else we perceive in reality are composed of interference patterns — "a fact that has undeniable holographic implications."
- **The Aspect experiment (Alain Aspect, Jean Dalibard, and Gerard Roger, Institute of Optics, University of Paris, 1982)** — in the 1970s technology finally allowed Bell's two-particle experiment; several researchers tried with promising but inconclusive results. In 1982 Aspect's team succeeded: they produced twin photons by heating calcium atoms with lasers, sent each photon in opposite directions through 6.5 meters of pipe and through special filters directing them to one of two possible polarization analyzers; each filter switched between analyzers in 10 billionths of a second — about 30 billionths of a second less than light needs to cross the 13 meters separating the photon sets — ruling out communication by any known physical process. Each photon still correlated its polarization angle with its twin. So either the light-speed ban was violated or the photons were nonlocally connected; since most physicists reject faster-than-light processes, the experiment is generally viewed as virtual proof of nonlocal connection. Physicist Paul Davies (University of Newcastle upon Tyne) adds that since all particles continually interact and separate, nonlocality "is therefore a general property of nature." Talbot: this doesn't prove Bohm's model, but gives it tremendous support.
- **Bohm on testability** — Bohm does not believe any theory, including his own, is correct absolutely — all are approximations, "finite maps" of infinite, indivisible territory — but he is confident future techniques will allow his ideas to be tested; when criticized, he notes theories like superstring theory will probably not be testable for decades.
- **The physics community's verdict** — Yale physicist Lee Smolin does not find Bohm's theory "very compelling, physically." Boston University's Abner Shimony admits he doesn't understand the theory and wonders how literally to take the metaphor, yet calls Bohm courageous, daring, imaginative, and credits him with bringing the questions to the forefront instead of leaving them swept under the rug. Sympathizers include Roger Penrose of Oxford (creator of the modern theory of the black hole), Bernard d'Espagnat of the University of Paris (a leading authority on the conceptual foundations of quantum theory), and Cambridge's Brian Josephson (1973 Nobel Prize in physics), who believes the implicate order may someday lead to including God or Mind within science — an idea he supports.
- **The Pribram synthesis: china cups and beach sand** — combining Bohm with Pribram: the brain mathematically constructs objective reality by interpreting frequencies projected from a deeper order beyond space and time. Pribram: "The kind of mathematical process that Bekesy simulated with his vibrators is basic to how our brains construct our image of a world out there" — the smoothness of fine china and the feel of beach sand are elaborate versions of the phantom limb syndrome. A china cup has two aspects: filtered through the brain's lens it is a cup; without the lens, an interference pattern. Asked which is real, Pribram answers "Both are real to me, or, if you want to say, neither of them are real." We too can be seen as bodies in space or as blurs of interference patterns enfolded in the cosmic hologram — and Bohm thinks the second view may be the more correct, though even "holographic brain looking at holographic universe" is an abstraction: we are not looking at the hologram, we are part of it. Bohm's furthest conclusion: we even construct space and time.

## Thread
Chapter 1 built the first half of the holographic model — Pribram's holographic brain; this chapter builds the second half, Bohm's holographic universe, and then explicitly welds them together: brains construct concrete reality out of a frequency domain that is itself a projection from the implicate order. It converts the hologram from a neuroscience metaphor into a claim about the cosmos itself, backed by the Aspect experiment's evidence for nonlocality. The chapter's closing lines announce the plan for the rest of the book: to explore the effect Bohm and Pribram's ideas have had on researchers in other fields — starting with Part II, "Mind and Body."
